Qualcomm pays $3.92 billion for Modular, names Meta as anchor for a 250-core Dragonfly CPU, and assembles a vertical stack against Nvidia

Qualcomm closed Investor Day on Wednesday with an all-stock $3.92 billion acquisition of Chris Lattner's Modular at 19.2 million shares, formally launched a Dragonfly C1000 250-core 5-gigahertz Arm server CPU with a multi-year, multi-generation Meta supply deal, and rebranded its data-center accelerator as Dragonfly AI300 for liquid or air cooling. CFO Akash Palkhiwala lifted fiscal-2029 non-handset revenue guidance to $40 billion from $22 billion and adjusted EPS to $18 against a $15.26 consensus. The stock jumped 14 percent after hours, and Reuters and Bloomberg reported parallel talks to buy RISC-V startup Tenstorrent for $8 to $10 billion.

SiliconANGLE · CNBC · Modular · 2026-06-24 · Source ↗ ·

Qualcomm closed its annual Investor Day on Wednesday, June 24 with three announcements that rearrange its pitch from a smartphone-modem business with a side data-center bet into a vertically integrated agentic-AI platform. The first is the all-stock $3.92 billion acquisition of Modular Inc., the AI infrastructure software company founded by LLVM and Swift creator Chris Lattner. Qualcomm is paying 19.2 million of its own shares for the company, a price that more than doubles Modular's most recent $380 million round from GV and Greylock. The deal is expected to close in the second half of 2026 subject to customary regulatory approvals. The second is the formal launch of a server-CPU family called Dragonfly C1000, a 250-plus-core processor running above 5 gigahertz that Qualcomm described as offering roughly double the performance-per-watt of comparable server parts. Qualcomm named Meta Platforms as the multi-year, multi-generation customer who will deploy the C1000 in production, without disclosing financial terms. The third is a refresh of the company's data-center accelerator roadmap under the Dragonfly AI300 name, designed to slot into either liquid-cooled or air-cooled racks. The combined package gave the stock a 14 percent jump in after-hours trading and let CFO Akash Palkhiwala lift fiscal-2029 non-handset revenue guidance to $40 billion from a previously communicated $22 billion, with adjusted earnings per share at $18 against a consensus of $15.26.

Why Modular is the load-bearing piece

Modular is best read as a compiler-stack bet against Nvidia's CUDA moat. The company's MAX platform is a vendor-neutral runtime that lets developers write inference code once and execute it across heterogeneous hardware: Nvidia GPUs, AMD chips, Intel processors, Qualcomm NPUs and custom ASICs, without rewriting per target. The companion language Mojo extends Python with the kind of low-level control Triton or CUDA-C provide on Nvidia silicon. The strategic logic is that every non-Nvidia compute platform faces the same headwind: customers who wrote their model-serving code against CUDA's primitives cannot move that code to a different stack without re-tuning. Modular's pitch is that the porting layer is itself a product, and that the supplier who controls it owns the migration path away from Nvidia. Buying it inside Qualcomm gives the Dragonfly C1000 and AI300 a software story they did not have last quarter, and lets Qualcomm sell the Modular layer to customers running other accelerators while still booking the Qualcomm-margin software dollar.

"As agentic AI scales across data centers and edge environments, the industry is moving toward disaggregated, multi-vendor architectures that demand a more open and modern software foundation."— Cristiano Amon, President and CEO, Qualcomm

Lattner reframed the move in personal terms in the Modular blog post that went up immediately after the announcement. He has been here before: LLVM at Apple, then Swift, then a stint inside Google running Brain's hardware-software interface team, then a year on Tesla autopilot. The thesis at each stop has been the same. Compiler infrastructure is where platform power gets cashed. Owning the runtime that everybody else's models route through compounds.

"Joining Qualcomm gives us the scale and platform reach to accelerate that mission. To make AI development more accessible and performant."— Chris Lattner, Co-founder and CEO, Modular

Dragonfly C1000, the Meta deal, and the fiscal-2029 number

The Dragonfly C1000 specification is aggressive. 250 cores at sustained clocks above five gigahertz puts the chip in the high-end x86 server territory currently held by AMD's Bergamo and Sienna SKUs and Intel's Sierra Forest, with a performance-per-watt advantage Qualcomm pegged at roughly two times comparable parts. The chip is Arm-based, derived from the Nuvia design team Qualcomm acquired in 2021. Meta is the first named customer. The supply agreement is multi-year and multi-generation, which is the structure Arm-server CPUs have been seeking since the early Graviton days at AWS: a hyperscaler committing to a roadmap, not a one-off purchase. Financial terms were not disclosed. The Dragonfly AI300 sits on the accelerator side. It is the same chip family Qualcomm previewed earlier this year as AI200 and AI250, now renamed and re-positioned alongside the CPU under one Dragonfly brand. The accelerator is engineered for both liquid and air-cooled deployment, which matters for the second-tier customers who cannot economically rebuild their existing data halls for direct-liquid only.

The fiscal-2029 guidance change is the part that priced into the stock. Non-handset revenue jumped to a $40 billion target from $22 billion previously, almost doubling the trajectory inside the same window. Adjusted EPS at $18 sits roughly 18 percent above the analyst consensus of $15.26. Qualcomm did not specify how the $40 billion splits between automotive, IoT and data center, but the bulk of the new headroom is data center: there is no other business line growing fast enough to add $18 billion of guidance in three fiscal years.

The parallel Tenstorrent talks

Reuters and Bloomberg reported during the day that Qualcomm is in separate talks to acquire AI chip startup Tenstorrent for $8 to $10 billion, several times the price Qualcomm just paid for Modular. Tenstorrent is the RISC-V-based AI compute company led by Jim Keller, and it has been the most credible non-Nvidia hardware bet outside the hyperscalers' internal ASIC programs. The pattern across the day reads as Qualcomm spending what it needs to spend, in equity, to assemble the full vertical stack: CPU and accelerator silicon for the hardware layer, Modular for the runtime, RISC-V optionality at the architecture layer, Meta as the anchor customer at the demand layer. Whether the Tenstorrent deal closes is the open question that determines whether the architectural breadth claim holds.

The competitive read for the rest of the industry is mixed. Nvidia keeps the CUDA installed base and the training market. AMD and Intel keep their x86 footprint. The pressure lands on the second-tier accelerator startups, who have largely competed on price and on per-workload performance and who now face a Qualcomm with shipping hyperscaler customers and a compiler runtime under one roof. The pressure also lands on the hyperscaler internal silicon programs at Google, AWS, Microsoft and Meta, all of whom face the same compiler problem Modular was built to solve. Meta's decision to take the C1000 first is the most informative single fact in the announcement: it suggests Meta sees value in a merchant-silicon partner with a credible runtime, not only in extending its own MTIA and Athena programs.

Anthropic tells the Senate that Alibaba ran 28.8 million Claude conversations through 25,000 fake accounts to distill Mythos Preview

CNBC · 2026-06-25 · Source ↗ ·

The June 10 letter to Senate Banking chair Tim Scott and ranking member Elizabeth Warren names DeepSeek, Moonshot and MiniMax campaigns at 150,000, 3.4 million and 13 million exchanges. Alibaba H-shares fell as much as 4.9 percent to a sixteen-month low.

CNBC and Bloomberg surfaced an Anthropic letter to the Senate Banking Committee on Wednesday alleging that Alibaba's Qwen language-model lab ran approximately 28.8 million conversations with Claude across roughly 25,000 fraudulent accounts between April 22 and June 5, 2026. The letter, dated June 10 and addressed to chair Tim Scott and ranking member Elizabeth Warren, describes the campaign as the largest known distillation attack Anthropic has documented against any of its models. The conversations targeted Mythos Preview, the pre-release checkpoint Anthropic ran inside a limited-access alpha during April and May. Anthropic's argument to the committee is that Alibaba systematically harvested Mythos-class outputs to train its next Qwen generation, an act the company frames as both intellectual-property theft and a national-security concern given Alibaba's recent addition to the Pentagon's Chinese military companies list.

The letter also names three smaller but adjacent campaigns. DeepSeek conversations totaled approximately 150,000 across the same window; Moonshot at 3.4 million; MiniMax at 13 million. Each campaign used patterns Anthropic's threat-intelligence team identifies as machine-orchestrated rather than organic: identical prompt scaffolds, distillation-shaped output requests, request distributions that match training-corpus harvesting rather than user behavior. The Alibaba campaign is the outlier in scale by roughly an order of magnitude. The timing is what gives the disclosure its political weight: the Commerce Department issued its June 12 export-control directive walling Mythos 5 and Fable 5 behind US-government access controls two days after the letter, and Anthropic's framing puts Alibaba's harvesting directly inside the chain of facts that motivated the directive.

"We have observed coordinated distillation campaigns against our Mythos-class models on a scale that, in our judgement, falls outside any plausible legitimate research or development use."— Anthropic letter to Senate Banking Committee, 2026-06-10

The market reaction was immediate. Alibaba H-shares in Hong Kong fell as much as 4.9 percent to a sixteen-month low on the day the letter surfaced, and ADRs dropped 3.99 percent in the prior US session as the story broke after hours. Bloomberg's Wednesday coverage put the H-share decline in the context of a broader Chinese-tech selloff and a $3.8 billion foreign net-selling print across Hong Kong-listed names. Alibaba responded with a public denial calling the allegations "speculative and uncorroborated," and did not engage with the specific account count or conversation totals. The Chinese ministries of foreign affairs and commerce did not comment by Friday.

The downstream policy story is the more durable one. The letter lands as Senate Banking is drafting the framework for a Chinese-AI-investment review that mirrors the Treasury outbound-investment screen. Anthropic's framing of distillation as a vector for capability transfer feeds directly into that framework. The company has effectively shifted from defending its model perimeter to using its model perimeter as evidence for policy escalation, a move that is harder to undo than the technical countermeasures Anthropic could deploy at the API layer. The letter also marks the first time a US frontier lab has publicly named distillation totals on a Chinese lab in regulatory correspondence, setting a documentation precedent OpenAI and Google have so far avoided.

Commerce Secretary Lutnick partially lifts the Mythos 5 export ban for roughly 100 US institutions; Fable 5 stays globally offline

Semafor · 2026-06-27 · Source ↗ ·

A Friday-evening letter from Howard Lutnick reopens Mythos 5 to a vetted list of roughly 100 institutions while Fable 5 remains suspended. Anthropic chief compute officer Tom Brown led the two-week negotiation.

Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick sent a letter the evening of Friday, June 26 that partially lifts the June 12 export-control suspension on Anthropic's Mythos 5 model. The release applies to a vetted list of US institutions enumerated as Annex A to the letter, totaling roughly 100 universities, national labs, defense and critical-infrastructure operators, and named US technology companies. The Annex A institutions regain Mythos 5 access on the existing Anthropic deployment surface beginning Monday, June 29, under audit requirements that include per-query logging, monthly transparency reports to Commerce, and a $50 million escrow against unauthorized re-distribution. Fable 5, the general-purpose flagship, remains globally suspended.

The negotiation was led on Anthropic's side by chief compute officer Tom Brown, with Commerce undersecretary for industry and security Alan Estevez and White House AI advisor Mira Murati on the government side. Brown spent two weeks in Washington starting the Monday after the June 12 directive, an account Semafor pieced together from a Lutnick spokesperson, two White House officials and four Anthropic employees. The structural concession Anthropic agreed to is the audit and escrow framework. The structural concession Commerce agreed to is the partial release. Neither side framed the result as final: Lutnick's letter describes Annex A as "the first tranche" and commits to a 60-day review of Fable 5 access.

"Annex A is the first tranche. We will conduct a 60-day review of additional institutions and a separate review of Fable 5 in parallel, with the objective of restoring lawful commercial deployment as expeditiously as national security permits."— Howard Lutnick, letter to Anthropic, 2026-06-26

The list itself is the most-watched single document of the week in Washington. Annex A includes the national-lab triad (Los Alamos, Sandia, Lawrence Livermore), the four service academies, twelve research universities with active DARPA contracts, the major defense primes (Lockheed, Raytheon, General Dynamics, Northrop, L3Harris), three financial-infrastructure operators (DTCC, the Fed Reserve Bank of New York, OCC), and a list of named US technology companies that includes Microsoft, Google, AWS, Meta, Nvidia, AMD, Micron and Cisco. The pharmaceutical sector is conspicuously absent; the energy sector includes only the DOE national labs and not the utilities. Commerce officials told Semafor the second tranche will widen the energy and biotech inclusions following the 60-day review.

The market response was uneven. Anthropic's secondary-market shares traded at the post-Series H valuation of approximately $965 billion without material change in the after-hours session. Polymarket's $21.99 million end-of-June best-AI-model market closed Saturday with Anthropic at 99 percent. OpenAI's GPT-5.6 trusted-partner preview, restricted to about twenty partners under a parallel Trump-administration request, continued without expansion. The broader read is that the export-control framework, even in its partial-release form, is now the default architecture for Mythos-class deployment for the foreseeable horizon, and that the question for the second half of 2026 is which tranches expand and which sectors remain walled.

OpenAI previews GPT-5.6 in three sibling tiers (Sol, Terra, Luna) and gates access behind a Trump-administration trusted-partner list

VentureBeat · TechCrunch · 2026-06-26 · Source ↗ ·

Sol at $5/$30, Terra at $2.50/$15, Luna at $1/$6 per million tokens. Chief global affairs officer Chris Lehane tells TechCrunch the trusted-partner gate "shouldn't become the long-term default."

OpenAI unveiled GPT-5.6 on Thursday, June 25 in three sibling tiers branded as Sol, Terra and Luna. Sol is the flagship, priced at $5 per million input tokens and $30 per million output tokens. Terra is the mid tier at $2.50 input and $15 output. Luna is the cost-optimized tier at $1 input and $6 output. The three checkpoints share the same pre-training run; the differences are reasoning-depth, tool-use scaffolding, and post-training data, with Terra positioned as the everyday production workhorse and Luna for high-volume retrieval and routing. OpenAI's published benchmarks put Sol at 78.4 percent on Terminal-Bench, in striking distance of Anthropic Mythos-class numbers, with Terra at 72.1 and Luna at 64.3. The release is structured as a limited preview rather than a general-availability launch.

The trusted-partner gate is the editorially significant detail. OpenAI's announcement, reinforced by TechCrunch's Thursday reporting, restricts the GPT-5.6 preview to roughly twenty partners enumerated on a list shared with the company by the Trump administration. The list is tied to the June 2 White House executive order on responsible frontier deployment, which directs federal agencies to coordinate frontier-model access for partners in critical-infrastructure, defense and government-cloud sectors. OpenAI's chief global affairs officer Chris Lehane framed the company's compliance posture without enthusiasm, telling TechCrunch the trusted-partner gate "shouldn't become the long-term default" while confirming OpenAI is complying with the request. The gating mirrors, in structure if not in scale, the Annex A architecture Commerce applied to Anthropic's Mythos 5 the same week.

"This shouldn't become the long-term default for how frontier models reach the public. But the request was specific, the legal basis was clear, and we are complying."— Chris Lehane, Chief Global Affairs Officer, OpenAI

The GPT-5.6 system card discloses 700,000 A100e GPU-hours spent on automated jailbreak discovery during pre-release red-teaming, the largest single safety-evaluation compute budget OpenAI has reported. The card claims Mythos-class numbers on Terminal-Bench and exposes a new "deliberation" trace format that exposes reasoning steps to enterprise customers under DPA. The pricing structure is the operational play: at $1 input and $6 output, Luna undercuts every comparably-scaled frontier model OpenAI has shipped, and at $5 input and $30 output, Sol prices in the same envelope as Anthropic Fable 5 before the June 12 suspension. The market will not see those prices on a credit card until the trusted-partner window opens, an outcome Lehane did not commit to in either direction.

Polymarket's GPT-5.6 launch-window market collapsed across the same week, with traders abandoning the June 22 to 28 window as odds dropped from 83 percent to 18 percent and July priced near 94 percent. The trusted-partner restriction is part of what compressed the market: the launch happened, but the broader public availability that traders had been pricing did not. By the time GPT-5.6 reaches the general developer surface, the geopolitical architecture of frontier-model deployment will look meaningfully different from the API-key default that defined the field through 2025.

Google bakes computer use into Gemini 3.5 Flash at 78.4 on OSWorld-Verified for one-fifth the GPT-5.5 token price

TechTimes · Google AI · 2026-06-25 · Source ↗ ·

Computer use moves from a standalone Gemini 2.5 model to a native tool inside 3.5 Flash, with input pricing of $1.50 versus OpenAI's $5 and output of $9 versus $30. Gemini in Chrome adds a Select from screen tool.

Google promoted computer use on Wednesday, June 24 from a standalone preview model to a native tool inside Gemini 3.5 Flash. The change is structural rather than cosmetic. Where the previous computer-use surface sat behind a separate Gemini 2.5 Computer Use endpoint with a one-off invocation pattern, the new architecture exposes the screen-control capability as a first-class tool in the Gemini 3.5 Flash function-calling API. Developers can mix screen actions with other tools in a single multi-step reasoning loop, and the underlying vision-and-action model shares the Flash base-model context rather than handing off to a specialized checkpoint.

The benchmark numbers are aggressive. Gemini 3.5 Flash with computer use scores 78.4 on OSWorld-Verified, the consensus desktop-agent benchmark. The score puts Flash in the same neighborhood as OpenAI's GPT-5.5 with its Operator tooling, and 13.3 percentage points above Gemini 3 Flash. The pricing is what changes the deployment calculus. Input tokens are $1.50 per million against GPT-5.5's $5. Output tokens are $9 against $30. The five-to-one input ratio and the three-to-one output ratio compound when an agent runs multi-step browser or desktop workflows where token counts can hit hundreds of thousands per task. On a representative knowledge-worker agent loop Google ran during the announcement, GPT-5.5 cost approximately $4.20 per completed task versus Gemini 3.5 Flash at approximately $0.74.

"Computer use is not a separate model anymore. It is a capability you compose with the rest of the Gemini 3.5 Flash toolset, at Flash pricing."— Demis Hassabis, CEO, Google DeepMind

Gemini in Chrome got a parallel update. A new Select from screen tool lets the user draw a rectangle around any region of the browser viewport and pass it to Gemini as visual input, removing the screenshot-and-upload step that the prior generation required. The tool composes with Search, Maps and Workspace integrations, and Google framed the combination as the first browser-resident agent that can read, act and cite in one loop. Gemini 3.5 Pro continued to slip past its previously-communicated June general-availability window, with Google blaming "final alignment work" without specifying a new date.

The competitive read is more complicated than the price-comparison chart suggests. OpenAI's GPT-5.6 preview, gated behind the Trump-administration trusted-partner list, is not on the price-comparison chart at all because it is not commercially available. Anthropic's Mythos 5 and Fable 5 are in the same position post the June 12 suspension. Google therefore has, for the moment, the only frontier-tier computer-use surface a developer can put on a credit card. Whether that holds through July depends on which trusted-partner gates open and which do not.

The broader memory market is the context that makes the Micron print resonate. DRAM average selling prices ran up roughly 260 percent across the past twelve months on AI-server demand outstripping supply. Apple and Microsoft both hiked iPad, Mac and Xbox prices the same week and cited the memory shortage directly. CNBC's Friday feature called the supply imbalance an "absolute existential crisis" for smaller device makers that lack the SCA-scale purchasing leverage to hold their memory cost line. Micron's stock has run roughly 800 percent over the trailing twelve months. The company that sat in the cyclical-commodity drawer of the semiconductor industry now prices in the same valuation envelope as the model-layer giants whose training runs it supplies.

CNBC calls the memory crunch an "existential crisis" for smaller device makers as Micron DRAM ASP runs up 260 percent

CNBC · 2026-06-27 · Source ↗ ·

Apple and Microsoft hike iPad, Mac and Xbox prices and cite memory by name. Micron stock runs 800 percent over twelve months. IDC analyst says smaller OEMs without supply contracts face an "absolute existential crisis."

CNBC published a Friday-evening feature framing the AI-driven memory shortage as a structural threat to consumer electronics makers that lack hyperscaler-scale purchasing leverage. The piece quotes IDC analyst Mario Morales calling the supply imbalance an "absolute existential crisis" for OEMs operating below the tier that can sign multi-year capacity-reservation contracts with Micron, Samsung or SK Hynix. The article uses Apple and Microsoft as the demonstrative cases. Both companies hiked iPad, Mac and Xbox prices the same week and explicitly named memory cost as the driver. Mark Gurman's Sunday Power On column extended the pattern with M6/M7 silicon roadmap details and confirmed that the upcoming Touch MacBook Pro prices in at the new memory-inflated tier.

The supply math is unambiguous. Micron's DRAM average selling price has run up approximately 260 percent across the past twelve months, the largest sustained price increase the company has reported. HBM4e and HBM5 supply is committed through 2030 to hyperscaler and frontier-lab buyers under sixteen-quarter Strategic Customer Agreements. The non-HBM commodity DRAM supply that consumer device makers depend on flows through the same fab capacity, and Micron has shifted approximately 38 percent of its fab output toward HBM over the past six quarters according to the company's Q3 disclosure. The displaced commodity-DRAM supply is the line item that runs through Apple's iPad bill of materials, Microsoft's Xbox build sheet and every Android OEM's component table.

"For the smaller guys without long-term contracts, this is an absolute existential crisis. They cannot pass the cost forward fast enough to stay solvent."— Mario Morales, IDC Group Vice President for Semiconductors

The price-pass-through chain is visible at multiple levels. Apple hiked the entry-level iPad by $50 and the M5 MacBook Pro by $100 with the M-series memory-bump SKUs absorbing the full memory delta. Microsoft hiked Xbox Series X by $80. Samsung's mobile division reported gross-margin compression in the second quarter despite holding device prices flat, a pattern that suggests smaller Android OEMs who cannot lift price are absorbing the squeeze on margin. The Pinpoint Press piece by Rick Newman the same week argued AI-driven memory inflation is now feeding into headline CPI, with the index running from 2.4 to 4.2 percent over the past nine months.

The trend has a name now. CNBC framed it as "AI inflation" and quoted both buy-side and sell-side strategists pricing in a longer cycle than the last commodity-DRAM correction. The structural difference is the SCA architecture: prior memory cycles broke when supply caught up with discretionary demand. The current cycle is anchored to hyperscaler capex commitments through 2030 that do not flex with consumer-electronics demand. The squeeze is a feature, not a bug, of the long-term capacity-reservation contracts the memory makers signed with the AI buyers. That is what makes it existential for the device makers who sit outside that contract perimeter.

Sakana AI ships Fugu and Beijing's 360 unveils Tulongfeng and Yitianzhen as the US trusted-partner gate forces an Asian response

Inside 24 hours Tokyo's Sakana AI ships a frontier-agent model called Fugu and Beijing's 360 unveils Tulongfeng for vulnerability discovery and Yitianzhen for defense, both pitched at customers who do not want their AI access running through US export approval.

The Friday TechCrunch piece by Rita Liao groups three Asian frontier-model releases the same week as a coordinated market response to the US export-control architecture that has walled off Anthropic's Fable 5 globally since June 12 and that gated OpenAI's GPT-5.6 preview to roughly twenty trusted partners on June 25.
