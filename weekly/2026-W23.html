Anthropic vaults past OpenAI on valuation, ships Opus 4.8, queues Mythos, and hires Karpathy in one week

A $13 billion primary at $965 billion post-money closed Friday, days after Opus 4.8 went live with the highest SWE-bench Verified score on record and Anthropic disclosed plans to release Mythos-class cyber tooling broadly. Andrej Karpathy joined the pre-training team the prior Monday. The four moves landed inside ten trading days and reordered the frontier-lab ledger.

Fortune · CNBC · Axios · TechCrunch · 2026-05-19 — 2026-05-29 · Source ↗ ·

Anthropic closed a $13 billion primary fundraise at a $965 billion post-money valuation on Friday, May 29, becoming the most valuable private AI lab and overtaking OpenAI for the first time. Iconiq Capital led, with Fidelity, Lightspeed and Altimeter participating; secondary tender activity pushed effective valuation marks above $1 trillion in the days that followed. The round closed three days after Anthropic shipped Claude Opus 4.8 to general availability and four days after the company published its plan to bring "Mythos-class" cyber-capable models to enterprise customers in the coming weeks. The week opened with Andrej Karpathy, the OpenAI founding engineer and former Tesla AI lead, announcing he had joined Anthropic's pre-training team under Nick Joseph.

Each move is large on its own. Together they shift the lab leaderboard. OpenAI's last marked valuation sits at $850 billion from its March secondary; xAI's last raise valued it at $200 billion; Google's AI work runs inside a parent worth $2.4 trillion but does not pencil out separately. Anthropic's $965 billion mark is now the highest private valuation in technology, ahead of SpaceX's $400 billion and ByteDance's $400 billion last marks. The company's Q1 disclosure put annualized run-rate revenue at $44 billion, eighty times the prior year. Customers spending more than $1 million a year doubled to over a thousand in two months. The new capital is committed almost entirely to compute, against the AWS and Google Cloud contracts coming online through 2027.

Opus 4.8 and what it changes

Claude Opus 4.8 went live Tuesday, May 26 across the Claude API, Claude Code, Cursor and Anthropic's first-party products. Anthropic's reported scores put Opus 4.8 at 82.4% on SWE-bench Verified, the highest on the public leaderboard, ahead of GPT-5.5 (78.6%), Gemini 3.5 Flash agent harness (76.2% Terminal-Bench 2.1), and Qwen 3.7 Max (60.6 SWE-Pro). Pricing held flat at $15 per million input and $75 per million output tokens with prompt caching unchanged. The release adds explicit constitutional-AI improvements on tool-use refusal rates and a wider extended-thinking budget. The same release shipped Mythos-class capabilities behind a new "containment" wrapper Anthropic describes as required before any wider deployment.

"At present, no company — including Anthropic — has developed safeguards strong enough to prevent such models from being misused."— Anthropic, Mythos public-release announcement, May 25

Mythos and Project Glasswing

Anthropic said it intends to expand Project Glasswing, the controlled program through which select governments and enterprises currently access Mythos, before opening Mythos-class capability more broadly. Mythos identified 6,202 high-or-critical vulnerabilities across 1,000+ open-source projects in its preview run; of 1,752 high-or-critical findings reviewed, 90.6% were valid. A critical wolfSSL flaw discovered by Mythos affects billions of devices and enables certificate forgery; the company said only 75 of 530 reported high-or-critical bugs have been patched as of the announcement. The roadmap names "near future" as the target for wider release without committing to a date. The EU told CNBC it was seeking to intensify talks with the U.S. on advanced cyber AI models in the wake of the Mythos disclosure.

Karpathy's mandate

Andrej Karpathy started at Anthropic the week of May 18 under pre-training lead Nick Joseph. Reporting from TechCrunch and CNBC says his mandate is a new team using Claude itself to accelerate pre-training research on the next model. He paused Eureka Labs, the education startup he founded in 2024, to take the role. The hire matters for two reasons. Karpathy is one of a handful of researchers who has built both the theory and the production-scale training systems behind a frontier-quality model, having led GPT pre-training at OpenAI and the autopilot stack at Tesla. And his departure removes from OpenAI's orbit one of the most visible original-eleven names, two months after Ilya Sutskever's SSI raised at $32 billion and three months after John Schulman's exit announcement at Mira Murati's lab.

"I've joined Anthropic. I think the next few years at the frontier of LLMs will be especially formative. I am very excited to join the team here and get back to R&D."— Andrej Karpathy, May 19, on X

What the convergence means

The cluster matters because each piece reinforces the others. Opus 4.8 gives Anthropic the top frontier-model SWE-bench position. The $965 billion mark lets it secure the AWS and Google Cloud compute draw without further dilution. Karpathy gives it the rare combination of theory and infrastructure required to keep widening the model-quality lead. And Mythos turns the lab into a defense customer in addition to a developer customer. Anthropic now has the strongest revenue, the highest valuation, the top model on SWE-bench, the most credible cyber-capability story, and a fresh founding-tier engineer. OpenAI retains the larger user base, the Stargate infrastructure commitment, and the closer White House relationship under Greg Brockman. The two companies' valuation and revenue gap is the smallest it has been since the founding of Anthropic, and Anthropic crossed in front this week.

"The capital is committed to compute. The 2027 cloud commitments require it. The model quality requires it. The customer pipeline requires it."— Dario Amodei, Anthropic CEO, on the Fortune funding call

Risks remain. The $965 billion mark embeds a roughly 22x forward revenue multiple on the Q1 disclosed ARR, on the higher end of frontier-lab marks. The Mythos public-release plan is contingent on safeguards Anthropic itself says do not yet exist. Karpathy's mandate is research; the production pre-training stack stays with Nick Joseph's organization, and a meaningful team has to form around the new hire. Opus 4.8's lead on SWE-bench is on a benchmark that the field increasingly distrusts, with VentureBeat reporting this same week that buyers' deployment outcomes do not track benchmark rankings. None of those caveats undoes the week. They set the bar for what the next two quarters have to look like.

Standard tier is $0.50 per million input tokens and $2.50 per million output tokens; the Fast tier, which is the default inside Cursor, is $3 and $15. The training-method delta is what Cursor highlighted in the release post. First, targeted reinforcement learning with textual feedback: instead of issuing a single end-of-run reward, the model receives localized hints at failed tool calls and adjusts inside the trajectory. The post describes an on-policy distillation KL loss that moves the student's token probabilities toward a teacher's. Second, a 25x expansion in synthetic-data volume, including a novel "feature deletion" exercise where the model removes a feature, then reimplements it from the test suite. Third, infrastructure upgrades to sharded Muon optimizers with dual-mesh HSDP for MoE-scale training. Cursor's CursorBench v3.1 score landed at 63.2 and SWE-Bench Multilingual at 79.8, both inside the top-three open-or-closed band published this month.

"Composer 2.5 is a substantial improvement in intelligence and behavior over Composer 2, better at sustained work on long-running tasks."— Cursor Team, release post

The release post closes with a forward note: Cursor and SpaceXAI are training a substantially larger model from scratch using 10x more total compute. The implication is that Composer 2.5 is the last open-checkpoint-derived release, and the next model is Cursor's first true frontier-class training run. Promotional double-usage for the first week tracked the rollout and was over by May 25.

AgentDoG 1.5 proposes a lightweight alignment layer between an agent and its tools

Hugging Face Papers · 2026-05-29 · Source ↗ ·

A tool-call observer that scores each candidate invocation against a policy prompt and short-circuits the model when a call would violate the policy. Reports a 38% reduction in policy-violating tool calls without retraining.

AgentDoG 1.5 is the second release of a small policy layer that sits between an agent model and its tools. The paper, posted to Hugging Face Papers May 29, proposes a tool-call observer that scores each candidate invocation against a structured policy prompt before the call fires. When the score crosses a threshold the observer can short-circuit, return a synthetic refusal to the agent, or escalate to a human. The authors report a 38% reduction in policy-violating tool calls on a held-out benchmark with no model retraining required.

The interesting claim is the locality. AgentDoG does not modify the base model, sits at the tool-call boundary, and can be added to any MCP-compatible agent stack. The design echoes the "constitutional AI as runtime filter" pattern Anthropic uses internally but does not publish; the AgentDoG team's value is that they made the same pattern open-source and inspectable. The Hugging Face paper has model weights for the policy scorer and reproduction scripts; the authors do not provide the policy prompts themselves, which limits cross-team reproducibility.
