Apple names Tang Tan and Chang Liu in a trade-secret suit against OpenAI, and ties it to the Gemini-Siri pivot

Apple filed in the Northern District of California on Saturday, July 11, alleging Chief Hardware Officer Tang Tan directed candidates to bring "actual parts" to OpenAI interviews for show-and-tell sessions, and that former senior electrical engineer Chang Liu skipped his exit interview, kept his company laptop, and exploited a post-departure Apple internal-cloud bug to download more than a thousand pages of confidential documents. The complaint reaches beyond the two named defendants to the more than 400 ex-Apple employees now inside OpenAI or IO Products, and Apple ties the filing directly to its March 2026 decision to build the autumn 2026 Siri revamp on Google Gemini rather than OpenAI. Analysts read the suit as Apple's opening move to seek injunctive relief that could block OpenAI from onboarding further Apple hires while the case is pending.

Business Today · Washington Post · 2026-07-11 · Source ↗ ·

Apple filed suit against OpenAI in the United States District Court for the Northern District of California on Saturday, July 11, alleging that OpenAI's hiring of former Apple hardware executives crossed the line from ordinary lateral movement into orchestrated trade-secret misappropriation. The complaint names two individuals directly. The first is Tang Tan, Apple's former Chief Hardware Officer, who joined OpenAI in early 2025 and now runs hardware development for the joint OpenAI–IO Products device program alongside Jony Ive. The second is Chang Liu, a former senior electrical engineer on Apple's Vision Products Group. The suit names IO Products LLC, the Ive-founded design firm OpenAI acquired for $6.5 billion last year, as an added defendant.

The core factual allegations are unusually specific. Apple says Tan personally directed candidates for OpenAI hardware roles to bring "actual parts" from their Apple work to their OpenAI interviews for show-and-tell sessions, a claim that if credited would move the case out of the ambiguous territory of trade secrets kept in memory and into the far more actionable territory of physical hardware exfiltration. For Liu, Apple alleges he skipped his exit interview entirely, retained his company laptop after his separation, and exploited a bug in Apple's internal cloud system that had gone unpatched to download more than one thousand pages of confidential technical documents in the weeks after his departure was recorded.

Why the timing matters

Apple's decision to file now, more than a year after Tan's move, is inseparable from its March 2026 decision to base the autumn 2026 Siri revamp on Google Gemini rather than on any OpenAI model. That pivot was already read across the industry as a rebuke; the trade-secret complaint reframes it as the natural consequence of a supplier relationship that Apple now positions as having been used as cover for organized poaching. The complaint reaches beyond the two named defendants to the more than 400 former Apple employees now inside OpenAI or IO Products, characterizing the pattern as departure "at every level" of Apple's hardware organization.

"The pattern is not lateral movement. It is a coordinated departure at every level of Apple's hardware organization, engineered by executives who took their trade secrets with them and by systems designed to receive them."— Apple complaint, filed 2026-07-11 in N.D. Cal.

The relief Apple is asking for is broader than damages. The complaint requests injunctive relief that would block OpenAI from hiring further ex-Apple engineers during the pendency of the case, and it asks the court to enjoin OpenAI from using any hardware design work traceable to Tan or Liu. In practice, either injunction would be operationally severe for the joint OpenAI–IO Products hardware program, which is on a compressed schedule and whose most senior hardware leader is Tan himself.

OpenAI's expected defense

OpenAI has not filed a formal response. In pre-filing correspondence surfaced in the complaint's exhibits, OpenAI counsel argued that the trade-secret framing collapses the ordinary lateral-employment right into a conclusion that engineers cannot leave one Silicon Valley employer for another. The San Francisco tech-employment bar will be watching for whether Judge Cynthia Rocha, drawn in the initial case assignment, treats the "show-and-tell" allegation as the pleading's center of gravity or as decoration for a claim otherwise built on non-compete-shaped arguments California courts have historically been skeptical of.

The counter-narrative from OpenAI supporters is that Apple is using the courts to accomplish what it could not accomplish through retention: keeping its hardware leaders in place. That framing was pushed hardest on X by ex-Apple hires now inside OpenAI, several of whom posted variations of the same argument that Apple's hardware culture had made departure inevitable and that the "1,000 pages" allegation against Liu is being treated as representative when it is a single-instance claim.

"California is a right-to-work state. If Apple's hardware leaders had wanted to stay, Apple would have kept them. This complaint reads like retention by subpoena."— Aravind Narayan, ex-Apple senior director now at IO Products (X)

The Gemini-Siri backdrop

Apple confirmed in March 2026 that Siri's autumn revamp would run on Google Gemini rather than on any OpenAI or in-house Apple Intelligence model. The choice was framed publicly as a capability decision. The complaint's exhibit E ties it to Tan's departure directly, arguing that the hardware and software Siri roadmap Apple built internally was compromised by the leakage of hardware-side design work, and that Google was chosen over OpenAI because Apple could not credibly build a Siri stack that depended on models trained by a supplier its own former hardware chief now works for.

Wall Street's read on the filing was mixed. Apple shares closed down 1.4 percent on Monday July 13's trading, more on the injunctive-hiring risk to the Ive program than on Apple's damages-recovery position. OpenAI's private secondary-market pricing softened on the Circle secondary marketplace by roughly 8 percent Monday morning against the pre-filing close, more on tail-risk pricing than on any operational disruption. The most likely near-term consequence is a temporary restraining order phase over the next thirty days that will determine whether OpenAI can continue to hire from Apple's hardware organization while the case proceeds.

OpenAI posts a claimed GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra proof of the 50-year-old Cycle Double Cover Conjecture

Crypto Briefing · Ethan Knight X · 2026-07-11 · Source ↗

OpenAI's Ethan Knight posted a claimed proof of the Cycle Double Cover Conjecture generated by GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra in under an hour using 64 coordinated subagents. The 50-year-old conjecture, posed by Szekeres in 1973 and formalized by Seymour in 1979, has attracted prior arXiv proof claims that were later withdrawn. Peer review is beginning now.

OpenAI's Ethan Knight posted a claimed proof of the Cycle Double Cover Conjecture to X on Saturday, July 11, along with the complete PDF and the coordinating prompt used to generate it. The proof was generated by GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra running with 64 coordinated subagents, all on one hour of wall-clock time. The Cycle Double Cover Conjecture, posed by Szekeres in 1973 and formalized independently by Seymour in 1979, states that every bridgeless graph admits a collection of cycles such that every edge belongs to exactly two cycles in the collection. The conjecture is one of the longest-standing open problems in graph theory.

Prior arXiv proof claims for the conjecture have been withdrawn over the last decade, most recently a claimed 2021 proof by a graduate student at ETH Zurich that survived initial community review before a gap in the induction case was identified. The mathematician community's default posture on any new claim is skepticism until independent verification lands. The OpenAI submission has been sent to Combinatorica for peer review; Terry Tao acknowledged the paper on Saturday evening, saying he had begun a read-through but had no view yet on soundness.

"I have not verified the proof but I have started the read-through. The structure of the induction case is different from every previous attempt I have seen, and the argument at page 47 is where I will spend the most time."— Terry Tao, mathematician, on X 2026-07-11

The 64-subagent scaffold

The prompt Knight posted alongside the PDF describes the 64-subagent scaffold. Sol Ultra runs a top-level orchestrator that decomposes the proof into subclaims, dispatches each to a subagent for a proof attempt, collects results, and re-decomposes on failure. The 64-count is the maximum parallelism Sol Ultra can address at the Ultra tier ($40 input, $200 output per million tokens); the actual proof generation cost was quoted by Knight as roughly $1,800 in inference. The scaffold is closer to a search process than a single-shot generation, and the community's initial technical read is that the interesting result is less the proof itself (peer review pending) than the scaffold's ability to sustain a 50-page argument coherently across 64 parallel workers.

The competitive context sits alongside DeepMind's AlphaProof, which moved to general availability in Gemini Enterprise the same week. AlphaProof is a specialized theorem-proving system; Sol Ultra is a general-purpose frontier model running a scaffold. If the CDC proof holds, it would be the first major open-conjecture result claimed by a general model rather than by a purpose-built prover. Community verification will be the deciding factor over the next 60 to 90 days.

Search crawlers cover any bot whose primary purpose is indexing for a search result (Googlebot, Bingbot, Applebot Extended, DuckDuckBot). Agent crawlers cover any bot fetching a page to feed an AI agent's live response (Anthropic's ClaudeBot when invoked by a user, OpenAI's SearchGPT, Perplexity, Grok Search). Training crawlers cover any bot fetching a page for model training (OAI-SearchBot with the training header, Common Crawl, GPTBot, CCBot).

The most consequential piece of the release is the September 15 default flip: on that date, Cloudflare will block Agent and Training crawlers by default on ad-supported pages for all new domains, while Search crawlers remain allowed. Existing domains can opt into the new defaults immediately; the September 15 date affects only domains onboarded after that date, but Cloudflare said it expects operational pressure on existing domains to migrate to the same defaults inside 12 months.

"A search crawler pays the publisher in traffic. An agent crawler pays the publisher in nothing. The default should distinguish the two, and starting September 15 it will."— Matthew Prince, CEO, Cloudflare

The multi-purpose crawler problem gets a clean answer in the new controls. A bot that identifies as both a search crawler and an agent crawler (Googlebot, Applebot Extended, Bingbot with the Copilot header) inherits the most restrictive policy per class from the domain's rule table. In practice that means if a domain blocks Agent traffic and allows Search traffic, Googlebot's search-only fetches pass through and its Copilot-triggered fetches are blocked. Cloudflare rolled out a header-inspection layer to enforce the split, which the major AI companies (Google, Microsoft, Anthropic, OpenAI, Perplexity, xAI) said they would support by August 1.
