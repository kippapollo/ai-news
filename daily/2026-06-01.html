Commerce closes the China-subsidiary loophole on Nvidia Blackwell, Rubin and AMD MI350x exports

The Bureau of Industry and Security issued weekend guidance Sunday extending advanced-chip license rules to any entity "headquartered in China, regardless of location," shutting a year-old gap that let frontier accelerators reach Chinese AI labs through Malaysian and Singaporean shells. Industry sources told CNBC that "hundreds of thousands" of restricted chips moved through the gap. Nvidia and AMD shares are set up for a Monday reset.

CNBC / Reuters · 2026-05-31 · Source ↗ ·

The Bureau of Industry and Security issued weekend guidance Sunday clarifying that license requirements for advanced AI accelerators now follow the corporate parent rather than the address on the shipping label. Chips destined for any company "headquartered in China, regardless of location" are now subject to the same end-use restrictions that apply to shipments to the mainland itself. CNBC and Reuters carried the guidance on Sunday morning before the Monday market open. The change closes a loophole that opened in May 2025 when the Trump administration declined to enforce the Biden-era AI Diffusion Rule, and that industry sources estimate moved hundreds of thousands of restricted chips through Malaysian and Singaporean subsidiaries during the gap year.

The covered hardware list spans the entire current and near-term frontier. Nvidia's Blackwell family (B100, B200, GB200 racks) and the Rubin successor sampling now into hyperscalers both sit on the list; AMD's MI350x, the accelerator the company has been positioning against Nvidia's Hopper and early Blackwell stock, is named alongside them. Smaller-die variants Nvidia previously shipped through the gap, including the H20 and a Blackwell-class chip designed to clear earlier export thresholds, fall under the same parent-tracing rule.

How the loophole worked

Under the AI Diffusion Rule the Biden administration finalized in January 2025, controlled accelerators required validated end-user licenses to ship to any of approximately fifty countries, with country-level quotas that rose with the receiving entity's compliance posture. The Trump administration shelved enforcement of that rule shortly after taking office, leaving in place only the older country-specific controls that targeted shipments physically destined for the People's Republic, Macau, and a list of specified hostile entities. Chinese-headquartered firms responded by routing orders through Singaporean, Malaysian, and Emirati subsidiaries, which were not on the restricted list and whose end-use certifications were treated as their own.

Chris McGuire, a former State Department official focused on technology and national security under both the Biden and first Trump administrations, told CNBC that the routing was an open secret. "Chinese companies have been buying these chips, very likely at scale," he said, summarizing what U.S. and allied intelligence agencies had been reporting since late 2025. A Friday May 30 internal Commerce paper described the diversion patterns; the Sunday guidance is the operational response.

"License requirements apply to advanced AI chips going to entities headquartered in China, regardless of location."— Bureau of Industry and Security guidance, May 31, 2026

What it does to Nvidia and AMD

Nvidia has spent the past two earnings cycles guiding investors away from China revenue. Jensen Huang's most recent commentary treated the mainland market as fully closed for the Blackwell generation and described Rubin sampling plans that explicitly excluded mainland Chinese OEMs. Even so, the company carried Singapore as a billed-to geography that grew aggressively through 2025; Wall Street has assumed for months that a meaningful share of that volume was diverted through Chinese-affiliated buyers. The Sunday guidance forces Nvidia, AMD, and their distribution partners to verify the corporate parent of every advanced-accelerator order and to refuse shipments where the parent traces back to a Chinese-headquartered firm.

The mechanical effect for the two chipmakers is a tighter compliance overhead and a measurable downward revision to addressable demand from the so-called "rest-of-Asia" geography. Analysts who covered the prior diversion patterns put the headline number in the low single-digit billions of annualized Nvidia revenue, with a smaller AMD slice. The Bloomberg story circulating Sunday that Jensen Huang had personally asked Supermicro to harden its export-control screening was the precursor signal; the BIS note is the codification.

The diplomatic frame

The Sunday guidance follows a year of intermittent public pressure on the Commerce Department to act, including from a bipartisan group of senators who pointed to the same diversion patterns. It also lands days before the Trump administration's next round of trade talks with Beijing, which gives the United States a fresh chip on the table that does not require congressional action. Officials in Singapore and Kuala Lumpur received advance notice of the guidance and are not expected to challenge it publicly; Chinese commerce officials called it "discrimination" in a brief Sunday-night statement to state media.

The deeper question, which the guidance does not answer, is how aggressively BIS will move to claw back chips already in country at Chinese-owned facilities outside the mainland. A formal end-use audit program is reportedly being drafted but has not been published. Until it is, the practical effect of the Sunday note is to freeze new orders from the targeted entities and to put the next quarter of Nvidia and AMD shipments under a microscope.

Big picture

The Bureau of Industry and Security's Sunday guidance is the biggest piece of AI export-control policy since the original AI Diffusion Rule. By tying license requirements to the corporate parent rather than the address on the box, Commerce closes a year-old gap that industry sources estimate moved hundreds of thousands of Blackwell, Rubin, and MI350x-class chips through Singaporean and Malaysian shells. Nvidia and AMD open Monday with a real revision in their addressable Asia book.

Hardware is the day's second story. Nvidia trailered its first consumer CPU since the 2015 Tegra X1 ahead of Jensen Huang's June 1 Computex keynote, with a 20-core Arm-v9.2 N1X paired with a Blackwell GPU and a Surface variant queued up. Apple slipped its first AI smart glasses to late 2027 in twin Sunday reports from Mark Gurman and Ming-Chi Kuo, with Vision Air resurrected for 2028-2029. Super Micro tore 37.8% higher on the week as Dell's $43.8 billion AI server quarter pulled the whole rack-integration tier with it.

Coordinated "new era of PC" teasers from Nvidia, Microsoft and Arm pre-stage Jensen Huang's June 1 Computex keynote, where the 20-core Arm-v9.2 N1X paired with a 6,144-CUDA-core Blackwell GPU will be unveiled with Dell, Lenovo, Asus, MSI and a Surface variant queued.

Nvidia, Microsoft, and Arm circulated coordinated "A new era of PC" social teasers on Sunday, pre-staging Jensen Huang's June 1 Computex keynote at the Taipei Music Center. gHacks reported the centerpiece will be the N1X (also referenced internally as N1), Nvidia's first consumer-targeted CPU since the 2015 Tegra X1 powered the original Nintendo Switch. The chip pairs twenty custom Arm-v9.2 cores in a 10-plus-10 big.LITTLE configuration with a Blackwell-based integrated GPU carrying 6,144 CUDA cores, roughly an RTX 5070 class of mobile-GPU throughput. Memory tops out at 128 GB and the TDP envelope runs from 45 watts to 80 watts depending on form factor.

The fabric is TSMC's 3 nm node, the same process Apple uses for the M5 generation and Qualcomm uses for the Snapdragon X2 Elite. Sunday's teaser cycle named Dell, Lenovo, Asus, and MSI as launch OEMs, and earlier supply-chain reports from DigiTimes and Sammobile have placed a Microsoft Surface variant of N1X on the same launch window. Dell's XPS announcement is reportedly embargoed for Sunday evening Taipei time, which translates to the early-morning U.S. East Coast hours of the keynote itself.

The strategic frame

The N1X is Nvidia's first serious attempt at the Windows-on-Arm market and the company's clearest move yet against Qualcomm's Snapdragon X2 Elite, which has held the throughput lead on the Arm Windows side since launch. Nvidia's pitch to OEMs combines its CUDA software ecosystem with an inference NPU that targets the on-device AI workloads Microsoft's Copilot+ PC certification requires. The N1X's Blackwell GPU also unlocks DirectX 12 Ultimate gaming and CUDA-accelerated content workflows that Snapdragon X2 cannot match.

The longer arc is harder to read. Nvidia has hinted at an N2 successor for late 2027 and a workstation-class N1 variant for the prosumer tier. gHacks reports that the first N1X laptops will ship before the 2026 holiday season at a limited OEM count, with broader availability in the first half of 2027. The reveal will sit alongside the next Rubin-generation data center roadmap update and the company's annual robotics-platform push, both of which Jensen Huang is expected to cover in the same keynote.

"The biggest threat to Snapdragon X2 Elite isn't an x86 chip. It's a CUDA-equipped Arm SoC that finally gives Windows-on-Arm the application story it has been missing."— gHacks editorial framing, May 31, 2026

The risk is Apple Silicon. The M5 generation that shipped in late 2025 still leads the Arm Windows landscape on perf-per-watt by a comfortable margin, and Nvidia has not detailed an N1X variant that targets the fanless, sub-25-watt envelope where Apple does its best work. The N1X has to be enough to drag the Windows premium-laptop tier upward without giving Qualcomm enough oxygen to ship a competitive X3 design in 2027. The market will read Sunday's teasers and Monday's keynote against that benchmark.

Apple's first AI smart glasses, internally coded N50, slipped from a late-2026 to early-2027 reveal-and-ship plan to a late-2027 ship window, per Mark Gurman's Sunday "Power On" newsletter as reported by 9to5Mac. Ming-Chi Kuo issued a parallel supply-chain note over the weekend that aligned with Gurman's timeline. Two of Apple's most-watched analysts converging on the same window — and converging downward — is unusual enough that AppleInsider treated it as a defining datapoint for Apple's wearables roadmap.

The hardware itself stays in the Meta Ray-Bans shape. Oval-cropped cameras, microphones, and speakers will sit inside four candidate frame styles Apple is testing: a Wayfarer-style large rectangular, a slim rectangular shape Tim Cook reportedly prefers, and large and small oval variants. Frame colors include black, ocean blue, and light brown. There is no in-lens display. Siri will handle the voice surface, with notifications, photo and video capture, and turn-by-turn walking directions as the headline use cases. Price targets land between $200 and $500, sitting squarely on top of Meta's Ray-Ban Display pricing.

Vision Air resurrected

Gurman also reports that Vision Air, the slimmer, lighter Vision Pro successor that Apple had reportedly shelved in late 2025, is back on the roadmap for a 2028-or-2029 ship. Apple's spatial-computing category is therefore effectively on ice through 2027: no new Vision Pro hardware before Vision Air ships, and no AR-display glasses before the true HUD optics that Gurman says are "at least a few years" beyond N50. The category that Apple's 2024 Vision Pro launch was supposed to anchor is, in effect, paused.

The decision is reportedly Cook's. Gurman frames the smart glasses as the CEO's top priority before his stated September 1 handoff to senior vice president John Ternus, and Cook is said to have ordered the slim-rectangular frame as the design Apple will lead with. The framing is generous to Cook's tenure: it lets him exit on a product he personally chose, even if the product does not ship until after he has handed the title.

"The slim rectangular shape is reportedly Tim Cook's preferred frame, with the project framed as his top priority before his September 1 transition to John Ternus."— 9to5Mac summary of Gurman's Sunday "Power On"

What it means for Meta and the category

Meta's Ray-Bans get an extra clear year. The Ray-Ban Display product Meta launched in late 2025 is now the only credible AI-glasses platform in market through end of 2026 and most of 2027. The implication is that Meta's installed base, which crossed 4 million pairs in early 2026, has another full retail cycle to grow before Apple ships a competing device. The Meta Quest hand-tracking work and the lighter Orion successor are on parallel tracks; neither is gated by Apple's slip.

The longer-arc reading is that Apple has decided to compete on industrial design and Siri voice-quality rather than on the display optics it cannot yet make work at consumer prices. MacRumors framed Cook's pick of the slim rectangular shape as the most distinctively Apple decision in the spec list, and AppleInsider's Wesley Hilliard noted that two independent leakers converging on late 2027 strengthens the thesis that the slip is real rather than negotiated optimism. The next public datapoint is WWDC on June 8, where Apple is expected to detail Siri 2.0 and the Gemini integration that the Ray-Ban-shape device will depend on.

Tempus AI launches an agentic Lens for oncology drug development at ASCO with 19 of the top 20 biopharmas already on it

Business Wire · 2026-05-31 · Source ↗ ·

Tempus AI unveiled a redesigned Lens platform Sunday at the American Society of Clinical Oncology meeting, layering multi-agent Co-Scientist workflows on top of 8.5 million de-identified patient records. The system targets the parts of drug-discovery research that have eaten the most analyst time.

Tempus AI (NASDAQ: TEM) used the opening day of ASCO 2026 in Chicago to unveil the next generation of its Lens platform, an agentic AI workspace aimed at oncology drug developers. The release frames Lens as the operating layer for translational research at the nineteen of the twenty largest biopharma companies that already license the platform, and adds a multi-agent system the company calls Co-Scientist that decomposes drug-development research questions into orchestrated subtasks.

The data substrate is the same Tempus has been building for half a decade: more than 8.5 million queryable de-identified patient records spanning genomics, transcriptomics, imaging, and clinical history. The new Lens layers on top of that substrate the kind of agentic plumbing that, in the consumer LLM market, has been the front line of competition between OpenAI and Anthropic for the past year. Tempus's pitch is that the same agentic patterns translate to drug-discovery analyst workflows where the user is a translational researcher rather than a software engineer.

What the Co-Scientist does

Tempus describes the agentic stack as a Custom Research Plan Generator, an On-Demand Execution agent, and a set of domain-specific subagents that handle biomarker validation and trial design. The company brands the orchestration "Reproducible Intelligence," with code transparency on every agent action so that an oncology research lead can audit the path the agents took to reach a recommendation. The framing matters because regulated drug-development workflows have long resisted black-box AI; an auditable agentic trace is the table-stakes upgrade Tempus is offering as it moves beyond simple data retrieval.

Lens is available at lens.tempus.ai. Tempus's biopharma customers will access it through their existing enterprise contracts. The Sunday rollout is part of a broader push by Tempus to position itself as the dominant clinical-grade AI platform for oncology, a niche that Foundation Medicine, Flatiron Health, and Recursion have each contested. The company's edge is volume: the 8.5 million-record figure is roughly an order of magnitude above Flatiron's and grew through 2025 via additional health-system partnerships.

"Drug development requires thousands of pivotal decisions between molecule and approval. The next generation of Lens consolidates this workflow into a single platform."— Ryan Fukushima, CEO of Data and Apps, Tempus AI

Why the ASCO timing matters

ASCO is the largest annual oncology meeting in the world and the venue where biopharma companies most aggressively unveil clinical-data updates and partnership announcements. Tempus chose Sunday, opening day, to put Lens on the floor in front of an audience that includes virtually every clinical-trial designer in the field. The decision also lets the company seed the next set of customer conversations against the agentic vocabulary that ASCO attendees have been hearing about from generic LLM vendors but rarely seeing applied inside their regulated workflows.

The longer-term context is Tempus's bet that translational research is the part of biopharma where agentic AI lands first, before clinical-trial operations or commercial. Trial-design and biomarker-validation workloads are large, expensive, and constrained by the availability of senior translational researchers. If the agentic stack reduces the per-question analyst load by even a meaningful percentage, the platform's pricing power scales with each new molecule moving through development. The competitive question is whether Foundation Medicine, Flatiron, or the model labs themselves can match the data substrate Tempus has spent years assembling.

Admiral Frank Bradley, the head of U.S. Special Operations Command, used an AP interview published Sunday to issue the first on-record public pushback by a Pentagon four-star against Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth's accelerated battlefield-AI push. Bradley's framing was strict: humans must remain confident that any AI system involved in lethal decision-making "is going to deliver violence only where we intend it to be delivered." Hegseth told a closed-door SpaceX employee gathering earlier in the month that the Pentagon needed to deploy AI "without ideological constraints," language Bradley's interview implicitly rejects.

The disagreement is the latest piece of fallout from the Pentagon's late-April decision to terminate Anthropic's $200 million Maven-related contract and to label the company a supply-chain risk over what officials described as autonomous-weapons concerns. The named alternatives the Pentagon has been steering work toward are Google, OpenAI, and SpaceX's Starshield unit. Bradley's interview is the first time a serving combatant commander has publicly questioned the speed and posture of the post-Anthropic transition.

The named officials in the AP piece

The AP story carried statements from several other officials. Sergeant Major Andrew Krogman and Melissa Johnson, both of whom work on Special Operations' AI integration program, framed Bradley's caution as a doctrinal floor rather than a policy shift; Lieutenant General Michael Conley described the Special Operations posture as "deploy when you can, defer when you must." Helen Toner of Georgetown's Center for Security and Emerging Technology was quoted on the institutional design question: how the Pentagon writes in checks on autonomous lethality matters more than which vendor it picks.

The Army's 18th Airborne Corps, the AP noted, already uses AI for artillery target selection. The system in question still requires a human in the loop, and Bradley's framing covers that loop without naming the corps directly. The longer-arc question is what happens as the artillery-selection model improves: whether the human remains a meaningful check or whether the workload pushes commanders to delegate review in practice if not in writing.

"We, as humans, have to have the confidence that any AI involved in the kill chain is going to deliver violence only where we intend it to be delivered."— Adm. Frank Bradley, Commander, U.S. Special Operations Command

What the Anthropic termination says about the politics

The Anthropic decision matters because it set a precedent. The company had been deliberately conservative on weapons-related deployments and had publicly described its sponsorship of Scott Wiener's SB 53 and Alex Bores's RAISE Act in California and New York as part of a strategy to keep frontier-model deployment paths predictable. Hegseth's team treated those positions as evidence of "ideological constraint," a label Bradley's interview now disputes from the operational side.

The next datapoint sits with the Pentagon's June procurement cycle. Bradley's pushback gives the working group on autonomous-systems doctrine a clearer mandate to push the framework upward through the Joint Chiefs. The Pentagon is also expected to release an updated version of Directive 3000.09, the original 2012 policy on autonomy in weapons systems, in late June; Bradley's Sunday remarks set a marker for what the revised directive cannot leave out.

An adjacent piece in the Anchorage Daily News carried Bradley's same quotes against a longer profile of the 18th Airborne Corps's AI integration. CNN becomes the ninth publisher suing Perplexity; the AI compliance picture forks into three independent fronts

Tech Times · 2026-05-31 · Source ↗ ·

CNN filed in the Southern District of New York over 17,000 allegedly scraped stories, joining the Times, News Corp, the Post, the Chicago Tribune, Britannica, Merriam-Webster, Reddit, and Yomiuri Shimbun. Tech Times's Sunday analysis maps the three legal fronts now operating in parallel.

CNN filed a copyright and trademark lawsuit against Perplexity AI in the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York, alleging the company scraped and redistributed more than 17,000 of its stories, photos, and videos. The filing makes CNN the ninth active plaintiff against Perplexity, alongside the New York Times, News Corp and Dow Jones, the New York Post, the Chicago Tribune, Encyclopedia Britannica, Merriam-Webster, Reddit, and Japan's Yomiuri Shimbun. Time, Gannett, Le Monde, and Der Spiegel chose licensing deals over litigation. Perplexity's chief communications officer Jesse Dwyer told reporters, "You can't copyright facts," a defense the plaintiff list reads as exactly the wrong line to draw against the published expression they say was lifted.

Tech Times's Richard L. Wells used the CNN filing as the anchor for a Sunday analysis that mapped the three independent legal and regulatory fronts the AI industry now has to manage. The article's framing is that no single compliance program addresses all three; licensing agreements do not satisfy EU disclosure rules, constitutional victories at the state level do not reduce training-data acquisition costs, and a published frontier-safety framework provides no defense against a copyright suit. Companies have to staff for all three at once.

Front one: copyright litigation

The first front is the publisher and author copyright suits. The Bartz v. Anthropic settlement, which Judge Araceli Martínez-Olguín granted preliminary approval to in September 2025 and took under submission after a May 14 fairness hearing, sits at $1.5 billion covering roughly 120,000 authors. Bartz remains the largest copyright settlement in U.S. history. The CNN filing extends the pattern from books to news content, and Perplexity is the company against which the most active suits cluster because its product directly substitutes for the publisher pages it scrapes.

Front two: federal preemption

The second front is the federal challenge to state AI laws. xAI filed in April to block Colorado's AI Act before its June 30 effective date; the Department of Justice intervened on April 24, deploying the AI Litigation Task Force created by Executive Order 14365 of December 11, 2025. A court stayed enforcement on April 27. Colorado responded with SB 26-189, a narrower replacement focused on automated decision-making disclosures, which Governor Jared Polis signed on May 14, 2026, with an effective date of January 1, 2027. The litigation stay extends to the replacement statute, which means the Colorado test of state AI authority is now on the federal court's calendar through at least the second half of 2026.

Front three: EU and California frameworks

The third front is the EU AI Act and California's Transparency in Frontier AI Act. OpenAI's Frontier Governance Framework, published May 28, explicitly maps the company's internal Preparedness Framework to the EU Code of Practice for General-Purpose AI and the California statute, which took effect January 1, 2026. EU transparency obligations become fully enforceable August 2, 2026. OpenAI's preemptive disclosure sets a compliance baseline that the other frontier labs (Anthropic, Google DeepMind, xAI) now have to match or explain why not.

"A single compliance program cannot address all three fronts. Licensing agreements don't satisfy EU requirements, constitutional victories don't reduce training-data costs, and published frameworks provide no litigation defense."— Richard L. Wells, Tech Times analysis, May 31, 2026

The Sunday picture is that AI compliance is fragmenting in a way that compounds costs. Each front operates through different plaintiffs, different venues, and different mechanisms. The cost of running parallel programs is rising, which advantages the labs with the largest legal and policy budgets. The CNN filing is small in absolute terms relative to the Anthropic settlement; what it signals is that the publisher front has not slowed, and that the AI industry's litigation overhead is growing rather than consolidating.
