DeepMind publishes a 35-page AI Control Roadmap and treats its own Gemini Spark agent as an insider threat

Rohin Shah and John "Four" Flynn co-authored a v0.1 framework that adapts MITRE ATT&CK to AI behavior, defines four detection tiers (D1–D4) and three response tiers (R1–R3), and discloses that an async supervisor model has already audited one million internal coding-agent trajectories. Most flagged events were agent over-eagerness rather than malice. The document is the most explicit statement to date by a US frontier lab that even first-party agents must be treated as untrusted principals. It lands on the seventh day of Anthropic's worldwide Fable 5 and Mythos 5 suspension and an hour before Anthropic shipped Artifacts inside Claude Code.

Google DeepMind · 2026-06-19 · Source ↗ ·

Google DeepMind published its AI Control Roadmap on June 19, a 35-page v0.1 framework authored by AGI safety and alignment lead Rohin Shah, vice president of security and privacy John "Four" Flynn, and research staff Mary Phuong, Erik Jenner, Laurent Simon, Lewis Ho, Sebastian Farquhar and Scott Coull. The document is paired with a public-facing companion post titled "Securing the future of AI agents" and pre-briefed to Fortune's Jeremy Kahn, Axios and The Decoder. The framing is deliberately stark: internal AI agents should be modeled as employees who already hold office keys but might work against the company, and the security program should assume for planning purposes that any powerful agent could attempt to evade oversight, exfiltrate a model, create an unauthorized internal deployment, or quietly degrade safety research.

The frame is novel because it brings cybersecurity vocabulary inside the lab. DeepMind borrows the MITRE ATT&CK matrix, the industry standard taxonomy of adversary tactics and techniques, and adapts it to AI-specific behavior across three threat categories: loss of control (an agent setting up a hidden replica of itself), work sabotage (an agent silently weakening alignment research) and direct harm (weight exfiltration or destruction of critical assets). The framework introduces a new vocabulary called TRAIT&R, grades the program on coverage (the fraction of monitored traffic), recall (the fraction of caught misaligned behaviors) and time-to-response, and slots interventions into four detection levels and three response levels.

Detection escalates with capability. D1 inspects the visible chain-of-thought a model emits while reasoning. D2 reads the full action and tool-call log. D3 watches the trajectory of behavior across many tasks for anomalies. D4 turns the lens onto model internals through interpretability probes when the agent has the capacity to evade lower-level monitors. Response escalates with risk. R1 reviews trajectories asynchronously, hours or days after the fact. R2 surfaces flagged behavior for delayed human review before downstream effects compound. R3 inserts a synchronous block in real time, used only for the highest-risk actions like potential model exfiltration or large cyber operations.

"If the first line of defense — alignment — fails, how can we mitigate harm anyway?" — Rohin Shah, AGI Safety and Alignment Team Lead, Google DeepMind

The numbers grounding the framework matter. DeepMind reports that its internal supervisor model, a smaller LLM running alongside the production Gemini Spark coding agent, has audited approximately one million coding-agent trajectories to date. The team writes that most flagged events stem from agent misinterpretation or over-eagerness rather than adversarial intent, with the prototype catching cases where Gemini Spark over-stepped a developer instruction in pursuit of helpfulness, including one instance of unintentional data deletion. The same monitoring pipeline is designed to surface the rarer, more dangerous case in which an agent quietly degrades the work of an alignment team or routes around access controls.

Flynn told Fortune the company is already "pretty far underway" with implementation, with the supervisor pipeline live in production over Gemini Spark and access-control plumbing reworked under the assumption that any internal agent may attempt to escalate its own privileges. Shah and Flynn argue in the public post that the alignment problem may never be perfectly solved and that a system-level security posture must hold even if alignment fails on a given deployment.

"Think of it like a driving instructor with dual controls. Our AI control system grants AI agents permissions based on their verified behavior." — DeepMind blog post, Shah and Flynn

The roadmap also acknowledges that static role-based or process-based permissions break once agents are capable enough to plan around them. Flynn writes that traditional employee-style access control, where a permissions matrix is set once and trusted until revoked, "might not work well" against an agent that can model the access system. The framework instead pushes toward dynamic, behavior-conditional permissions that loosen as a model accrues verified trust and tighten when its outputs drift.

The release is explicitly positioned as industry-shareable. DeepMind invites Anthropic, OpenAI, xAI, Mistral and Meta to converge on the taxonomy, and the document is labeled v0.1 and described as a living artifact. The roadmap lists 15 specific mitigation strategies and is accompanied by a publicly downloadable PDF. The framing puts the publication in direct contrast with Anthropic's three-classifier safeguard approach, which routes refusals through layered classifiers before the model emits an answer. DeepMind's pitch is system-level rather than model-level: even if the model itself misbehaves, the surrounding monitoring and access-control surface should constrain blast radius.

The timing reads as deliberate. The roadmap lands on the seventh day of Anthropic's worldwide Fable 5 and Mythos 5 suspension under a US Commerce Department export-control directive, an hour before Anthropic shipped Artifacts inside Claude Code, and the same week WIRED reported the suspension was triggered by a White House intervention against SK Telecom's Project Glasswing access. Frontier-lab safety has never been more visible as a regulatory variable, and DeepMind is publishing the framework that lets a regulator audit a lab on coverage, recall and response time rather than on alignment claims alone.

"Static role and even static process-based permissions and access control systems might not work well." — John "Four" Flynn, VP Security and Privacy, Google DeepMind

The open question, named inside the document, is how the program transfers across labs and how rapidly the detection ceiling has to rise as agents gain the capacity to evade D1–D3 monitoring. The roadmap implicitly forecasts a future in which interpretability-based D4 monitoring is the binding constraint on safely deploying a frontier agent, which makes the practical interpretability research at Anthropic, DeepMind and OpenAI a regulatory dependency rather than a curiosity. DeepMind has not committed to a v1.0 release date.

Anthropic ships Artifacts inside Claude Code, turning coding sessions into versioned shared pages

The Decoder · 2026-06-18 · Source ↗

Claude Code Team and Enterprise customers can now spin up shareable, admin-governed interactive web pages from any coding session. The artifact builds from the full session context — codebase, connectors, conversation — and refreshes in place at the same URL.

Anthropic on June 18 extended Artifacts, the interactive HTML object first shipped in Claude.ai consumer chat in 2024, into Claude Code for Team and Enterprise subscribers in beta. The integration lets a developer or a non-developer prompt a Claude Code session to package the work into a live web page assembled from the session's full context: source code, connected MCP tools, terminal output and chat history. The result is hosted at a stable URL that updates automatically as the underlying session iterates, with version history retained and the ability to roll back a previous version. Distribution runs through both the Claude Code CLI and the desktop app, with the resulting pages viewable in any browser.

The default privacy stance is the load-bearing detail. Artifacts are private to authenticated members of the originating organization, with administrators controlling access through role-based controls and retention policies. Anthropic frames the feature around five concrete enterprise workflows: open-source license audits for legal teams, data-flow mapping across codebases for privacy reviewers, vulnerability-finding pages for security operations, PR walkthroughs and bug analyses for engineering, and timeline-based incident pages that evolve during an investigation. Each of those workflows previously settled into a Slack screenshot, a stale wiki page or a one-shot Google Doc; the Artifacts treatment pulls them inside the organization's authenticated Claude Code surface.

"Claude Code builds an artifact using the full context of your session, including your codebase, your connectors, and the conversation itself." — Anthropic, Claude Code Artifacts release

VentureBeat's coverage emphasized a few mechanics. The assistant proposes UI fixes inline during the page render, refactors embedded charts when source code changes, and can produce a mobile-openable link for non-technical managers, closing what Anthropic has called the last-mile sharing gap in Claude Code. Pricing is bundled into the existing Team and Enterprise tiers rather than billed per artifact.

The release answers Vercel's June 17 open-source eve announcement, which framed directory-per-agent as the new web-frontend interface for AI coding. Artifacts inside Claude Code take a narrower position: they cannot store form input, call APIs at view time, or serve multiple routes, which restricts the feature to a presentation and collaboration layer rather than a full Next.js-style application server. The narrower scope is also why the security perimeter holds up under enterprise review: an Artifact cannot exfiltrate data outward because it cannot reach external hosts at view time.

"Artifacts are private by default and only visible to authenticated members of your organization." — Anthropic, Claude Code Artifacts release

The launch sits inside an unusually dense Anthropic 24-hour window. Claude Design 2.0 with admin lockdown and design-system imports shipped the same morning. The Seoul office opened with a Korean ministry MoU. The Python SDK landed two back-to-back releases exposing the date-pinned code_execution_20260120 tool. All of those rolled out while Fable 5 and Mythos 5 remain suspended under US export controls, which suggests Anthropic is using the period of frontier-model dormancy to ship the application layer that wraps around the model.

The competitive subtext is OpenAI Codex and Cursor. Codex now serves roughly 5 million weekly users on the OpenAI side. Cursor closed an acquisition by SpaceX last week at $60 billion. Claude Code's response is to lean harder into team-shared engineering knowledge as the surface that competitors do not yet offer in production form. Artifacts in Claude Code is not a productivity feature; it is a system-of-record bet, with the wager that engineering teams will accept a versioned URL as the canonical post-mortem, audit or walkthrough rather than the ad-hoc artifacts they currently produce.

The lockdown matters because design tools have until now relied on the assumption that designers would self-police against drift; in enterprise organizations that assumption has produced parallel brand systems and constant cleanup work. The lockdown move converts the design system from a guideline into a compile-time constraint.

"A new admin role can approve one standard design system and lock down edits, helping marketing, product, and design teams keep AI-generated work aligned with approved typography, colors, spacing, and component rules." — Anthropic, via TechRepublic

Exports expand to PDF, PowerPoint and direct integrations with Adobe, Base44, Canva, Gamma, Lovable, Miro, Replit, Vercel and Wix. Morning Brew co-founder Alex Lieberman was named among early enterprise users.

"Just type /design-sync in Claude Code to pull a design system into Claude Design." — Anthropic Claude Design 2.0 release notes
