Moonshot AI has told its shareholders it is preparing a Hong Kong listing within six months at a $30-billion-plus post-money valuation, three days after the July 16 Kimi K3 release rewrote Chinese open-weight benchmarks and pulled global chip and hyperscaler stocks into their worst week of the year. The $30 billion mark represents roughly a 7.5x rerating from Moonshot's late-2025 valuation of about $4 billion, and puts the Beijing lab inside striking distance of DeepSeek's $74 billion pre-IPO round announced the same weekend for a Shanghai STAR Market filing before year-end.

"Zhilin was quite determined to go back and build a start-up. I remember him telling me that if he didn't at least try starting his own company, he would regret it for the rest of his life."— Russ Salakhutdinov, University of Toronto professor and Yang's CMU PhD supervisor

The revenue trajectory behind the mark

Annual recurring revenue at Moonshot reached $300 million in June, up from $200 million in April, a $100 million pickup across two months that Moonshot attributes to Kimi K3 consumption on the Kimi app, Kimi Work desktop, and the API. The company has raised between $3.9 billion and $6 billion cumulatively since its 2023 founding. The pending private round, expected to close before the IPO, targets approximately $2 billion in fresh capital at the $30-billion-plus mark, with anchor investors Alibaba, Tencent and China Mobile taking the majority of allocations.

What Kimi K3 actually shipped

Kimi K3 is a 2.8-trillion-parameter mixture-of-experts model activating 16 of 896 experts per token, delivering roughly 50 billion active parameters per query with a 1-million-token context window. It opened at Elo 1,679 on Arena.ai's Frontend Code leaderboard against Anthropic's Claude Fable 5 at 1,631 and OpenAI's GPT-5.6 Sol at 1,618, taking the top slot with 1,757 votes recorded within the first 48 hours. Artificial Analysis places K3's intelligence tier alongside Claude Opus 4.8 and GPT-5.5, one notch behind Fable 5 and Sol. API pricing runs $3 per million input tokens on cache-miss, $0.30 on cache-hit, and $15 per million output tokens, roughly one-third of Anthropic's forthcoming $10 input and $50 output rate card for Fable 5 metered access. Moonshot has committed to publish full open weights on Hugging Face by July 27, with an intermediate preview scheduled for July 22 and a technical report on architecture, training, and evaluation running in parallel.

"As he approached the end of his studies at CMU, Mr Zhilin had several opportunities to work for US technology companies, including Apple."— Russ Salakhutdinov, on Yang's 2019 job decision

The VIE unwind and Beijing's overseas-listing rules

Moonshot is restructuring away from a Cayman-registered variable-interest-entity model toward a joint-venture structure that satisfies revised China Securities Regulatory Commission rules gating offshore listings of AI and data-heavy mainland businesses. The rewired cap table is what the shareholder resolution now circulating asks investors to approve, alongside the IPO mandate itself. The path Moonshot is walking mirrors what Zhipu and MiniMax have signaled for their own Hong Kong or Shanghai filings, and the sequencing runs alongside Beijing's own preliminary discussions with Alibaba, ByteDance, and Z.ai about potential export controls covering both closed and open-source Chinese AI models, mirroring the June US action against Anthropic.

Anthropic bundles Claude Fable 5 into Max and Team Premium at 50 percent of limits from Monday

Anthropic / Dawn · 2026-07-19 · Source ↗

Anthropic's Sunday policy tweet closed three prior week-long extensions and made Claude Fable 5 a permanent Max and Team Premium inclusion at half of a reduced usage base. Pro and Team Standard subscribers lose bundled Fable access, take a one-time $100 credit, and revert to metered API pricing.

The @claudeai account posted the Fable 5 subscription rework Sunday July 19 as a policy tweet that reversed weeks of week-by-week extensions and formalized a permanent split effective 24 hours later. The tweet reads: 'Beginning July 20, Claude Fable 5 will be included in all Max and Team Premium plans, at 50 percent of limits. Pro and Team Standard users will continue to have access to Fable via usage credits, and will receive a one-time $100 credit. Demand for Fable has been challenging to predict, which is why it was rolled out to subscription plans in stages, extending access several times as capacity was secured.' The announcement follows three prior extensions of a promotional included-access window that had been due to expire at 11:59 PM PT Sunday July 19.

"Beginning July 20, Claude Fable 5 will be included in all Max and Team Premium plans, at 50 percent of limits."— Anthropic, official announcement

Tier-by-tier mechanics

Max 5x and Max 20x plans will include Claude Fable 5 at 50 percent of standard weekly usage limits, a reduction from the prior period during which Claude Code enjoyed a 50 percent usage boost. Team Premium plans mirror the Max terms. Pro and Team Standard users lose bundled Fable 5 access entirely and receive a one-time $100 usage credit to soften the transition to API-priced consumption at approximately $10 per million input tokens and $50 per million output tokens. Discounted bundle purchases are available up to $2,000 monthly for Pro accounts. All Claude models draw from a shared weekly pool, so heavy Sonnet 5 or Opus 4.8 consumption reduces effective Fable 5 headroom on Max and Team Premium and vice versa. Anthropic committed to continue investing in additional inference capacity but acknowledged Fable 5 demand has been 'hard to manage and frustrating for users.'

The reversal in context

The Decoder headlined the change as Anthropic 'slashing' Fable 5 limits in Max and Team Premium and 'pushing Pro users toward API pricing,' noting that when the bonus phase ends, regular limits drop 33 percent and Fable 5 is available at only 50 percent of the already-reduced ceiling. Simon Willison's Friday 'Claude make Fable 5 permanent' post had argued OpenAI's GPT-5.6 Sol was winning subscriber conversions on price and predictability alone during the rolling extensions, and pinned the reversal on competitive pressure from Sol at roughly one-third of Fable 5's per-token cost and from Moonshot's Kimi K3 at $3 input and $15 output per million tokens. Basic $20-per-month Claude plans get no Fable 5 access at all.

"The reversal reflects competitive pressure from GPT-5.6 Sol and Kimi K3. Subscribers won't pay premium fees for a plan that excludes Anthropic's best model."— Simon Willison, weblog

Why the frontier goes back in the bundle

The move matters most for the agent workload envelope. Fable 5 is Anthropic's most capable long-horizon model for the tool-use, computer-use, and multi-step planning workloads that dominate Claude Code and Claude Cowork usage. Permanent inclusion at 50 percent of Max limits materially changes the economics for coding-agent shops that had begun designing Fable 5 out of default configurations to avoid credit burn. It also mirrors the WAIC 2026 premium-tiering pivot reported by CGTN on July 19, in which Chinese AI vendors abandoned flat-rate 40-yuan tiers for per-token or 200-yuan premium models. Both moves reflect a Q3 2026 industry consensus that flagship-model inclusion in mass-market subscriptions at bounded pricing is no longer viable at current inference costs. Anthropic remains the only Western frontier lab to preserve any flagship-model bundling in an under-$100 consumer tier, and only for the buffer that ends Sunday.

Claude Code v2.1.215 stops auto-running the /verify and /code-review skills

GitHub (anthropics/claude-code) · 2026-07-19 · Source ↗

Anthropic engineer @ashwin-ant tagged v2.1.215 at 02:56 UTC Sunday with a single behavioral change: Claude no longer auto-runs /verify or /code-review mid-session. Twelve minutes later Claude Agent SDK Python v0.2.123 pinned the new CLI version for every Python-hosted agent.

Anthropic engineer @ashwin-ant tagged Claude Code v2.1.215 at 02:56:01 UTC on Sunday July 19 as commit 015170d3fd84fb57ef4685a64b673fadd0690dc1, a one-line release whose sole change reads: 'Claude no longer runs the /verify and /code-review skills on its own; invoke them with /verify or /code-review when you want them.' The 27-reaction release page carries 12 assets covering the standalone macOS, Linux, and Windows binaries, the npm tarball, and the plugin surface. Twelve minutes later at 03:08:55 UTC, GitHub Actions cut Claude Agent SDK Python v0.2.123 as commit 2d4ef94 with a single internal-changes bullet: 'Updated bundled Claude CLI to version 2.1.215.'

"Claude no longer runs the /verify and /code-review skills on its own; invoke them with /verify or /code-review when you want them."— Claude Code v2.1.215 release notes

What the change stops Claude from doing

The /verify skill drives the built-in end-to-end verification harness, exercising affected flows and observing runtime behavior before a change is claimed complete. /code-review runs the reuse, simplification, efficiency, and correctness sweep that ships with the CLI, optionally posting inline PR comments or applying fixes with --comment or --fix. Both had been included in the default skill set that the model could pick up unprompted mid-session under v2.1.214's broader stability update, which shipped Friday July 18 with EndConversation, the PowerShell 5.1 permission bypass fix, and the rewritten single-segment dir/** semantics. Enough sessions saw Claude reach for /verify or /code-review during unrelated tasks, or auto-fire on diffs that only touched tests or docs, that Anthropic pulled the trigger overnight.

Explicit invocation, no other behavioral change

Users still get both skills at the /verify and /code-review slash commands. The v2.1.215 diff does not touch permissions, hooks, subagent caps, plugin loading, or any of the OTel or Bash-check surfaces reworked over the prior week. There is no new flag, no configuration key, no environment variable to restore the auto-run behavior. The change is unilateral and ships to every installer channel at the same GitHub tag; the CHANGELOG.md file inside the repository is 5,112 lines long as of this release.

"Verify that a code change actually does what it's supposed to by exercising it end-to-end and observing behavior — drive the affected flow, not just tests or typecheck."— /verify skill description in the CLI harness

Agent SDK pins the new CLI

The companion release, Claude Agent SDK Python 0.2.123 on PyPI, is a pure metadata bump updating the bundled Claude CLI version pin from 2.1.214 to 2.1.215. Python callers that shell out to the CLI via the SDK's harness pick up the new /verify and /code-review behavior on their next pip install without a code change. Anyone using the SDK to drive sub-agents that used to expect the skill picker to auto-fire during a code review or verification pass now needs to add an explicit slash-command step to their orchestration.

Willison confirms Claude Code binaries ship the Rust port of Bun v1.4.0 in production

simonwillison.net · 2026-07-19 · Source ↗

Simon Willison ran strings against his local Claude binary Sunday morning and pulled a 'Bun v1.4.0 (macOS arm64)' stamp plus 563 Rust source paths, verifying Jarred Sumner's claim that Claude Code v2.1.181 and later run the Zig-to-Rust Bun rewrite in production at roughly 20 percent smaller footprint.

Simon Willison posted 'Claude Code uses Bun written in Rust now' as a blogmark on his weblog at 03:54 UTC Sunday July 19, verifying Jarred Sumner's Bun-blog claim that Claude Code v2.1.181 released June 17 and every subsequent release ship the Rust rewrite of Bun rather than the original Zig build, and that on Linux this cut CLI startup by 10 percent. Willison used three techniques on his ~/.local/bin/claude executable, an unstripped 88-to-70 MiB binary that bundles the Bun runtime, all of Claude Code's TypeScript, the model client, and the skill and hook systems.

Three ways to see the Rust port from a shell

Running strings ~/.local/bin/claude | grep -m1 'Bun v1' returned 'Bun v1.4.0 (macOS arm64)' on macOS Apple Silicon, a build stamp that does not exist in any tagged Bun GitHub release yet because v1.4.0 was committed to the Bun repo on May 17 and remains a canary-only pre-release outside Anthropic. A second command, strings ~/.local/bin/claude | grep -Eo 'src/[[:alnum:]_./-]+\.rs', pulled 563 unique Rust source-file paths embedded as panic-location strings, headed by src/runtime/bake/dev_server/mod.rs, src/runtime/bake/production.rs, and src/bundler/bundle_v2.rs. Willison also demonstrated a runtime query using BUN_OPTIONS='--preload=/tmp/bun-version.ts' claude --version to have the Bun runtime dump its own version straight from the embedded interpreter.

"Claude Code v2.1.181 (released June 17th) and later use the Rust port of Bun. Startup got 10% faster on Linux but otherwise, barely anyone noticed."— Jarred Sumner, Bun blog

Why an agent CLI cares which Bun it ships

Sumner wrote up the Zig-to-Rust port in a Bun blog post that Willison quotes. Bun is the JavaScript runtime that packages Claude Code as a single-file executable; the Bun rewrite took 11 days in May, roughly 64 parallel Claude instances, 5.9 billion uncached input tokens, 690 million output tokens, and $165,000 of API spend at published rates. Shipping the Rust build in a paid agentic tool that runs against arbitrary user code was the higher-stakes deployment path Sumner chose because, as Willison summarizes, 'if Bun breaks, Claude Code breaks.'

"If Bun breaks, Claude Code breaks."— Simon Willison, summarizing the deployment stakes

Production footprint and shrinkage numbers

Across Rust-ported Bun, an ICU library swap, and identical-code folding the Bun team applied at the same time, the Linux binary shrank from 88 MB to 70 MB and the Windows binary from 94 MB to 76 MB, roughly 20 percent smaller. Willison notes this is the largest production deployment of a Rust-based JavaScript runtime to date and highlights that it landed silently through Claude Code's auto-update path more than a month before Bun tagged an official v1.4.0 release. His verification post landed on Hacker News as item 48966569 the same morning.

Hugging Face top weekend paper: LongStraw pushes on-policy RL past 2 million tokens on 8xH100

Hugging Face Papers · 2026-07-19 · Source ↗

Mind Lab's LongStraw takes the top Hugging Face daily papers slot at 176 upvotes for lifting on-policy RL from 800K to 2 million tokens on a fixed 8xH100 budget, rearranging rollout and gradient computation into disjoint memory partitions with an NVLink KV spill.

Mind Lab's LongStraw (arXiv 2607.14952) posts 176 upvotes across the weekend on Hugging Face and holds the top slot on the daily papers list. The paper attacks a specific bottleneck in on-policy RL for reasoning-heavy language models: the KV cache for million-token rollouts blows through GPU memory long before the gradient update phase. LongStraw rearranges the rollout and gradient computation into two disjoint memory partitions, spills the rollout KV cache to host memory during the update step, and pipelines the two phases across a rolling window so that no single GPU ever holds both the full rollout tensor and the full gradient graph. The technique lifts effective RL context length from roughly 800K tokens under vanilla GRPO to more than 2M tokens on the same 8xH100 node without changing the underlying policy architecture.

"We lift effective RL context length from roughly 800K tokens under vanilla GRPO to more than 2M tokens on the same 8xH100 node without changing the underlying policy architecture."— LongStraw abstract

Results and comparisons

LongStraw reports RL fine-tuning of a Qwen-3.7-32B base to full 2M context on a fixed 8xH100 80 GB node with wall-clock overhead within 12 percent of a 512K baseline. Held-out benchmarks include RULER, LongBench-v3, and the newer InfiniteBench-Reason suite. On InfiniteBench-Reason, the LongStraw-trained model gains 14 to 21 percentage points against SFT-only baselines and 6 to 9 points against short-context RL, with the largest gains on multi-hop chains that require reading and cross-referencing distant portions of the prompt. Against SEED, the sibling self-evolving distillation paper released the same week, LongStraw wins on any task that requires the full context but loses on tasks that fit in 128K, where SEED's simpler pipeline benefits from a stronger teacher signal.

"On InfiniteBench-Reason, the LongStraw-trained model gains 14 to 21 percentage points against SFT-only baselines."— LongStraw results section

Runner-up papers on the same list

The weekend list also elevates Alaya Studio's From Pixels to States (407 upvotes), which recasts interactive world models as event-sourced game engines with explicit state deltas rather than pixel-space diffusion, cutting rollout compute by 5 to 8 times on Genie-style benchmarks. VideoChat3 from the Multimedia Computing Group at Nanjing University lands at 145 upvotes for a fully open 4B video multimodal model that beats proprietary VideoLLM-Online on 7 of 9 video QA benchmarks. Ant Group's SearchOS-V1 (65 upvotes) frames open-domain agent collaboration under a formal robustness axiom set and hits state of the art on the Meta-Search bench.

Limitations and open questions

Mind Lab flags three limitations. First, the host-memory KV spill costs 3-4 GB/s of NVLink and PCIe bandwidth in the steady state, which caps the technique for cost-optimized inference-heavy clusters that lack CPU-GPU bandwidth headroom. Second, the paper only validates on Qwen-3.7 and DeepSeek-V4 base models; results on Llama-4 and GPT-OSS families remain untested. Third, the analysis treats speculative decoding as out of scope, and integrating LongStraw with tree search or diffusion-style decoding remains an open problem. The paper releases model checkpoints under Apache 2.0 and a fork of TRL with the memory reshaping patches under a permissive license.

PYMNTS on July 19 confirmed a Bloomberg News report from July 17 that the White House is weighing a plan to establish an independent regulator focused on artificial intelligence safety. The proposed body would report to the Securities and Exchange Commission and would model its structure on the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority, the self-regulatory organization that supervises broker-dealers. White House Chief of Staff Susie Wiles is reviewing the plan. If the proposal advances, it would represent the most substantive federal step toward AI-specific oversight since President Trump signed Executive Order 14409 on June 2, and it would arrive as the December 2025 executive order challenging state AI laws faces implementation questions.

"An independent regulator focused on artificial intelligence safety, reporting to the Securities and Exchange Commission, modeled after the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority."— Bloomberg News, July 17, cited by PYMNTS

Silicon Valley pressure and government restrictions

The proposal follows a series of federal actions that Silicon Valley companies have called inconsistent. Anthropic faced export controls that affected access to its Fable 5 and Mythos 5 models under national-security review earlier in July. OpenAI made modifications to its GPT-5.6 Sol model at government request. Founders and investors argued that ad hoc restrictions from Commerce and other agencies leave frontier labs unable to plan capacity, pricing, or research roadmaps. A FINRA-style regulator would provide one point of contact for model testing, incident reporting, and dispute resolution, replacing the current mix of Commerce, FTC, and executive-branch directives.

"A U.S.-led standards body to independently test advanced AI models prior to release."— Demis Hassabis, Google DeepMind CEO

Wall Street and cybersecurity angle

The plan is designed to satisfy Wall Street firms concerned about AI-related cybersecurity risks, including the emergence of autonomous AI attackers documented by Check Point and Hugging Face during the past month. Placing the regulator under SEC jurisdiction ties model risk to disclosure obligations for public companies that deploy frontier systems in customer-facing workflows. FINRA supervises roughly 3,400 broker-dealer firms and about 620,000 registered representatives with a $1.4 billion annual budget funded by member fees. A parallel structure for AI would draw registration fees from Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, xAI, Meta AI, and smaller frontier labs.

Industry backing and open questions

Google DeepMind CEO Demis Hassabis recently called for a US-led standards body to test advanced AI models before release, proposing a similar FINRA-based organizational model. His remarks provided industry cover for the Wiles review. Anthropic co-founder Dario Amodei has endorsed California SB 53 and New York's RAISE Act as bridges to a federal framework. OpenAI has pushed for federal preemption via the Great American AI Act. The two labs' divergent lobbying positions would collapse if the FINRA-style body absorbed most inspection and disclosure functions from state regulators. The PYMNTS piece did not identify the White House staffer who drafted the proposal, whether Treasury and Commerce concur, or whether Congress would need to authorize the new body via statute. FINRA operates as a private nonprofit rather than a federal agency; replicating that structure for AI would require frontier labs to fund the regulator through membership fees.
