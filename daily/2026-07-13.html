Anthropic's Claude Fable 5 goes metered at midnight Pacific, $10 input and $50 output per million tokens for every paid seat

The five-day extension Anthropic granted on July 7 runs out at 23:59:59 PT tonight. From that moment Fable 5 stops counting against Pro, Max, Team and Enterprise weekly usage limits and starts drawing from per-seat usage credits, at a rate exactly double Claude Opus 4.8 and roughly five times the promotional rate on Claude Sonnet 5. The company frames the change as demand rationing during a Fable 5 re-introduction that still has not returned to the pre-June-12 supply picture, not a permanent pricing shift.

BigGo Finance · 2026-07-12 · Source ↗ ·

At the end of business Pacific time on Saturday, July 12, Anthropic's five-day extension of Claude Fable 5 flat-rate access on paid plans expires. From 00:00 Pacific on Sunday, July 13, the model no longer counts against the weekly usage caps that Pro, Max, Team and select Enterprise seats already pay for. It draws from usage credits at $10 per million input tokens and $50 per million output tokens. That is exactly double the corresponding rates on Claude Opus 4.8 ($5 in, $25 out) and roughly five times the $2 / $10 promotional rate Claude Sonnet 5 carries through August 31. It is also the first time in Anthropic's history that a model has shifted mid-life from included to metered on paid tiers without a corresponding version bump.

The company's July 8 statement, relayed through BigGo Finance and echoed by Digital Applied and TechTimes, framed the cutover as a phased response to a demand curve Anthropic did not want to publish. Fable 5 launched on June 9 and was suspended three days later after Amazon researchers flagged a safety vulnerability that triggered a Commerce Department export intervention. Global reinstatement began on July 1 once controls lifted, and demand for Fable 5 completions ran hot enough on Pro and Max seats that Anthropic pushed the July 7 cutoff back once already. Sunday's activation is the second attempt at the same handover; the three-day sample from the first flat-rate window and the demand curve since July 1 was the input.

"This is a phased measure to address the initial demand surge during the model's reintroduction, not a permanent shift to paid access. Standard pricing will be restored once compute capacity improves."— Anthropic developer, statement relayed through BigGo Finance, July 8 2026

What the 50% cap looked like inside the extension window

During the July 7 to July 12 extension, Pro, Max, Team and premium Enterprise seats could route up to half of their weekly Claude usage through Fable 5 without incremental billing. Standard Enterprise seats without usage credits enabled did not receive included access at any point; Fable 5 requests from those tenants failed outright unless the org toggled usage credits on. The 50% cap survived multiple internal debates because Anthropic wanted to protect Sonnet 5 and Opus 4.8 throughput on the same seats. Cutting the cap lower would have pushed premium customers to overshoot into Opus 4.8, which would have shifted the incremental cost onto Anthropic in a way the finance team had not modeled.

Digital Applied's July 8 breakdown of the extension terms noted the arithmetic that pushes small teams over the metered edge quickly. A Pro seat consuming 1.5 million Fable 5 output tokens per week under the metered structure spends $75 in usage credits beyond the seat fee. A Max seat running heavier Fable 5 usage at 5 million output tokens per week spends $250 beyond the seat fee. Team seats with 20 users at 3 million output tokens each per week generate $3,000 in weekly incremental billing on top of the seat total. Enterprise contracts with usage credits enabled can burn a five-figure weekly credit balance in a single sprint of extended-context Fable 5 code review. Anthropic's engineering-facing docs made clear that customers can bound the spend by disabling Fable 5 at the org level and routing coding tasks to Sonnet 5 or Opus 4.8, but that shifts real work off the model where Anthropic thinks its lead is largest.

Why $10 / $50 and not something between

The $10 / $50 rate matches the July 1 confidential rate card that circulated to enterprise counterparties and holds exactly at 2x Opus 4.8. Anthropic ran that pairing through pricing modeling in April as part of the Fable 5 launch prep and repeatedly landed on 2x as the number that would compensate for the additional Fable 5 tokens-per-decision-point overhead without pushing enterprise coders back to Opus 4.8 as their default. The 5x-of-Sonnet-5 multiple is a coincidence of the Sonnet 5 promotional pricing window, which runs through August 31 and steps up to $3 / $15 on September 1. Once Sonnet 5's regular rate takes effect, Fable 5 sits at roughly 3.3x Sonnet 5 rather than 5x, which is the ratio Anthropic actually chose.

"Fable 5 at $10 and $50 is Anthropic pricing to the ceiling of what the code-agent market will absorb, and then modulating access via the 50% cap during the ramp. Opus 4.8 at $5 and $25 stays the default for a reason."— iwoszapar analyst note, July 10 2026

The distribution stack that watches this closely

Three surfaces move on Sunday's cutover. Claude Code v2.1.207, released Friday, now defaults to Claude Opus 4.8 across Bedrock, Vertex AI and Foundry, which means teams that never explicitly opted into Fable 5 through the console will not see incremental usage-credit draws even after the extension expires. Cursor, Windsurf and Cline reroute their default Fable 5 offerings behind a checkbox that surfaces the metered rate ahead of the first prompt, on Anthropic's request. Windsurf's changelog Friday named "usage transparency for Fable 5" as the shipping item; Cursor pushed the same behavior a day earlier. Aggregators including Vercel AI Gateway and OpenRouter surface the metered rate as of Sunday but continue to route Fable 5 through the same tokens-per-minute quota Anthropic assigned them last week; that quota is the ceiling above which routed traffic falls back to Opus 4.8.

The market read on the cutover is uneven. TradingKey's coverage over the weekend pointed at the timing coincidence with OpenAI's July 9 GPT-5.6 general-availability drop and read Sunday's activation as a defensive move to shore up per-seat revenue during a week in which GPT-5.6 Sol at $5 / $30 per million tokens undercuts Fable 5's metered price by 50 percent on input and 40 percent on output. Anthropic has publicly declined to compare the two directly. The company's line is that Fable 5's Terminal-Bench 2.1 lead at 92.7 percent still outstrips GPT-5.6 Sol's 88.8 percent for the code-agent workloads that dominate Fable 5 usage, and that the price premium is priced against benchmark leadership, not against Sol at face value.

What breaks next

Three follow-ons matter this week. First, Anthropic's public status page will surface Fable 5 quota-exceeded errors at the seat level starting Monday, and how the company handles the customer-support wave is the operational test of whether it can hold Pro users through the metered transition. Second, Fable 5 usage on the confidential $30 billion annualized revenue run-rate that Anthropic reported through July matters directly to how the company thinks about the September 1 pricing review; if Sunday's cutover holds and metered draw is high enough, the next move likely is not a Fable 5 rate cut but a Sonnet 5 rate hold at $2 / $10 past August 31 to keep the affordability floor. Third, the cutover establishes the template for how Anthropic manages the Fable 5.1 launch window later this quarter. Anthropic engineers have said publicly that Fable 5.1 will target the same $10 / $50 tier at launch rather than the Sonnet 5 tier; Sunday morning's activation is the empirical test of whether the market will pay it.

"OpenAI, through its hardware subsidiary IO Products, engaged in a scheme to acquire Apple's trade secrets at every level of its organization, from members of its Technical Staff to its Chief Hardware Officer, and in coordination with business partners."— Apple complaint, N.D. Apple's language calls OpenAI's hardware program "the shakiest of foundations, rotten to its core." Reuters, US News and Hardware Busters over the weekend added that testing took only six weeks and found no major issues, that Iris joins Broadcom's Trainium-partner track as a co-designed chip aimed at inference and internal ranking workloads, and that the chip is part of a four-generation MTIA roadmap through Meta's 2027 compute plan. The second piece is Sturgeon County. Meta broke ground on a CA$13 billion 1-gigawatt datacenter in Alberta with headroom to 1.8 GW, powered on 100 percent clean and renewable energy behind a closed-loop liquid-cooling system with zero operational water use. The Alberta site is Meta's 33rd datacenter globally and its first in Canada, and Meta's public briefing put the peak-construction workforce at 3,000-plus and the operational headcount at 300-plus.

Meta's compute target is 14 gigawatts across the global fleet by end of 2027. That is a doubling of current capacity and it lines up with the internal memo Reuters sourced on Thursday. Iris production in September is the load-bearing element under that doubling. Iris carries the ranking, retrieval and inference workloads Meta has historically run on Nvidia H100 and H200 racks; peeling those onto MTIA silicon frees GPU capacity for Muse frontier-model training and lets Meta stretch its 2026 GPU procurement into 2027 without a corresponding Nvidia budget expansion. The stock market's read of the combined announcements pushed META shares up 7 percent intraday on Friday, and Susquehanna's Sunday note put the combined Iris-plus-Alberta cost avoidance versus a pure-Nvidia buildout at $18 to $22 billion through 2028.

"We're breaking ground on a new 1GW data center in Sturgeon County, Alberta, our first data center in Canada and 33rd in our global fleet."— Meta Newsroom, July 8 2026

That structure mirrors the co-design pattern OpenAI booked with Broadcom for the Jalapeño inference accelerator disclosed last week and the pattern Apple and Amazon each already established with Broadcom on Trainium and the R1 Ultra series. Meta's Iris is the third live Broadcom-anchored ASIC program at hyperscaler scale in 2026. Broadcom's semi-custom silicon revenue guide for the fiscal year now sits at $17 to $19 billion, and the Meta program is the largest single input to that number.

Alberta's site selection matters at the policy level as much as at the operations level. Meta chose Sturgeon County partly for grid capacity, partly for permitting speed, and partly to build the political case that Canadian AI infrastructure investment is on the table if Ottawa can hold the pattern together. The province of Alberta paired the Meta announcement with a companion CA$4.6 billion Project Greenlight natural-gas plant to bank first-of-power reliability under the Alberta Electric System Operator interim gap plan. That combination, on 100 percent clean energy under Meta's power-purchase side but with gas peakers under the utility's side, is now the template the Aether Infrastructure consortium bidding for InvestAI's Strasbourg gigafactories references in its own filing.

FTC opens comment window on policy statement declaring state laws that require alteration of "truthful outputs of AI models" impliedly preempted

FTC · 2026-07-12 · Source ↗

The FTC's proposed policy statement on AI accuracy declares state laws requiring alteration of truthful AI-model outputs impliedly preempted under the December 11 2025 executive order on a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence. Public comment runs through July 31.

The Federal Trade Commission's July release of a proposed policy statement on AI accuracy landed with a specific legal claim: state laws that require model providers to alter the "truthful outputs of AI models" are impliedly preempted by federal law under the framework the President established in the December 11 2025 executive order "Ensuring a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence." The proposed statement runs a public comment window through July 31, 2026, and the Commission's release identifies specific state AI transparency and content-moderation statutes that would be invalid under the preemption theory if the Commission adopts the statement in its current form.

The mechanism the FTC lays out is a Section 5 authority claim over unfair or deceptive practices. The Commission argues that a state statute compelling a provider to alter model output that is otherwise factually accurate creates a compliance regime that federal AI development priorities cannot coexist with, and therefore is preempted under the Supremacy Clause read through the December executive order. The DOJ's Artificial Intelligence Litigation Task Force is the enforcement arm that would carry preemption challenges into court after the FTC statement is adopted. The DOJ task force was set up by the same December 11 2025 executive order and has been signalling since April that state AI content laws are the first-priority target.

Comment filings so far include the New York Times (opposing preemption), the Motion Picture Association (supporting preemption on First Amendment grounds), the Chamber of Commerce (supporting preemption), Public Citizen (opposing), a coalition of 22 state attorneys general (opposing), and OpenAI, Google and Meta (all supporting). Anthropic has not yet filed a comment; the company's public position is that the Commission's read is legally sound but that the statement's scope should be narrowed to a specific class of outputs. Enforcement uncertainty remains the compliance headache for AI model providers dealing with a patchwork of state statutes now in effect in California, Colorado, New York, Illinois and Texas.

"The federal government's priorities for AI innovation cannot coexist with a compliance regime that compels model providers to alter otherwise-truthful output on a per-state basis."— FTC proposed policy statement, July 2026
