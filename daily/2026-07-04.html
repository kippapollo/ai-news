Altman pitches Trump on a 5 percent federal stake in every frontier AI lab, worth $42.6 billion at OpenAI alone

The Financial Times broke it Wednesday night and Bloomberg matched Thursday morning: Sam Altman spent June pitching President Trump, Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick and Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent on an "Alaska Permanent Fund for AI" that hands Washington common stock in OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, xAI and Meta AI. A term sheet is due to Bessent by mid-July. Musk on X called the plan "a shakedown dressed as a dividend." Anthropic told Reuters it has had no such conversation.

Financial Times · Bloomberg · CNBC · 2026-07-03 · Source ↗ ·

The Financial Times reported Wednesday night and Bloomberg's Kate Clark and Shirin Ghaffary matched Thursday morning that OpenAI CEO Sam Altman has spent June and early July pitching senior members of the Trump administration on a plan under which the United States government would take a 5 percent common-stock equity stake in every leading US frontier-AI developer. CNBC's Steve Kovach and Kate Rooney followed on Friday morning with a detailed policy read. At OpenAI's most recent $852 billion secondary-market mark, the government's stake would sit at $42.6 billion. Combined stakes across OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, xAI, Meta AI and other qualifying labs could exceed $180 billion in initial value.

Altman is framing the arrangement as an "Alaska Permanent Fund for AI," arguing that a dividend from those stakes should flow directly to US taxpayers. Two people familiar with the conversations told the FT that Altman has raised the proposal directly with President Trump in a June 24 Oval Office meeting, and separately with Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick and Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent. Bessent is understood to have asked Altman to prepare a written term sheet by mid-July that would specify the equity class, the dividend distribution mechanism and the treatment of any liquidity event.

Giving the American public a financial interest in the frontier is the honest way to align AI outcomes with public benefit.— Sam Altman, CEO, OpenAI, per FT sources

The White House review

CNBC reported that senior White House economic adviser Kevin Hassett is coordinating the internal review. Hassett's team is working through legal opinions on whether a common-stock stake would trigger CFIUS reporting for foreign investors already holding equity in the labs, and whether the government's stake would be treated as a passive investment or as a controlling interest under the Bank Holding Company Act. The FT's sources described the plan as an opening move rather than a fully baked proposal. A White House official told CNBC that "the President is willing to have the conversation but nothing about this is agreed."

Trump has publicly floated the idea of taxpayer stakes in AI companies. At a June 30 rally in Ohio he told supporters that "Americans deserve to own a piece of the AI companies that they helped make possible." The rally line lands close enough to Altman's pitch language that both Bloomberg and CNBC reporters treated it as a signal the White House is at least receptive to the framing.

Anthropic declines, Musk pushes back

Anthropic told Reuters on Thursday that neither CEO Dario Amodei nor the Anthropic board has discussed a US government equity stake with the Trump administration or with OpenAI. Fortune added that Anthropic is instead proposing a digital dividend funded through AI-sector taxes rather than through direct equity ownership. A source close to Google DeepMind told CNBC that Google "would need to see a very specific structure" before commenting. Meta declined to comment. xAI's Elon Musk posted on X that the plan is "a shakedown dressed as a dividend."

Anthropic has had no discussion with the Trump administration about the US government taking an equity stake in the company.— Anthropic spokesperson to Reuters

The Musk objection matters because xAI is one of the five labs Altman's proposal would cover. Any voluntary framework that xAI declines to sign creates an obvious asymmetry: the labs that accept federal ownership get regulatory alignment cover, and the labs that refuse operate outside that shield. Bloomberg noted the Musk-Altman feud makes the coordination problem sharper than the pitch acknowledges.

Legal analysis

Sullivan & Cromwell partner H. Rodgin Cohen told Bloomberg that a passive federal-government common-stock stake in a private company "is not without precedent but is highly unusual outside of the crisis-era interventions in AIG, General Motors and Citigroup." Cohen said Congressional authorisation would almost certainly be required for a permanent, non-crisis equity stake, and that the dividend-distribution mechanism to taxpayers would need dedicated legislation. All three crisis-era stakes were wound down within five years, none of them structured as permanent claims on private-company cash flows.

The IPO gate

OpenAI filed a confidential S-1 on June 8 targeting a public-market debut in late 2026 or early 2027 at valuations that market sources say could exceed $1 trillion. Altman's political-alignment pitch is being read by bankers and investors as the equity-market gate that must clear before a public offering can price. A 5 percent stake at the $852 billion secondary mark implies $42.6 billion. A 5 percent stake at a $1 trillion IPO valuation implies $50 billion in OpenAI alone. The proposal creates an implicit ceiling on the political risk any government body can create for OpenAI post-IPO, since Washington itself would be a shareholder.

The proposal is in its early stages, would likely require Congressional approval, and it is unclear whether other major AI companies would support a similar arrangement.— Financial Times, citing two people familiar

The pitch lands one week after the White House delayed the domestic release of OpenAI's GPT-5.6 Sol frontier model pending a national-security review, and two weeks after the Department of Commerce's June 12 order that briefly restricted Claude Fable 5. Altman argued to Trump that a government equity stake would make Washington a stakeholder in the labs' financial health and would reduce the political appetite for future export-control shocks. Fable 5's nineteen-day export cutoff, which Anthropic exited on July 1, is the reference case Altman is asking Washington to avoid repeating.

The bigger frame

The 5 percent proposal sits inside a wider realignment of the frontier-AI regulatory perimeter this week. The White House is finalising a separate voluntary framework for pre-release testing that OpenAI, Google DeepMind and Anthropic are expected to sign in the week of July 7. Anthropic published a five-band Cyber Jailbreak Severity scale co-signed by Amazon, Microsoft and Google. Anthropic entered custom-silicon talks with Samsung Foundry, closing the last gap in hyperscaler in-house-chip roadmaps. Each move sits at a different point on the frontier value chain, and each carries its own implicit relationship with Washington.

Altman's federal-equity pitch is the boldest of the moves and the least likely to close in its original form. But even a version that lands in Congress six months from now reshapes the political economy of frontier AI. If Washington ends up owning $50 billion of OpenAI at the IPO, every export-control episode after that point becomes a shareholder question, and the Fable 5 precedent becomes the exception rather than the model for how the frontier gets governed.

Mistral open-sources Leanstral 1.5, solves 587 of 672 PutnamBench proofs at $4 each

MarkTechPost · Mistral · 2026-07-03 · Source ↗ ·

Mistral shipped Leanstral 1.5 on Friday as a 119B mixture-of-experts model with 6.5B active parameters, targeting Lean 4 theorem proving under an Apache 2.0 license. Test-time scaling from 50K to 4M tokens moves PutnamBench solves from 44 to 587 out of 672, at roughly $4 per problem against $300 for competing systems built on Seed-Prover.

Mistral released Leanstral 1.5 on July 3 as an Apache 2.0 Lean 4 code agent, publishing weights on Hugging Face under the repository name Leanstral-1.5-119B-A6B. The model runs a 119-billion-parameter mixture-of-experts routing four of 128 experts per token, activating 6.5 billion parameters per step. Context length is 256K tokens. Input accepts text and images, output is text only, and the free API endpoint leanstral-1-5 ships alongside the download.

The Mistral team benchmarked Leanstral 1.5 on PutnamBench, the 672-problem corpus of Putnam mathematical competition problems ported to Lean 4. At a 4-million-token budget the model closes 587 problems. At 25K tokens it clears 44, at 200K it clears 244, at 1M it clears 493. The team reports roughly $4 per PutnamBench problem attempted, versus $300 or more per problem for competing systems built on top of Seed-Prover and similar closed harnesses.

A proof assistant checks every logical step mechanically.— Mistral AI, Leanstral 1.5 announcement

Leanstral 1.5 saturates miniF2F on both validation and test splits at 100 percent. It sets state-of-the-art on FATE-H at 87 percent and FATE-X at 34 percent, the two hardest algebra tracks in the FATE benchmark suite. On FLTEval, pass@1 climbs to 28.9 from a prior 21.9, and pass@8 hits 43.2, ahead of Claude Opus 4.6 at 39.6.

The code agent loop

Leanstral 1.5 lives inside a raw filesystem, edits files, runs bash commands, and reads the Lean language server for goals, errors, and type information. The team highlights an AVL tree proof of O(log n) time complexity that Leanstral closed in a single session, editing and recompiling lemmas across the run.

This lets it complete partial proofs, build auxiliary lemmas, and persist through context compaction.— Mistral AI

Training ran in three stages: mid-training on Lean-heavy corpora, supervised fine-tuning on curated proof traces, then reinforcement learning with CISPO in a multiturn theorem-proving environment and a parallel code agent environment with filesystem operations. Mistral cross-trained both environments so the model learned to switch between raw proof search and general Lean 4 project engineering. The company says the resulting weights find real bugs in open-source code as a side effect, and lists five previously unreported issues Leanstral identified across 57 tested repositories.

What it costs versus what it saves

The 4-million-token budget that closes 587 PutnamBench problems is not cheap in absolute terms. Running the model at that budget costs on the order of hours per problem on two 80GB GPUs. The Mistral team argues the tradeoff shifts once formal proofs enter production settings where a single verified lemma replaces months of manual proof work. At $4 per Putnam problem, undergraduate math olympiad training pipelines can afford to sweep entire problem sets during development. The Apache 2.0 license removes the last friction: derivatives can ship inside commercial products without royalty terms.

Adam Zambo of Carnegie Mellon called Leanstral's saturation of miniF2F "the end of the line for that benchmark," arguing that the community now needs a harder replacement corpus if it wants to keep measuring proof-agent progress. Mistral says a Leanstral-2 update is already in training on a broader Lean 4 corpus expanded through the Mathlib community.

Anthropic publishes CJS-0 to CJS-4 jailbreak severity scale co-signed by Amazon, Microsoft, Google

Cyber Security News · Crypto Briefing · 2026-07-03 · Source ↗ ·

Anthropic paired the global Fable 5 redeployment with a five-band Cyber Jailbreak Severity scale scored across capability gain, breadth, weaponisation effort and discoverability. Amazon's CJ Moses, Microsoft's Alex Weinert and Google DeepMind's Vijay Bolina co-signed. CJS-3 and CJS-4 findings trigger mandatory CISA and NSA notification within 48 hours under the White House framework.

Anthropic published a policy note on Wednesday accompanying the global redeployment of Claude Fable 5, proposing that the frontier-AI industry adopt a shared severity-scoring framework for reported jailbreaks. The framework, which Anthropic developed jointly with Amazon, Microsoft, Google and other members of the Project Glasswing consortium, is designed to give lab security teams, government reviewers and defensive-cybersecurity vendors a common vocabulary for how dangerous a given jailbreak is.

The proposal scores jailbreaks on four independent axes. Capability gain measures how far beyond an attacker's existing tools the jailbreak takes them, from "no meaningful uplift" to "expert operator capability." Breadth of capability gain measures how many distinct offensive tasks the same jailbreak works on. Ease of weaponisation measures how much engineering effort it takes to turn the jailbreak into a running attack. Discoverability measures how easily an attacker without insider knowledge would find the technique independently. The four axes combine into a five-band rating from CJS-0 (Informational) through CJS-4 (Critical).

The industry has been talking about jailbreaks like they are all the same event. They are not. We need a CVSS-equivalent for cyber jailbreaks and we need it now.— Jason Clinton, CISO, Anthropic

Four safety categories

Fable 5's safety classifier now sorts cybersecurity requests into four categories rather than blocking every security-adjacent prompt. Prohibited use covers ransomware, wipers, cyber-physical sabotage, malware development, C2 infrastructure, and defense evasion. Anthropic blocks these outright. High-risk dual use covers penetration testing, exploit development, privilege escalation, and high-uplift vulnerability discovery. Fable 5 blocks these pending better authorization controls. Low-risk dual use covers OSINT gathering, work with already-known vulnerabilities, and cryptographic protocol testing. The model allows these subject to a safety margin. Benign use covers secure coding, patch management, log analysis, malware reverse engineering, and security education.

June 12 as reference case

The framework exists because the government reaction to Fable 5's original June jailbreak had no shared severity baseline. Amazon researchers reported a bypass to Anthropic on June 11. The Commerce Department ordered Anthropic to suspend Fable 5 and Mythos 5 access for foreign nationals the next day, without publicly defining what threshold triggered the order. Anthropic redeployed the model on July 1 after Commerce lifted the export controls June 30. The CJS scale gives the next disclosure a common yardstick before either side reaches for emergency measures. The June 12 Fable 5 jailbreak was assigned a preliminary CJS-3 rating under the draft rubric.

When the four largest US frontier labs agree on the same severity taxonomy, the defensive community finally gets a common signal to act on.— CJ Moses, Vice President and CISO, Amazon

Government interface

The CJS framework connects directly into the White House voluntary release framework expected to be announced the week of July 7. Under that framework, labs must submit a CJS rating for every reported jailbreak of a covered model within 14 days. CJS-3 and CJS-4 ratings trigger mandatory notifications to CISA and the NSA within 48 hours. Anthropic said the framework is a working draft and that the consortium is soliciting feedback through August 15. A finalised v1.0 rubric will be published in September.

OpenAI declined to comment on the record but a source close to OpenAI's safety team said OpenAI "is broadly supportive of a shared severity taxonomy" but wants to negotiate specific rubric definitions before signing. Meta AI and xAI have not yet responded publicly. Mistral said it will "evaluate the framework carefully and respond by end of July." Anthropic committed to publishing retroactive CJS ratings for every reported jailbreak of the Claude 4-series and Claude 5-series models by year end.
