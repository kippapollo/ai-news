Qualcomm puts a CUDA-agnostic compiler stack under its data-center play, paying $3.92 billion in stock for Modular and naming Meta as its first big CPU customer

Qualcomm closed Investor Day on June 24 with an all-stock $3.92 billion acquisition of Chris Lattner's Modular at 19.2 million shares, a new Dragonfly C1000 250-plus-core 5-gigahertz server CPU with a multi-year, multi-generation Meta Platforms supply agreement, and a Dragonfly AI300 accelerator built for liquid and air cooling, alongside fiscal-2029 non-handset revenue guidance lifted to $40 billion from $22 billion and adjusted EPS to $18 against a $15.26 consensus. The stock jumped 14 percent in after-hours trading and reports surfaced of separate Qualcomm talks to buy Tenstorrent at $8 to $10 billion.

SiliconANGLE · CNBC · Modular · 2026-06-24 · Source ↗ ·

Qualcomm closed its annual Investor Day on Wednesday, June 24 with three announcements that reorganise the company's pitch from a smartphone-modem business with a side data-center bet into a vertically integrated agentic-AI platform. The first is the all-stock $3.92 billion acquisition of Modular Inc., the AI infrastructure software company founded by LLVM and Swift creator Chris Lattner. Qualcomm is paying 19.2 million of its own shares for the company, a price that more than doubles Modular's most recent funding round of $380 million from GV and Greylock. The deal is expected to close in the second half of 2026, subject to customary regulatory approvals. The second is the formal launch of a new server-CPU family called Dragonfly C1000, a 250-plus-core processor running above 5 gigahertz with what the company described as roughly double the performance-per-watt of comparable server processors. Qualcomm named Meta Platforms as the multi-year, multi-generation customer who will deploy the C1000 in production, without disclosing the financial terms. The third is a refresh of the company's data-center accelerator roadmap under the Dragonfly AI300 name, a machine-learning chip designed to slot into either liquid-cooled or air-cooled racks. The combined package is what gave the stock a 14 percent jump in after-hours trading and what let CFO Akash Palkhiwala lift fiscal-2029 guidance for non-handset revenue to $40 billion from a previously communicated $22 billion, with adjusted earnings per share at $18 against a consensus of $15.26.

Why Modular matters more than the price tag suggests

Modular is best understood as a compiler-stack bet against Nvidia's CUDA moat. The company's MAX platform is a vendor-neutral runtime that lets developers write inference code once and execute it across heterogeneous hardware, Nvidia GPUs and AMD chips and Intel processors and Qualcomm NPUs and custom ASICs, without rewriting per target. The companion language Mojo extends Python with the kind of low-level control that Triton or CUDA-C provide on Nvidia hardware. The strategic logic is that any non-Nvidia compute platform faces the same headwind: customers who have written their model-serving code against CUDA's primitives cannot move that code to a different stack without re-tuning. Modular's pitch is that the porting layer is a product, and that the platform supplier who controls it owns the migration path away from Nvidia. Buying it inside Qualcomm gives the Dragonfly C1000 and AI300 a software story they did not have last quarter, and it lets Qualcomm sell the Modular layer to customers who buy other accelerators while still cashing the Qualcomm-margin software dollar.

"As agentic AI scales across data centers and edge environments, the industry is moving toward disaggregated, multi-vendor architectures that demand a more open and modern software foundation."— Cristiano Amon, President and CEO, Qualcomm

Lattner reframed the move in personal terms in the Modular blog post that went up immediately after the announcement. He has been here before: LLVM at Apple, then Swift, then a stint inside Google running Brain's hardware-software interface team, then a year on the Tesla autopilot team. The thesis at each stop has been the same. Compiler infrastructure is where platform power gets cashed. Owning the runtime that everybody else's models route through compounds.

"Joining Qualcomm gives us the scale and platform reach to accelerate that mission. To make AI development more accessible and performant."— Chris Lattner, Co-founder and CEO, Modular

Dragonfly C1000, the Meta supply deal, and what the fiscal-2029 number means

The Dragonfly C1000 specification is aggressive. Two hundred and fifty cores at sustained clocks above five gigahertz puts the chip in the high-end x86 server territory currently held by AMD's Bergamo and Sienna SKUs and Intel's Sierra Forest, with a performance-per-watt advantage that Qualcomm pegged at roughly two times comparable parts. The chip is Arm-based, derived from the Nuvia design team Qualcomm acquired in 2021. Meta is the first named customer. The supply agreement is multi-year and multi-generation, which is the structure that ARM-based server CPUs have been seeking since the early Graviton days at AWS: a hyperscaler committing to a roadmap, not a one-off purchase. Financial terms were not disclosed. The Dragonfly AI300 sits on the accelerator side. It is the same chip family Qualcomm previewed earlier this year as AI200 and AI250, now renamed and re-positioned alongside the CPU under a single Dragonfly brand. The accelerator is engineered for both liquid and air-cooled deployment, which matters for the second-tier customers who cannot economically rebuild their existing data halls for direct-liquid only.

The fiscal-2029 guidance change is the part that priced into the stock. Non-handset revenue jumped to a $40 billion target from $22 billion previously, almost doubling the trajectory inside the same window. Adjusted EPS at $18 sits roughly 18 percent above the analyst consensus of $15.26. Qualcomm did not specify how the $40 billion splits between automotive, IoT and data center, but the bulk of the new headroom is data center: there is no other business line growing fast enough to add $18 billion of guidance in three fiscal years.

The Tenstorrent rumour and the broader pattern

Reuters and Bloomberg reported during the day that Qualcomm is in separate talks to acquire AI chip startup Tenstorrent for $8 to $10 billion, several times the price Qualcomm just paid for Modular. Tenstorrent is the RISC-V-based AI compute company led by Jim Keller, and it has been the most credible non-Nvidia hardware bet outside the hyperscalers' internal ASIC programs. The pattern across the day reads as Qualcomm spending what it needs to spend, in equity, to assemble the full vertical stack: CPU and accelerator silicon for the hardware layer, Modular for the runtime, RISC-V optionality at the architecture layer, Meta as the anchor customer at the demand layer. Whether the Tenstorrent deal closes is the open question that determines whether the architectural breadth claim holds.

The competitive read for the rest of the industry is mixed. Nvidia keeps the CUDA installed base and the training market. AMD and Intel keep their x86 footprint. The pressure lands on the second-tier accelerator startups, who have largely competed on price and on per-workload performance and who now face a Qualcomm with shipping hyperscaler customers and a compiler runtime under one roof. The pressure also lands on the hyperscaler internal silicon programs at Google, AWS, Microsoft and Meta, all of whom face the same compiler problem Modular was built to solve. Meta's decision to take the C1000 first is the most informative single fact in the announcement: it suggests Meta sees value in a merchant-silicon partner with a credible runtime, not only in extending its own MTIA and Athena programs.

OpenAI moved its specialised cybersecurity model GPT-5.5-Cyber out of limited preview on Wednesday, June 24, broadening the set of organisations that can apply for Trusted Access for Cyber and publishing the underlying benchmark numbers. On CyberGym, the agent-vulnerability discovery benchmark, GPT-5.5-Cyber scores 85.6 percent against 81.8 percent for standard GPT-5.5. On ExploitGym, which measures end-to-end exploit construction, the model scores 39.5 percent against 25.95 percent. On SEC-bench Pro, which evaluates security-engineering tasks, the score is 69.8 percent. The commercial partners cleared into the Trusted Access tier as of Wednesday are Akamai, Cisco, Cloudflare, CrowdStrike, Fortinet, Oracle, Palo Alto Networks and Zscaler. The government partners are Australia, Canada, France, Germany, Japan, South Korea and EU institutions. OpenAI continues to recommend standard GPT-5.5 as the default cybersecurity model for most workflows, with GPT-5.5-Cyber reserved for the more permissive end of the Trusted Access programme.

Alongside the model graduation, OpenAI shipped Patch the Planet, the Daybreak-branded open-source security programme run jointly with Trail of Bits and HackerOne. Patch the Planet pairs full-time security engineers from Trail of Bits with AI-assisted vulnerability research, then routes findings through HackerOne for coordinated disclosure. The initial set of projects publicly enrolled is cURL, NATS Server, pyca/cryptography, Sigstore, aiohttp, the Go project, freenginx, Python and python.org, with more than 30 projects committed to participate. The five-day initial sprint reported hundreds of security issues identified and dozens of patches merged, with many more findings still going through coordinated disclosure. Participating projects receive access to ChatGPT Pro, conditional access to Codex Security, and API credits earmarked for core development, maintainer automation and release workflows.

"GPT-5.5 remains our primary recommended model for most cybersecurity workflows through Trusted Access for Cyber. GPT-5.5-Cyber is designed to provide more permissive access for a smaller set of highly verified defenders and organizations."— OpenAI Trusted Access for Cyber announcement, June 24

The architectural design of Trusted Access is the part worth attending to. OpenAI's standard policy stack restricts capabilities like vulnerability identification, malware analysis and binary reverse engineering with classifier-based refusals, because the same capabilities that defenders use overlap with the capabilities that attackers use. Trusted Access flips that envelope for vetted organisations: vulnerability identification, triage, malware analysis, binary reverse engineering, detection engineering and patch validation pass through with reduced refusal rate, while requests that look like credential theft, stealth, persistence, payload deployment or unauthorised exploitation of third-party systems continue to refuse. The vetting layer is the gate. Patch the Planet is the productisation of the policy: a defined defender set, a defined open-source-project surface, an external security partner that owns the human-review step, and a disclosure pipeline that moves the output back into upstream packages.

The competitive context is Anthropic's parallel cybersecurity program, which has been offline since June 12 along with the rest of Fable 5 and Mythos 5 under the export-control directive, and Google's Project Zero plus Big Sleep automated-vulnerability programme inside DeepMind. OpenAI is moving on the empty space.

Qwen-AgentWorld posts a 35B and a 397B-A17B language world model that simulates seven agent environments and edges past GPT-5.4 on AgentWorldBench

arXiv 2606.24597 · Hugging Face Daily Papers · 2026-06-24 · Source ↗

The 397B-A17B model scores 58.71 on AgentWorldBench against GPT-5.4 at 58.25, simulating MCP, Search, Terminal, Software Engineering, Android, Web and OS environments through long chain-of-thought, trained in three stages of CPT, SFT and rubric-and-rule RL.

The Qwen Team's Qwen-AgentWorld paper at 86 upvotes is the first language world model to simulate seven distinct agent environments through long chain-of-thought reasoning. The seven covered are MCP, Search, Terminal, Software Engineering, Android, Web and OS. The release ships two model sizes: Qwen-AgentWorld-35B-A3B and Qwen-AgentWorld-397B-A17B, both mixture-of-experts. The training recipe is three-stage. CPT, continued pre-training, injects state-transition dynamics and world knowledge from environment interaction logs. SFT activates next-state-prediction thinking patterns. RL with hybrid rubric-and-rule rewards sharpens simulation fidelity against ground-truth environment behaviour. The companion benchmark AgentWorldBench is built from real environment interactions of frontier proprietary models such as Claude Opus 4.6 on widely used agent benchmarks including Terminal-Bench 1.0 and 2.0 and OSWorld-Verified, an explicit out-of-distribution evaluation set.

The headline number is 58.71. Qwen-AgentWorld-397B-A17B beats every frontier proprietary model the authors evaluated on the unified AgentWorldBench score, edging GPT-5.4 at 58.25 and beating Claude Opus 4.6 and Gemini 3.5 Pro variants. The 35B-A3B variant, with only 3 billion active parameters, sits below the frontier models on the overall score but inside the same general envelope on individual environments where state-transition complexity is lower. The Qwen team frames the work as the first time a small or mid-size open model has fully simulated an agent environment instead of just producing the next action inside one.

The downstream consequence is the part that matters for the broader research direction. World models are the substrate for offline RL and for planning at training time. If a language world model can simulate a terminal session, a browser or an Android UI faithfully enough that a separate policy model can train against the simulator instead of against the live environment, the cost of training capable agents drops by the cost of all the environment interactions. The paper hints at exactly that direction in its experiment design. Both models, the benchmark and the AgentWorldBench evaluation set are available on Hugging Face and GitHub at the QwenLM/Qwen-AgentWorld and Qwen/AgentWorldBench repositories.

NatureBench shows the strongest coding agent matches Nature-family SOTA on only 17.8 percent of 90 cross-discipline ML tasks

arXiv 2606.24530 · Hugging Face Daily Papers · 2026-06-24 · Source ↗

Frontis AI's NatureBench distils 90 tasks from peer-reviewed Nature-family papers across six scientific domains. The strongest evaluated model beats published state of the art on 17.8 percent of tasks. The companion NatureGym pipeline turns a paper into a containerised task package automatically.

NatureBench, the second-most-upvoted Hugging Face paper on June 24 at 48 upvotes, is a cross-discipline benchmark of 90 tasks distilled from peer-reviewed publications in the Nature family of journals, spanning six scientific domains. The construction pipeline, called NatureGym, takes a published paper as input, extracts the dataset and held-out test set, builds a containerised task package with an automated evaluator, and scores any candidate model against the source paper's reported state of the art. The framing question Frontis AI puts on the cover is whether coding agents can move beyond reproduction toward discovery. The answer is mostly no.

The strongest model the authors evaluated surpasses published SOTA on only 17.8 percent of the 90 tasks under the g greater than 0.1 criterion, the threshold the authors set for declaring an agent's solution meaningfully better than the published baseline. The methodological analysis pushes harder. When the authors trace the pathway by which agents succeed on the 17.8 percent of tasks they do solve, the bulk of the wins come not from genuine scientific invention but from methodological translation, the agent reframing the published problem as a familiar supervised-prediction task and applying a strong off-the-shelf model. The agents are not doing science, they are doing pattern matching from a science context onto the ML context where they have leverage.

The release ships the benchmark, NatureGym, and a public leaderboard with maintainer-side reproduction. The framing matters for the broader debate on AI-for-science. Recent OpenAI work on GPT-5 hypothesis generation, the announcement that an immunologist used GPT-5 Pro to predict an unpublished T-cell experiment, and the John Jumper move from DeepMind to Anthropic last week have all pushed the narrative that frontier models are starting to do real scientific work. NatureBench is the first systematic dataset that lets that claim be measured against the published SOTA in mainstream-journal science, and the first read is that the gap to actual discovery is wider than the demos suggest.

A Stanford, Berkeley, UT Austin, NYU, UW, UCSD, ASU, CMU, UCLA, UNC, TUM and LAION collaboration releases SFT and RL pipelines, NL2Bash plus InferredBugs source tasks, and OpenThinker-Agent-v1 post-trained from Qwen3-8B for terminal and SWE workflows.

OpenThoughts-Agent, the 27-upvote project on Wednesday's Hugging Face Daily Papers, is the open-source counterpart to the proprietary data pipelines that frontier labs use to train coding and computer-use agents. The release is a research collaboration anchored at Stanford, UC Berkeley, UT Austin, NYU, the University of Washington, UCSD, Arizona State, Carnegie Mellon, UCLA, UNC Chapel Hill, the Technical University of Munich and the LAION research network. Kwai's MobileForge ships annotation-free adaptation for mobile GUI agents via hierarchical feedback-guided policy optimisation

Hugging Face Daily Papers · 2026-06-24 · Source ↗

MobileForge from kwai, at 34 upvotes on the day, removes the human-annotation step from cross-app mobile GUI adaptation by replacing it with a hierarchical feedback signal that drives policy optimisation on the agent's own rollouts.

MobileForge, the third-most-upvoted Hugging Face paper on June 24 from the kwai research team, attacks the annotation bottleneck that has slowed the cross-app generalisation of mobile GUI agents. The standard pipeline for training a mobile agent on a new app or a new device family today requires human annotators to label which UI element the agent should click, in what order and with what expected effect. The cost of that annotation is the main reason mobile agents work reasonably well on the apps inside their training distribution and degrade quickly on apps outside it. MobileForge replaces the human label with a hierarchical feedback signal computed from the agent's own rollouts. A top-level evaluator scores whether the agent completed the user-level task. A mid-level evaluator scores whether intermediate sub-goals were reached. A bottom-level evaluator scores individual action quality. The three layers feed into a policy-optimisation loop that improves the agent without per-step human supervision.

The result is a deployable agent that adapts to a new app from rollouts alone. The companion paper, also from kwai and at 33 upvotes, is MemGUI-Agent. Where MobileForge solves the no-annotation problem, MemGUI-Agent solves the cross-session memory problem: it manages context proactively so the agent can continue a multi-step task across app launches and time gaps. The two together describe a working stack for autonomous mobile workflows that does not depend on offline labelling. Both papers fit into kwai's broader Doubao-adjacent research push on consumer-facing agents, and both ship with code released to the project's GitHub.

MemGUI-Bench finds existing mobile agents use 5 to 11 percent of memory-relevant tasks and exposes five distinct failure modes

arXiv 2602.06075 · Hugging Face Daily Papers · 2026-06-24 · Source ↗

The kwai MemGUI benchmark covers 128 tasks across 26 mobile apps, 89.8 percent of which require cross-temporal or cross-spatial memory retention. The companion MemGUI-Eval pipeline runs progressive scrutiny with seven hierarchical metrics across 11 state-of-the-art agent systems.

MemGUI-Bench, the kwai paper at 33 upvotes on Wednesday's Daily Papers, opens with a finding that reframes the mobile-agent evaluation landscape. Existing mobile GUI agent benchmarks systematically under-test memory: between 5.2 and 11.8 percent of tasks across the major benchmarks actually require an agent to remember anything between session boundaries, and none evaluate cross-session learning explicitly. The MemGUI-Bench suite corrects the gap with 128 tasks across 26 applications, 89.8 percent of which require cross-temporal or cross-spatial memory retention to complete. The benchmark adopts a systematic memory taxonomy, evaluating 11 distinct agent systems across 5 architectural families.

The companion evaluation tool, MemGUI-Eval, runs the agents through Progressive Scrutiny, a staged LLM-as-judge protocol with seven hierarchical metrics. The headline result is that all 11 evaluated state-of-the-art agents show significant memory deficits relative to the task floor. Five distinct failure modes emerge: forgetting earlier user preferences, inability to resume after an app reload, contamination from prior sessions, loss of intermediate computed state, and failure to use memory for confirmation prompts. Five corresponding design implications are synthesised. The benchmark, evaluator and a leaderboard are open-source at the lgy0404/MemGUI-Bench repository on GitHub.
