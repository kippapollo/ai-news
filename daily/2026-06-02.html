Anthropic files a confidential S-1 at $965B with $47B annualized revenue, opening the AI IPO season

Anthropic told the SEC on Monday morning that it has submitted a confidential draft registration statement for a U.S. listing, ten days after closing a $65 billion Series H at a $965 billion post-money valuation. The company disclosed that Claude's revenue run rate now sits at $47 billion, up from $9 billion at the end of 2025. The filing puts Anthropic ahead of OpenAI and the SpaceX-merged xAI in the race to test public-market appetite for frontier AI.

CNBC / Bloomberg / TechCrunch · 2026-06-01 · Source ↗ ·

Anthropic confirmed on Monday morning that it has submitted a confidential draft Form S-1 to the Securities and Exchange Commission for a proposed U.S. listing. The company released the news through its standard channels at the open of New York trading. The filing comes ten days after Anthropic closed a $65 billion Series H led by Altimeter Capital, Dragoneer, Greenoaks, Sequoia Capital, Capital Group, Coatue, and D1 Capital Partners at a $965 billion post-money valuation, the largest single venture round on record and one that pushed Anthropic past OpenAI's $852 billion March 2026 valuation for the first time.

The S-1 will not become public until after SEC review and the company sets a price range. Anthropic disclosed only the bare minimum required to confirm the filing: it is preparing to list common stock, the number of shares and price range remain undetermined, and the actual offering depends on market conditions and SEC clearance. The framing language tracks the JOBS Act confidential-submission template that has been the default for technology IPOs since 2012.

The revenue print that made the filing possible

Anthropic stated that Claude's revenue run rate had crossed $47 billion, up from $9 billion at the end of 2025. The growth multiple is the headline number for investors. A $47 billion run rate against a $965 billion valuation implies a forward revenue multiple just above 20x, well inside the multiples software-as-a-service buyers paid for fast-growing infrastructure companies during the 2020-2021 cycle, and a defensible number for the level of growth Anthropic is showing. The comparable for OpenAI, which the Wall Street Journal in late May placed at a $25 billion run rate against an $852 billion valuation, implies a 34x multiple, which is the gap Anthropic's bankers will lean on.

The Claude business itself splits across three loosely tracked segments: the consumer Claude.ai subscription, the Claude API and enterprise platform business, and Claude Code plus the agent SDK product line that ships into developer tools and IDEs. Anthropic has not broken out segment revenue publicly, but the company's executive statements over the past six months have repeatedly placed Claude Code as the fastest-growing segment, and its KPMG, PwC, and ten-financial-services-agents announcements over the past two weeks were meant to set up exactly this filing.

"This gives us the option to go public after the SEC completes its review. The proposed initial public offering will depend on market conditions and other factors."— Anthropic statement accompanying the confidential S-1 submission, June 1, 2026

Sequencing against OpenAI and xAI-SpaceX

Anthropic moved first. OpenAI is widely reported to be preparing its own confidential filing on a similar timeline, with bankers describing a debut window in the third or fourth quarter of 2026. The xAI-SpaceX merged entity is targeting a $1.75 to $2 trillion valuation on its own listing, with Bloomberg's late-May reporting placing the share sale at $75 billion in primary capital. If the three list inside a single twelve-month window, the combined raise approaches the entire 1995-2000 dot-com IPO total, which is the comparison Wedbush analysts seized on Monday morning. "We believe this represents an opening of the floodgates for the IPO market," the firm wrote in a note circulated before the open.

The timing is favorable for Anthropic. The S&P 500 has held within 4% of its all-time high through May, the Nasdaq AI subindex is up 31% year to date, and the bankers handling the deal have a clean six-month forward calendar in which to set the roadshow. Bank syndicate names have not been disclosed in the confidential submission; the company is widely expected to use Morgan Stanley and Goldman Sachs as joint book-runners, the pair that handled most of the late-2025 mega-rounds for Anthropic's preferred-stock investors.

What the prospectus will eventually show

The forthcoming public S-1 will be the first comprehensive look the market gets into a frontier AI lab's economics. Anthropic will have to disclose gross margins on Claude inference, customer concentration, compute supply contracts with Amazon and Google, hardware-purchase obligations, the financial structure of its $65 billion Series H, and the per-segment economics of Claude consumer, API, and Code. The compute cost-of-revenue line is the most-watched number; venture investors have argued for two years that frontier AI margins will hold above 50% once training amortizes, while skeptics have pointed to depreciation schedules on rented GPU capacity that could collapse the operating-margin picture.

The prospectus will also be where Anthropic addresses its $1.5 billion authors-class-action settlement, its outstanding Universal Music Publishing copyright case, and the regulatory posture toward AI-safety-driven enterprise sales that has been a Dario Amodei talking point throughout 2026. CEO Dario Amodei has not yet given an on-the-record interview about the filing; his public statements over the past month have framed the company's mission language around "deploying frontier models safely at scale," which the prospectus will need to convert into language a bond-market reader can underwrite.

Why the AI sector reads this as a starting gun

Anthropic going first puts pricing tension on every other AI raise in the calendar. A successful debut at or above the $965 billion private mark resets the floor for OpenAI's IPO and gives xAI-SpaceX a public comp. A weak debut or a wide first-day-trading gap forces every late-stage AI lab to defer its filing or to accept a down round. Bankers, board members, and growth-stage GPs across the AI ecosystem will watch Anthropic's roadshow with more attention than they paid to any cohort of tech IPOs since the 2012 Facebook listing. The company that arrived in 2021 with a $124 million Series A is the company that will set the public-market frame for the next decade of AI.

Nvidia unveils RTX Spark superchip for Windows-on-Arm laptops and desktops, 30 laptops and 10 desktops in fall

Tom's Hardware · 2026-06-01 · Source ↗ ·

A 20-core MediaTek-co-designed Grace CPU pairs with a 6,144-CUDA-core Blackwell GPU and 128 GB unified LPDDR5X to deliver 1 petaflop of AI compute, run 120-billion-parameter LLMs locally, and bring Windows agents into the taskbar. Surface, ASUS, Dell, HP, Lenovo, and MSI ship first.

Jensen Huang opened his Computex GTC Taipei keynote on June 1 by walking through Nvidia's first consumer-facing chip line in a decade. The RTX Spark superchip combines a custom 20-core Arm Grace CPU co-designed with MediaTek, a Blackwell RTX GPU with 6,144 CUDA cores and fifth-generation Tensor cores running at FP4 precision, and up to 128 GB of unified LPDDR5X memory. The two dies sit on a single package linked over NVLink-C2C. Nvidia is rating the combined silicon at 1 petaflop of AI compute, enough to run a 120-billion-parameter LLM with a one-million-token context entirely on-device, edit 12K 4:2:2 video, render 90 GB 3D scenes, and play AAA games at 1440p above 100 fps.

Fall 2026 launch partners are ASUS, Dell, HP, Lenovo, Microsoft Surface, and MSI, with Acer and Gigabyte joining the lineup later in the year. Over thirty RTX Spark laptops and ten desktops will ship across that partner set, all running Windows on Arm. Reference industrial designs include laptops as thin as 14 millimeters and weighing roughly three pounds, with color-accurate tandem OLED panels and G-SYNC, and compact small-form-factor desktops with the same superchip and memory pool.

The PC reinvention frame

The Spark line is the centerpiece of an explicit Nvidia-Microsoft pitch that Windows is becoming an agentic operating system. The superchip's 128 GB of unified memory is the load-bearing claim, since most current Copilot+ PC silicon tops out at 64 GB and cannot host the 70-to-120-billion-parameter models that the new wave of local agents wants to call. Huang framed Spark as the platform that closes that gap. "Everything we've learned over 33 years distilled into one chip," he said. "The PC is being reinvented. For forty years, you launched apps. Click. Type. With RTX Spark and Microsoft Windows, you ask, the PC does the work."

Microsoft's joint pitch, delivered by Satya Nadella in a pre-recorded segment that aired during the keynote, framed the launch as "a real breakthrough" toward "unmetered intelligence to every home and every desk with Windows." The two companies separately announced a set of Windows security primitives, OpenShell, that wraps each agent in a containment boundary with policy, identity, and end-to-end security on the host, and Hermes Agent, a reference agent runtime that uses those primitives to mediate model calls.

"Our goal is to deliver unmetered intelligence to every home and every desk with Windows. RTX Spark marks a real breakthrough towards that vision."— Satya Nadella, Microsoft CEO, in the joint Spark segment

The roadmap behind it

Nvidia gave Spark a three-generation roadmap in the same keynote slide deck. The current generation is built on TSMC 3 nm and ships in fall 2026. A 2027 follow-on, internally called Vera Rubin Spark, will switch to LPDDR6 memory and pair the next-generation Vera CPU with a Rubin client GPU. The 2029 generation, named Rosa Feynman Spark, is on the deck but has no announced memory or process generation. The pattern reads as a CPU-GPU pair refresh every two years that mirrors Nvidia's data-center cadence.

Adobe announced alongside the launch that it is re-architecting Photoshop and Premiere from the ground up for RTX Spark, promising two-times-faster AI and graphics performance, with updates rolling out at Spark availability. The broader Windows ecosystem support roster runs past one hundred titles and includes Blackmagic Design, Blender, CapCut, ComfyUI, and games from Krafton, NetEase, Remedy, Riot, and Xbox. Pricing has not been disclosed and is being held until the OEM launches.

Cosmos 3 ships as an open OpenMDW-licensed physical AI omnimodel with native vision-reasoning and action generation

NVIDIA Blog / GamesBeat / WinBuzzer · 2026-06-01 · Source ↗ ·

A mixture-of-transformers architecture pairs a reasoning block with an expert generator that produces text, image, video, ambient sound, and numerical action data, joint angles and trajectories that robots can execute. Cosmos 3 Super and Nano ship now under OpenMDW 1.1; Edge is queued. Agile Robots, Black Forest Labs, Runway, and Skild AI are the launch coalition.

Cosmos 3 is Nvidia's third-generation world foundation model for physical AI and the company's most aggressive open-source release of the year. The model is what Nvidia is calling an omnimodel with native action generation: it produces text, image, video, and ambient sound in the modalities expected of a multimodal generative model, and it also produces numerical action data, joint angles, gripper positions, and trajectory points that a robot can execute. The architecture is a mixture-of-transformers, with a reasoning transformer that interprets scenes paired with an expert generator that emits the physically grounded outputs.

The release is licensed under OpenMDW 1.1, the Linux Foundation's permissive license for model distribution that allows training, modification, redistribution, and deployment. Nvidia is shipping two variants at launch, Cosmos 3 Super for post-training of robotics and AV models that need the highest physics accuracy and generation quality, and Cosmos 3 Nano for high-quality video and action reasoning at fractions of a second per frame. A third variant, Cosmos 3 Edge, is queued for real-time inference deployments later in the year. Distribution is through build.nvidia.com, Hugging Face, GitHub, and the NVIDIA NIM microservice catalogue.

The Cosmos Coalition

Nvidia named a Cosmos Coalition of partner labs and companies that will train, fine-tune, and distribute models on top of the base. The founding members are Agile Robots, which is using Cosmos 3 for action-conditioned humanoid robot data; Black Forest Labs, the German image-generation lab behind FLUX; Runway, the video-generation startup; and Skild AI, the multi-robot generalist policy lab founded by CMU's Deepak Pathak. Linker Vision is named as the launch application partner using vision-language reasoning for smart-city analysis, and Nvidia's internal GEAR team is using Cosmos 3 to build video-action models for embodied agents.

The Coalition framing is meant to make Cosmos 3 the de facto open base for physical-AI work the way Llama became the de facto open base for language-model work in 2024. The bet is that a shared backbone that already encodes physics, motion, and action representation will let downstream labs focus on the task-specific fine-tuning that has historically been the bottleneck for robotics. Open the base and let the partners diverge on the verticals is the strategy Nvidia learned from CUDA and is now applying to robot policies.

"Cosmos 3 understands object interactions, motion, and spatial-temporal relationships before it generates video and action trajectories. That is the difference between a video model and a robotics model."— Nvidia GEAR team summary, Cosmos 3 release post

How it gets used

The intended downstream workflow is to take a base Cosmos 3 checkpoint, post-train on a customer-owned dataset of teleoperation traces, simulation episodes, or re-projected third-person video, and deploy the resulting policy as either a robot controller or a synthetic-data generator for the next training round. Cosmos 3 ships with an open toolkit covering data generation, simulation, training, and validation, which makes it usable end-to-end without a custom stack. The Isaac GR00T reference humanoid Nvidia announced on the same day uses Cosmos 3 as its base.

Cosmos 3 also ships with a re-projection capability that takes ordinary third-person video and converts it into the first-person robot perspective the policy needs to learn from. That is the data-augmentation trick the GR00T-Dreams blueprint introduced in 2025; Cosmos 3 makes it a first-class API. The practical effect is that the volume of robot-relevant training data available to researchers expands by roughly the volume of YouTube and stock video that the licensing terms allow.

The Isaac GR00T reference humanoid is Nvidia's effort to do for humanoid robotics what the Jetson developer-kit ecosystem did for embedded AI: provide an open, well-supported, complete-stack design that academic and startup labs can buy, run, and build on without reinventing the chassis. The reference design combines a nearly six-foot Unitree H2 Plus humanoid chassis, 150 pounds with 31 degrees of freedom, with dual Sharpa Wave five-finger tactile hands that contribute another 22 degrees of freedom. Onboard compute is the Jetson AGX Thor T5000, with a Blackwell GPU and 128 GB of unified memory, drawing 40 to 130 watts and powered by a 15 Ah battery that gives the robot roughly three hours of operational endurance.

Sensing is a head-mounted stereo camera, wrist cameras, and inertial measurement; the arms can apply up to 120 newton-meters of torque, which is sufficient for the kind of object manipulation that demonstration policies want. The base Unitree H2 Plus is priced at $29,900, which keeps the system inside the budget of a U.S. graduate research group and an order of magnitude below the industrial-grade humanoids Boston Dynamics and Figure ship.

Why open the design

Nvidia's framing is that humanoid robotics is bottlenecked on data, not on hardware. A reference design that researchers across the field share lets them combine training data, share policy checkpoints, and reproduce results, all of which speeds the cumulative learning curve for the whole field. "Robotics moves fastest when researchers can build on open platforms, share code, and test ideas on actual hardware," the Stanford Robotics Center's executive director said in the launch material. Ai2, ETH Zurich, Stanford Robotics Center, and UC San Diego are the announced launch academic partners.

The robot ships with the full Nvidia Isaac GR00T software stack: data capture, synthetic data generation, simulation in Isaac Lab and Isaac Sim, training, model validation, and on-robot deployment. The base policy is Cosmos 3, the open omnimodel Nvidia released on the same day. A research group can buy the H2 Plus, pair it with the Sharpa hands and the Thor T5000, post-train a Cosmos 3 checkpoint on their own dataset, and run the result on the robot inside a single workflow.

"Robotics moves fastest when researchers can build on open platforms, share code, and test ideas on actual hardware."— Stanford Robotics Center executive director, GR00T launch material

The supplier picture

The GR00T design picks Unitree and Sharpa as the supplier base for the open reference, which is the first time Nvidia has anchored its humanoid hardware reference on Chinese-origin suppliers rather than on U.S. or European primes. The pragmatic case is cost and shipping availability; the political case is that the reference is open and any compatible chassis can be substituted. Nvidia is not building robots itself and the Cosmos 3 stack runs on any compatible Jetson Thor system, including ones built around the lower-cost Unitree G1 humanoid. The choice is meant to signal that the open reference is a starting point, not a vendor lock-in.

Commerce confirms in writing that AI-chip export licenses follow the corporate parent, not the shipping address

Al Jazeera / Reuters · 2026-06-01 · Source ↗ ·

The Department of Commerce formally confirmed Monday that advanced-AI-chip license requirements apply to any business with a parent or headquarters in China, regardless of the subsidiary's location. Nvidia said the guidance "reaffirms" its existing vetting; AMD and Intel declined to comment. Blackwell remains banned; H200 stays the most advanced chip allowed for export.

The Bureau of Industry and Security followed its weekend guidance with a formal Monday confirmation that export-license requirements for advanced AI accelerators apply to any business with a headquarters or parent in China, regardless of the address on the shipping label. Reuters and Al Jazeera both published the confirmation early Monday, with the BIS spokesman cited directly. The clarification closes the year-old gap created when the Trump administration shelved enforcement of the Biden-era AI Diffusion Rule in May 2025 and is the operational counterpart to Sunday's weekend guidance.

Nvidia confirmed in a statement that the guidance "reaffirms that NVIDIA's sales and vetting process is correct" and that licenses are required for shipments to PRC-headquartered companies. AMD and Intel did not respond to requests for comment; TSMC declined to comment. The covered hardware list spans Blackwell, banned for export to China since the family launched, alongside Rubin and AMD MI350x. The H200, approximately six times more powerful than the H20, remains the most advanced accelerator allowed for export to Chinese-affiliated buyers under the December 2025 license-by-license regime.

The loophole that the confirmation closes

The clarification's substantive effect is on the corporate structure Chinese AI labs have used to procure advanced accelerators outside the formal license regime. Chris McGuire, a former State Department technology and national-security official, characterized the practice in plain language. "Chinese companies have been buying these chips, very likely at scale," he said. Industry estimates over the past year placed the diverted volume in the hundreds of thousands of restricted chips, routed through Malaysian, Singaporean, and Emirati subsidiaries whose end-use certifications were treated as independent of the Chinese parent.

The Monday confirmation requires the chipmakers and their distribution partners to verify the corporate parent of every advanced-accelerator order and to refuse shipments where the parent traces to a Chinese-headquartered firm. The mechanical effect is a downward revision to the rest-of-Asia revenue line for Nvidia and AMD, with the Singapore line, which had ballooned through 2025 as Chinese-affiliated buyers routed through it, the most affected.

"License requirements apply to advanced AI chips going to entities headquartered in China, regardless of location."— Bureau of Industry and Security, June 1, 2026

What is still open

The confirmation does not address chips already in country at Chinese-owned facilities outside the mainland. A formal end-use audit program is reportedly being drafted but has not been published. The diplomatic frame matters: the Monday confirmation lands days before the next round of U.S.-China trade talks, which gives the United States a fresh chip on the table without requiring congressional action. Chinese commerce officials called the guidance "discrimination" in a brief Sunday-night statement, the same line they used at the original AI Diffusion Rule publication.

GitHub Copilot flips to AI Credits billing today, ending unlimited request quotas across Pro, Business, and Enterprise

GitHub Blog · 2026-06-01 · Source ↗ ·

Every paid Copilot subscription now consumes GitHub AI Credits at $0.01 per credit, with monthly allotments matching the subscription price. Code completions and Next Edit suggestions stay unlimited and free. Pro+ gets $39 in credits, Business $19, Enterprise $39 per seat. Promotional flex credits cushion the transition through August.

GitHub Copilot moved every paid subscription to usage-based billing on June 1. The new system replaces the request-based quotas that defined Copilot since the 2023 GA launch with GitHub AI Credits, a per-token meter pegged at $0.01 per credit and billed at the standard API rates for whichever model the request uses. The Pro tier ships with $10 of monthly credits at the $10 subscription price; Pro+ ships with $39 at $39; Business is $19 per user at $19; Enterprise is $39 per seat at $39.

Code completions and Next Edit suggestions stay unlimited and outside the credit meter on every paid plan, which preserves the workflow that drove Copilot's original adoption. Everything else, agent chats, multi-file edits, refactors, branch operations, code review, and the full Claude Code-style agent surfaces, is metered against the credit pool at the model's API rate. Promotional flex credits add a temporary cushion through August: $30 to $70 of additional monthly credits for Business and Enterprise tiers, with the exact amount keyed to plan and seat count, are added on top of the base pool to give organizations a window to instrument usage before the bills land in full.

Why GitHub had to make the change

The 2023 Copilot pricing assumed an inference cost per request that no longer holds for the agentic-coding workloads Copilot now runs. Cursor, Claude Code, and OpenAI's Codex have all converged on agentic patterns that issue dozens or hundreds of model calls per task, each potentially against a frontier reasoning model that costs more per token than the original Copilot completion. Holding unlimited quotas against that workload pattern meant GitHub was subsidizing power-user behavior at margins that did not work; the move to per-token billing pushes the cost transparency onto the customer.

The new structure also gives GitHub the freedom to route requests to different models without rebalancing pricing. Whether the request runs against Anthropic's Claude Sonnet 4.6, OpenAI's GPT-5.2, or Google's Gemini 3.5 Pro, the credit charge is computed at the published API rate for that model on the input, output, and cached tokens. Heavy Claude Code users on the GitHub stack will see different bills than heavy Codex users; the credit transparency is the lever GitHub is giving organizations to manage that.

"Code completions and Next Edit suggestions remain included in all plans and do not consume AI Credits."— GitHub Copilot billing announcement

What changes for organizations

Enterprises gain pooled credit sharing across teams, with budget controls at the organization, cost-center, and individual-user level. Annual subscribers keep their existing pricing until the plan expires, which gives them a contractual buffer through the end of their current term. Monthly subscribers are migrated automatically. The flex credits run through the end of August, after which the pool reverts to the base allotment and the metered rates apply in full.
