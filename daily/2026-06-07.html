The four frontier-lab CEOs put their names on a single biosecurity ask to Congress

Dario Amodei, Sam Altman, Demis Hassabis and Mustafa Suleyman signed a joint open letter on June 5 urging Congress to make synthetic-DNA screening and order recordkeeping mandatory for every nucleic-acid synthesis vendor operating in the United States. The letter, organized by the Foundation for American Innovation and the Institute for Progress, argues that frontier AI has dropped the expertise barrier for designing biological weapons low enough that the gene-synthesis supply chain is now the binding constraint. Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind and Microsoft AI together account for the labs whose models the UK AISI flagged for expert-level biosecurity uplift in its spring 2026 Mythos and GPT-5.5-Cyber evaluations.

Fortune / Foundation for American Innovation · 2026-06-05 · Source ↗ ·

The joint open letter, titled "An Open Letter in Support of Mandatory Nucleic Acid Synthesis Screening and Recordkeeping," went up on the Foundation for American Innovation site on the evening of June 5 with the four signatures attached. Fortune, NPR, Interesting Engineering and CryptoBriefing all filed parallel write-ups within hours. The letter asks Congress to require every company that sells synthetic DNA and RNA in the United States to screen orders against pathogen and toxin sequence databases before fulfilling them, and to keep records of order specifications and customer identity so that federal biosecurity investigators can trace a misuse incident back through the supply chain.

The Foundation for American Innovation, a center-right tech-policy think tank, and the Institute for Progress, a nonpartisan science-policy group, organized the document. They circulated drafts privately for several weeks before the four labs signed. Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind and Microsoft AI represent the labs whose top models the UK AI Security Institute flagged earlier this year as having crossed expert-level biosecurity uplift thresholds during evaluation. Anthropic's Mythos preview scored 73 percent on the AISI's expert-level offensive-security evaluations in April; OpenAI's GPT-5.5-Cyber, which the company extended to EU government cybersecurity teams on June 5, is the parallel safety-vetted release on the OpenAI side.

What the letter actually asks

Two specific things. First, that any vendor synthesizing nucleic acids for commercial sale to US customers must run incoming orders against curated screening databases of pathogen and toxin sequences. The current US standard, the 2010 HHS Guidance, is voluntary. The four labs want it to be mandatory and enforceable by federal regulators. Second, that vendors must keep records of order specifications and customer identity for an unspecified retention period, so that if a biosecurity incident traces back to a specific synthetic-DNA order, federal investigators can identify the customer.

"AI is rapidly eroding the technical barriers that previously kept biological weapons design out of reach. Mandatory biosecurity screening at the gene-synthesis supply chain is the most tractable lever Congress can pull this year." — excerpt from the joint letter

Why these four CEOs at once

The biosecurity ask is the first policy point on which Amodei, Altman, Hassabis and Suleyman have publicly aligned without dissent. Amodei has been the loudest of the four on biosecurity for two years; his Senate testimony last August warned that frontier models could compress what was a multi-year, PhD-level skill stack down to days of guided work. The OpenAI signature is more politically loaded because the company has spent the past year arguing for voluntary review under the June 2 Trump executive order; signing onto a mandatory-screening ask on a different node of the supply chain lets the company support biosecurity controls without conceding ground on model licensing.

Hassabis brings the credibility of Google DeepMind's protein-design work through Isomorphic Labs, which now manages 17 active drug-development programs and is targeting clinical trials by year-end. Suleyman's signature lands four days after Microsoft Build, where MAI-Thinking-1 and MAI-Code-1-Flash signaled the company's move toward its own frontier-model stack. Microsoft AI signing on means the letter is not a closed-AI-company-versus-closed-AI-company artifact; it is a frontier-lab consensus.

What this means for the policy fight

The June 2 Trump executive order on AI explicitly prohibited federal preemption of state AI laws on biosecurity and child-safety topics. The four signatories are therefore asking Congress to legislate a federal floor on biosecurity that the executive branch's policy direction effectively leaves room for. The Foundation for American Innovation's involvement matters: FAI has close working relationships with House Republicans on the AI working group, including Jay Obernolte, whose June 4 Great American AI Act draft included a three-year preemption of state AI laws but exempted biosecurity. The letter is functionally a request that the Great American AI Act package screening mandates and recordkeeping into the same vehicle.

The pushback is concrete. Biotech industry groups have historically argued that mandatory screening would slow gene-synthesis turnaround times from days to weeks, hurting academic research. Privacy advocates have raised concerns about customer-record retention at a stage in the supply chain where some orders are for legitimate dual-use research. The Foundation for American Innovation said the letter explicitly leaves the customer-record retention period to Congress. The four CEOs did not commit to a specific implementation timeline; the letter's framing is "this calendar year." Markup of the Great American AI Act draft begins June 17 in the House AI Task Force.

Microsoft launches MXC, an OS-level execution sandbox for AI agents, with OpenAI and Nvidia as launch partners

VentureBeat / Windows Developer Blog · 2026-06-06 · Source ↗ ·

Microsoft Execution Containers (MXC) is a policy-driven execution layer built into the Windows kernel that lets developers and administrators declare what an AI agent can and cannot access at runtime. Launch partners include OpenAI, Nvidia's OpenShell on Windows, Manus, Nous Research's Hermes agent and the OpenClaw open-source project. The SDK ships first in Windows 11 24H2 Enterprise and Pro, with Windows Server 2027 following later in 2026.

Microsoft launched MXC alongside Build 2026 last week and the technical write-ups landed across the weekend: VentureBeat on June 6, the Windows Developer Blog at Build on June 2, and follow-on coverage from Cloud Native Now and ByteIota. MXC is the answer to a problem Microsoft has been articulating publicly for a year: agents that write code, navigate software interfaces, manage files and orchestrate workflows need a Windows-native security boundary that the OS kernel enforces, rather than a series of application-level sandboxes that each agent framework has to invent on its own.

The technical claim is specific. MXC is a cross-platform, policy-driven execution layer that runs on Windows and the Windows Subsystem for Linux. Developers declare what to constrain, and Windows enforces those constraints at runtime through MXC. File system access, network egress, application launching and resource consumption are policy-bound. The MXC SDK exposes the policy primitives to agent runtimes and IDEs; the actual enforcement happens at the kernel layer so an agent's policies cannot be bypassed by the agent process itself.

The launch partners and why they matter

OpenAI is the headline partner. David Wiesen on OpenAI's technical staff said in the Microsoft announcement that "working with Microsoft on the Microsoft Execution Containers (MXC) allows us to explore new patterns for AI agents to safely and efficiently generate and execute code." For OpenAI this is the safe-execution substrate underneath the Codex Computer Use agent that the company put into general availability for Windows on June 3. Nvidia is shipping a project called OpenShell on Windows that uses MXC for shell-style agent execution. Manus, the China-based agent company that Meta tried to acquire for $2 billion before regulators blocked the deal, signed on. Nous Research is using MXC for Hermes; the OpenClaw open-source project rounds out the announced set.

"Working with Microsoft on the Microsoft Execution Containers (MXC) allows us to explore new patterns for AI agents to safely and efficiently generate and execute code." — David Wiesen, OpenAI technical staff

The strategic shape of the move

Three things are happening at once. First, Microsoft is moving its runtime-security story from per-application sandboxes (App Container, AppContainer-V) up to a kernel-level execution-policy plane purpose-built for AI agents. Second, the launch-partner list is a deliberate consolidation of the agent ecosystem onto a Microsoft-controlled execution substrate, the same shape Microsoft used a decade ago when it pulled the Linux developer community onto Windows via WSL. Third, the fact that OpenAI signed on as a partner the same week that Microsoft announced seven proprietary MAI models indicates that the agent execution layer is being treated as separable from the model layer. Microsoft and OpenAI can compete on models and cooperate on the runtime.

MXC ships first in Windows 11 version 24H2 Enterprise and Pro editions, with Windows Server 2027 following later in 2026. A preview is available immediately for Windows Insiders in the Dev Channel. The SDK is open and cross-platform between Windows and WSL; Microsoft has not committed to Linux-host MXC as a separately maintained project. For enterprise IT, the practical effect is that agents shipped by ISVs can be deployed against MXC policies declared centrally by the IT admin team, the way mobile device management policies are declared centrally today for the iOS and Android device fleets.

Perplexity demos a hybrid local-cloud inference orchestrator with Intel onstage at Computex

MarkTechPost / VentureBeat · 2026-06-05 · Source ↗ ·

Perplexity CEO Aravind Srinivas demonstrated the first hybrid local-server inference orchestrator at Intel's Computex keynote, software that autonomously decides which AI workloads stay on the user's device and which route to frontier models in the cloud. The demo used Perplexity's Personal Computer agent to process confidential deal materials. The feature ships in Perplexity Computer in July 2026.

Perplexity announced the hybrid orchestrator during Intel CEO Lip-Bu Tan's keynote at Computex 2026 in Taipei. Srinivas was on stage with Tan to demo the system; the MarkTechPost write-up on June 5 and VentureBeat's parallel coverage explain the technical claim. The orchestrator is software that sits inside the Perplexity Computer agent and decides at request time whether to run an individual model call on the local GPU and NPU on the user's machine or to route it to a frontier model in the Perplexity cloud. The routing is driven by a combination of cost, latency, capability and confidentiality flags on the request.

The demo case Srinivas chose was deliberate: a confidential deal-materials review. The orchestrator kept entity extraction, document-classification and summarization on the local machine using a Cohere TinyAya-class small model running on the Intel laptop, and pushed only the synthesis step to a frontier model in the cloud once the sensitive content had been redacted locally. The user-visible behavior is a single agent loop; the routing is invisible. The economic argument is that the cost per agent task drops because most of the tokens never leave the device, and the confidentiality argument is that the parts of the document that contain identifying information never reach a third-party cloud model at all.

Why this lands now

The hybrid story has been telegraphed for a year. Nvidia's RTX Spark superchip, announced June 1 at Computex with 6,188 Blackwell GPU cores and 128 GB LPDDR5X, is the laptop-side hardware. Microsoft's MXC sandbox, announced June 2-6, is the OS-level execution boundary. The Holo3.1 family that H Company shipped June 2 is the open computer-use agent that runs on consumer GPUs as small as 12 GB. Perplexity's orchestrator is the request-routing layer that ties this stack into a single product surface for the end user. Apple's user-pick-default-model in iOS 27 is the same idea from the phone end, and Microsoft's Aion on-device tier in Windows is the analogous Windows pattern.

"You don't ship hybrid because it's elegant. You ship hybrid because most of the tokens in a real agent loop are entity extraction and tool calls, not synthesis. Run those locally and frontier-model cost drops by an order of magnitude." — Aravind Srinivas, Perplexity CEO, at Computex

Perplexity also pushed a heavier update to the broader Computer product over the weekend: GPT-5.5 added as the default orchestration model, GPT Image 2 as the image generator, plan approvals, inline diffs, website publishing, Teams access, native Snowflake and Databricks data workflows, reusable Workflows and a Space-skills feature for shared agent capabilities across workspaces. The Personal Computer agent with the hybrid orchestrator ships to Pro and Enterprise users in July, with general availability later in Q3.

TSMC's C.C. Wei warns AI chip supply will fall short of demand for years

Bloomberg · 2026-06-04 · Source ↗ ·

At TSMC's annual general meeting in Hsinchu on June 4, CEO C.C. Wei told shareholders that the company's global chip supply will fall short of AI-fueled demand for years, even as Arizona and Kumamoto capacity comes online. Wei said TSMC will not match memory-style abrupt price hikes but cannot fulfill US customer demand at the rate AI infrastructure orders are arriving.

The AGM commentary is unusual because TSMC executives generally avoid demand-side projections at shareholder meetings. Wei's statement that demand will outrun supply "for years" puts a duration on what had been a quarter-to-quarter conversation. The Bloomberg write-up June 4 noted that Wei made the comments in the context of US customer orders specifically: even with N2 and N1.6 capacity ramping in Arizona, the rate of incoming orders from Nvidia, AMD, Broadcom, Marvell and the hyperscalers' custom-silicon teams is outpacing TSMC's ability to add wafer starts at the leading nodes.

Two adjacent commitments Wei made are price discipline and customer prioritization. TSMC will not match the memory-chip industry's pattern of abrupt large price increases, Wei said, because the company believes price stability is the foundation of the long-term hyperscaler-design partnerships that have built TSMC's AI-customer base. The customer prioritization point was less concrete: Wei said TSMC continues to allocate capacity by long-term partnership depth rather than spot-market bidding. That allocation rule favors the named early customers of the next-generation processes (Nvidia, AMD, Apple) over the newer entrants competing for the same wafer pool.

The Korean angle

SK Group Chairman Chey Tae-won spent Wednesday and Thursday in Taiwan meeting Foxconn and TSMC executives on next-generation high-bandwidth memory and advanced packaging. Chey met with TSMC Chairman Mark Liu on June 3, the pair's first face-to-face meeting in two years. The joint readout said both sides agreed to deepen cooperation on next-generation HBM and advanced packaging technologies. SK Hynix is one of the two suppliers (with Micron) of HBM4 to TSMC's CoWoS packaging line. The deeper cooperation likely means earlier HBM4 process node sharing and joint design work on HBM5 specifications.

"We see strong AI-related demand. Our capacity will be tight for years." — C.C. Wei, TSMC CEO, AGM June 4

The downstream effect for the AI infrastructure cycle is unambiguous. Hyperscaler capital expenditure for 2026 is already projected at well over $300 billion across the four largest US buyers; if TSMC's leading-edge capacity is the binding constraint, the cycle's bottleneck is not money or power but wafer starts on N2 and N1.6. The Vera Rubin platform Nvidia put into full production at Computex is the leading consumer of that capacity. Wei's statement is functionally a customer-allocation note dressed up as macroeconomic guidance.

Nvidia's Vera Rubin in full production; Rubin then Rosa Feynman generations laid out on the Computex roadmap

Tom's Hardware / ServeTheHome · 2026-06-05 · Source ↗ ·

At Computex 2026 Nvidia confirmed Vera Rubin in full production for fall 2026 shipment with five-rack AI-factory configurations targeting agentic workloads. The Vera CPU is an 88-core Olympus-based design; the Rubin GPU drives NVLink 6, ConnectX-9 SuperNIC, BlueField-4 DPU and Spectrum-6 networking. The laptop roadmap names Rubin's successor as "Rosa Feynman" running on LPDDR6.

ServeTheHome's Computex coverage and Tom's Hardware's deep-read on the laptop roadmap consolidated what Nvidia disclosed at the Taipei keynote. Vera Rubin is the next data-center platform, now in full production with first systems shipping in fall 2026. The Vera CPU is an 88-core Arm design built on the in-house Olympus core, replacing the Grace CPU at the head of the rack. The Rubin GPU is the Blackwell-Ultra successor that drives the platform's compute density. Vera Rubin configurations consist of five purpose-built racks operating as one AI supercomputer, optimized for agentic workloads where memory bandwidth and inter-rack latency dominate end-to-end throughput.

The systems-partner list is the standard major-OEM set: Dell, HPE, Lenovo, Supermicro, ASUS, ASRock Rack, Foxconn, GIGABYTE, IBM, Pegatron, Compal and a long tail of secondary integrators. Pegatron's June 4 announcement of its Vera Rubin AI Factory validation kit at Computex was the first signaling that the broader ODM ecosystem is ready to ship rack-scale systems at customer turn-up. NVLink 6 is the inter-rack fabric generation that ships with Rubin; ConnectX-9 SuperNIC and BlueField-4 DPU are the host-side networking parts; Spectrum-6 is the Ethernet AI fabric.

Beyond Rubin: Rosa Feynman on the laptop roadmap

Tom's Hardware's June 5 read on the laptop roadmap got the most attention because Nvidia named the post-Rubin generation explicitly. The DGX Spark / RTX Spark roadmap covers three generations of laptop AI silicon: the current Spark (a MediaTek-collaborated Arm-plus-Blackwell design with up to 20 Grace CPU cores and 6,188 Blackwell GPU cores), followed by a Rubin-based laptop generation using LPDDR6 memory, and then "Rosa Feynman," the successor platform that follows Rubin. The naming pattern (astronomer, physicist, astronomer, physicist) tracks Nvidia's standard pattern, and Rosa Feynman is the first time the post-Rubin name has appeared publicly.

"Vera Rubin was built for this moment, an AI factory engine that delivers intelligence at scale, with the performance, efficiency and security needed to power the next industrial revolution." — Jensen Huang, Nvidia CEO, Computex 2026 keynote

The named early data-center customers of the Vera CPU are OpenAI, Anthropic and SpaceX, the three buyers Jensen Huang called out from the Computex stage. Each is buying capacity in the 1-gigawatt-plus tier, and the Vera CPU is the head-of-rack part underneath every Rubin GPU in their deployments. The Computex disclosure puts Rubin shipments and customer turn-up on the same calendar timeline TSMC's wafer-allocation discipline allows: high-priority partners first, broader market access as N2 and N1.6 yields ramp.

AMD MI455X flagship: 40 PFLOPS FP4 and 432 GB HBM4, with Helios rack shipping Q3 2026

AMD's Q1 2026 earnings on May 5 already confirmed the Data Center segment at a $5.8 billion record up 57 percent year-over-year, but the specifics on the MI455X flagship and Helios rack reached the press across the weekend in long-form analysis pieces and SEC filings. The MI455X is the flagship of the MI400 series, AMD's response to Nvidia's Blackwell Ultra and Rubin. Peak compute is 40 PFLOPS at FP4 precision. Memory is 432 GB of HBM4 stacked in 12-Hi configurations on the substrate, the deepest HBM4 die stack any vendor has shipped. The TDP is in the 1,200-watt range, paired with liquid cooling at the chassis level and a redesigned voltage regulator module set on the OAM module.

The Helios rack is AMD's parallel to Nvidia's NVL72-class racks: a full rack of MI455X OAMs interconnected with Infinity Fabric Gen 5 and CXL 3.1 to a common pool of CPUs and HBM-attached host memory. AMD has guided first-year MI400-series revenue at $7.2 billion, a number that anchors the company's near-term growth model. The hyperscaler commitments

"The MI455X is the first AMD GPU where we are not winning on price alone. We are winning on memory bandwidth and HBM4 density." — Lisa Su, AMD CEO, May 5 earnings call

The downstream effect of the AMD ramp is that hyperscaler buyers no longer have to choose Nvidia for HBM-heavy frontier-model inference and training. AMD's MI455X is the first part where the memory-bandwidth-per-dollar ratio favors AMD over Nvidia at the flagship tier, which matters for the 1-million-context-window inference loads that Anthropic, OpenAI and Google have all moved to. The Q3 ship date puts MI455X in deployment ahead of Nvidia Rubin, giving AMD a window to capture the upgrade cycle from MI300X-era buyers.
