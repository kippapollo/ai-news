SoftBank books up to €75 billion to build 5 gigawatts of AI data centers across northern France

Masayoshi Son commits an initial €45 billion to deliver 3.1 gigawatts of capacity in Dunkirk, Bosquel and Bouchain by 2031, with sites built and operated alongside Schneider Electric, EDF and SB Energy. The package, unveiled at Emmanuel Macron's Choose France summit, is the largest European AI infrastructure investment any single firm has ever announced. It lands days after SoftBank disclosed a 9.2-gigawatt natural-gas-backed campus in Ohio.

SoftBank Group · 2026-05-30 · Source ↗ ·

SoftBank Group disclosed plans to invest up to €75 billion (about $87 billion at current rates) to build 5 gigawatts of AI data center capacity in France, with an initial €45 billion committed to deliver 3.1 gigawatts of capacity in the Hauts-de-France region by 2031. The first three sites sit in Dunkirk (the Loon-Plage industrial zone at the port), Bosquel near Amiens, and Bouchain on the site of an existing EDF gas turbine plant. Masayoshi Son announced the commitment at the 2026 edition of Emmanuel Macron's Choose France summit at the Château de Versailles, where French ministers court foreign capital one weekend a year.

The deal vaults France into the second tier of national AI infrastructure plays behind the United States. It is the largest single AI infrastructure commitment any company has ever directed to a European country, and SoftBank itself describes the package as its largest AI infrastructure investment outside the U.S. SoftBank Energy will originate power supply, EDF will provide grid interconnection and low-carbon generation, and Schneider Electric will partner on the build-out and operate a power-module integration facility at the Port of Dunkirk that will sit next to a SoftBank-run enclosure manufacturing line. French Economic Minister Roland Lescure framed the announcement as evidence that "President Macron's ambition to position France as a leading destination all along the AI value chain" is taking hold.

What gets built, where, and when

The plan covers three buckets. Phase one is the 3.1-gigawatt initial buildout in the three named sites; SoftBank says construction begins inside 18 months at the Dunkirk port-adjacent campus and at Bouchain, with Bosquel following. Phase two takes the program to 5 gigawatts through additional sites yet to be named. The third bucket is the industrial cluster at the Port of Dunkirk, where SoftBank and Schneider Electric will assemble enclosures, power distribution modules and cooling integration for the program and, the companies say, eventually for export to other European data center developers. SoftBank's framing leans on the jobs counts a Choose France announcement is expected to deliver: thousands of high-skill construction, operations and manufacturing roles, alongside regional university partnerships.

The 3.1-gigawatt number invites a direct comparison with the U.S. Stargate program, where SoftBank, OpenAI and Oracle are the lead participants and individual campuses are sized at multiples of one gigawatt. SoftBank, an investor in and customer of OpenAI, did not name an anchor tenant for the French sites. French and EU officials have repeatedly named Mistral as a national champion in need of compute, and the choice of Hauts-de-France keeps the buildout inside the European Union for sovereignty and AI Act compliance purposes. The Dunkirk port allocation also keeps the option of grid-edge generation alive: the existing French nuclear baseload routes through Bouchain, and the Loon-Plage zone has direct access to the offshore wind buildout in the North Sea.

"AI is entering a new era, and the countries that build the infrastructure for this transformation will shape the future."— Masayoshi Son, Chairman and CEO, SoftBank Group

The package against SoftBank's broader compute ledger

The France commitment lands two weeks after SoftBank confirmed a 9.2-gigawatt natural-gas-backed campus in Ohio, and it sits alongside the Stargate commitments of the past 18 months. Stack the new French capacity against the SoftBank-allocated portions of Stargate and the company's effective pipeline is now north of 20 gigawatts, an order of magnitude above any AI infrastructure portfolio that existed three years ago. The capital comes from SoftBank Group's balance sheet, with project-level co-investors expected for the French sites and the Hauts-de-France region likely to provide tax abatements and accelerated permitting in exchange for the jobs.

The contrast with U.S. data center politics is the subtext of the day. TechCrunch noted that SoftBank's separate Ohio commitment had drawn opposition over the 9.2-gigawatt gas plant required to power it, and several Texas and Virginia campuses faced ratepayer pushback this spring. France, with its EDF-led nuclear baseload and an industrial policy that treats heavy electricity loads as a feature rather than a problem, offers a backstop the American grid does not. The French sites are designed to be powered almost entirely without new fossil generation, an explicit pitch SoftBank used in the announcement and one the company has not been able to make about its U.S. campuses.

"This investment demonstrates President Emmanuel Macron's ambition to position France as a leading destination all along the AI value chain."— Roland Lescure, French Minister of Economy

Why the French location matters for the AI-policy fight

The buildout is the largest AI infrastructure investment ever directed to the European Union. It immediately changes the political math around the AI Act and the Commission's debates over compute thresholds, sovereign AI, and competitive cloud markets. Stargate sits in the United States. The major Chinese campuses sit in Inner Mongolia and Guizhou. Until today, the EU had no comparable single-country anchor; today, France does. Mistral, the Commission, and France's broader Choose France agenda will all carry the SoftBank commitment forward as evidence that the bloc has secured a meaningful share of the global AI infrastructure capex without using sovereign capital to do so. The investment will also raise the bar for Germany, the U.K. and the Nordics, all of which have separately courted similar capital with smaller offers.

Risks remain. The 2031 delivery target sits inside two French presidential cycles, and Hauts-de-France's grid build-out depends on EDF capex that has slipped repeatedly. SoftBank's own cash flow profile is leveraged against the same Vision Fund investments that have produced uneven returns; the company will have to find project finance partners for at least part of the €75 billion. And the absence of a named anchor tenant means the build risk falls on SoftBank if Mistral or another European champion fails to absorb the capacity at the planned rates. None of that disqualifies the announcement; all of it sets the bar for what the next 24 months of execution have to look like.

"Tokenmaxxing" collapses as Uber, Amazon and Microsoft ration AI usage

Wall Street Journal / Fortune / FT · 2026-05-30 · Source ↗ ·

Uber burned its full 2026 token budget in four months; Amazon shut down the Kiro Rank internal leaderboard; Microsoft canceled Claude Code seats in product divisions; Salesforce expects a $300 million Anthropic bill this year.

The WSJ piece this weekend is the clearest cross-company picture yet of the productivity-versus-cost reckoning AI customers are running. Uber chief operating officer Andrew Macdonald told a recent podcast the company exhausted its entire 2026 token budget in the first four months of the year, with Claude Code the largest line item. Inside Amazon, employees were observed spinning up agents to perform unnecessary work just to keep their numbers on Kiro Rank, the internal leaderboard that tracked individual AI tool use; Amazon scrapped the leaderboard. Microsoft canceled Claude Code subscriptions in several product divisions after similar overspend, per The Verge's reporting cited in the piece, while Meta took down an informal token leaderboard staff had built.

The headline number on the other side is Salesforce. CEO Marc Benioff has said publicly that Salesforce's Anthropic bill will run about $300 million in 2026. Across Fortune's coverage, executives describe the same pattern: aggressive token consumption was rewarded internally as a proxy for engagement, the reward structure produced Goodhart's Law in textbook form, and the bills came in at multiples of the original plan with no clear company-wide productivity number to point at.

What changes from here

Three things follow from the reset. First, the inference-revenue base case for Anthropic and OpenAI has to be re-underwritten: if the largest customers ration usage and kill the leaderboards, the per-seat token velocity that anchored 2026 forecasts compresses. Second, the internal-tools market for "AI cost management" suddenly has runway, and the early movers (LangSmith pricing analytics, Helicone, OpenRouter's gateway billing) will get pulled into procurement faster than the budget cycle would have implied. Third, the agentic-coding category will get reframed: the question is no longer "how many tokens per dev per day" but "how many merged PRs per token spent," which favors models with better tool-use efficiency and worse-looking raw consumption.

"The reward structure created exactly the behaviour the metric was supposed to detect. Big Tech is now scrapping leaderboards that lack substance."— Fortune, on the unwinding of internal tokenmaxxing programs

The reset does not change the model providers' top line for 2026 — pre-paid contracts and committed-use agreements bake in the revenue — but it changes the slope going into the next renewal cycle. Anthropic and OpenAI have both been pricing on the assumption that enterprise per-seat consumption keeps doubling every quarter; the WSJ and FT reporting this weekend makes that assumption harder to defend in the second half.

Memory chip makers leverage AI demand into multi-year locked contracts

The Wall Street Journal · 2026-05-30 · Source ↗ ·

SK hynix, Samsung and Micron crossed $1 trillion each last week. The WSJ reports there is no spot market left for HBM in 2026 and that 2027–2028 allocations are now signed in firm contracts to a handful of AI buyers.

The WSJ's weekend piece picks up where the trillion-dollar market-cap milestones left off. As of last week, Samsung, SK hynix and Micron each carry market values above $1 trillion, a combined $3+ trillion oligopoly that did not exist 18 months ago. The structural story behind the prints is that the high-bandwidth memory the largest AI buyers need is no longer available on the spot market — every wafer that can be allocated to HBM has been forward-sold under multi-year contracts. SK hynix retains the largest share of HBM revenue at roughly 57%, with Samsung at 22% and Micron at 21% as of Q3 2025; all three have shifted material DRAM wafer capacity into HBM production.

The contracts side has the most operational read-through. The October 2025 letter of intent under which Samsung Electronics and SK hynix agreed to supply OpenAI with up to 900,000 DRAM wafers per month for Stargate is now public; Nvidia has the majority of SK hynix's HBM output through 2026 under separate multi-year agreements; and the second-tier buyers — Anthropic via its hyperscaler partners, the Chinese labs, the European sovereign clouds — are now competing for a residual allocation that is smaller than the cumulative demand the same buyers projected six months ago.

The downstream effects

Three follow on. First, the cost of training compute has stopped declining in the way it had through 2024–2025: cluster-level training cost-per-token is now flat or rising because HBM is the binding constraint. Second, consumer DRAM and DDR5 supply is being squeezed; smartphone bills of materials are up 14% year-over-year because the wafer mix shifted to HBM. Third, the strategic-investor allocations on the Anthropic Series H — Samsung, SK hynix and Micron each took meaningful pieces of the round — read very differently in light of the contract picture. Memory supply is now a fundraising primitive, not just a procurement line.

The WSJ's framing — that memory chips are now "more valuable than oil" — overstates the comparison on absolute dollar value, but it captures the structural fact that HBM allocation is the binding constraint for AI growth through at least 2027. The labs that lock supply now ship the next two model generations; the labs that do not are betting on capacity that does not yet exist.

VC robotics and physical-AI funding hit $26 billion in 2025, up from $4.2 billion in 2019

The Wall Street Journal · 2026-05-30 · Source ↗ ·

Kate Clark catalogs the venture turn into hardware: $26 billion into robotics and physical AI in 2025, more than double 2024, as VCs hedge against the model providers' direct path into application software.

The Wall Street Journal's Kate Clark put a number on the shift the entire venture industry has been quietly executing. Global VC investment in robotics and physical AI reached roughly $26 billion in 2025, up from $4.2 billion in 2019 and more than double 2024's $13.7 billion, with 1,000-plus disclosed deals. The headline number puts hardware on a faster trajectory than the AI applications category that funded the 2023–2024 cycle, and the reason VCs gave Clark on background is consistent: the model providers' aggressive entry into application software (Anthropic into agentic coding, OpenAI into enterprise deployment) has made horizontal software bets riskier, and hardware is harder to disrupt with a model release.

The deal mix Clark describes is dominated by humanoid robotics (Figure, 1X, Apptronik), warehouse autonomy (Symbotic, Locus, Covariant), foundation-model robotics labs (Physical Intelligence, Skild, Pi Labs), and defense-adjacent integration plays (Anduril's robotics extensions, Shield AI, Picogrid). The defense-tech share specifically has tripled year-over-year; Picogrid's $45 million Series A this week, led by Bessemer, is the most recent illustration. Drone-related deals contributed a 139% surge in defense-robotics funding tracked separately by other industry data.

The hedge thesis

Clark's reporting frames the shift as a hedge against software-AI consolidation. If the largest model providers can ship vertical applications on top of their own model platforms — Anthropic's Code, OpenAI's enterprise apps, Google's Spark agent — then the addressable market for independent application software shrinks. Hardware investments require physical supply chains, manufacturing partnerships, and on-the-ground deployment teams that the model labs have so far chosen not to build. The bet is that physical AI is the right place to put dollars during a period when software AI consolidates around three or four vendors.

The risk is that physical AI also consolidates around the model providers eventually. NVIDIA's robotics platform partnerships with BlackBerry QNX, Toyota's Woven manufacturing program, and Google's Spot integration with DeepMind's Gemini Robotics 1.6 all point to the same end state: the model lab provides the brain, the platform vendor provides the runtime, and the startup ends up in a similar squeeze to the application-software wave. For now, the dollars are flowing in regardless, and the headline number is what investors will quote for the next two quarters.

Project Jupiter switches to fuel cells, lands a 2.8-GW OpenAI campus in New Mexico

CleanTechnica · 2026-05-30 · Source ↗ ·

BorderPlex Digital Assets revises the Santa Teresa site to fuel cells, cuts water use 50× and NOx 92% versus a gas-fired alternative, but the campus would still emit 10.1 million tons of greenhouse gases a year.

BorderPlex Digital Assets disclosed the revised power architecture for Project Jupiter, the 2.8-gigawatt data center campus it is building in Santa Teresa, New Mexico, on behalf of an Oracle-OpenAI tenant. The site, near the El Paso, Texas border, was originally planned with natural-gas-fired generation. The April 2026 revision replaces it with on-site fuel cells, a configuration that cuts nitrogen-oxide emissions by approximately 92% and water consumption to 20,000 gallons per day, down from roughly 1 million gallons per day under the original gas plan.

The fuel-cell version is the largest behind-the-meter fuel-cell data center on record by a wide margin. Construction started in fall 2025 on the gas plan; the fuel-cell substitution adds capital cost but reduces local environmental opposition and the regulatory exposure of the original air-permit application. The campus will host OpenAI inference and training capacity under the Oracle relationship that anchors the southern portion of the Stargate program.

What the fuel cells don't change

The Center for Biological Diversity flagged the underlying emissions number, which the fuel cells reduce but do not eliminate. Project Jupiter at 2.8 GW would emit approximately 10.1 million tons of greenhouse gases per year — "more than the greenhouse gas emissions from Albuquerque, Las Cruces, and Santa Fe combined," per a Center attorney quoted in the article. The fuel cells improve the NOx and water profile substantially; they do not turn the campus carbon-neutral, and the CleanTechnica piece is explicit that the underlying load is the constraint.

The Project Jupiter announcement lands the same week as SoftBank's 9.2-GW Ohio campus, the Texas grid-interconnection queue's latest revision, and Anthropic's xAI Colossus contract. In aggregate, U.S. AI campus capacity under construction or in late-stage permitting now exceeds 60 gigawatts, and the New Mexico site is a representative case for how the politics of siting are evolving: fuel cells, batteries, and small modular reactor pre-orders are becoming the consolation prizes that allow new gas peakers to be dropped from the project envelope.

Simon Willison surfaces Anthropic's containment architecture, including a 24-of-25 phishing failure mode

A Saturday post pulls Anthropic's recently published engineering documentation into wider view: gVisor for claude.ai, Seatbelt and Bubblewrap for Claude Code, a full VM for Cowork, and an internal phishing exercise that succeeded 24 times out of 25.

Simon Willison's weekend post highlighted Anthropic's "How we contain Claude across products" engineering documentation, putting the more technical disclosures in front of a wider audience. Anthropic published the document earlier this week with credits to Max McGuinness, Mikaela Grace, Jiri De Jonghe, Jake Eaton and Abel Ribbink. The post lays out the containment architecture across the company's three agentic products: claude.ai runs code server-side inside gVisor containers on ephemeral filesystems; Claude Code runs on user machines with Seatbelt on macOS and Bubblewrap on Linux providing the OS-level sandbox; Claude Cowork runs a full virtual machine using Apple's Virtualization framework on macOS and Hyper-V Container Services on Windows.

The two findings Willison emphasizes are the ones that will get cited in product review. First, an internal phishing exercise against Claude Code's user-as-injection-vector surface — where an attacker crafts a prompt that asks the user to ask Claude to do something malicious — succeeded 24 of 25 times. The model layer cannot reliably distinguish legitimate user intent from social engineering at the prompt level; only environmental controls (filesystem boundaries, egress restrictions) prevent damage. Second, Claude Code users were observed to approve roughly 93% of permission prompts pre-sandbox rollout, the textbook approval-fatigue failure mode; adding the OS sandbox cut the prompt rate by 84% and, by the company's measurement, materially improved the actual safety of execution.

What the post says about persistent-agent threats

The Anthropic team also documented a Cowork-specific vulnerability around domain-allowlist exfiltration: a malicious workspace file used attacker-controlled credentials to upload data through api.anthropic.com, a destination the allowlist permitted by domain. The fix was a man-in-the-middle proxy inside the VM that only accepts VM-provisioned session tokens. That is the kind of disclosure most labs do not publish, and Willison's frustration with the broader industry — that sandboxing products "are rarely thoroughly documented" — runs through the post.

"A complaint I often have about sandboxing products is that they are rarely thoroughly documented, and in the absence of detailed documentation it's hard to know how much I can trust them."— Simon Willison

The disclosure is timely given the rollout of Google Spark this week and the broader push into persistent always-on agents. The threat surface Anthropic describes — persistent memory poisoning, multi-agent trust escalation, the gap between scoped session tokens and cross-platform identity — applies to every product in the category. The headline conclusion is that environmental defense is doing the load-bearing work, and the model layer is not going to fix prompt injection on its own.
