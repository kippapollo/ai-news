Google DeepMind ships DiffusionGemma, an open-weights 26B MoE that denoises 256 tokens at a time

DiffusionGemma 26B-A4B is the first production-grade open text-diffusion model from a frontier lab. Built on the Gemma 4 backbone with 8 of 128 experts active per token and 3.8 billion active parameters, it abandons sequential autoregression and instead refines a 256-token canvas in parallel through iterative denoising. Throughput lands at more than 1,000 tokens per second on a single NVIDIA H100, 700-plus on an RTX 5090, and up to 2,000 tokens per second on a DGX Station — roughly 4x an autoregressive model at the same parameter budget. The model is multimodal across text, image and video inputs with a 256K-token context window and 140-plus languages, and ships under Apache 2.0 on Hugging Face with day-zero support in Transformers, vLLM, MLX and Unsloth. Google is direct in the model card that quality lags Gemma 4 in absolute terms and recommends Gemma 4 for production today; the release is positioned as an experimental open building block for in-line editing, code infill and real-time agents where denoising's self-correction loop matters more than absolute log-likelihood.

Google DeepMind · 2026-06-10 · Source ↗ ·

Google DeepMind on June 10 released DiffusionGemma 26B-A4B, an open-weights text diffusion mixture-of-experts model that the team frames as a public building block rather than a competitor to Gemma 4 on absolute capability. The architecture takes the Gemma 4 backbone — 30 layers, 128 experts, 8 active per token, plus one shared expert — and grafts on a discrete text diffusion head. Generation no longer runs left-to-right one token at a time. Instead, the decoder applies bidirectional attention over a 256-token canvas and iteratively denoises the canvas through multiple forward passes, finalizing roughly 15 to 20 tokens per pass. An autoregressive encoder processes and caches the prompt context so the generation work is concentrated in the parallel decoder.

The throughput numbers are the marketing line. A single NVIDIA H100 sustains more than 1,000 tokens per second; a consumer-grade RTX 5090 holds 700-plus; an NVIDIA DGX Station pushes the model to 2,000 tokens per second. NVIDIA's day-zero blog says that is "roughly 4x faster than an equivalent autoregressive model" at the same parameter budget. The total parameter count is 26 billion, but only 3.8 billion are active per token thanks to the sparse MoE routing, and the quantized weights fit inside the 24 GB VRAM ceiling of an RTX 4090 or 5090. Context window is 256K tokens. The vision encoder adds about 550 million parameters and accepts interleaved text, image and video inputs up to 60 seconds. The model speaks 140-plus languages.

How parallel denoising changes what the model can do

The architectural shift matters for a category of tasks autoregressive models handle poorly. Because the decoder applies bidirectional attention across the entire canvas, the model can revise tokens it has already committed to, by re-noising and re-denoising regions that look inconsistent with later context. Autoregressive transformers cannot do this — once a token is sampled, it is fixed. Google's developer guide leans into use cases where that property pays off: in-line editing of existing text, code infill where the model has to thread new content between fixed anchors above and below, and real-time interactive applications where the model can keep refining a draft as the user types.

"DiffusionGemma abandons the sequential, token-by-token process of typical autoregressive Large Language Models."— Google DeepMind, DiffusionGemma model page

The model card is unusually honest about the trade. On MMLU Pro the score lands at 77.6%, GPQA Diamond at 73.2%, LiveCodeBench v6 at 69.1%, and MMMU Pro at 54.3% for vision tasks. Those numbers sit below standard Gemma 4 at the same parameter budget. Google states it directly: "output quality is lower than standard Gemma 4," with a recommendation that production workloads still use Gemma 4 today. The trade is that the bottleneck moves from memory bandwidth to compute, which is exactly the regime modern accelerators are designed for, so the same hardware delivers more usable tokens per second.

What ships on day one

The weights are on Hugging Face at `google/diffusiongemma-26B-A4B-it` under Apache 2.0. Day-zero support lands in Hugging Face Transformers, vLLM, Apple's MLX runtime and Unsloth, with llama.cpp work in progress. The Google Cloud Model Garden carries the model behind the standard Vertex API, and NVIDIA NIM offers a containerized inference endpoint optimized for Blackwell's NVFP4 quantization. Unsloth simultaneously published a GGUF quantization at `unsloth/diffusiongemma-26B-A4B-it-GGUF` for local self-hosting.

The licensing posture matters. Apache 2.0 is the maximally permissive open-weights license Google has used; previous Gemma releases shipped under the Gemma terms. Pairing the model with Apache 2.0 invites third-party fine-tunes, distillations and downstream commercial products without a separate license agreement. Combined with the lower active parameter count, that lowers the cost floor of running an in-house diffusion text model from "you need an A100" to "you can use the RTX card you already have."

The training cutoff and safety posture

The pre-training mixture spans web documents in 140-plus languages, code, math and images with a January 2025 cutoff. Google ran the standard preprocessing pipeline — CSAM filtering, sensitive personal data removal, quality and safety filtering. The card spells out the limitations: the model depends on training data quality, struggles with ambiguity and sarcasm in nuanced language, can generate factually incorrect text, and has limited common-sense reasoning. There is built-in reasoning and function-calling support, with variable image-token budgets between 70 and 1,120 tokens per image to trade resolution against latency.

The strategic read: this is the first time a frontier lab has published an open, production-runnable diffusion language model with the same release rigor as a standard autoregressive Gemma. The 4x throughput claim is real on paper and will get stress-tested across the next month on agent loops, code-completion benchmarks and edit-in-place applications where the bidirectional canvas actually changes what the system can do. The quality gap to Gemma 4 is real too, and the model card's honesty about that gap is meant to keep production users from defaulting to DiffusionGemma when they should still be on Gemma 4.

"DiffusionGemma denoises up to 256 tokens in parallel by starting from noise and refining a whole block of text at once."— Michael Fukuyama, NVIDIA

Anthropic asks governments for power to block frontier deployments at the 10^25-FLOP line

Anthropic · 2026-06-10 · Source ↗ ·

The "Policy on the AI Exponential" paper is built around two frameworks. The Advanced AI Framework asks legislatures for explicit legal authority to block or deter deployment of frontier AI models judged to risk catastrophic harm. The Economic Policy Framework names three unemployment thresholds — 5%, 10%, and an unspecified "unprecedented" level — and pre-commits to policy responses at each. Civil penalties scale with global annual revenue and escalate for repeat violations. The thresholds for who falls inside the rules: 10^25 training FLOPs, $500M in AI revenue, or $1B in AI R&D spend.

The framing is unusual for a frontier lab. Anthropic is asking governments to take responsibility for blocking the deployment of models the lab itself or its competitors might want to ship. The Advanced AI Framework specifies five concrete asks: legal authority to block or deter dangerous deployments, an ecosystem of qualified independent evaluators, gene synthesis screening and biosurveillance to harden against biological misuse, hardened critical infrastructure software, and a dedicated government tracking function for frontier cyber capabilities. Transparency requirements include published safety frameworks, system cards, regular risk reports and engagement with independent evaluators.

The quantitative thresholds matter because they define who falls inside the rules. Models trained on more than 10^25 floating-point operations, or built by companies earning more than $500 million from AI or spending more than $1 billion on AI R&D, are inside the perimeter. That captures Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, xAI, Meta and a small number of others; it explicitly excludes the long tail of fine-tuners and application builders. Civil penalties are tied to global annual revenue and escalate for repeat violations — the EU GDPR enforcement model applied to frontier AI.

"AI is advancing at exponential speed, and the policymaking process was built for a slower world."— Anthropic, "Policy on the AI Exponential"

The Economic Policy Framework

The second half of the paper turns to economic disruption. Anthropic proposes a tiered response keyed to national unemployment: an initial 5% trigger, a 10% trigger that activates broader transfer programs, and an "unprecedented" level the paper does not numerically define but treats as the regime where existing labor-market institutions stop functioning. The framework asks for active program evaluation rather than committing to a specific policy mix — Anthropic wants the government to fund the research that decides what works, not to lock in answers in advance.

The companion announcement — covered in a separate Washington Times Associated Press report — landed the same day: Anthropic is committing $200 million to an Economic Futures Research Fund that backs research trials and program evaluations of public policies it deems promising, and a $150 million national fellowship program for early-career professionals working to "extend the benefits of AI to communities across America." The combined $350 million commitment is the largest single allocation by a frontier lab to economic-impact research.

The political backdrop

The paper drops on the same day OpenAI CEO Sam Altman met Senator Bernie Sanders to discuss a plan for the public to take an ownership stake in frontier AI companies, using their stock to seed a public wealth fund. Anthropic's framing — civil penalties, transparency, government authority — is more conventional. The Altman/Sanders conversation is the radical wing. Both arrived publicly on June 10, two weeks after President Trump signed the voluntary AI executive order conditioning $42 billion in BEAD funding on the absence of state-level AI laws.

Claude Managed Agents add cron-style scheduled deployments and credential vaults in public beta

TechTimes · 2026-06-10 · Source ↗ ·

Anthropic moved Managed Agents into recurring-task territory. Agents now attach to cron schedules and start a fresh session on every trigger, with administrators able to pause, resume, archive or fire manual runs. Credential vaults store environment variables as placeholders inside the agent sandbox and attach actual secrets only at the network boundary to allowlisted domains. Rakuten runs weekly spreadsheet-to-deck reports on the new schedules; Notion uses the vault to roll out its own CLI under its security guidelines. The feature bills under existing Claude Platform usage rather than per-credit.

The two pieces of the launch — scheduled deployments and credential vaults — share an underlying premise: production agents need to look more like ops infrastructure than like chat sessions. Scheduled deployments attach a Managed Agent to a cron-style schedule on the Claude Platform. Each trigger spins up a new session, the agent runs the configured task and exits. Administrators can pause, resume, archive or manually trigger extra runs from the console. The use cases the launch post calls out — nightly data syncs, weekly compliance scans, daily digests — are workloads previously handled by hand-rolled cron jobs plus the OpenAI API.

Credential vaults solve the secrets problem that breaks agent deployments in regulated companies. API keys are stored in the vault as placeholders. Inside the agent sandbox, the agent never sees the actual secret. When the agent calls an allowlisted external service, the network boundary injects the real key and removes it before the agent's session can log it. When the vault rotates, every agent's effective key rotates automatically. The construction is meant to neutralize prompt-injection attacks that try to exfiltrate credentials by tricking the agent into echoing them.

"Teams across Rakuten use scheduled deployments to analyze data in a spreadsheet and produce a report or deck on a weekly or monthly schedule."— Yusuke Kaji, GM of AI for Business, Rakuten

Customer names and pricing posture

Anthropic put named customers behind the launch: Actively AI replaced its own scheduling infrastructure with scheduled deployments and called the change a stack simplification; Notion uses vaults to roll out the Notion CLI under its security team's strict guidelines; Rakuten built weekly spreadsheet-to-deck reporting on top of the schedules. Ando, Browserbase and Milana appear on the launch's customer list. Compatible third-party tools include Browserbase, KERNEL, Notion, Ramp and Sentry CLIs; Browserbase and KERNEL also bring browser automation into Managed Agents for the first time.

"Environment variables in vaults let us securely roll out the Notion CLI, meeting our security team's strict guidelines."— Quan Nguyen, Public API Lead, Notion

The pricing contrast with OpenAI's competing workspace agents is intentional. Anthropic attaches no separate fee to scheduled deployments — usage bills under existing Claude Platform pricing. OpenAI's workspace agents, which begin token-credit billing on July 6, charge approximately 7.25 credits for a GPT-5.5 run consuming 20,000 input tokens and 5,000 output tokens, with typical runs landing between 5 and 25 credits. For shops weighing which platform anchors their agent deployments, the per-run versus per-token economics is now the calculation the procurement teams have to do.

Xcode 27 keeps inline code completion on the Neural Engine and routes agent work through mcpbridge

TechTimes · 2026-06-10 · Source ↗ ·

Day three of WWDC dropped Xcode 27's dual-engine architecture. The inline completion model is a Swift- and Apple-SDK-tuned model that runs exclusively on the Neural Engine; no source code transits any server. For multi-file refactors, test-suite generation and autonomous debugging, developers opt in to third-party agents from Anthropic, Google and OpenAI through the new `mcpbridge` binary, which translates Model Context Protocol calls into Apple's XPC sandbox transport rather than HTTP. The protocol exposes 20 Xcode capabilities — file reads, builds, test runs, live diagnostics, SwiftUI preview rendering — through structured JSON.

Apple split Xcode 27's AI work into two engines with very different privacy and latency profiles. The inline-completion model is Apple's own. It is specifically trained for Swift idioms and Apple SDK patterns, not a general-purpose LLM, and it runs entirely on the Apple Silicon Neural Engine. The unified high-bandwidth memory shared between the CPU, GPU and Neural Engine — M1's 68 GB/s, M1 Max's 400 GB/s — gives the model access to the working set without cross-bus transfer. Latency drops because there is no network round-trip. Source code never leaves the device.

The agent track is separate. For multi-file refactors, test-suite generation and autonomous debugging — work the inline model is not sized for — developers opt in to third-party agents from Anthropic, Google or OpenAI through Xcode's settings panel. The mechanism is a new binary called `mcpbridge` that translates the Model Context Protocol over Apple's XPC, the sandboxed inter-process communication layer Apple uses for cross-process work on iOS and macOS. Routing MCP over XPC rather than HTTP keeps the agent inside Apple's sandbox primitives, which is the privacy posture Apple has spent the year building toward.

"Inline completions never leave the device; agentic tasks do, when the developer chooses to invoke them."— Apple, WWDC 2026 session description

What the protocol exposes

The Xcode MCP surface gives external agents 20 capabilities: file reads, building, test runs, live diagnostics, SwiftUI preview rendering and 15 other Xcode internals, each addressed through structured JSON requests. The inline-completion model is not exposed through the API — Apple keeps it strictly inside the editor's own completion path, which the company says prevents app-level inference re-use of the Xcode-tuned weights.

Xcode 27 reaches public release in September 2026 alongside iOS 27 and macOS Golden Gate. The day-three session paired naturally with Apple's day-two announcement of the LanguageModel protocol, which lets third-party cloud providers implement Foundation Models as Swift packages — Anthropic and Google published the Swift packages the day before, so the agentic-cloud path in Xcode is already plumbed.

MarkTechPost: DiffusionGemma uses Uniform State Diffusion and lets the model re-noise to revise tokens

MarkTechPost · 2026-06-10 · Source ↗ ·

MarkTechPost's launch-day write-up gets into the mechanics. DiffusionGemma uses a Uniform State Diffusion mechanism over a 256-token canvas, finalizes 15-20 tokens per forward pass, and lets the model re-noise regions during decoding — a capability autoregressive transformers cannot match, because once a sample is committed it stays committed. The bidirectional attention pattern is what makes the self-correction loop possible, and it is the architectural reason the throughput math works out: the bottleneck moves from memory bandwidth to compute.

The MarkTechPost coverage is the cleanest mechanical description of how DiffusionGemma actually generates. The model uses a Uniform State Diffusion mechanism: every token in the canvas occupies one of a finite set of states, and the denoising step samples a transition for each token in parallel based on the current state of every other token. Each forward pass advances the canvas one denoising step closer to a final sample. The model finalizes roughly 15 to 20 tokens per pass — that is, commits them as no-longer-noisy — and continues denoising the rest.

The architectural payoff is the re-noising step. If, partway through decoding, the model decides a committed region is inconsistent with later context, it can re-noise that region and re-denoise. Autoregressive transformers cannot do this; once a token is sampled, the rest of the sequence is conditioned on that sample and the model has no mechanism to revise upstream choices. Bidirectional attention across the canvas makes the re-noising loop coherent — every token sees every other token at every step. The Uniform State Diffusion formulation is what makes it tractable to train.

Why the bottleneck moves

Autoregressive decoding is memory-bandwidth bound: each token requires a fresh KV-cache read and write. Parallel denoising over a 256-token canvas is compute-bound: each forward pass processes all 256 tokens at once and the work scales with the number of denoising steps, not the sequence length. Modern accelerators are designed around the compute-bound regime — the Tensor Cores on H100 and Blackwell are at their best when they have parallel work to do. NVIDIA's day-zero acceleration post reads as the obvious sequel: the new model shape is exactly the workload Blackwell rewards.
