Anthropic prints a $965B valuation, ships Opus 4.8, and queues Mythos for wide release

A $65 billion Series H led by Altimeter, Dragoneer, Greenoaks and Sequoia closes at a $965 billion post-money mark, putting Anthropic above OpenAI's $852B on paper for the first time. Run-rate revenue crossed $47 billion this month. Opus 4.8 lands as a quiet upgrade; the Mythos-class model that finds and chains software vulnerabilities is promised for all customers in the coming weeks.

Fortune · 2026-05-29 · Source ↗ ·

Anthropic closed a $65 billion Series H at a $965 billion post-money valuation, the largest private financing for an AI company on record and the first private round to put any AI lab above OpenAI's $852 billion mark from March 2026. Altimeter Capital, Dragoneer, Greenoaks and Sequoia Capital co-led. Capital Group, Coatue, D1 Capital Partners, GIC, ICONIQ and XN co-led a second tier. Behind them: Baillie Gifford, Blackstone, Brookfield, D.E. Shaw Ventures, DST Global, Fidelity, General Catalyst, Insight Partners, Jane Street, Lightspeed, MGX, NTTVC, T. Rowe Price and Temasek. Strategic infrastructure investors Samsung, SK hynix and Micron also took allocation, a tell that memory supply is now a fundraising primitive.

The round triples the $380 billion Series G that closed in February. $15 billion of the new total is previously committed money from hyperscaler partners, including $5 billion from Amazon announced in April. The cash gets routed into three buckets per the company's own framing: safety and interpretability research, compute, and product. Anthropic discloses that run-rate revenue crossed $47 billion this month, up from roughly $10 billion at the end of 2025. That figure puts Anthropic ahead of OpenAI's most recently disclosed $25 billion ARR by a wide margin.

Opus 4.8 ships at the same prices, a faster fast mode

Anthropic released Claude Opus 4.8 alongside the round. The headline benchmark is SWE-bench Verified at 88.6%, up from 87.6% on Opus 4.7. SWE-bench Pro jumps from 64.3% to 69.2%, Terminal-Bench 2.1 lands at 74.6%, GPQA Diamond at 93.6%, and 1890 Elo on GDPval-AA. Pricing holds at $5 per million input tokens and $25 per million output tokens, matching Opus 4.7. Fast mode, the lower-latency configuration that trades small amounts of capability for ~2.5× throughput, is now three times cheaper than the same option on 4.7. Claude Code picks up "dynamic workflows," a parallel-subagent orchestration primitive that lets a single Claude Code session farm out subgoals to worker subagents that run concurrently. The Messages API gains mid-task system messages.

Anthropic's alignment write-up is the more interesting half of the model card. The company says Opus 4.8 shows "substantially lower" rates of misaligned behavior than 4.7, including deception and cooperation with user misuse, and rates approach those of the Mythos preview that was previously the company's safety high-water mark. Anthropic claims 4.8 is "around four times less likely than 4.7 to let flaws in its own code pass unremarked," which is the kind of property that matters when the model is the one writing PRs at $25/Mtok. Independent evaluator Andon Labs flagged performance degradation on adversarial benchmarks and characterized the model as more cautious; the company acknowledged the tradeoff.

"Claude is increasingly indispensable to our growing global community of customers."— Krishna Rao, Chief Financial Officer, Anthropic

Mythos is the next step

Mythos is the codename for the model class above Opus. Anthropic says Mythos is "notable for its coding and cyber capabilities, including the ability to find vulnerabilities in existing software and chain these vulnerabilities together to execute sophisticated cyber attacks." The company has been running a limited preview, including Project Glasswing, which gave AWS, Apple, Cisco, Google, JPMorgan Chase and Microsoft access to Mythos Preview to find and patch critical vulnerabilities in their own code. Anthropic says it expects to ship a Mythos-class model to all customers "in the coming weeks," contingent on additional safeguards.

That's a long way from the company's previous public posture. Anthropic spent the prior twelve months arguing that the bigger model wasn't ready because the eval surface for cyber and bio capabilities wasn't mature. The Mythos timeline implies the company believes its preparedness machinery has caught up. It also implies that the lab that publishes the loudest safety position now plans to ship the most capable cyber-offensive frontier model in the same quarter. The internal tension is real, and the disclosure is the tell.

Compute is the structural cost

The capital commitments behind Opus 4.8 and Mythos are not subtle. Anthropic has 5 gigawatts of Amazon Trainium capacity coming online, a 5-gigawatt TPU agreement with Google/Broadcom, and access to xAI's Colossus systems via SpaceX. Reporting confirms Anthropic is paying $1.25 billion per month to xAI for Colossus access through May 2029, an obligation that maps to roughly $45 billion in commitments over the contract life. The lab will not own the silicon; it will lease everything and pay the bill out of inference revenue. That model works if and only if the next two Claude generations can each absorb 3–4× the per-query token spend without collapsing margin.

Anthropic also confirmed an IPO is the next path, though not the immediate next move. The company filed for confidential review with the SEC in April, and the Series H is framed in the documents as a likely final private round. The Fortune piece pegs the comparison: OpenAI is at $852 billion private and roughly $25 billion ARR; Anthropic is at $965 billion private and $47 billion ARR. On a revenue multiple, Anthropic is more expensive in absolute dollars but cheaper relative to revenue.

"Mythos has the ability to find vulnerabilities in existing software and chain these vulnerabilities together to execute sophisticated cyber attacks."— Anthropic, Mythos capability disclosure

What this changes about the race

Three things shift on the day. Hyperscaler dependency is now mutual; Samsung, SK hynix and Micron taking allocation means memory supply is paired to model demand at the cap-table level, not just the contract level. Second, the OpenAI/Anthropic gap on revenue widens at the same moment OpenAI's preparedness framework lands; the two labs are now arguing different cases, and the customers will decide which case is more credible. Third, the Mythos timeline forces every other frontier lab to publish a comparable cyber-offensive capability disclosure or accept the appearance of being behind. The "find and chain vulnerabilities" capability is the first frontier capability claim that's specific enough to bench externally, and the labs that don't run the bench will be assumed to be unable to.

OpenAI publishes a Frontier Governance Framework aimed at California and EU obligations

OpenAI · 2026-05-29 · Source ↗ ·

The new framework slots OpenAI's Preparedness work into a public-facing governance document that maps to California's Transparency in Frontier AI Act and the EU AI Act's Code of Practice for General Purpose AI. Risk tiers cover cyber offense, CBRN, harmful manipulation and loss of control.

OpenAI published its Frontier Governance Framework on May 29, a document that re-states its internal Preparedness Framework in terms designed to satisfy two specific regulatory regimes: California's Transparency in Frontier AI Act, signed in 2025, and the EU AI Act's Code of Practice for General Purpose AI, which begins binding compliance in August 2026. The Preparedness Framework remains the operational engine. The Frontier Governance Framework is the version OpenAI hands to regulators and posts publicly.

The four risk domains

OpenAI sorts threats from frontier capabilities into four buckets: cyber offense, chemical/biological/radiological/nuclear (CBRN) risks, harmful manipulation, and loss of control. Each domain gets defined risk tiers that escalate as model capability rises. The framework commits OpenAI to specific actions at each tier: capability evaluations before deployment, mitigation requirements, gated release patterns, and reporting obligations to the Safety Advisory Group. The framework does not list specific thresholds publicly; it lists the categories and the process by which thresholds are set.

The cyber-offense category is the one most likely to be tested in the next 60 days. Anthropic's Mythos-class model is queued for general availability and explicitly disclosed cyber capability claims. OpenAI's framework now provides a public scaffold against which its own next model's cyber disclosures will be measured. The CBRN category sits adjacent to OpenAI's parallel Rosalind Biodefense announcement on the same day: the framework defines how high-capability bio models are governed; Rosalind is the channel through which trusted users get them.

External input and reporting

The framework formalizes external participation. OpenAI commits to soliciting input from "external domain experts and independent third-party evaluators" who stress-test safeguards on models approaching a new risk tier. Their outputs go to OpenAI's internal Safety Advisory Group, which writes the deployment recommendation that the executive team acts on. The framework names model reporting, security risk management, incident response, external expert input, and framework updates as the five process pillars.

"The Preparedness Framework remains the foundation for OpenAI's approach to managing serious risks from advanced AI systems."— OpenAI, Frontier Governance Framework

Reading the timing

The publication date is not accidental. California's Transparency in Frontier AI Act requires frontier developers to publish a governance framework that covers specific topics; the deadline falls in summer 2026. The EU AI Act's General Purpose AI Code of Practice goes binding on August 2, 2026. OpenAI publishing now buys the company three months of regulator review before the EU deadline lands and roughly six weeks before California's compliance window opens. Anthropic's $965 billion round on the same day will pull the policy spotlight; OpenAI gets the policy paper out before the comparison piece is written.

The framework also reframes how OpenAI talks about its own preparedness internally. The Preparedness Framework was an internal commitment; the Frontier Governance Framework is a public obligation. Once a company writes down a process and publishes it, the regulator's question stops being "what do you do?" and becomes "did you do what you said?" That second question has an answer that can be audited. OpenAI is now in the audit posture.

The remaining gap is enforcement asymmetry. The framework lists processes; it does not list capability thresholds or trigger numbers. Anthropic's Mythos disclosure named a specific capability (find and chain vulnerabilities) and a target release window. OpenAI's framework does not. Whether that asymmetry holds through Q3 will depend on whether the next OpenAI frontier model lands with capability disclosures that match the framework's structure.

OpenAI opens Rosalind Biodefense to U.S. and allied public-health partners

OpenAI / Axios · 2026-05-29 · Source ↗ ·

GPT-Rosalind becomes accessible to vetted developers and government partners for outbreak modeling, early detection, screening and countermeasure design. Lawrence Livermore, Johns Hopkins APL and CEPI are named launch collaborators.

OpenAI launched Rosalind Biodefense on May 29, a structured program that extends "trusted access" to GPT-Rosalind, the life-sciences-tuned model OpenAI introduced in March 2026. The program targets vetted developers, U.S. government partners and allied public-health institutions working on biodefense, public health and pandemic preparedness. Application gating is the central design choice: GPT-Rosalind is not generally available, and Rosalind Biodefense is the channel by which qualified users get it.

What partners can build

OpenAI lists the workload surface explicitly: epidemiological modeling, early-warning systems, outbreak response planning, diagnostics, non-pharmaceutical interventions, medical countermeasure development, and protein engineering. Three named collaborators ship at launch. Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory will use Rosalind for biopreparedness work. Johns Hopkins Applied Physics Laboratory takes on protein-engineering applications. CEPI, the Coalition for Epidemic Preparedness Innovations, is named on vaccine-development work. OpenAI sponsors compute access for selected developers and provides launch support so partner teams can ship in months rather than years.

The bar to get in is the inverse of how OpenAI normally onboards customers. Standard ChatGPT Enterprise and API access ask whether the customer can pay; Rosalind Biodefense asks whether the customer has a mission, a track record, and a willingness to submit to OpenAI's safety review for every published artifact. The model card for GPT-Rosalind covers the most consequential capability OpenAI has shipped to date, and the company is choosing to gate it through institutional trust rather than KYC.

"Expanding trusted access to GPT-Rosalind for vetted developers and U.S. government partners advancing biodefense, public health and pandemic preparedness through frontier AI."— OpenAI, Rosalind Biodefense announcement

Read against the Frontier Governance Framework

Rosalind Biodefense ships on the same day as OpenAI's Frontier Governance Framework, which lists CBRN as one of four risk categories. Rosalind is the operationalization. The framework defines the rules; Rosalind defines who gets the keys. A bio-capable model that's open to the world has loss-of-control properties; a bio-capable model that's open to Lawrence Livermore and CEPI has, in OpenAI's calculation, net defensive value.

The other partner story sits underneath the named three. OpenAI's Trusted Access program now spans biology (Rosalind) and cyber (the Trusted Access for Cyber initiative announced earlier in May). Both are "frontier capabilities, restricted distribution, named defensive partners" in the same template. That template is OpenAI's bet that the most-capable models can be deployed responsibly only if access is institutional rather than retail.

Open questions remain. Rosalind's evaluation regime is not public. The selection criteria for non-government developers are described as "vetted" without specifying the gate. And the relationship between Rosalind Biodefense and the international community is left for follow-up: CEPI is international; the rest of the named partners are U.S. The framework will be tested when an EU or UK request lands and OpenAI has to choose which way the partner list grows.

Microsoft is building a single Copilot super-app to absorb its scattered surfaces

Fortune · 2026-05-29 · Source ↗ ·

Fortune reports Microsoft is consolidating chat, coding, and the rest of its Copilot tools into one application after research showed users dislike switching between the eight or so Copilot SKUs the company has shipped.

Fortune broke on May 29 that Microsoft is developing a unified Copilot "super-app" that would absorb the chat, coding, GitHub, Studio, and consumer Copilot surfaces into a single application. The driver, per the reporting, is user-research telling Microsoft that customers find the eight-or-so distinct Copilot products confusing and dislike switching between them. The super-app is positioned as the next step beyond Microsoft 365 E7, the Frontier Suite Microsoft launched on May 1.

How fragmented the surface had become

The Copilot family currently includes Microsoft 365 Copilot, GitHub Copilot, Copilot Studio, Copilot for Sales/Service, Windows Copilot, Edge Copilot, the standalone consumer Copilot app, and the recently announced Agent 365 control plane. Some are productivity-flow tools; some are developer-flow tools; some are end-consumer apps. Microsoft's enterprise customers have repeatedly told the company that the SKU sprawl is itself a procurement blocker. The unified super-app is the response.

The plan, per Fortune's sources, is to fold the surfaces while keeping the underlying capabilities intact: coding stays available, chat stays available, the connectors stay available, but the user enters through one door. Microsoft is also redesigning the in-product Copilot experience across the Microsoft 365 apps so the assistant looks and behaves the same regardless of which app the user is sitting in.

"Microsoft found that customers dislike shifting between its Copilot tools, and seeks for people to see more value from Copilot."— Fortune, summarizing internal user research

Why now

Three pressures converge. First, Microsoft 365 Business Standard with Copilot launches July 1, bundling Copilot into the productivity tier for small businesses and removing the optional-add-on framing. Second, the Microsoft-OpenAI restructuring closed in April, giving Microsoft a nonexclusive license to OpenAI IP through 2032 and freeing OpenAI to ship its own competing products on other clouds. Microsoft can no longer assume OpenAI's distribution will route through Microsoft's; it needs its own front door. Third, Cursor at $29.3 billion, Cognition at $26 billion, and ChatGPT's continued share of consumer AI have made it clear that the "AI app" category will consolidate around a small number of brands, not a portfolio of feature-set names.

The risk is the inverse of the consolidation pitch. A super-app collapses surfaces but also collapses the per-SKU revenue line, and Microsoft's enterprise model has historically rewarded SKU sprawl by letting it upsell adjacent capabilities. Whether the super-app launches as a re-skinning of existing surfaces or as a genuinely new application will determine whether Microsoft is consolidating its product line or rebuilding its product. Fortune's reporting suggests the latter, but the launch date is not specified.

METR can't run productivity studies any more because coders refuse to work without AI

TechCrunch · 2026-05-29 · Source ↗ ·

METR published a follow-up survey showing developers self-report doubled organizational value from AI tools. The same survey notes 44% of company tokens go to fixing AI-generated bugs and Code Rabbit data shows 1.7× more issues in AI-written PRs.

METR, the evaluation lab that ran the most-cited 2025 productivity study on AI coding tools, published a follow-up on May 29. The lab's headline finding is that it can no longer replicate the original study's methodology because most developers will not participate in tasks without AI assistance, even for short experimental sessions. The 2025 study found that developers felt more productive with AI but actually spent extra time on debugging that ate the perceived gains. METR's 2026 self-reported survey, by contrast, finds employees believe AI has doubled their organizational value.

The numbers that don't line up

The TechCrunch piece pulls four data points worth lining up. METR's self-reported survey: developers say AI doubles their value. Amazon's now-shuttered Kirorank token-tracking leaderboard found that 44% of company tokens were spent on bug fixes against AI-generated code. Code Rabbit's review of open-source PRs found 1.7× more problems in AI-generated code than human-written code. Uber exceeded its 2026 AI budget in the first four months of the year. Each data point is consistent with the others if and only if you accept that developers are over-rating productivity gains relative to maintenance cost.

James Shore, quoted in the piece, frames the trade in plain terms: "You write code twice as quick now? Better hope you've halved your maintenance costs. Otherwise, you're screwed." Aiswarya Sankar at Entelligence and Scott Wu at Cognition are quoted on the opposite side; the tool builders point to organizational throughput gains that the maintenance-cost math doesn't capture. Singapore Management University researchers are cited on the secondary finding that the productivity gains shrink as developer skill rises, which is the opposite of the early-2025 assumption that AI tools were a senior-developer accelerator.

"You write code twice as quick now? Better hope you've halved your maintenance costs. Otherwise, you're screwed."— James Shore, programmer and author

The structural finding

The point that matters more than the productivity-percentage debate is the participation finding. METR cannot run a control arm. The control arm requires developers willing to spend an afternoon coding without AI assistance, and METR's recruiting funnel reports that those developers no longer exist at the company scale METR samples from. The lab now relies on self-report, which it acknowledges is "dubious" because the perceived gain is exactly the variable under test.

The structural finding is that the AI coding tool is now a precondition of the work rather than an accelerant of it. That changes how productivity should be measured. Tool adoption rate stops being interesting; cost-per-shipped-feature net of maintenance becomes the only meaningful number, and very few organizations measure it. Cognition's $1B round at $26B on May 27 prices the bet that the precondition will hold. Uber's blown 2026 AI budget prices the same bet from the other side.

AgentDoG 1.5 proposes a lightweight alignment layer that sits between an agent and its tools

Shanghai AI Lab / arXiv via Hugging Face · 2026-05-29 · Source ↗ ·

Friday's top-trending Hugging Face paper, from Shanghai AI Lab, presents an alignment scaffold that constrains agent behavior at the tool-call boundary without retraining the underlying model. The framing is that agent safety is plumbing, not capability.

The top-trending paper on Hugging Face's May 29 Daily Papers list is AgentDoG 1.5, a "lightweight and scalable alignment framework for AI agent safety and security" from Shanghai AI Lab. The work proposes that agent alignment can be separated from model alignment: rather than fine-tune the base model for safety, AgentDoG intercepts the agent's tool calls and applies policy at the call boundary. The framework runs as a per-agent wrapper that examines proposed actions against a policy graph and either allows, rewrites or refuses the call.

What the framing implies

The interesting move is conceptual. Alignment research has spent eighteen months arguing about whether RLHF, constitutional AI, or some successor method scales to agentic systems. AgentDoG sidesteps the argument: the base model can be whatever the customer wants, and the alignment story is a runtime check on the actions the agent emits. That makes alignment composable with closed and open-weight models, which is exactly what's needed in a world where Qwen, DeepSeek and Mistral are running agents inside enterprise stacks alongside Claude and GPT.

The paper's "lightweight" claim is the marketing word that earns the paper's upvotes. The runtime overhead is reported as minimal, and the policy graph is small enough to maintain by hand. The "scalable" claim covers what happens when the agent population grows: AgentDoG's wrapper is a per-agent install, so adding agents scales linearly rather than retraining the base.

Why it lands on May 29

The timing aligns with the Five Eyes "Careful Adoption of Agentic AI Services" guidance from earlier in May, which named privilege, design, behavioral, structural and accountability risks as the categories deployers must control. AgentDoG is a candidate implementation of the first three. The paper does not claim CISA conformance, but the recipe maps cleanly onto the published recommendations.

"Until security practices, evaluation methods and standards mature, organisations should assume that agentic AI systems may behave unexpectedly and plan deployments accordingly."— Five Eyes joint guidance, the regulatory frame AgentDoG addresses

The open question is whether wrapper-based alignment survives capability scaling. A model that can plan around a static policy graph will route around AgentDoG, and the paper's authors acknowledge the obvious adversarial case. The framework's defenders argue that the wrapper buys time and observable failure modes, both of which are valuable even if the wrapper is eventually outgrown. The paper is now the most-discussed agent-safety primitive of the week, and Shanghai AI Lab's prior work in this area means the reference implementation is likely to ship.

Qwen-VLA unifies vision, language and action across tasks and robot embodiments

Alibaba Qwen / arXiv via Hugging Face · 2026-05-29 · Source ↗ ·

The Qwen team's new VLA model is trained on a single objective that spans desktop, mobile, simulation and physical-robot embodiments. The paper claims policy transfer between embodiments without per-platform fine-tuning.

The second-most-trending May 29 Hugging Face paper is Qwen-VLA, the Qwen team's contribution to the unified vision-language-action stack. The work trains a single model on data drawn from desktop GUI agents, mobile GUI agents, simulation environments and physical-robot teleoperation logs. The objective is consistent across embodiments: predict the next action, given the current observation and the task description. The novelty is the claim that the resulting model transfers between embodiments without per-platform fine-tuning, with policy losses small enough that a model trained predominantly on screen interaction can move a robot arm at a usable success rate.

The training mix

Qwen-VLA's training mix is the part the paper spends the most time defending. The team draws on Qwen's prior multimodal corpora, the open-weight robotics teleoperation datasets that emerged in 2025, and a smaller proprietary dataset Qwen collected from internal robotics partners. The mix is reported as roughly 60% screen-based interaction, 30% simulation, and 10% physical-robot. The team argues that the heavy screen weighting is justified because the GUI domain provides the richest action-grounded language data; physical-robot data adds the embodiment generalization signal.

The released variants are scaled to fit on commodity hardware. The smallest Qwen-VLA runs on a single consumer GPU, which makes it a candidate for on-device robotics and on-device GUI automation. The flagship variant requires a multi-GPU node and is targeted at simulation-and-deployment workflows where latency budgets are looser.

Where it lands in the open-source race

Qwen's release schedule through 2026 has been the heaviest in open-source: Qwen 3.6 in April, the Qwen3 235B-A22B MoE earlier in spring, and now Qwen-VLA. The pace is the strategy. The Qwen team's bet is that open-source frontier performance in narrowly-defined verticals (coding, vision-language, vision-language-action) is achievable without matching closed labs on general capability. Qwen-VLA fits that bet: it is not the strongest general agent on May 29, but it may be the strongest open-weight model with action grounding across embodiments.

The release is Apache 2.0. That matters because the robotics startups that have been waiting for a generally-deployable VLA backbone now have one. Physical Intelligence, 1X, Skild and Apptronik all have proprietary VLA stacks; Qwen-VLA gives the rest of the field something to fine-tune against without negotiating model access.

"A single objective that spans desktop, mobile, simulation, and physical-robot embodiments — with policy transfer between embodiments at usable success rates."— Qwen-VLA paper abstract, paraphrased

Open questions: the paper's success-rate numbers are reported on internal benchmarks, and the community will need a week or two to reproduce them against external suites. The model's safety story is also thin compared to the AgentDoG framing also trending on May 29. Qwen-VLA is a capability release; the alignment layer is left to integrators.

OmniRetrieval unifies retrieval across heterogeneous knowledge sources in one stack

KAIST AI / arXiv via Hugging Face · 2026-05-29 · Source ↗ ·

KAIST proposes a retrieval framework that treats web, code, structured data, image collections and audio archives as a single search surface. The architecture is built around a shared embedding space and modality-specific encoders.

KAIST AI's OmniRetrieval is the third-ranked Hugging Face paper on May 29. The work proposes a retrieval framework that treats the web, code repositories, structured tabular data, image collections and audio archives as a single addressable surface. The architecture is conventional in shape: modality-specific encoders project into a shared embedding space, and a single index serves queries that can mix modalities arbitrarily. The novelty is in the joint training objective: the paper trains the encoders against a contrastive loss that explicitly enforces cross-modal alignment, so a text query for "graph of latency over time" can return a chart from a PDF, a code function that emits the same chart, and a log file the chart was derived from.

Why retrieval matters now

Retrieval has been the load-bearing primitive under every agentic deployment of the last twelve months. Deep Research, Codex, Claude Code, Cursor and every enterprise agent stack live or die on retrieval quality, and the dominant pattern has been one retriever per modality stitched together with brittle fan-out. OmniRetrieval's pitch is the single-stack alternative. The KAIST team reports parity with modality-specific baselines and lift on cross-modal queries that the baselines cannot answer at all.

The harder claim is that the joint training objective generalizes beyond the modalities seen in training. The paper shows that adding a new modality requires only encoder training, not a full re-train of the shared space, which means video, 3D scenes, and time-series data can be onboarded incrementally. That is the architectural property enterprise RAG stacks need; it is also the property that's hardest to validate without reproductions.

"A unified retrieval framework that addresses retrieval across diverse knowledge sources under a single objective."— OmniRetrieval, paper abstract

Adjacent stack moves

OmniRetrieval lands the same week Google announced MCP server collaborations with FactSet, S&P Global and PitchBook for Deep Research and Deep Research Max. The MCP path is solving the same problem from the protocol side: standardize the channel by which models reach heterogeneous data. OmniRetrieval is solving it from the index side: standardize the index that the channel hits. Both bets are reasonable; the question is which composes better in practice.

The paper is released with code under a permissive license. The released checkpoint is competitive with proprietary retrievers but trails the closed frontier on the largest scale. KAIST's positioning is that the framework matters more than the weights: the architecture is the contribution, and the weights are a starting point that integrators will fine-tune.

minWM ships an open-source full-stack framework for real-time interactive video world models

Hugging Face Daily Papers · 2026-05-29 · Source ↗ ·

The fourth-ranked May 29 paper is the most-asked-for thing in the field right now: a reference stack for training and running interactive video world models. Includes the model, the data pipeline, and the inference server.

The minWM paper releases a full open-source framework for real-time interactive video world models. The pitch is end-to-end. The release ships the model architecture, a curated training corpus, a data pipeline, an inference server, and a small client capable of running the model interactively against user inputs. That last piece is the one that lifts minWM above the dozens of video world-model papers that landed in 2026: the framework is meant to be deployed, not just published.

Why "minWM"

The name is a deliberate echo of the "minGPT" lineage: a minimal, didactic reference implementation. Genie, World Labs, Runway and others have published world-model systems through 2026, but the implementations have either been closed (Genie 3, Runway's Gen-W) or partial open-source releases that left the inference path as an exercise. The minWM authors argue that the field's reproducibility ceiling has been the missing inference stack. The paper benchmarks the framework's inference path against three closed competitors at comparable model scales; minWM's open path lands within 1.3× the throughput of the best closed competitor on consumer hardware.

The model itself is mid-scale, around 4 billion parameters, which is small enough to run interactively on a single high-end consumer GPU and large enough to produce useful spatial coherence across multi-second video sequences. The training corpus is drawn from open robotic teleoperation video, video-game playthrough captures, and synthetic data generated from open simulation environments. The team explicitly avoided commercial video sources to keep the release license clean.

"An open-source framework enabling real-time interactive video world modeling, end-to-end."— minWM paper, contribution statement

What's downstream

Runway's pitch since 2025 has been that video generation is the path to world models, and Runway's $5.3B valuation reflects investor agreement. minWM is the open-source counter: if the framework reproduces, the moat of closed world-model stacks shrinks to data and scale. The training-data section of the paper is therefore the most strategically interesting part. minWM's curated corpus is much smaller than what Runway or Google have access to; the team's claim is that targeted curation under one architecture beats raw scale under three.

For robotics, the framework matters because cheap world models reduce the simulation cost of training agentic policies. Qwen-VLA, also on May 29's HF papers list, is one of the policy architectures that benefits. The combination of minWM and Qwen-VLA is the open-source version of the closed Physical Intelligence / Runway / 1X stack, and that combination shipped on the same day.

CollectionLoRA packs 50 image-edit effects into one LoRA via multi-teacher distillation

Hugging Face Daily Papers · 2026-05-29 · Source ↗ ·

A LoRA-adapter compression result that distills 50 effect-specific teacher models into a single adapter using on-policy multi-teacher training. The use case is image-edit menus that previously needed one LoRA per option.

CollectionLoRA collapses 50 distinct image-edit effects into one LoRA adapter using a multi-teacher on-policy distillation procedure. The motivating problem is concrete: image-edit products like Adobe's Firefly, Krea, Magnific and the various StableDiffusion-based editors ship menus of effects, and each effect has historically been backed by either a separate LoRA, a separate model, or a separate prompt pipeline. Loading 50 LoRA adapters at runtime is wasteful and slow; CollectionLoRA's claim is that a single adapter can carry all 50 with comparable per-effect fidelity.

The training recipe

The on-policy distillation step is the part that earned upvotes. Standard multi-teacher distillation samples from each teacher, treats the samples as targets, and trains the student to match. The "on-policy" variant samples instead from the student's own outputs, then asks the teachers to score and correct. The student therefore learns from its own failure modes, weighted by teacher-specific corrections, rather than from generic teacher outputs that may or may not be representative of where the student is weakest.

The paper reports per-effect fidelity within a small margin of the dedicated teacher models, with a single adapter that's roughly the size of one of the 50 teacher adapters. The runtime memory savings are obvious; the inference cost savings are real because the user no longer pays the LoRA-swap latency for every effect they preview.

"Distill 50 effects into one LoRA adapter using multi-teacher on-policy distillation."— CollectionLoRA, contribution statement

Why it matters beyond image editing

The recipe generalizes. The same technique can collapse multiple style-specific writing LoRAs, multiple voice-specific TTS LoRAs, or multiple persona-specific chat LoRAs. The bottleneck in adapter-heavy products has been the loading cost; CollectionLoRA suggests the right answer is consolidation rather than caching. That changes how products think about extensibility: a product with 200 user-installable effects no longer needs 200 disk reads per session.

The paper does not address the customization story. A user wanting to add a 51st effect to a CollectionLoRA still needs to either retrain the adapter or run their custom effect alongside it. The authors acknowledge that ongoing customization is the harder problem and leave it for follow-up. For the products that ship a fixed menu of effects, the result is immediately useful.

SB 315 sets compute thresholds above which a model is considered a "frontier" system subject to the act.
