Moonshot flips on Kimi K3 at 2.8 trillion parameters, tops Frontend Code Arena over Claude Fable 5

Kimi K3 landed Thursday with 2.8 trillion parameters across 896 experts, a 1-million-token context window, and a Frontend Code Arena Elo of 1,679 versus Claude Fable 5 at 1,631 and GPT-5.6 Sol at 1,618. Open weights are targeted for release by July 27 under a permissive license. Pricing sits at $3 per million input tokens and $15 per million output, matching Claude Sonnet.

Simon Willison · 2026-07-16 · Source ↗

Moonshot AI flipped Kimi K3 into general availability Thursday, positioning it as the largest open-weight model ever shipped from a Chinese lab and, on at least one head-to-head leaderboard, the strongest coding model in the world. The Kimi API Platform documentation carried a banner reading "Kimi K3 has launched!" with a live kimi-k3 model ID on the OpenAI-compatible endpoint. Weights are targeted for release by July 27 under a permissive license Moonshot describes as "the first open 3T-class model." Simon Willison published a same-day writeup crystallizing the specs: 2.8 trillion total parameters across a mixture-of-experts pool of 896 experts, of which 16 activate per token, a 1-million-token context window included at the standard rate, and native vision baked into the base model.

Underneath the parameter count sits an architectural bet on "Kimi Delta Attention with Attention Residuals," an extension of the Kimi Delta Attention line introduced in K2.6 that Moonshot claims scales better on extended coding tasks. Willison ran his pelican-on-a-bicycle SVG probe against the model and reported it produced 16,658 output tokens off a 95-token prompt at maximum reasoning, of which 13,241 were reasoning tokens, at a total cost of 25 cents. Vision came back strong, with high-quality alt text produced for images unrelated to the pelican prompt.

"Our most capable model to date, with 2.8 trillion parameters."— Moonshot AI, Kimi K3 launch banner, July 16

Frontend Code Arena and token efficiency

The commercial hook is the Arena.ai Frontend Code leaderboard, where K3 recorded an Elo of 1,679, ahead of Claude Fable 5 at 1,631 and GPT-5.6 Sol at 1,618. Moonshot reports the model topped six of seven sub-domains, including Brand and Marketing and Data and Analytics. K2.6 had ranked 18th on the same leaderboard, so K3's climb is a 17-position jump inside one generation. The company's own reporting also puts K3's Artificial Analysis long-horizon knowledge-work Elo at 1,547, a 732-point jump over K2.6, ranking behind only Claude Fable 5. Moonshot claims K3 "uses 21 percent fewer output tokens than K2.6" for the same tasks, which if it holds outside self-reported benchmarks moves the per-answer economics closer to the Claude Sonnet band even at matching per-token pricing.

Pricing on the API stands at $3 per million input tokens on cache miss, $0.30 per million cached input tokens, and $15 per million output tokens. That matches Claude Sonnet's rate card and undercuts Anthropic's flagship Claude Fable 5 by roughly 5x on output. K2.6's numbers were $0.95 input and $4 output, so K3 is a real step up in price too. Moonshot's argument is that K3's capability delta justifies the move; the market's answer will land as the open weights get benchmarked independently in the following two weeks.

"Kimi K3 uses 21 percent fewer output tokens than K2.6."— Moonshot AI, launch materials, July 16

The open-weights promise and the $31.5B round

Moonshot committed to release the K3 weights by July 27, under a license the company frames as unrestricted commercial use. If the release lands as promised, K3 will become the largest permissively-licensed model in circulation, ahead of DeepSeek V4 and Meta's Llama 4 flagship. TechCrunch's Dominic-Madori Davis, filing at 7:26 AM PDT on Thursday, cited Financial Times reporting that Moonshot is closing a funding round targeting a $31.5 billion valuation, up from $20 billion in May 2026 on a $2 billion raise. The K3 release lands in the middle of that raise.

Independent benchmarks and caveats

Willison's characteristically dry treatment of the self-reported benchmarks is worth reading in full. In his view, K3 "mostly outperforms Claude Opus 4.8 max and GPT-5.5 high" on Moonshot's own numbers, though "it trails Claude Fable 5 and GPT-5.6 Sol." That places K3 at roughly the strongest-open-weight tier, not a frontier-supremacy claim across the board. The Frontend Code Arena result is the single-benchmark case for closed-model parity; independent evaluations of coding-agent throughput, long-context recall, and multi-turn tool use will follow through the coming week. Decentralized-AI and crypto communities were already flagging K3 within hours as an unrestricted-license alternative to Anthropic and OpenAI closed models.

"Kimi K3 mostly outperforms Claude Opus 4.8 max and GPT-5.5 high on Moonshot's own numbers, though it trails Claude Fable 5 and GPT-5.6 Sol."— Simon Willison, Simon Willison's Weblog, July 16

The wider question K3 opens is how the frontier prices out. If a Chinese lab can ship a 2.8T MoE at Claude Sonnet rates that reaches within touching distance of Claude Fable 5 on coding, the closed-model pricing floor moves. Anthropic's mid-July price ladder is already under pressure from Meta's Muse Spark 1.1 at $1.25 input and $4.25 output. K3 adds a second axis of pressure. It is cheaper, portable, self-hostable, and unrestricted. Whether that reprices the frontier or just the second tier will depend on what the open weights measure when the community starts running them at scale on July 27.

OpenAI unveils GPT-Red, an internal-only red-team model that cuts GPT-5.6 Sol prompt-injection failures 6x

OpenAI · 2026-07-16 · Source ↗

Self-play reinforcement learning between attacker and defender models drives 84 percent attack success on held-out indirect prompt-injection scenarios against GPT-5.1, versus 13 percent for human red-teamers. Adversarial training against GPT-Red pushed GPT-5.6 Sol's direct prompt-injection failure rate to 0.05 percent.

OpenAI disclosed GPT-Red on Thursday, an internal-only adversarial red-team model trained by self-play reinforcement learning that the company says has driven the largest single reduction in prompt-injection vulnerabilities across its model family since GPT-4. The training methodology paired an attacker model, GPT-Red, against a rotating set of defender LLMs; the attacker was rewarded for eliciting failures, and the defenders were rewarded for resisting while completing legitimate tasks. Training took over a year at "comparable scale to OpenAI's largest post-training jobs." GPT-Red is not deployed publicly and will not be, per OpenAI policy, to prevent offensive-capability leakage.

The headline numbers are stark. On held-out indirect prompt-injection scenarios against GPT-5.1, GPT-Red hits 84 percent attack success versus 13 percent for expert human red-teamers. After adversarial training against GPT-Red, GPT-5.6 Sol's direct prompt-injection failure rate drops to 0.05 percent, which OpenAI frames as a 6x reduction from the best production model just four months earlier. Indirect prompt-injection benchmarks over developer tools and browsing scenarios cleared 97 percent accuracy on the adversarially-trained defender.

"GPT-Red is a strong red-teamer, and our previous models are highly vulnerable to its prompt injection attacks."— OpenAI, GPT-Red technical disclosure, July 16

The fake chain-of-thought attack

The most striking novel attack GPT-Red discovered is what OpenAI calls "fake chain of thought." The attacker inserts a fabricated reasoning trace into another model's context, spoofing what looks like the target's own internal deliberation. On GPT-5.1, the attack cleared 95 percent success. On adversarially trained GPT-5.6 Sol, it dropped below 10 percent. The attack is meaningful because it targets a specific defense pattern (chain-of-thought monitoring for safety violations) that most alignment work has treated as trustworthy.

Real-world red-teaming

OpenAI ran GPT-Red against two live agent systems. Against an Andon Labs AI vending machine simulator, after practice runs GPT-Red met all three adversarial goals: cutting a stocked item to the $0.50 floor, listing a pricey new item at that same floor, and canceling another customer's order. Against a Codex CLI agent running GPT-5.4 mini, GPT-Red outperformed a prompted GPT-5.5 baseline on data-exfiltration tasks. Attack categories tested spanned internal directory exfiltration, fraudulent payment instructions, AWS credential exfiltration, 2FA disabling, malicious scraper scripts, and API key forwarding.

"The risk surface grows and the blast radius also grows."— Nikhil Kandpal, GPT-Red co-creator, OpenAI

The limits and the roadmap

OpenAI named Kandpal, Dylan Hunn and Chris Choquette-Choo as the research leads. Georgetown CSET's Jessica Ji was cited as external commentary. The disclosure includes explicit caveats. GPT-Red is weaker on multi-turn conversation and image-based attacks. Coverage of Codex CLI agent categories is partial. OpenAI's roadmap promises to "continue to scale compute and data while making algorithmic improvements." Independent researchers had flagged in June that internal red-team automation was likely the reason GPT-5.6's alignment margins were rising against DeepSeek-derived jailbreaks; today's disclosure names the mechanism. The Help Net Security and Hacker News writeups landed the same day, and the piece is now the most-linked safety disclosure OpenAI has published since the o1-preview safety card in September 2024.

"If we were to find that we were reaching a critical capability level and we didn't have the appropriate mitigations, then we would not be launching."— Helen King, VP of Responsibility, Google DeepMind

Restricted release and the cross-lab consensus

The access model is deliberately gated. Trusted researchers and biosecurity partners get expanded access to models and agents under a "low-risk" restricted-release regime, meaning the frontier bioresilience tooling does not ship into DeepMind's general public API. King's public framing is that release decisions are contingent on demonstrated mitigation capability, a position that aligns DeepMind more closely with Anthropic's pre-deployment testing camp than with the OpenAI/xAI velocity-first camp. "Everyone agrees we can't get this wrong."— Helen King, VP of Responsibility, Google DeepMind

Today's bioresilience launch is DeepMind's demonstration that it will operate a restricted-release regime for high-capability programs even without a formal standards backbone. It also positions DeepMind for whatever preemptive-federal-framework legislation the OpenAI-driven Great American AI Act ultimately produces, since the bioresilience program's gating architecture maps neatly onto what a federal high-risk-capability regime would likely require.

The scientific consequence is more immediate. AlphaFold-derived vaccine design pipelines already sit inside Isomorphic Labs. DeepMind did not release a technical paper alongside the launch; a bioresilience-specific benchmark and evaluation suite are promised for Q4.

Anthropic's endorsement is meaningful because both statutes carry teeth Anthropic's own frameworks do not, and Anthropic's willingness to accept those teeth undercuts the industry-wide argument that state regulation stifles innovation.
