The MI400 and Helios specifics

Su used the same keynote to publish first volume-production details on the MI400 GPU family. Each MI400 chip carries 432 gigabytes of HBM4 memory and delivers 2.9 exaflops of FP4 performance; AMD claims roughly 10 times the frontier-model training throughput of the MI300X on comparable workloads. The Helios rack platform packages MI400 accelerators into a 3-exaflop, 31-terabyte-HBM4 chassis, positioned as an open-ecosystem alternative to Nvidia's proprietary NVL72 racks. EPYC "Venice" is the industry's first x86 server processor built on TSMC's 2 nm process node and the first AMD server generation to use the Zen 6 microarchitecture with a maximum core count in the 256 range for high-density AI-adjacent inference and orchestration duty. ROCm 7 posts 3.5x higher throughput than ROCm 6 across the framework surface AMD supports. Su also previewed the 2027 MI500 as promising "1,000 times the performance of the MI300X," a claim AMD did not third-party verify and one that lines up with the same generation-over-generation slope Nvidia is quoting for Feynman.

Dario Amodei has spoken publicly about not wanting to depend on any single accelerator vendor as Claude training runs push toward multi-hundred-billion-parameter budgets. The AMD announcement is Anthropic's answer: continue Nvidia and TPU footprints for existing production, take two gigawatts of MI450 for the next training epoch, and secure the ROCm software co-development that keeps Claude portable across silicon.

"Anthropic is committed to expanding our compute capacity to scale the frontier of AI capabilities. AMD Instinct MI450 delivers exceptional price-performance and memory capacity to help us serve our rapidly growing customer base."— Krishna Rao, Anthropic CFO

The $5 billion equity mechanic

AMD's commitment to invest up to $5 billion of equity into Anthropic mirrors, in structure if not in scale, the roughly $10 billion equity component of the OpenAI-AMD arrangement announced in June. Anthropic's fund-raising history over the past twelve months (Amazon's expanded $8 billion commitment, Google's $10 billion, the Coatue-led round marking the company north of $900 billion post-money) means the $5 billion arrives into an already-well-capitalized ledger; the strategic value is the deployment guarantee, not the cash.

"AI safety won't be solved by any single company working in secret. It will be solved in the open, collaboratively."— Clément Delangue, Hugging Face CEO, joint statement

What "reduced refusals" actually means

OpenAI's evaluation methodology for ExploitGym involves lowering the model's default refusal behavior against cyber-capability probes so the eval can measure raw offensive capability rather than the composite of capability plus deployed safeguards. EU fines Google €890 million under DMA, orders Search redesign inside 60 days

TechTimes · 2026-07-23 · Source ↗

The European Commission handed Google a €890 million DMA fine Thursday, €460 million for self-preferencing rival services in Search and €430 million for restricting Play Store developers from linking to cheaper checkout outside the store. Google has 60 days to remedy the violations; non-compliance triggers periodic penalties of up to 5 percent of Alphabet's daily worldwide turnover.

The European Commission handed down its first Digital Markets Act penalty against Google on Thursday, fining Alphabet a combined €890 million across two separate DMA infringements and ordering Google to remedy both inside 60 days under threat of ongoing periodic penalty payments of up to 5 percent of Alphabet's average daily worldwide turnover. The €460 million self-preferencing fine covers Google Search ranking Google-owned properties (Flights, Hotels, Shopping, Local) more prominently than rival services, an infringement Commission staff had flagged in the Q4 2025 preliminary findings. The €430 million Play Store fine covers Google's continuing restrictions on app developers routing users to cheaper purchase paths outside the Play Store, the "anti-steering" pattern the DMA was written specifically to eliminate.

"The DMA's promise is a fair digital market where gatekeepers cannot lock in their own advantage. Today's decisions enforce that promise."— Teresa Ribera, EU Competition Commissioner

The 60-day mechanic

The DMA structure differs from the older antitrust framework in that fines are not the primary enforcement lever. The 60-day remedy deadline is: if Google has not brought Search ranking and Play Store steering into compliance by 60 days after the decision, the Commission can impose periodic penalty payments of up to 5 percent of Alphabet's daily global turnover for every day of continued non-compliance. Alphabet's Q2 2026 daily worldwide revenue works out to roughly $1.3 billion, so the maximum periodic penalty ceiling runs to about $65 million per day. Google has not committed to a compliance path publicly and typically litigates DMA decisions to the General Court in Luxembourg, but the periodic-penalty threat runs during appeal.

Google's response

Google said in a statement that both decisions "misread how our services work" and signaled it would appeal, while committing to engage with the Commission on interim remedies. The company argued that Search ranking of Google properties is often quality-driven and that Play Store steering restrictions protect users from off-platform payment fraud. The Commission's own analysis found the self-preferencing bias measurable across 1.6 million Search queries sampled between January and May, with rival vertical-search sites ranked below Google-owned equivalents in roughly 71 percent of tested queries.

The wider context

The €890 million total lands as the largest DMA penalty to date, larger than the March €600 million Apple fine and the June €550 million AliExpress penalty. It also lands two weeks before August 2, when the Commission's General-Purpose AI enforcement authority under the AI Act comes fully online for GPAI providers, which will subject Google Gemini, OpenAI GPT-5.6 and Anthropic Claude to disclosure, documentation, model-restriction and fine authority up to €35 million or 7 percent of global turnover. Thursday's DMA decision is a message from Brussels about enforcement posture ahead of that transition.

Anthropic launched the Claude Security Plugin for Claude Code in public beta on Thursday, positioning the extension as a multi-agent vulnerability scanner that runs inline in the terminal alongside a developer's Claude Code session rather than as a separate post-commit review. The stated goal is to catch security issues as they are being written instead of during a downstream code-review or CI stage, compressing the feedback loop that in most enterprise codebases opens the window in which a vulnerability can ship. The plugin sits on top of Claude Code's existing subagent architecture, spawning specialized security-focused agents (secrets scanning, injection-pattern detection, dependency vetting) that run in parallel with the main coding session and interject into the terminal when they surface a finding.

"Catch problems as they happen rather than waiting until after deployment."— Anthropic Claude Security Plugin announcement

The multi-agent scan surface

Anthropic's plugin architecture launches multiple specialized Claude subagents at session start, each carrying a scoped tool surface. The secrets subagent watches file writes for high-entropy strings, environment-variable leakage patterns and common cloud-provider key formats. The injection subagent inspects any SQL, NoSQL, shell or template generation, flagging user-input paths that skip sanitization. The dependency subagent runs against every package install or manifest edit, cross-referencing against Anthropic-maintained CVE feeds and downgrade-attack patterns. Findings are surfaced with a Claude Code-style diff-and-suggestion presentation rather than as a raw scanner report, which is the affordance the beta is testing.

Positioning vs Google CodeMender

The launch lands 48 hours after Google shipped Gemini 3.5 Flash Cyber inside CodeMender, its own multi-agent vulnerability-discovery and patching pipeline restricted to government and trusted partners. Anthropic's plugin is developer-facing rather than infrastructure-facing (CodeMender is a fabric that runs against real code repos and infrastructure, not an editor plugin). The two products are complementary rather than directly competitive, but the timing signals the frontier labs' converging bet that inference-time security review will be a base-layer capability across the Claude, Gemini, and OpenAI Codex surfaces by end of year.

Availability

The plugin is available in beta to all Claude Code subscribers on Team and Enterprise tiers from Thursday. A GA date was not committed. Anthropic did not disclose which underlying Claude model powers the security subagents but the plugin behavior matches the shape of a Claude Sonnet 5 deployment with a security-focused system prompt. The mechanism

Self Gradient Forcing works by, during training, occasionally replacing a model's teacher-forced ground-truth conditioning with the model's own prior predictions, computing the gradient of the loss with respect to those self-generated predictions rather than with respect to the ground truth. Empirically, the training-time exposure to model-generated conditioning reduces train-inference distribution shift when the model runs beyond its trained sequence length. Ten days from Thursday, the European Commission and AI Office gain full enforcement authority against general-purpose AI providers under the EU AI Act: request documentation, run technical evaluations, demand mitigation measures, restrict or withdraw a model from the EU market, and issue fines up to €35 million or 7 percent of global turnover.

The August 2 activation of the EU AI Act's general-purpose AI enforcement provisions lands in ten days. From that date the European Commission and its AI Office gain full formal authority to request documentation from GPAI providers (technical dossiers, training data summaries, safety and downstream-use analyses); run their own technical evaluations of models on the EU market; demand compliance and risk-mitigation measures; restrict or withdraw a model from the EU market where mitigation is inadequate; and issue fines up to €35 million or 7 percent of global annual turnover under Article 99. The rules were adopted August 1, 2024 but the enforcement provisions phased in on a two-year schedule; August 2, 2026 is the date the last set of provisions turns on.

Which models are in scope

The definition of a general-purpose AI model under the Act covers models trained on very large datasets with sufficient generality and capability to be adapted downstream for a wide range of tasks. In practice this covers all frontier models sold or deployed inside the EU: Google Gemini, Anthropic Claude, OpenAI GPT-5 and 5.6, xAI Grok, Meta Llama, Mistral, Cohere, DeepSeek, Qwen, Kimi, Zhipu GLM, MiniMax and the smaller instruction-tuned open-source model families. The Act distinguishes between GPAI models with systemic risk (frontier compute or evaluation thresholds) and GPAI models without systemic risk, with heavier obligations on the systemic-risk tier.

The provider obligations

Providers must publish detailed model documentation (per the Commission's May 2026 template), maintain a public copyright policy for training data, and enable the Commission's AI Office to run capability evaluations against models on the EU market. Providers of systemic-risk GPAI models must additionally perform pre-release safety evaluations, report serious incidents, protect model weights against unauthorized release, and demonstrate cybersecurity practices commensurate with the model's capabilities. The specific evaluation methodology is not yet public but the AI Office has previewed that it will draw on the UK AISI's Inspect framework.
