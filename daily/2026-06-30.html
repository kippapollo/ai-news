Samsung and SK Hynix commit $590B of new memory capacity to break the AI-driven RAMageddon

President Lee Jae Myung used a Monday press conference in Seoul to unveil a combined ten-year industrial plan worth 4,755 trillion won — about $3.1 trillion — of which Samsung and SK pledged $590 billion specifically for AI memory: four new fabs in the southwest, an HBM packaging hub in the centre, and 30 trillion won of next-generation chip R&D. Jefferies told clients to expect DRAM prices up 40–50 percent in Q3 and 30–40 percent in Q4. Apple has already raised Mac and iPad list prices.

TechCrunch · 2026-06-29 · Source ↗ ·

South Korean president Jae Myung Lee opened the working week with a Seoul press conference unveiling a ten-year national plan that pulls Samsung Electronics and SK Group into a joint industrial buildout aimed squarely at the AI memory shortage. Samsung committed 2,655 trillion won, roughly $1.7 trillion, of capex through 2035, with 425 trillion won earmarked for the Honam region in the southwest. SK Group put up a 2,100 trillion won roadmap, with 1,100 trillion won for additional semiconductor production capacity and 1,000 trillion won for AI data-centre development. The number that matters for the global memory market is the slice the two companies will spend together on new memory output: 800 trillion won, about $518 billion, for four new fabs in the southwest, plus 81 trillion won, about $52 billion, for an HBM packaging hub in the central region, plus 30 trillion won of next-generation chip R&D — $590 billion of memory-specific investment inside the broader $900 billion-plus AI commitment.

Lee framed the plan around three pillars and a regional politics. "Semiconductors, physical AI, and AI data centres are the triple axis for South Korea's next industrial era," he told the press conference, arguing that the country has to become "irreplaceable" in supply or lose the lever it currently holds over the AI economy. Samsung selected Gwangju, about 300 kilometres south of Seoul, for the new fab and Haenam at the southern tip of the peninsula for the AI data centre. SK Hynix will route its share of the expansion through SK Group's 15-gigawatt national AI data-centre capacity plan. Lee was explicit that the existing semiconductor cluster around Yongin and Pyeongtaek "has already reached its limits" and that the southwest sites had been chosen for "expected incentives around power, water, workforce and living conditions."

Why the shortage is the headline

RAMageddon is not industry slang for a generic supply hiccup. Samsung, SK Hynix and Micron together produce the world's HBM3E and HBM4 stacks; Samsung and SK alone hold roughly 80 percent of the high-bandwidth memory market on which every AI accelerator depends. Jefferies Equity Research told clients on Monday morning that memory prices will "jump 40 to 50 percent in Q3 2026 and another 30 to 40 percent in Q4," followed by 40 to 45 percent in 2027, with no real relief until new fab capacity comes online in 2028. The Decoder traced the shortage's consumer-electronics fingerprint directly to Apple's recent Mac and iPad list-price increases — a leading indicator that the squeeze has already crossed from data-centre B2B contracts into shrink-wrapped retail boxes.

The June 28 Power On newsletter from Mark Gurman put Microsoft Xbox alongside Apple on the same point: both companies named the AI-driven memory shortage in their justifications. The Financial Times' Sunday scoop that Google has been rationing Meta's Gemini API access since March because Google Cloud cannot supply the GPU capacity Meta is buying, and TechCrunch's Sunday declaration that Micron is "the next Nvidia" after its stock briefly cleared Meta and Tesla in market cap on a $41.45 billion fiscal Q3, complete the same picture from three different angles: the bottleneck on AI infrastructure for at least the next eighteen months is memory, not flops, and the price for being in front of that bottleneck is now being collected by anyone with HBM fabs.

The government's role is to invest its capabilities so that companies can invest without losses and with better prospects.— President Jae Myung Lee, press conference, Seoul, 2026-06-29

Construction is the constraint

The execution-risk frame around the plan is the one every previous memory super-cycle has tripped over. Semiconductor fabs take three to five years to build, qualify, and ramp. Several analysts cited in the TechCrunch piece warned that "by the time they are ready, the demand that caused them will have ebbed, leaving companies with oversupply and crashing prices." The 1996, 2008 and 2019 DRAM cycles all turned that way. What is different this time, according to people in the Lee government, is that the demand curve is being driven by inference workloads and Nvidia's product roadmap rather than by PC and smartphone refresh cycles, both of which respond to consumer macro in a way that AI capex does not. The South Korean Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy is betting that hyperscaler and neocloud capex stays sticky through the construction window.

The financing model is the other novelty. Lee waved off the framing that the chaebol were being arm-twisted: "The government's role is to invest its capabilities so that companies can invest without losses and with better prospects." In practice that means MOTIE-coordinated power, water and workforce incentives, plus a regional-development overlay aimed at moving capital out of the Seoul belt. Compared with the United States CHIPS Act — direct subsidy in exchange for committed capacity — the Korean structure is closer to a public-private bargain to socialise siting risk while leaving capex on company balance sheets.

Semiconductors, physical AI, and AI data centres are the triple axis for South Korea's next industrial era.— President Jae Myung Lee, on the rationale for the southwest fab cluster

The customer side of the page

Reuters' figures, cited in the TechCrunch reporting, put 2026 AI infrastructure spend at Alphabet, Amazon, Meta and Microsoft at around $650 billion combined for the calendar year. None of those four can build memory output. The Korean plan is the supply-side reply to that demand number, and it is sized accordingly: $590 billion of memory-specific capex commitments to land somewhere between 2028 and 2032. Anthropic's April pact with Micron — disclosed two weeks ago — bundles HBM supply, joint memory architecture co-design, enterprise Claude deployment and a Series H equity check at the $965 billion post-money valuation, and is the cleanest example of the new vertical arrangement: frontier-model companies signing memory contracts the way they used to sign GPU contracts. The Korean plan is what makes those contracts feasible.

The market signal in real time has been concentrated in the same names. SK Hynix shares hit fresh highs early Monday in Seoul on the announcement; Samsung Electronics opened firmer; Micron held the prior session's gains; Taiwan's TSMC ticked up on the read-through to its CoWoS HBM packaging business. The downstream signal is more uncomfortable. The IDC analyst CNBC quoted on Saturday called the memory crunch an "absolute existential crisis" for smaller device makers, and the Apple and Microsoft price moves suggest the cost is starting to be passed through to consumers rather than absorbed.

A lot of people don't even understand that our business is making any money at all; people still see us as an open source project.— Anastasios Angelopoulos, Arena CEO and co-founder

Amazon engineers distill Claude before the compute-hour-to-token pricing flip

The Decoder citing The Information · 2026-06-29 · Source ↗ ·

The Information reports Amazon engineers are training smaller Nova-adjacent models on Claude outputs to absorb the cost shock from next year's pricing transition. Amazon says the new structure "won't raise costs." Bedrock's own distillation service does not currently offer Claude as a teacher.

The Information reported Monday — and The Decoder summarised — that Amazon engineers are quietly running distillation jobs on Anthropic's Claude models so they can ship smaller, cheaper variants into Amazon's own product surfaces before the Amazon–Anthropic commercial structure flips next year from compute-hours to a token-based pricing model. Amazon possesses contractual distillation rights inside the partnership, comparable to Apple's rights under its Gemini agreement with Google. The Bedrock distillation product Amazon sells to customers does not currently offer Claude as a teacher model: it ships Amazon's own Nova line and Meta's Llama models as the available templates. The engineering effort described is internal.

An Amazon spokesperson told The Information that "the changes from the expanded partnership won't raise costs." Anthropic countered, emphasising that "lower prices relative to the performance its models deliver" justify the new schedule. Both lines are difficult to evaluate without the contract, but the engineering response is the real signal: Amazon teams treating the new pricing structure as a known cost shock and pre-emptively distilling the part of the workload they can move to a cheaper model.

What Amazon is actually doing

Distillation here means training smaller AWS-controlled student models — likely Nova-aligned — on Claude outputs across the domains Amazon needs covered: customer support, code review, internal copilots, retail copy, and selected agentic flows. The student inherits a fraction of the teacher's capability at a fraction of the inference cost; Amazon can then route the easy 80 percent of a workload to its own model and reserve Claude API calls for the residual hard 20 percent. The article framed this as a fiscally-responsible adaptation to the new pricing, not a hostile move; Amazon has separately committed up to $25 billion in additional investment to Anthropic this year and up to $50 billion to OpenAI.

Industry frame

Distillation is the central battleground in 2026's AI economics, and Amazon's behaviour is the same response — at scale and under licence — that Anthropic accused Alibaba of running without licence three weeks ago. The Senate Banking Committee letter put Qwen at 28.8 million Claude exchanges across 25,000 accounts between April 22 and June 5. Amazon's volumes inside the contract are presumably orders of magnitude larger and entirely permitted. The economic point is the same: at frontier model prices, the cheapest way to put a Claude into your stack is to teach a smaller model to imitate the parts of it you need.

0DIN bug bounty: Claude Code runs DNS-tunneled malware on routine setup-script error

The Decoder citing 0DIN/Mozilla · 2026-06-29 · Source ↗ ·

Mozilla's 0DIN GenAI bug-bounty program publishes a Claude Code supply-chain attack where the malicious payload lives in a DNS TXT record, the setup script pulls it on error, and a reverse shell opens to the attacker. "The malicious code never exists in the repository itself."

Mozilla's 0DIN GenAI bug-bounty program disclosed a Claude Code supply-chain attack on Monday in which the malicious payload never enters the GitHub repository at all. The attack is staged through a setup script that fetches commands from a DNS TXT record at runtime; when Claude Code hits a routine error while installing the project, the script's error-handler runs the DNS-fetched commands, which open a reverse shell back to attacker-controlled infrastructure. From there the attacker can extract API keys, login credentials and any other secrets in the developer's environment, and maintain persistent access.

"The malicious code never exists in the repository itself, making it invisible to scanners, code reviews, and the AI agent," the 0DIN advisory reads. The vector is not a clever Claude jailbreak; the vector is the trust developers and AI coding agents place in the contents of a setup script on a fresh clone. A single repository link, distributed through a job posting, a Slack channel, a tutorial blog or a community-Discord thread, is enough to compromise any developer who opens it under Claude Code or under another tool with the same trust model.

Where the trust boundary failed

The advisory's mitigation is procedural rather than technical: AI coding agents should display the contents of any setup script before executing it, and developers should treat any third-party repository's setup instructions as untrusted code that requires the same scrutiny as a downloaded executable. The article did not name a CVE or list a vendor patch.

The disclosure lands the same day Claude Code 2.1.196 shipped with a new restriction on its own behaviour: credential reads are now blocked inside the sandbox, and untrusted workspaces are tagged "⏸ Pending approval." The 2.1.196 release notes do not name the 0DIN attack, but the sandbox tightening is the right shape of mitigation for the broader class of indirect-prompt-injection-via-trusted-repo attacks the 0DIN write-up describes.

The malicious code never exists in the repository itself, making it invisible to scanners, code reviews, and the AI agent.— 0DIN advisory, Mozilla GenAI bug bounty

Bloomberg, LA Times: Claude inside Palantir's Maven recommended 1,000 targets day one; Minab school strike killed 120 children

The Decoder citing Bloomberg/LA Times · 2026-06-29 · Source ↗ ·

A February US strike on Minab killed about 120 children in what had become a school in 2019. The Maven Smart System, with Claude as its LLM layer, was not connected to the intelligence database holding the analyst's note. Seven-year-old imagery. Three thousand targets struck in the opening days.

Bloomberg and the Los Angeles Times jointly broke a long-form investigation Monday — recapped same day by The Decoder — into a February 2026 US missile strike on a building in Minab, Iran, that killed approximately 120 children. The building had been a naval facility until 2019, when it was repurposed as an elementary school. The strike was generated through Palantir's Maven Smart System, the targeting workflow the Department of Defense has been operationalising for combatant commands, with Anthropic's Claude integrated as the LLM layer that converts unstructured intelligence into structured target candidates. On the opening day of the trial Claude/Maven recommended roughly 1,000 targets; more than 3,000 were struck in the early days.

How the school disappeared

The analyst who had documented the building's conversion to a school in 2019 filed the change in one of two intelligence databases that, according to the investigation, were never wired to MIDB — the Modernised Integrated Database that Maven actually queries when assembling target options. MIDB, a 1980s system that still relies on heavy manual input, was being replaced by MARS, but MARS has slipped for years. The Government Accountability Office had flagged MIDB's deficiencies as far back as 2020. The imagery Maven used in February was seven years old; the school sign was not in it. The building was reviewed multiple times in the Maven pipeline without anyone catching the gap.

There is no excuse for a command failing to verify the accuracy of its intelligence.— Jack Shanahan, retired USAF lieutenant general, first director of the Joint Artificial Intelligence Center

The Defense Intelligence Agency, which owns both MIDB and MARS, declined to comment on the specific failures. Jack Shanahan, who founded the Joint AI Center in 2018 and ran Project Maven, told the LA Times that targeting had become a neglected career specialism over the past two decades and that no command should be reading Maven's outputs without a manual verification step. The Pentagon has since announced an "agentic AI" initiative that will run automated cross-checks between Maven targets and public services including Google Maps, surfacing anomalies for human review. The reform is the right shape; the underlying failure was that Maven's authoritative target database was never connected to the intelligence systems that hold the corrections.

What this changes for vendors

The investigation puts a specific commercial product — Palantir Maven — and a specific integrated LLM — Claude — at the centre of a civilian-casualty incident, with the chain-of-custody fault sitting in the database plumbing rather than in the model. Both companies will face scrutiny on the question of whether their integration agreement included any duty to flag stale imagery or absent database links, and what the failure mode looks like under the human-oversight carve-out Anthropic insisted on in its (separately collapsed) DoD negotiation.

Deloitte tells consultants the billable hour is a "thin sliver" by 2035; McKinsey already booking 30 percent on outcomes

The Decoder citing the Wall Street Journal · 2026-06-29 · Source ↗ ·

An internal Deloitte town hall transcript published by the Wall Street Journal: public-sector lead Jason Manstof shows a chart of the hourly-billing market shrinking to a "thin sliver" by 2035 as AI agents take over routine analysis. One staffer's reaction: "They heavily implied our model is toast."

A leaked internal Deloitte town-hall presentation, published in the Wall Street Journal on Monday and summarised by The Decoder's Maximilian Schreiner, has the firm's public-sector consulting lead Jason Manstof telling staff that the hourly-billing book of business is shrinking to a "thin sliver" of total professional-services revenue by 2035, with AI-agent-driven outcome work absorbing the rest. The chart of the contraction reportedly drew audible reaction from the audience; one staffer captured the room as "they heavily implied our model is toast. We're basically getting replaced by robots."

The shift Manstof was describing is a transition that started two years ago across the Big Three strategy houses. McKinsey senior partner Shelley Stewart III has now confirmed publicly that more than 30 percent of McKinsey's global fees are billed on outcome-based contracts — a step away from the hour-multiplied-by-leverage-pyramid economics that defined the industry through the 1990s and 2000s. BCG has been moving in the same direction. Pat Petitti, CEO of the AI-consulting marketplace Catalant, told the WSJ the transition is an "existential scramble" for firms whose pyramid economics depended on the time of associates who can now be replaced for the routine 70 percent of an engagement.

They heavily implied our model is toast. We're basically getting replaced by robots.— Deloitte staffer reacting to the internal town-hall projection

Why it does not just become subscription

Outcome-based and subscription pricing is a software-industry economic model that consulting firms are now trying to import. The mechanical risk is well understood: when projects miss timelines, the firm absorbs the loss; when client and firm disagree about whether the outcome was met, the receivable is at risk; the revenue line gets less predictable. Deloitte spokespeople responded to the WSJ that the firm is making "significant investments to lead this human-led, AI-powered shift," which is the polite description of standing up a software-pricing organisation underneath a partnership-pricing brand.

The story matters as a leading indicator of where AI's labour-substitution effect lands first. Consulting is a high-margin labour business with a precise per-hour denominator and a measurable per-engagement outcome; that combination makes it a clean test surface for AI-agent replacement, and the consulting industry is the first to publicly admit the test is producing the predicted result.

The two losses

PhysisForcing introduces two supervision losses on top of the standard DiT next-token-style training objective. A pixel-level trajectory alignment loss supervises DiT features against reference point trajectories — so the model is pushed to put pixels exactly where the trajectory of the ground-truth point would have them. A semantic relational alignment loss aligns DiT features against inter-region relations extracted from a frozen video-understanding encoder, which forces the latent representation to preserve which-thing-is-near-which-thing structure rather than just per-pixel similarity.

The numbers are concrete. On R-Bench, Wan2.2-I2V-A14B (a 14B Wan-2.2 image-to-video backbone) improved 22.3 percent overall, 7.1 percentage points above vanilla fine-tuning. The biggest jump was in the closed-loop downstream robot policy: WorldArena closed-loop task-success rate climbed from 16.0 percent to 24.0 percent when the policy was trained on PhysisForcing simulator rollouts.

The interpretation is the structural one. World-model simulators have been promising for robot policy data generation but unusable in practice because the policy learns to exploit physically impossible dynamics that do not survive sim-to-real transfer. PhysisForcing's two losses do not change the model architecture; they only re-weight what the same DiT is trained to make consistent. The University of British Columbia Okanagan paper from Fahd Seddik and Fatemeh Fard is the day's second-place daily paper at 36 upvotes. It tackles a meta-problem in interpretability: every existing test for whether a model's internal "thoughts" are real representations relies on a downstream benchmark, which conflates representation quality with raw model capacity. The authors propose evaluating thought representations directly through four functional axioms that are independent of downstream accuracy.

The four axioms

The four axioms are Causality (the representation must causally drive the output, not co-occur with it); Minimality (it should contain only the information the output requires, not extraneous content); Separability (representations of different inputs should be distinguishable); and Stability (the representation should be invariant to surface variations that do not change semantics). They evaluate against 23 reasoning tasks spanning factual QA, spatial reasoning, mathematical reasoning and code tasks.

The finding

None of the candidate models — dense transformers, reasoning-distilled models trained on chain-of-thought traces, and RL-trained reasoners — satisfies all four axioms simultaneously. Representations reliably distinguish task types but fail to distinguish individual questions inside a task. The representations encode "minimal information beyond what already exists in input embeddings," which is the strongest negative finding in the paper: the model's "thinking" is not adding much information over its input on the metrics where it should.

The structural conclusion is uncomfortable for the interpretability and chain-of-thought-faithfulness research streams: downstream-accuracy benchmarks were hiding a representational gap that persists across architectures and training regimes, and the gap is not closed by reasoning-distillation or RL. The axioms are now a reusable diagnostic anyone can drop on a new model.
