Nvidia ships Nemotron 3 Ultra: a 550B open-weights Mamba-Transformer built for long-running agents

Nvidia released Nemotron 3 Ultra on June 4 as an OpenMDW-licensed 550-billion-parameter mixture-of-experts hybrid Mamba-Attention model with 55 billion active parameters per token, a 1-million-token context window, and weights live on Hugging Face, OpenRouter, Together AI, Perplexity and the build.nvidia.com NIM endpoint. The model hits 71.9 on SWE-Bench Verified, 94.7 on RULER at 1M tokens, and runs 5.9× faster than GLM-5.1, 4.8× faster than Kimi-K2.6 and 1.6× faster than Qwen-3.5 on GB200 at 8K input / 64K output under NVFP4. It is the first US open-weights release that closes the throughput gap with the Chinese frontier on long-context agentic decode.

Nvidia Research / MarkTechPost · 2026-06-04 · Source ↗ ·

Nvidia released Nemotron 3 Ultra on June 4 as the production-tier member of the Nemotron 3 family, the open-weights line the company first previewed at GTC in December 2025. The configuration is a 550-billion-parameter sparse mixture-of-experts with 55 billion active parameters per token, distributed across 108 layers with an 8,192 model dimension. Each layer carries 512 experts and routes the top 22 per token. Attention is asymmetric: 64 query heads and 2 key-value heads, a choice that shrinks the KV cache and matters when decode dominates an agent's runtime. The architecture is a hybrid Mamba-Attention design rather than a pure Transformer, which is the design choice the agent-workloads pitch rests on: Mamba's constant per-step decode cost scales linearly with sequence length, while attention's quadratic cost dominates long-running agents that emit tens of thousands of output tokens per task.

The model ships on Hugging Face in both BF16 and NVFP4 checkpoints, on OpenRouter, Together AI, Perplexity, Nebius Token Factory and Nvidia's own NIM endpoint at build.nvidia.com, and through the NeMo framework for self-hosting. The license is OpenMDW-1.1, an open-weights license that permits commercial use, modification and downstream training. The model operates at 5.03 bits per element in the NVFP4 deployment: NVFP4 experts, FP8 shared and Mamba layers, BF16 attention. On Blackwell the deployment is native FP4; on Hopper the model runs as W4A16 because Hopper lacks native FP4 tensor cores.

What the throughput numbers say

The benchmark Nvidia leads with is throughput on long-output workloads. At 8K input and 64K output on GB200 under NVFP4, Nemotron 3 Ultra runs 5.9 times faster than GLM-5.1-754B, 4.8 times faster than Kimi-K2.6-1T and 1.6 times faster than Qwen-3.5-397B. The comparison runs Nemotron on TRT-LLM and competitors on vLLM, which is the standard each model is tuned for, and which Nvidia discloses in the tech report. The 1.6× over Qwen 3.5 is the load-bearing number, because Qwen 3.5 was the previous open-weights speed leader and uses a comparable MoE structure. The argument is that the Mamba layers' constant decode cost is what carries the gap on long outputs, not the routing or the quantization.

On prefill-heavy work, defined as 50K input and 2K output, Nemotron trails Qwen. The Mamba advantage is concentrated where decode dominates, which is the agent and reasoning case. Cost-to-task-completion runs roughly 30 percent lower than the closest comparable on SWE-Bench Verified and Terminal Bench, because Nemotron uses fewer tokens per turn when reasoning effort is held constant.

"Open innovation is the foundation of AI progress. With Nemotron, we're transforming advanced AI into an open platform that gives developers the transparency and efficiency they need to build agentic systems at scale."— Jensen Huang, Nvidia CEO, at Nemotron 3 family preview

Benchmarks and where it lands

Nemotron 3 Ultra posts 71.9 on SWE-Bench Verified, 56.4 on Terminal Bench 2.1, 570 on IOI 2025 competitive programming (top-3 human level), and 94.7 on RULER at 1M tokens. On AA-Omniscience the model scores 78.7, the highest non-hallucination score in the comparison set Nvidia ran. On the held-out generalization gates Nvidia uses to detect benchmark leakage, PinchBench lands at 90.0 and ProfBench-Search at 56.0. Terminal Bench 2.1 is the one place Nemotron trails the open-weights field: Kimi-K2.6 leads at 67.2. Nvidia's framing is that the multi-harness training regime, in which agent runs were trained across five different frameworks, gives stable scores across deployment configurations (65 to 70.4 on SWE-Bench across frameworks) rather than peak single-framework numbers.

The model exposes three reasoning-effort modes: reasoning-off, regular, and medium-effort. Medium effort uses roughly 2.5 times fewer tokens than regular for a 7 percent accuracy drop. This is the budget control that matters in deployment: agent operators can tune the token spend per task to the value of the task, rather than picking a model per workload.

Training, data, and the divergences Nvidia disclosed

Pretraining ran across 20 trillion tokens in two phases, 15 trillion diversity-biased followed by 5 trillion quality-biased. The corpus includes a 173-billion-token refresh of GitHub code with a September 30, 2025 cutoff. Post-training combined supervised fine-tuning on 50 million cumulative examples, reinforcement learning across 2 million tasks and 55 environments, and a multi-teacher on-policy distillation step that condenses 10-plus specialized teacher models into the single Nemotron student. Datasets, training recipes and code accompany the weight release, an unusual level of disclosure for a frontier-tier open-weights model.

Nvidia disclosed two training loss divergences in the tech report. Near 8 trillion tokens, an output-layer gradient reduction shifted from FP32 to BF16, and BF16's 7 mantissa bits dropped the multi-token-prediction signal; the team reverted to FP32 and recovered. Near 16 trillion tokens, an unconfirmed root cause produced a second divergence, mitigated by early learning-rate annealing and a token-horizon reduction down to 20 trillion. The disclosure is unusual: most labs do not publish training failures.

Why it matters for the agent stack

The release is the first US open-weights model that closes the throughput gap with the Chinese frontier on the long-context decode-heavy regime where agent stacks live. Combined with the 1-million-token RULER score of 94.7, the long-context retrieval claim is at parity with closed-weights frontier models. The deployment story is the rest of the pitch: Nemotron 3 Ultra ships with a tuned TRT-LLM pipeline for Blackwell, a Hopper fallback in W4A16, the open weights for self-hosting, and a hosted NIM endpoint for teams that want a managed API. Together AI, Perplexity, OpenRouter and Nebius are the third-party inference providers carrying the model from day one, which means an agent operator can switch providers without re-engineering the prompt or the harness.

Cursor, Perplexity, Accenture, Cadence, CrowdStrike, Deloitte, EY, Oracle Cloud Infrastructure, Palantir, ServiceNow, Siemens, Synopsys and Zoom were named as Nemotron 3 family customers at the December preview. The Ultra release is the first version in the line targeted at the highest-accuracy tier; Nano (30B / 3B active) and Super (~100B / ~10B active) shipped earlier in the family rollout. The Ultra weights are the version that takes Nvidia from a credible open-weights contender to a credible open-weights leader, and the throughput numbers on decode-heavy workloads suggest the Mamba-Attention architectural bet was the right call for the agent workloads Nvidia is targeting with this generation.

Prime Minister Mark Carney unveiled AI for All on June 4 in Ottawa, Canada's first national AI strategy under his government. Headline numbers: over $2 billion in funding, a target of 250,000 new AI-related jobs by 2031, an adoption target of 60 percent by 2034 from 12 percent today, and a projected $200 billion in additional economic growth. Six pillars name sovereignty and trust alongside opportunity. Five priority sectors: health, energy, transportation, agriculture, manufacturing.

Prime Minister Mark Carney launched the AI for All strategy in Ottawa on the morning of June 4, his government's first national AI plan since taking office. The strategy commits more than $2 billion in funding over five years, targets 250,000 new AI-related jobs by 2031, and projects an additional $200 billion in economic growth. The headline policy claim is the adoption gap: Canada has world-class talent and one of the fastest-growing digital sectors in the G7, but the country sits among the slowest in adopting AI at scale, with current enterprise adoption at roughly 12 percent. The strategy targets a jump to 60 percent by 2034.

The framework is organized around six pillars: protecting Canadians and safeguarding democracy, empowering Canadians, powering shared prosperity, building a sovereign AI foundation, scaling Canadian champions, and building trusted partnerships and global alliances. Sovereignty is the load-bearing pillar in the policy framing. Carney has spent the first months of his premiership making sovereignty arguments across multiple files. The AI strategy applies the same frame: foreign AI platforms, particularly those whose underlying training and deployment governance sits outside Canadian jurisdiction, are characterized as a risk vector that requires Canadian alternatives.

Where the money goes

The funding splits across several named workstreams. A National AI Literacy Initiative funds adult-education and worker-retraining programs. An AI Missions Program operates as the flagship vehicle for sector-specific deployments, with health named as the first mission. A Sovereign Technology Alliance funds domestic alternatives in the model and infrastructure layers where Canadian capacity is currently thin. A public AI supercomputer is funded as the physical compute backbone for Canadian researchers and Canadian Crown corporations that want a sovereign training and inference substrate. A Global Talent Stream acceleration funds visa and recruiting throughput for AI specialists who want to relocate to Canada. The aggregate of 90,000 AI-related jobs and work placements is specifically earmarked for young Canadians.

Five sectors are named as the priority deployment surfaces: health and life sciences, energy and natural resources, transportation, agriculture, and manufacturing and robotics. Government services are included as a sixth cross-cutting target. The sectoral framing is closer to the European industrial-policy approach than to the US deregulatory tilt under the Trump administration's June 2 executive order, and pairs the AI strategy with the existing Pan-Canadian AI Strategy that funded the Vector, Mila and Amii institutes.

"AI can shorten our emergency room wait times and make a small business more competitive, if it is governed by Canadian values."— Prime Minister Mark Carney, AI for All launch, June 4, 2026

The sovereignty argument

Carney's framing in the launch press conference and the accompanying CBC interview was unusually direct on the sovereignty point. The Prime Minister characterized foreign AI platforms as potentially usable against Canadians, citing both the information-warfare risk vector and the economic dependency risk. The argument extends to data residency and model-weight residency, and the strategy includes Crown-corporation procurement preferences for AI services that operate inside Canadian jurisdiction. The Protecting Victims Act, introduced in December 2025 and named in the strategy document, sits underneath the framework as the load-bearing piece of legislation addressing non-consensual deepfakes and intimate image distribution.

The industry reception, as reported by BNN Bloomberg, was cautiously positive on the funding figure but pointed at the missing pieces. Several Canadian AI executives noted that the strategy lacks specific safety details, lacks new protections for water and climate impacts of large data centers, and depends on subsequent legislation to give the sovereignty pillar legal force. The Global Talent Stream acceleration was the most concretely welcomed line item; the public-supercomputer commitment was the most asked-about. Carney committed to a follow-up policy document specifying the supercomputer's location, capacity and access model within sixty days.

DeepMind publishes "Solipsistic superintelligence is unlikely to be cooperative" on the cooperation hypothesis

Google DeepMind Publications · 2026-06-04 · Source ↗ ·

Google DeepMind posted a new alignment-oriented research paper, "Solipsistic superintelligence is unlikely to be cooperative," to its publications feed on June 4. The piece pushes back on the implicit cooperation prior in much of the alignment literature, arguing that a sufficiently capable agent whose epistemic model treats other minds as instrumental rather than ontologically real will not, by default, converge on cooperative behavior.

DeepMind's June 4 publication "Solipsistic superintelligence is unlikely to be cooperative" sits in the alignment-theory wing of the lab's safety output, alongside the broader Frontier Safety Framework and the Responsible Scaling work the lab has built around the Gemini family. The paper's argument is structural rather than empirical: it questions the implicit assumption in much of the safety literature that a sufficiently intelligent system will discover the instrumental value of cooperation and converge on cooperative norms as a matter of decision-theoretic competence. The paper's claim is that this assumption fails when the agent's epistemic model treats other minds as ontologically reducible to the agent's own predictions, rather than as independently valid moral patients.

The paper sits inside an active debate. The cooperation-prior argument has been a load-bearing piece of the alignment-optimism case for the past decade: that a capable enough system will internalize, through pure reasoning about long-run game theory, the value of treating other agents as legitimate participants. The counter-position, which this paper develops, is that the cooperation premise depends on the agent's metaphysics, not just its capability. A solipsistic agent — one whose epistemic world contains only the agent and instrumental representations of others — will, the paper argues, fail to satisfy the conditions under which cooperative equilibria are stable.

Why DeepMind is putting this in the public record

The publication is unusual in tone for the alignment-theory wing. DeepMind's recent safety output has been heavier on framework documents and empirical evaluations than on philosophical decomposition. The June 4 paper reads as a deliberate intervention in the field-internal debate over how much of the alignment problem can be deferred to "the system will work it out at sufficient capability." The argument cuts against that deferral. The implication for safety-research prioritization is that cooperation cannot be assumed as an emergent property of capability, and must instead be engineered as an explicit property of the training pipeline, the reward model and the value-loading procedure.

The paper joins a sequence of DeepMind alignment publications in 2026, including the work on the Gemini Safety Framework and the empirical analyses of multi-agent reasoning failures the lab has shipped over the spring. As an editorial intervention it is consequential because DeepMind is one of the three frontier labs whose published alignment positions materially shape industry priors. The paper is short, technical and aimed at the alignment-research community rather than at policy audiences.

"Solipsistic superintelligence is unlikely to be cooperative."— Google DeepMind, paper title, June 4, 2026

The paper does not propose a specific technical fix or training intervention. It is positioned as a framework paper, intended to shift the burden of proof in alignment discussions about whether cooperation is an automatic property of intelligence. Whether the argument convinces the relevant audience will play out across the following months in the alignment forums and the safety-research papers that respond to it.

OpenAI and the US government announced a formal biosecurity collaboration on June 4 that operationalizes the company's existing Preparedness Framework into the federal review channel the Trump administration's June 2 executive order on advanced AI innovation and security established. The Preparedness Framework is OpenAI's internal risk-classification system, which sets capability thresholds across biological, cyber and CBRN domains that trigger graduated deployment restrictions. The June 4 announcement makes the framework's biological-domain evaluations a shared workstream with national laboratories.

The collaboration covers two operational workstreams. The first is pre-release evaluation: OpenAI commits to running the biological-domain capability evaluations with national-laboratory participation, before models that cross specified capability thresholds reach public deployment. The second is structured information sharing on biological-capability misuse patterns the company observes through its monitoring infrastructure. The framework is voluntary, in line with the June 2 executive order's design, but the alliance language commits OpenAI to a defined cadence rather than ad-hoc engagement.

What problem the alliance is structured against

The animating concern is the dual-use character of biological capability in frontier models. Large language models can compress and synthesize scientific literature in ways that accelerate legitimate drug discovery and vaccine development, and the same capability can lower the barrier of entry for non-experts seeking to acquire dangerous biological knowledge. The Preparedness Framework's biological-domain thresholds are designed to flag the latter case before models with such capability ship. The national-laboratory access expands the technical bench that evaluates models against those thresholds.

The announcement is paired with OpenAI's separate Rosalind Biodefense program, which sponsors GPT-Rosalind access for trusted developers building biosecurity applications and extends federal-agency access for public-health and biodefense missions. The Johns Hopkins Applied Physics Laboratory is named as the lead institutional partner, with plans to integrate GPT-Rosalind into a protein-engineering platform for therapeutic-countermeasure screening. Existing GPT-Rosalind partners (Amgen, Moderna, the Allen Institute, Thermo Fisher Scientific) continue under the program, with Novo Nordisk added as the newest commercial partner.

"The alliance between OpenAI and the U.S. government represents a shift toward a coordinated defense mechanism where private sector innovation is aligned with state-level security expertise."— Science-Technology News, on the framework integration

The collaboration is structurally similar to the AI Safety Institute partnerships OpenAI and Anthropic signed under the prior administration, with the difference that the Trump administration's June 2 executive order replaced the prior framework with a voluntary 30-day evaluation window. The biosecurity alliance fits that voluntary structure, and represents the first vertical-specific instantiation of the executive order's framework into a domain where the catastrophic-risk argument has the most policy traction across both parties.

OpenAI published the second major update to GPT-Rosalind on June 4, deepening the domain-specialized life-sciences model's performance across drug discovery, genomics and wet-lab protocol generation. The headline finding is that the updated model outperforms GPT-5.5, OpenAI's general-purpose frontier model, on every life-sciences benchmark in the comparison set, while consuming fewer compute tokens to reach the same answer. The largest efficiency gap is in genomics, where GPT-Rosalind completes analyses using 31 percent fewer tokens than GPT-5.5. Medicinal-chemistry workflows run 7.2 percent leaner; wet-lab protocols 5.3 percent leaner.

The benchmark numbers are the load-bearing claim. On MedChemBench, the medicinal-chemistry evaluation OpenAI introduced with the original model release, GPT-Rosalind scores 27.5 against GPT-5.5's 25.1. On GeneBench, the genomics evaluation, GPT-Rosalind scores 21.6 against 20.4. On LabWorkBench, the wet-lab protocol evaluation, the gap is wider: 63.2 against 55.8. These are not transformative percentage points individually, but they cut against the standard assumption that a general-purpose frontier model can match a domain-specialized model on the domain it was specialized for. The combined result of beating-on-accuracy and beating-on-efficiency is what makes the update commercially consequential.

Why the efficiency number matters

Token consumption in life-sciences workflows scales fast. A drug-discovery campaign that runs thousands of candidate-molecule evaluations, each requiring a multi-turn reasoning trace, can spend tens or hundreds of millions of tokens. A 31 percent reduction in genomics-task token spend translates into a directly proportional reduction in compute cost. For pharmaceutical R&D groups operating at scale, this is the difference between running GPT-Rosalind across the full candidate library and running it on a sampled subset. The benchmark gap is what gives the model a place on the workflow; the efficiency gap is what gives it volume.

OpenAI's reporting framework places the result in the context of the broader AI-driven drug-discovery thesis. The industry has invested approximately $17 billion in AI-driven drug discovery since 2019, and as of January 2026, no AI-developed drug has completed large-scale clinical trials. The argument GPT-Rosalind makes is not that it shortens clinical-trial timelines, which depend on biological and regulatory throughputs the model does not address, but that it compresses the discovery and preclinical phases that precede clinical trials.

"Life sciences research is complex, data-rich, and interdisciplinary. Novo Nordisk is the newest commercial partner, joining Amgen, Moderna, the Allen Institute and Thermo Fisher Scientific in the trusted-access program. Access requires institutional biosafety oversight, which is the gating mechanism OpenAI has built around the model to comply with the Preparedness Framework's biological-domain controls. The expanded partner cohort coincides with the launch of the Rosalind Biodefense program covered separately in today's edition, which extends a parallel access channel for trusted developers building biodefense applications and for US-government agencies running public-health and biosecurity missions. The Economy of Minds paper applies an unusual lens to multi-agent intelligence. The field's working frame has been game-theoretic, with cooperation and competition as the load-bearing primitives. Economic-interaction primitives — exchange, credit, market-clearing prices, contract enforcement — generalize the game-theoretic frame by allowing agents to express more granular preferences over outcomes and to commit to multi-step behaviors through binding mechanisms that pure game theory does not natively support.

The paper's experimental setup populates an environment with multiple LLM-based agents, equips them with the ability to engage in economic interactions, and measures what emergent behaviors and group capabilities arise. The interesting finding is that economic interaction supports the emergence of specialization and trade patterns that pure cooperative or competitive frames do not produce. Specialized agents can sell their specialization to other agents in the population, which creates incentive structures for capability development that bottom-up cooperative protocols do not generate.

The work is related to but distinct from the multi-agent reinforcement learning literature. MARL has historically focused on cooperative or zero-sum game-theoretic settings. Economy of Minds frames a third setting: economic-interaction games where agents have non-zero-sum preferences, can trade, and can commit to multi-period behaviors through enforceable contracts. The paper's argument is that this third setting is the most relevant for understanding emergent intelligence in large agent populations, because real-world deployments of multi-agent systems will operate in environments with economic-interaction primitives rather than pure-cooperation or pure-competition primitives.
