Anthropic ships Claude Sonnet 5 with a mid-tier that edges Opus 4.8 on knowledge work

The new default at claude.ai and Claude Code lands at $2 input and $10 output per million tokens through August 31, hits 63.2 percent on SWE-bench Pro and 80.4 percent on Terminal-Bench 2.1, and scores 1,618 on GDPval-AA v2 against Opus 4.8's 1,615. Simon Willison measures the new tokenizer producing roughly 1.4 times more tokens for identical English input, cancelling the headline discount on English workloads.

Anthropic · 2026-06-30 · Source ↗ ·

Anthropic released Claude Sonnet 5 on Tuesday morning and pushed it to the default seat at claude.ai for free and Pro users, inside Claude Code, and across Cursor, VS Code, GitHub Copilot and the raw API. The model landed with introductory pricing of $2 per million input tokens and $10 per million output tokens through August 31, 2026, then reverts to standard rates of $3 input and $15 output in September. Context window: 1,000,000 tokens. Output window: 128,000 tokens. Training cutoff: January 2026. Adaptive thinking is on by default and cannot be turned off through the Claude Code CLI, only through per-organization allowance limits.

The benchmark board is where Anthropic wanted the coverage focused. Sonnet 5 scored 63.2 percent on SWE-bench Pro against Sonnet 4.6's 58.1 and GPT-5.5's 58.6, trailing Opus 4.8 at 69.2. On Terminal-Bench 2.1 the model reached 80.4 percent, up from Sonnet 4.6's 67.0, though GPT-5.5 still leads at 83.4. OSWorld-Verified came in at 78.5 percent no-tools and 81.2 with tools, matching Sonnet 4.6's baseline. On Humanity's Last Exam with tools, Sonnet 5 scored 57.4 percent, close to Opus 4.8's 57.9. The one number Anthropic keeps quoting is the GDPval-AA v2 knowledge-work index: Sonnet 5 at 1,618, Opus 4.8 at 1,615. That is the first time a Sonnet has crossed an Opus line on any of Anthropic's public knowledge-work benchmarks.

It can make plans, use tools like browsers and terminals, and run autonomously at a level that, just a few months ago, required larger and more expensive models.— Anthropic, Sonnet 5 launch post

The tokenizer footnote

Simon Willison published his usual same-day measurement of a new Anthropic model on Tuesday afternoon and found the news the launch page did not surface. Sonnet 5 ships with a new tokenizer that produces roughly 30 percent more tokens for identical text than Sonnet 4.6. His measurements: 1.42 times as many tokens for the English text of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, 1.33 times for Spanish, 1.27 times for a 4,279-line Python file, and 1.01 times for Simplified Mandarin. English workloads pay Sonnet 4.6 economics for equivalent prose even at the $2/$10 promo rate. Willison also reported that Sonnet 5 mistook the pelican in his standard pelican-on-a-bicycle SVG evaluation for a goose. Anthropic also removed the temperature, top_p, and top_k sampling parameters from the API and told developers to use adaptive thinking instead.

The same input text produces approximately 30% more tokens than on Claude Sonnet 4.6. Effectively a 30% price increase.— Simon Willison, simonwillison.net, 2026-06-30

A model that knows when to say no is just as important as one that knows how to build.— Fabian Hedin, Lovable co-founder, in the Anthropic testimonials

DeepSeek open-sources DSpark speculative-decoding for 85 percent per-user speedup and 661 percent throughput

The Decoder · 2026-06-30 · Source ↗ ·

The DSpark drafter drafts multi-word chunks and lets the target model batch-verify them, with adaptive verification depth tuned to fleet load. It outperforms EAGLE3 and DFlash across math, code and chat, works on Gemma and Qwen not just DeepSeek, and ships under MIT license.

DeepSeek released DSpark on Tuesday, an inference-side framework designed to extract more tokens per second from the Chinese hardware still lagging Nvidia H200 and Blackwell on raw memory bandwidth. The framework is MIT-licensed on GitHub and Hugging Face and shipped the same week Beijing kept insisting that inference cost, not compute cost, is the binding constraint under the June 12 export controls. The Decoder's Matthias Bastian tracked both the technical detail and the political frame.

The mechanism: a small drafter proposes token candidates, a larger target model verifies them, and the framework batches those verifications rather than doing them one token at a time. DSpark's twist is that the drafter proposes multi-word chunks rather than single tokens, and the verification depth adjusts on the fly based on the confidence signal and current compute load. When the fleet is quiet, DSpark verifies deeper drafts; when it's under load, shorter drafts keep queue times low.

Most LLMs generate text one word at a time. That leads to low GPU utilization and long wait times for lengthy responses.— DeepSeek, DSpark paper

The numbers

DeepSeek's paper reports per-user response-time gains of 60 to 85 percent and throughput improvements of up to 661 percent. Tests ran on DeepSeek-V4-Flash and DeepSeek-V4-Pro. Beyond DeepSeek's own models, the framework was validated on Google DeepMind's Gemma and Alibaba's Qwen. That cross-family reach matters: the drafter design generalizes across architectures rather than binding to one. DSpark outperformed EAGLE3 and DFlash, the two well-known open speculative-decoding drafters, across math, code and chat benchmarks.

DSpark enables performance tiers that were previously unattainable, shifting the Pareto frontier.— DeepSeek, DSpark paper

The MIT license is aggressive. Anyone can ship DSpark inside a commercial product without strings. DeepSeek released DeepSeek-V4-Pro weights and inference stack together, which puts a frontier open-weight coding model plus a 60 to 85 percent effective throughput gain on Hugging Face for anyone to serve. Layered on top of Meituan's LongCat-2.0, the same-day open-weight aggregate picture in China's ecosystem shifted meaningfully.

Bastian raised the economic point that freed-up compute rarely stays free. In practice, cheaper tokens get absorbed immediately by longer contexts, more agentic loops, or additional users. DSpark is likely to accelerate end-user features that had previously assumed frontier-model quality was available only from Anthropic and OpenAI at scale. The tight coupling to llama.cpp is separate work: b9829-b9835's DFlash v2 sliding-window-per-layer-types has already begun landing in Ollama and the broader inference-runtime stack.

Hugging Face Daily Papers ranks Agentic Abstention #1 with 120 upvotes, Intern Science 35B Agents-A1 at 68

Hugging Face · 2026-06-30 · Source ↗ ·

University of Washington, Leeds and Allen AI's "Agentic Abstention" benchmark spans 28,000 tasks and shows larger models sometimes abstain worse. Tsinghua's LiveEdit hits 12.66 FPS streaming video edits at state of the art quality. Intern Science's Agents-A1 posts leading scores on SEAL-0, IFBench, HiPhO and FrontierScience-Olympiad from a 35B MoE.

Hugging Face's Daily Papers leaderboard landed on a shape worth noting Tuesday: the top slot is a safety-flavored abstention paper, the number-three slot is an architectural counter to raw parameter scaling, and the number-five slot is threshold-free inference-side compression. The community vote pattern is pushback on the "just scale it" narrative.

At 120 upvotes, Han Luo (Leeds and Southwest Jiaotong University), Bingbing Wen (University of Washington) and Lucy Lu Wang (UW and Allen AI) posted "Agentic Abstention: Do Agents Know When to Stop Instead of Act?" The benchmark is 28,000 tasks across web shopping, terminal environments and question answering. The authors evaluated 13 LLM systems and two agent scaffolds. Headline result: bigger models sometimes get worse at timely abstention, not better. Their scaffold-side intervention, Convolve, needs no parameter updates and raised Llama-3.3-70B's timely-recall rate from 26.7 percent to 57.4 percent.

A reliable agent should recognize that further interaction is unlikely to help and abstain from additional tool calls.— Luo, Wen, Wang, Agentic Abstention abstract

LiveEdit and Agents-A1

Second at 70 upvotes, Tsinghua's LiveEdit ships a real-time diffusion-based streaming video editor. A three-stage distillation pipeline converts a bidirectional foundation into a unidirectional streaming editor and adds an AR-oriented mask cache that reuses per-frame computation. On Wan2.1-T2V-1.3B trained on eight A100s, LiveEdit hits 12.66 FPS at state-of-the-art quality against LucyEdit, InsV2V, VideoCoF, StreamV2V, StreamDiffusion and StreamDiffusionV2.
