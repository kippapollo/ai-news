SK Hynix ADRs rip 19 percent on ETF launch, dragging Micron up 5 percent and inverting Monday's memory crash

GraniteShares and ProShares switched on 2x leveraged single-stock ETFs against the new New York SK Hynix ADRs Tuesday. Volume flooded in on open, the ADR closed up 19 percent, Micron rallied 5 percent, SanDisk and Western Digital took roughly 6, and the KOSPI-led July 13 selloff was gone by lunch. The move lands on Micron at 229 percent year to date through Monday and a fresh Bank of America $1,550 price target that assumes HBM4 tightness carries into 2028.

24/7 Wall St. · 2026-07-14 · Source ↗

SK Hynix's New York-listed American depositary receipts jumped 19 percent on Tuesday, July 14 in the ticker's third full session since the July 11 debut. The catalyst was the same-morning launch of 2x leveraged single-stock ETFs from GraniteShares and ProShares tied to the ADR, which pulled volume in on the open and never let it out. Micron rallied 5 percent in the New York cash session, and SanDisk and Western Digital climbed roughly 6 percent apiece on the same pull.

The rebound erased Monday's 15 percent selloff in a single session. That crash was driven by South Korean brokerage KIS publishing a Q2 profit estimate 8 percent below Street consensus and citing slower-than-expected HBM4 shipments and heavy reliance on a small number of HBM contracts. Nothing in Tuesday's session actually contradicts the KIS thesis. The ETF launch simply pulled in leveraged retail flow and forced-buy hedging from the two ETF sponsors, and dip buyers who had watched Micron's 229 percent year-to-date run treat SK Hynix's freshly discounted ADRs as the cleanest way to catch up.

"Semiconductor stocks charged higher today, buoyed by a broad-based rally in memory chip stocks, following an overnight surge in Asian peers SK hynix and Samsung Electronics."— 24/7 Wall St., July 14 market wrap

Micron's leadership and the $1,550 target

Bank of America published a Micron price target of $1,550 during the same session, resting on the view that HBM4 supply stays tight into 2027 and that Micron's HBM revenue mix expands faster than DRAM ASP compression. Micron's fiscal Q3 2026 print came in at $41.46 billion in revenue and $25.11 non-GAAP EPS per ADR, both above the top end of guidance, and CEO Sanjay Mehrotra characterized memory as "strategically differentiated" in the AI era on the call. The company's stock closed Monday up 229 percent year to date, making it the second-best performer in the S&P 500 for the calendar year behind SpaceXAI's post-IPO rally.

SK Hynix's $26.5 billion New York offering

SK Hynix priced its US listing on July 10 at $149 per ADR against 177.9 million shares, raising $26.5 billion in the largest IPO ever completed by a foreign company on a US exchange. The offer was reportedly seven times oversubscribed. Chairman Chey Tae-won had told the roadshow that every customer he met said the 2027 memory-capacity plan "was not enough," and CEO Kwak Noh-Jung told Seeking Alpha the memory crunch extends past 2030. Neither statement changed on Monday when KIS cut its estimate, and neither is what fund managers are buying. What they are buying is the ADR as a proxy for HBM tightness, and the ETF launch converted that thesis into a mechanical bid.

"Every customer I met on the roadshow said the same thing: our 2027 capacity plan is not enough."— Chey Tae-won, SK Group Chairman, on the SK Hynix ADR roadshow

What the reversal signals

Monday's crash and Tuesday's rip is a 34-point round trip in two sessions on a stock the market values above $1.2 trillion. That is what the memory trade looks like now: mechanical, leverage-driven, and prone to dislocations that have very little to do with the underlying fundamentals. The KIS estimate that triggered Monday's selloff has not been withdrawn. The HBM4 shipment concerns underneath it have not been resolved. What changed is the launch of two ETFs that pull retail leverage into the ADR and force delta-hedging supply into the tape. For chip investors watching the second half, that means volatility now runs on ETF flow as much as it runs on earnings prints. For the AI capex story, the underlying signal has not moved: TSMC just printed 68 percent June revenue growth, Nvidia has 60 percent of TSMC's advanced-packaging capacity locked up, and Meta is bringing 14 gigawatts of compute online by end-2027. The memory tape has become a leveraged expression of that same view, and Tuesday priced accordingly.

A vimInsertModeRemaps setting lets teams remap two-key sequences like jj to Escape. A new CLAUDE_CODE_PROCESS_WRAPPER hook lets background agents and the agent view honor corporate launcher scripts.

Anthropic tagged Claude Code version 2.1.208 to the GitHub releases feed at 01:10 UTC on Tuesday July 14. The headline change is the addition of an opt-in accessibility mode designed to work reliably with NVDA and JAWS. Users activate the mode with the CLI flag claude --ax-screen-reader, the environment variable CLAUDE_AX_SCREEN_READER=1, or the settings entry "axScreenReader": true. The mode disables ANSI color output, animated spinners, live-updating status lines and any redraw-in-place widget that screen readers routinely mis-read as fresh input. Output falls back to a plain-text serial stream that screen readers process linearly, with logical markers where interactive prompts previously lived. The feature closes an outstanding accessibility issue that had accumulated 200-plus reactions on GitHub over the preceding six weeks.

Two additional workflow features shipped in the same tag. The vimInsertModeRemaps settings key exposes user-defined two-key insert-mode sequences that map to editor commands, most commonly jj to Escape. This closes the gap for the vim-mode users who have been running Claude Code through third-party terminal wrappers to get the muscle-memory shortcut back. The second addition is CLAUDE_CODE_PROCESS_WRAPPER, an environment variable that specifies a launcher command Claude Code invokes when spawning subprocesses in the agent view or background agent service. Enterprises with corporate launcher wrappers ("sudo -u", credential-injection scripts, sandboxed shells) can now direct Claude Code to route all shell invocations through those launchers rather than through the shell inherited from the parent process. Both changes address feedback from Anthropic's Enterprise Success team.

"Screen reader mode: opt-in plain-text rendering for screen reader users."— Claude Code v2.1.208 release notes

The release also improves background agents (more predictable checkpoint intervals), CLI output (thinner default status line, no repaint on Ctrl-L), and usage tracking (per-command token and dollar attribution surfaced in the interactive TUI usage panel). The mouse-click support in fullscreen menus lets users select entries with a trackpad instead of arrow-key navigation, addressing another accessibility gap. Anthropic did not tag any Sonnet 5 or Opus 4.8 model updates alongside the client release, and the changelog entry does not touch the Fable 5 pricing footnotes. The v2.1.208 tag is client-only and drops into existing installations through claude update.

Direct On-Policy Distillation transfers RL policy shifts from weak teachers into stronger targets

arXiv (Tsinghua AIR + ByteDance Seed) · 2026-07-14 · Source ↗

Top of Hugging Face's July 14 daily papers list at 93 upvotes. Tsinghua's SIA-Lab and ByteDance Seed argue that repeating RLVR on every new frontier model is prohibitively expensive, so run RL on the cheaper weak model then distill only the teacher's induced policy shift into the target. Direct-OPD isolates the delta from the teacher's final policy so the target avoids inheriting the teacher's ceiling.

The paper "Weak-to-Strong Generalization via Direct On-Policy Distillation" from Tsinghua AIR's SIA-Lab, Tsinghua CST, Peking University and ByteDance Seed topped Hugging Face's July 14 daily papers list at 93 upvotes. The framing problem is economic: reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) has become the standard post-training recipe for frontier reasoning models, and it is prohibitively expensive to repeat on every new base model. Running the same RL loop on GPT-5.6 Sol, Claude Fable 5, Gemini 3.5 Pro and DeepSeek V4 individually would consume more compute than the base pretrains. The paper's proposal: run the RL loop once on a small weak model where rollouts are cheap, extract the policy shift the RL induced, and transfer that shift into a stronger target without re-running the loop.

The core insight is that directly distilling the post-RL weak teacher into the target does not work. The weak teacher's final policy mixes useful RL-derived gains with the small model's own limitations, and the target model absorbs both together. Direct-OPD instead computes the policy delta between the pre-RL weak base and the post-RL weak teacher, and transfers only that delta to the strong target model through an on-policy distillation loss. The strong target's own base policy remains, and the RL-induced behavior stacks on top rather than replacing it.

"Direct-OPD distills only the teacher's RL-induced policy shift, rather than imitating or pushing past the teacher's final policy."— Weak-to-Strong Generalization via Direct On-Policy Distillation, §3

Empirically the paper reports Direct-OPD applied to a weak Qwen3-1.7B teacher transfers RL-derived reasoning improvements into a Qwen3-14B target with roughly 12 percent of the compute that RLVR on the target directly would require, while recovering approximately 91 percent of the direct-RL performance gain on AIME 2024, MATH-500 and GPQA. On the target model side, the method requires only forward passes through a fixed set of prompts under the delta loss; no reward-model or rollout infrastructure is needed. The paper positions Direct-OPD as a template for the next 12 months of open-source post-training: run expensive RL on a shared small teacher; distribute the delta rather than the fine-tune weights. ByteDance Seed's participation on the author list is notable given the company's Seedream 5.0 Pro release last week and Doubao product line: the distillation-of-policy-shifts approach fits ByteDance's need to keep Doubao's reasoning tier close to frontier without paying the frontier RL bill on every checkpoint.

Alibaba AMAP pairs ABot-AgentOS runtime with lifelong multi-modal memory for robotic agents

Hugging Face Daily Papers · 2026-07-14 · Source ↗

Number three on Tuesday's HF list at 68 upvotes. ABot-AgentOS is Alibaba AMAP's runtime companion to ABot-N1, wrapping vision, language and action streams in a persistent lifelong memory that carries across sessions. The system exposes a standard tool-use interface so downstream planning agents can query historical trajectories, environment maps and object detections without recomputing embeddings on every request.

ABot-AgentOS ships as the runtime-layer companion to ABot-N1, published the same day by the same Alibaba AMAP CV Lab team. Where ABot-N1 is a foundation model that maps instructions to actions, ABot-AgentOS is the surrounding software layer that persists what the agent saw, said, and did across sessions. The paper describes a lifelong multi-modal memory that stores vision, language and action streams as time-indexed embeddings in a vector database, exposes a standard tool-use API for downstream planners, and rehydrates relevant memory chunks into context on demand.

The architectural bet is that a robotic agent operating for hundreds or thousands of hours will accumulate context that no single-session model can hold in-window, and that letting the model choose what to remember through a tool-use interface produces better long-horizon behavior than either always-on retrieval or session-only working memory. The paper reports a 47 percent reduction in task-completion time on the RoomR benchmark when the agent's memory persists across 48-hour experimental runs, compared to a stateless baseline that re-explores the same environment on each new instruction.

"The robotic agent that operates for a thousand hours cannot hold its own state in a single model context window. It must externalize memory, and it must decide when to look."— ABot-AgentOS, §1 Motivation

ABot-AgentOS's tool-use interface is intentionally MCP-compatible, per the paper's implementation section. That decision keeps the release usable inside Claude Code, Cursor, Codex CLI and Antigravity as an MCP server exposing memory queries and action-trace lookups. It also lands during a week in which Google, Microsoft, Salesforce, Snowflake and ServiceNow have publicly aligned behind a competing Agentic Resource Discovery standard (see yesterday's edition). Alibaba's decision to ship the release on MCP rather than ARD is a mild but concrete data point that the academic and open-source layer of the agent-tools ecosystem is leaning MCP even as the SaaS layer builds an alternative. The paper is accepted to ICRA 2026 and lists Xiao Zhou, Bo Wei, Wei Chen and Jingjing Liu as senior authors.

Seoul National University reconstructs 4D human-scene interactions from low-overlap camera captures

Hugging Face Daily Papers · 2026-07-14 · Source ↗

SNU's paper at 41 upvotes tackles dynamic reconstruction from sparse-view video where camera overlap is under 15 percent per frame. The method combines per-frame monocular depth priors with a temporal consistency loss on human-skeleton keypoints, producing full 4D scene reconstructions from smartphone captures where prior methods fail.

Seoul National University's paper "4D Human-Scene Reconstruction from Low-Overlap Captures" places at fourth on Hugging Face's July 14 daily papers list with 41 upvotes. The problem is straightforward and previously unsolved: reconstruct a full moving 4D scene from multiple handheld smartphone captures where cameras rarely see the same viewpoint. Real footage from social events, sporting matches and family gatherings typically has camera-frame overlap below 15 percent, which breaks classical multi-view stereo pipelines and defeats most monocular reconstruction methods that assume single-viewpoint temporal coherence.

The SNU team's approach layers monocular depth priors (per-frame Depth Anything V3 predictions) with a temporal consistency loss anchored on skeletal keypoints for any humans in the scene. The keypoint loss constrains the per-frame depth predictions to produce a consistent 3D human trajectory over time, which in turn pins the surrounding static scene geometry into a shared coordinate system. The result is a full 4D reconstruction ("3D + time") of both the human motion and the surrounding environment, from captures that no prior method could stitch together.

"Camera overlap below 15 percent per frame is where reconstruction pipelines silently fail. Every dataset we assembled from real handheld captures sits below that threshold."— 4D Human-Scene Reconstruction, §2 Related Work

Quantitative results on a new 4D-Overlap benchmark introduced with the paper show the SNU method achieves 0.72 Chamfer distance on 15 test scenes captured with 3-4 smartphones during unscripted events, compared to prior best 1.34 for a Kinect-based baseline and 2.11 for the strongest monocular-only reconstruction. Human trajectory error runs at 8.3 cm mean per-joint position error, within the range of studio-grade motion capture. The paper's practical target is sports analytics, insurance-claim reconstruction and content-creator VR footage. Code and the 4D-Overlap benchmark are being released alongside the paper.

The University of Wisconsin-Madison paper "Multi-Agent LLMs Fail to Explore Each Other" landed at 5 upvotes on Hugging Face's July 14 list. The authors take a cooperative multi-agent LLM setup — several agents share a task, communicate through a scratchpad, and jointly search for solutions — and measure exploration diversity across rounds. The finding: agents converge on correlated exploration strategies within 3-5 communication rounds, and the exploration space they cover shrinks rather than expands.

The mechanism the paper documents is that each agent's next action depends heavily on the scratchpad summary the other agents have written, and the shared scratchpad acts as an attractor pulling exploration toward the region already covered. Adversarial-style prompting ("look for solutions the other agents missed") reduces the effect only slightly. The failure mode is invisible to standard task-success metrics because the correlated-exploration equilibrium often still solves the task; the loss shows up in a solution-diversity metric the paper introduces (measuring how many distinct search paths appear across agents).

"Multi-agent architectures were sold on the assumption that agents would explore complementary regions of the solution space. They do not."— Multi-Agent LLMs Fail to Explore Each Other, §4

The practical implication is that current multi-agent frameworks (LangGraph multi-agent, CrewAI, AutoGen) may be delivering less lift than users assume, particularly on open-ended search or research-style tasks where diversity of exploration matters more than raw task-completion rate. The paper suggests explicit information-hiding between agents (each agent works from a private scratchpad summary that omits the other agents' full traces) as a partial fix. The Direct On-Policy Distillation paper's success in transferring RL policy shifts from a single weak teacher (rather than a multi-agent teacher ensemble) is consistent with the Wisconsin finding: multi-agent search buys less than the field's marketing implies.

The topology flow, conditioned on the vertex output, samples the edge and face structure that connects the vertices.
