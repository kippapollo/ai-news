Microsoft puts computer-using agents into production. First hyperscaler to ship screen-and-keyboard automation at GA.

Copilot Studio's CUA tier reaches every commercial Power Platform geography with OpenAI CUA and Claude Sonnet 4.5 as the launch models, Key Vault credential handling, Purview audit logs, and configurable human review at 5 Copilot Credits per step.

Microsoft Copilot Studio Blog · 2026-05-26

Microsoft moved computer-using agents (CUA) in Copilot Studio to general availability on May 26, ending a five-month preview and rolling them out to every commercial Power Platform geography. The move makes Microsoft the first hyperscaler to put screen-and-keyboard automation behind a production-tier SLA, with Key Vault credential storage, Purview audit logging, and configurable human-in-the-loop review as standard. The GA build supports OpenAI's CUA and Anthropic's Claude Sonnet 4.5 as the launch models, billed at 5 Copilot Credits per step rather than per token, so customers can model the cost of an agent run as a fixed UI-action count.

The functional change matters more than the licensing math. Until now, robotic-process-automation tools have leaned on brittle UI selectors that snap when an application's layout drifts. Microsoft's CUA uses vision and reasoning to read whatever is on screen and pick the next action, which means an agent can complete a workflow inside a desktop app or a SaaS portal that exposes no API at all. The first launch partners (Lumen Technologies, Graebel Companies, Aon, and Westat) say they have lined the new agents up against approval queues, claim reconciliation, and IT-onboarding tasks that previously demanded a human in the loop because the underlying system was un-scriptable.

"By adopting Microsoft Copilot Studio and AI agents, we've moved beyond traditional automation to a more intelligent, scalable operating model."— Matt Brownlee, Chief Revenue Officer, Graebel

The wider competitive picture is that production-grade computer use is no longer a research demo. OpenAI's Operator hit preview in early 2025 and only ChatGPT Pro users can run it; Anthropic's "computer use" tool has lived in the developer API since Claude 3.5 Sonnet but without a managed enterprise wrapper. By bundling CUA into Power Platform tenants under existing data-residency and compliance boundaries, Microsoft has now made the easier sell to a regulated buyer than either of the model labs whose technology it runs. Expect Google to push Gemini-based screen agents into Workspace Flows in the following weeks, with the same pricing-per-step playbook.

Beijing widens overseas-travel curbs for top AI staff at Alibaba and DeepSeek.

Bloomberg · 2026-05-26 · Source ↗

Founders, researchers, and senior engineers working on frontier AI must now seek government approval before going overseas. The list of affected names is set by national-tech relevance, not job title.

China has extended a passport-control regime previously aimed at DeepSeek's leadership to AI staff across private firms, including Alibaba's Qwen team, Bloomberg reported on May 26. Founders, principal researchers, and senior engineers working on advanced AI must now obtain government approval before traveling abroad, with the determination based on the strategic value of an individual's work rather than their formal title. Two co-founders of agent-startup Manus were already blocked from leaving the country earlier in the year, presaging the wider rule.

The architecture of the policy is what makes it consequential. Until now Western reporting treated Beijing's AI-talent controls as a niche measure aimed at one or two state-sensitive labs. Folding Alibaba, DeepSeek, and (per the reporting) a handful of other private-sector frontier teams into the same regime turns it into a quasi-industrial-policy instrument: the government decides which AI workers carry national-priority knowledge and which can negotiate in the open labor market. Neither Alibaba nor DeepSeek has commented, and the State Council has not publicized the rule.

The move parallels the export-control posture the US has tightened around frontier chips and weights. With the EU AI Act becoming fully applicable on August 2 and Washington's own Center for AI Standards and Innovation onboarding Google, Microsoft, and xAI for pre-deployment evaluations as of early May, the three large blocs are now treating AI capability as a state asset rather than a private-sector product. The practical effect for multinationals: hiring or partnering with senior researchers based in mainland China just gained an additional layer of regulatory uncertainty around international travel and conference attendance.

Anthropic buys Stainless for $300M-plus, taking ownership of the SDK pipeline shipping OpenAI's and Google's developer libraries.

TechCrunch · 2026-05-18 · Source ↗

Stainless turns OpenAPI specs into production SDKs in Python, TypeScript, Kotlin, Go, and Java. Its customers include OpenAI, Google, Cloudflare, Replicate, and Runway. Anthropic is winding the hosted product down.

Anthropic acquired Stainless on May 18 for a price The Information reported at more than $300 million, double the New York startup's $150 million valuation from December 2024. Stainless built an AI-powered compiler that turns an OpenAPI spec into a maintained, production-ready SDK across Python, TypeScript, Kotlin, Go, Java, and other languages. The customer roster is the punchline: Stainless ships the official client libraries that OpenAI and Google publish to developers, alongside SDKs for Cloudflare, Replicate, Runway, and dozens of smaller AI vendors. By the company's own count, around a quarter of professional software developers have used a Stainless-generated SDK or documentation site.

Anthropic announced it will wind down all hosted Stainless products, including the SDK generator service. Existing customers retain full rights to SDKs already generated and can keep modifying them, but the company has flagged a transition window after which the managed product disappears. For OpenAI and Google, that means the developer-facing tooling that wraps their APIs was, until last week, partly under the control of a direct frontier-lab competitor, and the wind-down now forces them to internalize, rebuild, or migrate that pipeline before the cut-off lands.

"SDKs deserve as much care as the APIs they wrap. Anthropic was one of the first teams to bet on this with us."— Alex Rattray, founder, Stainless

The strategic logic for Anthropic is plain. Frontier-lab competition is increasingly downstream of the model itself: at the developer surface, the agent runtime, and the tooling that connects an AI to the rest of a company's stack. Owning the canonical SDK-generation pipeline lets Anthropic shape how its own MCP-style tool-use primitives and managed-agent integrations get represented in client libraries across the ecosystem. The acquisition follows Anthropic's $14 billion February raise and lands in the same week the company is reportedly closing a fresh $30 billion round at a $900 billion valuation.

DeepMind pays roughly $100M to absorb the Contextual AI team. Antitrust scrutiny on acqui-hires hardens.

Bloomberg / Benzinga · 2026-05-19 · Source ↗

More than 20 researchers, including co-founder and CEO Douwe Kiela, move to DeepMind in a non-exclusive licensing deal that avoids triggering a merger filing. DOJ leadership has now flagged the pattern as a red flag.

Google DeepMind agreed on May 19 to license technology from retrieval-augmented-generation pioneer Contextual AI and to hire more than 20 of its researchers, including co-founder and CEO Douwe Kiela. Bloomberg pegged the total value at $80–90 million, with other sources reporting figures up to $100 million. The shape of the deal, licensing plus hiring rather than an outright purchase, keeps Alphabet outside the merger-review process while delivering the same operational result: Contextual's RAG and grounded-reasoning bench joins DeepMind's research org, and Contextual AI continues as a shell entity for its remaining customers.

The structural choice is the same template Google used for its 2025 Windsurf deal (about $2.4 billion in licensing fees) and its 2024 Character.AI arrangement, and that Nvidia used for its absorption of part of the Groq team. Acting Assistant Attorney General Omeed Assefi has now characterized that pattern as a potential "red flag" for antitrust enforcement, signaling that DOJ may treat licensing-plus-talent transactions as functional mergers when reviewers look at concentration effects in the AI labor market.

Contextual AI's investors include Greycroft, Bain Capital Ventures, and Lightspeed; the team had raised an $80 million Series A in 2024 and has been backed by Jeff Bezos, per separate StartupHub.ai reporting on what it calls "four labs, four acquisitions in five days." The week of May 18 alone saw similar arrangements from Anthropic (Stainless), Mistral, and Meta. The pattern is a consolidation cycle that is real, sizeable, and almost entirely invisible to standard merger trackers. For investors, the read is that the absence of headline AI acquisitions does not mean the consolidation phase has not arrived.

Penn physicists switch logic with light at four femtojoules, a step toward optical AI hardware.

Penn Today · 2026-05-18 · Source ↗

Bo Zhen's group demonstrated all-light signal switching using exciton-polariton quasiparticles, at roughly four quadrillionths of a joule per operation. The work was published in Physical Review Letters.

A group at the University of Pennsylvania led by Bo Zhen, Jin K. Lee Presidential Associate Professor of Physics and Astronomy, has demonstrated an all-light logic switch with an energy cost of approximately four femtojoules (four quadrillionths of a joule) per operation. The work, published in Physical Review Letters on April 23 and amplified by Penn Today and ScienceDaily in mid-May, relies on exciton-polaritons: hybrid light-matter quasiparticles formed when photons couple strongly with electrons in an atomically thin semiconductor inside a nanoscale optical cavity.

The physics matters because of what it removes. Conventional photonic computing is fast and cheap for moving data, but every time a system needs a nonlinear operation (the choice points at the core of any logic or activation function) it has to convert the photon signal back into electrons, perform the switch, and convert back. That round-trip is where most of the energy goes. By making the photons themselves interact strongly enough to switch each other, the Penn group sidesteps the conversion entirely and pushes the per-operation energy budget orders of magnitude below what a small LED would draw.

"Because they are charge-neutral and have zero rest mass, photons can carry information quickly over long distances with minimal loss."— Li He, co-first author

The headline use case is AI hardware. A photonic chip that can do both linear data transport and nonlinear activation in the same medium would let processors handle camera, lidar, and sensor input without repeated electronic conversions. Energy use of large model training is the bottleneck the field is most willing to spend capital on; if exciton-polariton switching scales, the multiplicative effect on data-center power is what makes the result worth tracking past its lab milestone. The Penn group, with co-authors Li He, Zhi Wang, and Bumho Kim, has not yet announced a foundry partner.

Nvidia reported total revenue of $81.6 billion for the first quarter of fiscal 2027, the three months ending April 26, up 85% year on year and ahead of consensus at $78.8 billion. The accelerating share of the non-hyperscaler bucket is the chart Nvidia wants investors to look at: it signals that frontier-model training is no longer the only thing buying Blackwell, and that inference deployments at sovereign clouds, financial-services labs, and large enterprises are catching up to the cap-ex cycle.

Guidance for Q2 FY27 lands at roughly $91 billion in revenue plus or minus 2%, which would imply the data-center segment alone clearing $85 billion. The stock dipped on the print despite the beat, with analysts citing valuation discipline at roughly 30× forward earnings rather than any softness in demand. Hachette, Macmillan, Elsevier and Scott Turow sue Meta over Llama training on pirated books.

The Washington Post · 2026-05-05 · Source ↗

A class-action filed in San Francisco names Mark Zuckerberg personally and accuses Meta of training Llama on millions of titles copied from LibGen and Anna's Archive. The case lands after the Anthropic settlement reset the fair-use boundary.

A coalition of publishing houses (Hachette, Macmillan, McGraw Hill, Elsevier, and Cengage) joined bestselling novelist and former Authors Guild president Scott Turow on May 5 in a class-action lawsuit against Meta Platforms and CEO Mark Zuckerberg. The complaint alleges that Meta knowingly trained successive Llama models on copies of millions of books and journal articles sourced from the pirate libraries LibGen, Z-Library, and Anna's Archive, and that internal communications surfaced in earlier discovery show Meta engineers explicitly debating the legal exposure before proceeding.

The case lands at an inflection point in the law. The class-action authors' suit against Anthropic ruled that training on copyrighted books can be fair use, but that storing pirated copies is not. The settlement ultimately rang in at $1.5 billion. Thomson Reuters v. Ross Intelligence, meanwhile, found that Ross's use of Westlaw headnotes was not fair use and is now on appeal. Federal judges are converging on a two-step test: courts now look first at how training data was sourced, and second at whether the AI's outputs reproduce protected expression.

For Meta the stakes are concrete. Llama is the largest open-weight model family in active circulation, with downloads in the tens of millions through Hugging Face. A settlement on Anthropic's scale would not threaten Meta's solvency but would set a precedent that any model trained on un-licensed scanned books carries a per-title liability. The plaintiffs are seeking statutory damages of up to $150,000 per infringed work plus injunctive relief that could require Meta to retrain or filter the affected model checkpoints. Discovery is expected to focus on the chain of custody by which the LibGen corpus entered Meta's training pipeline.

Gemini API ships a breaking change to the Interactions schema, with the legacy version disappearing June 8.

Google AI for Developers · 2026-05-26 · Source ↗

The new Interactions schema became the default on May 26, with the legacy schema set to be removed on June 8. Production callers get a two-week window to migrate.

Google flipped the Gemini API's Interactions schema to its new shape as the default on May 26, with the legacy version scheduled for full removal on June 8. The Interactions API is the surface developers use to wire Gemini into multi-step agent workflows and tool-use loops, so the change touches anything from a small Cloud Run agent up to enterprise integrations through Vertex AI and Agentspace. Google's developer documentation flagged the cut-off as a hard deadline rather than a graceful sunset: callers still on the legacy schema after June 8 will see errors, not redirects.

The shape of the new schema reflects where Gemini's product roadmap has landed. The redesign promotes a single Interaction object that bundles input, output, tool calls, and structured outputs together, in line with the agent-first surface Google has been building since the Antigravity platform's GA at I/O. It also normalizes how Gemini 3.5 Flash and Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite expose their action-and-thought traces, removing a quirk that had forced agent frameworks to special-case each model variant.

For teams running production traffic, the practical risk is the two-week window. Google's pattern with prior Gemini deprecations has been to honor sunset notices on the published date with no extension, and the Interactions schema sits inside the request body itself rather than in a header, so feature-flag rollouts that swap fields cleanly are non-trivial. The change has already triggered chatter in the LangChain and LlamaIndex repos; both projects shipped updated adapters within 48 hours, but downstream consumers will need to bump their dependencies and re-test before June 8.

Anthropic's Claude Managed Agents add self-hosted sandboxes and MCP tunnels, keeping data inside the customer perimeter.

Anthropic Engineering · 2026-05-19 · Source ↗

A public beta lets enterprises run agent code-execution inside their own VPC while continuing to orchestrate through Anthropic. A research-preview "MCP tunnels" feature lets internal tools be reached without exposing them to the public internet.

Anthropic updated its Claude Managed Agents platform on May 19 with two features aimed squarely at regulated buyers: a public-beta self-hosted sandbox and a research preview of "MCP tunnels." The self-hosted sandbox lets a customer run the code-execution and tool-use portions of an agent inside their own virtual private cloud while continuing to use Anthropic's managed orchestrator for planning and turn-taking. The MCP tunnels feature opens an encrypted reverse-tunnel between the managed orchestrator and an internal MCP server, so an agent can reach line-of-business tools that live on a private network without those tools being exposed to the public internet.

The product motion is the explicit answer to a question Anthropic's enterprise sales team has been hearing all year: how do you run a fully managed agent without pulling sensitive data and credentials into a third-party SaaS plane? Until now, the choice was either to self-host the entire stack (which negates the managed-platform value) or to accept Anthropic-side execution of every tool call, which is a non-starter for healthcare, financial, and government tenants. The new architecture lets the managed plane stay thin while keeping execution inside the customer perimeter.

The release lands alongside competing moves from Salesforce (Agentforce Coworker, May 22), Camunda (ProcessOS, closed beta May 20), and ServiceNow's own May agent-governance push. Together they describe a market that is moving past the experimental-agent phase and beginning to wire production-safe agents into the same governance, audit, and identity systems that already manage human employees. For Anthropic, holding the high ground on data-residency and tunnel security is also a defense against Microsoft's same-week CUA launch.
