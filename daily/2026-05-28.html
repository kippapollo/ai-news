Cognition raises $1B at $26B as Devin writes 90 percent of its own code.

Lux, General Catalyst and 8VC co-led. Run-rate revenue climbed from $37M to $492M in twelve months. Goldman Sachs, Mercedes-Benz, NASA, Santander, the US Army and US Navy run Devin on production code. Co-founder Scott Wu says enterprise usage grew tenfold since January.

Bloomberg · 2026-05-27 · Source ↗

Cognition AI confirmed Wednesday that it raised more than $1 billion in a round led by Lux Capital, General Catalyst and 8VC. The round prices the maker of the autonomous coding agent Devin at $26 billion. The new mark is up from $10.2 billion in September, a price more than doubled in eight months. Ribbit Capital, Atreides Management, and Peter Thiel's Founders Fund participated alongside the leads. Cognition has now raised about $2.5 billion since emerging from stealth with a $21 million seed in March 2024.

The revenue trajectory explains the markup. Cognition reported a $37 million annualized run rate twelve months ago. The figure now stands at $492 million and Wu has said the company is targeting a $1 billion run rate by year end. Wu told investors that enterprise usage has grown roughly tenfold since January. Goldman Sachs, Mercedes-Benz, NASA, Santander, the US Army and the US Navy are among the disclosed customers running Devin against production code.

The 90 percent claim

The line out of the round that drew the most attention is internal rather than commercial. Cognition says 90 percent of its own code now ships from Devin. The figure sits at the top of a small cluster of self-eating-dog-food disclosures from coding-AI shops over the past quarter. Google has put new-code AI assistance at roughly three-quarters. Snap put the figure at 65 percent before laying off a thousand engineers in April. Meta's internal target for agent-assisted commits is 55 percent or above. Cognition's 90 percent is the highest year-of-2026 disclosure anyone in the category has attached a date to.

Devin runs as a hosted agent that plans, writes, debugs and deploys code across multi-step workflows. Cognition operates against frontier models from OpenAI and Anthropic alongside its own training work. The company picked up Windsurf's remaining engineering assets in July 2025 after Google paid $2.4 billion to license the rest of the business and hire most of the team. That transaction handed Cognition both an editor codebase and a pool of engineers familiar with IDE-level integration.

What changed since September

September's $10.2 billion price reflected a customer base mostly running Devin on isolated tickets. Eight months later, banks and a small set of national security customers route meaningful slices of their engineering throughput through it. The product positioning has not shifted. Devin is sold as a software engineer rather than a chat tool, with seat pricing layered on top of usage-based consumption. The selling motion is task assignment.

"Our enterprise usage has grown more than tenfold since the start of the year." — Scott Wu, Cognition co-founder and CEO

The investor list signals a maturing thesis on the category. Lux Capital and General Catalyst have both led prior rounds, and Founders Fund has been on the cap table since 2024. The addition of Atreides and Ribbit, two firms that historically write large checks into late-stage software with predictable revenue, suggests the late market is pricing Devin against SaaS multiples rather than research-lab multiples. At $26 billion on roughly $500 million of run-rate revenue, that is a 52x multiple. Expensive, but not out of line against Cursor, the closest direct peer.

The reliability question

Inside engineering communities, reliability remains the soft underbelly of the agent category. Cognition has not published an updated public benchmark in months. SWE-Bench Verified, the most-cited reference, now sits above 90 percent for frontier models from Anthropic and OpenAI. That ceiling matters less than wall-clock reliability on long-running tasks, where multi-step agents continue to drop tickets. Investors appear to be discounting the risk against the revenue trajectory and renewal speed at named enterprise accounts.

The round closes Cognition's move from research bet to category leader in autonomous coding agents. The competitive set is crowded: Microsoft (Copilot), Google (Antigravity and Jules), GitHub (Copilot Workspace), Anysphere (Cursor) and a handful of model labs are all selling into the same use case. Wu's framing is that Cognition is the only firm running both a serious frontier-model investment and a product designed end-to-end for software delivery. The $26 billion valuation says enough investors agree to write the check.

SafetyTech

Reasoning models autonomously jailbreak frontier peers at 97 percent.

Nature Communications · 2026-05-27 · Source ↗

Four reasoning models, including DeepSeek-R1, Grok 3 Mini, Gemini 2.5 Flash and Qwen3 235B, broke through the safety alignment of nine victim models across 25,200 attempts. Resistance varies 31-fold from Claude 4 Sonnet to DeepSeek-V3.

Thilo Hagendorff, Erik Derner and Nuria Oliver at ETH Zurich published "Large reasoning models are autonomous jailbreak agents" in Nature Communications this week. The paper's central finding is uncomfortable for the safety-training pipelines that frontier labs have built since 2023. With nothing but a single system prompt, four open-weight reasoning models were able to compromise the safety policies of nine target systems including GPT-4o, Claude 4 Sonnet, DeepSeek-V3, Grok 3, and Llama 4 Maverick. The combined attack rate across 25,200 inputs landed at 97.14 percent.

The attackers used chain-of-thought reasoning in invisible scratchpads to plan strategies, refining their approach across as many as ten conversational turns when the first try failed. The most-used techniques were flattery and rapport-building in 84.75 percent of attempts, educational or research framing in 68.56 percent, and hypothetical-scenario construction in 65.67 percent. The average prompt ran 532 tokens, with the longest reaching 8,001 tokens. A control group using non-reasoning attacker models succeeded in only 4 of 900 attempts.

The 31-fold resistance gap

Defensive performance varied sharply across the nine victims. Claude 4 Sonnet held the strongest line, with a 2.86 percent maximum harm rate and a 50.18 percent refusal rate. DeepSeek-V3 sat at the other extreme, with a 90 percent maximum harm rate and a 4.18 percent refusal rate. The authors attribute the gap to differences in the quality and breadth of safety-alignment training data rather than to base-model capability.

Inter-rater reliability for the LLM-based evaluator landed at ICC 0.883, with human validation at ICC 0.925.

"As reasoning capability improves, attack capability improves in tandem." — Hagendorff, Derner and Oliver, Nature Communications

The mechanism the authors call alignment regression is straightforward in principle. Safety training data captures patterns that human red-teamers use to elicit harmful behavior. It does not capture the distinct patterns that reasoning agents produce when they plan multi-step attacks against another model. The coverage gap is what the 97 percent number measures. The implication for labs is that safety training will need to incorporate adversarial agent traces, not only human-authored ones.

Harvard proposes bidirectional evolutionary search for self-improving LLMs.

Hugging Face Papers · 2026-05-27 · Source ↗

A Harvard paper in yesterday's feed proposes a method for language models to optimize their own parameters and behavior through bidirectional evolutionary search, with no external supervision required during the loop.

"Self-Improving Language Models with Bidirectional Evolutionary Search" from Harvard pulled five votes on Wednesday's Hugging Face Papers list. The method belongs to a small but growing family of self-improvement approaches that have moved from speculation to working systems over the past nine months. The pattern is to set up an environment in which a model proposes its own training signal, executes it, and consolidates the improvement, all without a human-curated supervised dataset for the loop itself.

Bidirectional evolutionary search differs from straight reinforcement learning in that it maintains a population of candidate model states and samples both forward (toward higher reward) and backward (toward more diverse states) at each round. The authors argue that this stabilizes the loop on tasks where reward shaping is brittle, which is most of the practical cases.

The release joins a thread of self-improvement research that has been recurring across labs. DeepMind's Lab40, Anthropic's published interpretability-led training experiments, and earlier Anthropic and OpenAI work on self-generated curricula all share the broad shape of the problem. The open question across the field is whether self-improving methods converge to genuinely new capability or to a model that scores well on the metric it was trained against.

"From Pixels to Words" pushes native one-vision models at scale.

Hugging Face Papers · 2026-05-27 · Source ↗

A 21-author paper proposes native end-to-end vision models that process pixels directly and generate text without intermediate token-level visual representations. Thirteen votes on a busy submission day.

"From Pixels to Words: Towards Native One-Vision Models at Scale" appeared on the May 27 Hugging Face Papers list with thirteen votes. The 21-author byline is a tell: this is the kind of collaborative work that crosses lab boundaries. The technical argument is that most production vision-language systems still rely on a discrete tokenization step, where a vision encoder produces a fixed-vocabulary representation that the language model then consumes. The authors propose dropping that step and learning a native end-to-end pathway from pixels to generated text.

The motivation comes from a known efficiency penalty. Vision tokens are usually packed into a fixed budget, which throws away information for tasks that need pixel-level detail (counting small objects, reading dense text, measuring distances). Native pixel-in architectures can in principle preserve that information at the cost of more compute per token, and the paper argues that recent improvements in tokenizer-free language modeling make the tradeoff favorable at scale.

The work sits alongside Apple's recent native-pixel research and Google's Gemini Live work, both of which have moved away from clean pipeline boundaries between modality encoders. The broader trajectory in 2026 has been toward end-to-end native architectures across modalities, with text-image-audio-video all flowing through a single trunk and being separated only at output.

Chartographer benchmarks vision-language models with counterfactual charts.

Hugging Face Papers · 2026-05-27 · Source ↗

A four-author paper releases a generation pipeline for counterfactual charts to stress-test how vision-language models read graphical data, addressing a known evaluation blind spot in multimodal benchmarks.

"Chartographer: Counterfactual Chart Generation for Evaluating Vision-Language Models" lands at the intersection of two recurring problems in current multimodal evaluation. The first is that chart understanding is one of the practical failure modes most aggressively reported by enterprise users. The second is that the canonical benchmarks (ChartQA, PlotQA) are saturated, with frontier models pushing the ceiling and leaving little headroom to discriminate among them.

Chartographer's contribution is a generation pipeline that produces counterfactual charts. The same underlying data is rendered into systematic variations (changed axis scale, swapped color encoding, added or removed gridlines, altered labels), and the model is evaluated on whether its readings track the underlying data rather than the surface form. The framework lets researchers measure spurious correlations between visual presentation and answer.

The submission picked up three votes on Wednesday but is the kind of paper that often resurfaces six months later when teams begin reporting Chartographer numbers in model cards. Counterfactual generation has become a workhorse of LLM evaluation; extending it cleanly into the multimodal axis is overdue.

Seven votes is modest. The audience for video-generation optimization papers tends to be narrower than for LLM work, but the techniques crossover. Most of what OSP-Next proposes is portable to other diffusion-transformer pipelines.

Tech

UCLA: early-stopping rollout cuts on-policy distillation cost.

Hugging Face Papers · 2026-05-27 · Source ↗

A short UCLA paper proposes that on-policy distillation pipelines can stop rollouts well before completion without quality loss, reducing rollout compute on long-horizon trajectories.

"Less is More: Early Stopping Rollout for On-Policy Distillation" from UCLA picked up two votes but plants a flag in the most-discussed efficiency conversation of the spring. On-policy distillation, where a smaller student model is trained on a teacher's behavior under matched state distributions, has overtaken straight supervised distillation as the default approach for shipping smaller production models in 2026. The headline cost in the loop is rollout time. The teacher has to act, observations have to be processed, and the student has to be updated against the joint trajectory.

The UCLA team's claim is that rollouts can be truncated at the point at which the teacher's behavior converges with high confidence, without degrading the resulting student. The savings are not constant. They depend on the task and the architecture, but the paper reports cases where the rollout budget can be cut substantially without measurable loss on downstream evaluation. That matters for the practical economics of the distillation pipeline at frontier-model scale, where each second of teacher inference is expensive.

The technique slots into existing distillation pipelines as a stopping rule rather than as a new objective. That kind of drop-in optimization tends to get adopted fast because it doesn't disrupt the rest of the stack.

LangGraph v1.2 adds per-node timeouts and DeltaChannel checkpointing.

LangChain · 2026-05 · Source ↗

The release adds per-node timeouts, error recovery, and graceful shutdown for long-running graphs. DeltaChannel cuts checkpoint overhead on long threads, and a streaming API v3 is content-block-centric.

LangGraph v1.2 shipped this month with three pieces that matter for production agent deployments. Per-node timeouts are the first. The framework previously enforced timeouts only at the graph level, which made it hard to bound time spent on individual tool calls without rolling custom wrappers. Per-node timeouts give pipeline authors the granularity to enforce strict bounds on slow tools (web fetches, API calls into rate-limited services) while leaving fast nodes alone.

The second is DeltaChannel, a new checkpoint format that materializes incremental state changes rather than full state snapshots at each step. Long-running threads that previously paid a quadratic-ish checkpoint cost now pay roughly linear cost. For agents that run for tens of minutes or hours, the difference is the difference between checkpointing being a notable overhead and being free.

The third is a streaming API v3 that uses content-block-centric streaming rather than token-level streaming. This aligns with the model-side trend toward block-structured responses (text blocks, tool use blocks, thinking blocks) that Anthropic introduced and that other vendors have since adopted in compatible forms.

LangGraph remains one of the most-used agent orchestration frameworks in 2026, alongside CrewAI, AutoGen, OpenAI's Agents SDK, and Anthropic's recommended pattern using Claude's tool-use primitives directly. The v1.2 release tightens its position for long-running, fault-tolerant agent deployments at scale.
