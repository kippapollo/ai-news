Cognition raises $1B at $26B as Devin writes 90% of its own code.

The Devin maker more than doubled its September valuation in eight months. Run-rate revenue jumped from $37M to $492M in twelve. Lux, General Catalyst and 8VC co-led; Founders Fund, Ribbit and Atreides followed in. Goldman Sachs, Mercedes-Benz, NASA and Santander now run Devin on production code.

Bloomberg · 2026-05-27 · Source ↗

Cognition AI raised more than $1 billion in a financing led by Lux Capital, General Catalyst and 8VC, the company confirmed on Wednesday. The round values the maker of the autonomous coding agent Devin at $26 billion. That is up from $10.2 billion in September, a price more than doubled in eight months. Other participants include Ribbit Capital, Atreides Management and Peter Thiel's Founders Fund. Cognition has now raised about $2.5 billion in total.

The funding lands against a backdrop of revenue numbers that explain the markup. Cognition reported a $37 million annualized run rate twelve months ago. It is at $492 million now and targeting a billion-dollar run rate by year end. Co-founder and chief executive Scott Wu says enterprise usage has grown roughly tenfold since January. Goldman Sachs, Mercedes-Benz, NASA, Santander and several US government departments are paying customers running Devin on production code.

The "90 percent" claim

The most-quoted line out of the round is internal rather than commercial. Cognition says 90 percent of its own code now ships from Devin. The number is part of a small cluster of self-eating-dog-food disclosures from coding-AI companies that have piled up over the past quarter. Google has said roughly three-quarters of its new code is AI-generated. Snap put the figure at 65 percent before laying off a thousand engineers in April. Meta's internal target is "55 percent or more" for agent-assisted commits. Cognition's 90 percent is the highest disclosure anyone has put a year-of-2026 date on.

Devin runs as a hosted agent that plans, writes, debugs and deploys code across multi-step workflows. Cognition operates a mix of its own model training and inference against frontier models from OpenAI and Anthropic. The company picked up Windsurf's remaining engineering assets in July 2025, after Google paid $2.4 billion to license the rest of the business and hire most of the team. That deal gave Cognition both an IDE codebase and a pool of engineers familiar with editor-level integration.

What changed since September

September's $10.2 billion price reflected a company whose customers were mostly early adopters running Devin on isolated tickets. Eight months later, banks and a small set of national security customers are routing meaningful slices of their engineering throughput through it. The US Army and Navy are publicly disclosed customers. The growth has come without any change to the headline product positioning: Devin is sold as a software engineer, with seat-style pricing on top of usage-based consumption. The model is not chat. It is task assignment.

"Our enterprise usage has grown more than tenfold since the start of the year." — Scott Wu, Cognition co-founder and CEO

The investor list signals a maturing thesis. Lux Capital and General Catalyst have both led prior rounds, and Founders Fund has been on the cap table since the company emerged from stealth in March 2024 with its first $21 million seed. The addition of Atreides and Ribbit, two firms that historically write large checks into late-stage software businesses with predictable revenue, suggests the late-stage market is pricing Devin against SaaS multiples rather than research-lab multiples. At $26 billion on roughly $500 million of run-rate revenue, that is a 52x multiple, expensive but not out of line with peers like Cursor.

The reliability question

Critics inside the engineering community continue to point at agent reliability as the soft underbelly of the category. Cognition has not published an updated public benchmark in months. SWE-Bench Verified, the most-cited test, now sits above 90 percent for frontier models from Anthropic and OpenAI. That ceiling matters less than wall-clock reliability on long-running tasks, which is still where multi-step agents drop tickets. Investors appear to be discounting that risk against the revenue trajectory and the speed at which enterprise customers are renewing.

The round closes Cognition's transition from research bet to category leader for the autonomous coding-agent niche, in a market where Microsoft (Copilot), Google (Antigravity, Jules), GitHub (Copilot Workspace), Anysphere (Cursor) and a handful of model labs are all competing on the same use case. Wu's framing is that Cognition is the only firm with both a serious frontier model investment and a product designed from day one for end-to-end software delivery. The $26 billion valuation says enough investors agree to write the check.

Big picture

Cognition's $1 billion round on Wednesday joined a market where AI coding agents now write the majority of code at their own makers. Fujitsu signed strategic deals on the same day with both Anthropic and OpenAI, putting Claude in the hands of 100,000 employees and a thousand new Forward Deployed Engineers. The frontier labs split publicly over what comes next for workers: Sam Altman conceded he was wrong about a near-term jobs apocalypse, while Anthropic co-founder Chris Olah used a Vatican stage to insist labor displacement remains the central question. The Holy See has now made it one. Pope Leo XIV's first encyclical, Magnifica Humanitas, treats AI as the defining test of human dignity and asks states to "disarm" lethal autonomous weapons. Underneath it all, the physical layer keeps doubling: Nvidia booked $75.2 billion of data center revenue last week, and NextEra moved to buy Dominion for $67 billion to feed the Virginia data centers that consume it.

Fujitsu announced strategic partnerships with both Anthropic and OpenAI from its Kawasaki headquarters. About 100,000 Group employees will use Claude; a new 1,000-engineer Forward Deployed team will sell Claude-built systems to regulated Japanese industries.

Fujitsu Limited used a single morning press cycle to formalize what most large Japanese systems integrators have spent the year arranging quietly: full-frontier dependencies on both Anthropic and OpenAI. The Anthropic deal was announced jointly in Kawasaki by Takahito Tokita, Fujitsu's chief executive, and Paul Smith, Anthropic's chief commercial officer. The OpenAI partnership was disclosed simultaneously through Fujitsu's corporate channels. Both are framed as multi-year commercial agreements with two operative components: Fujitsu Group's internal use of the models across roughly 100,000 employees, and a customer-facing services business built on top.

The Anthropic announcement carries the more specific structure. Fujitsu will stand up a thousand-person Forward Deployed Engineer (FDE) team built around Claude, modeled on Anthropic's own FDE model that has driven much of the lab's enterprise penetration over the past eighteen months. Those engineers will be embedded into customer environments in finance, healthcare, defense, government and critical-infrastructure operations. Fujitsu's existing AI platform, Kozuchi, and its Takane large language model, developed jointly with Cohere, remain in the catalog. Claude joins as the frontier-tier option for tasks that need the most capable reasoning available.

Why Japan, why now

Fujitsu reported FY2026 consolidated revenue of 3.5 trillion yen, about $23 billion. It is the largest IT-services provider headquartered in Japan and one of the small number of vendors trusted by the national government on mission-critical systems. The Anthropic announcement positions Claude into precisely those environments: power generation, defense procurement, the social-security backbone. Anthropic's framing of "social infrastructure" reliability suggests it is positioning Mythos-class safety practices for the contract pipeline, even if Mythos itself remains restricted.

"We see the rapid evolution and growth of AI as something that must be swiftly implemented in society." — Takahito Tokita, Fujitsu CEO

The OpenAI deal is broader and less architecturally specific. Fujitsu will integrate OpenAI's models into its AI service portfolio and use them internally across development, operations, proposal-writing and service delivery. The press release uses the same "human and AI agents working together" framing OpenAI has been pushing in its enterprise sales motion through 2026. Fujitsu has not disclosed how it plans to route customer workloads between the two frontier providers, beyond saying both are now part of the supported menu.

The dual-vendor pattern

Fujitsu's dual signing fits a pattern that has become the default for large enterprise buyers this year. The biggest customers no longer pick a single frontier provider. They pick two and route based on cost, latency and capability per task. SAP did the same with Anthropic and OpenAI in May. Salesforce, ServiceNow and Workday have all moved to multi-frontier architectures. The losing position is the single-provider lock-in. The winning position, for the labs, is being on the menu.

For Anthropic, the deal extends a string of Asia-Pacific commercial wins. The Tokyo announcement comes one day after Anthropic disclosed its Seoul office and Korea Representative Director appointment. Japan and Korea are the two highest per-capita Claude markets outside the US, and both are markets where regulated industries pay a premium for vendor diligence on safety and reliability. The Fujitsu agreement, with its explicit cybersecurity-of-critical-infrastructure positioning, is the largest Japanese enterprise contract Anthropic has disclosed publicly.

Altman concedes the AI jobs apocalypse hasn't arrived. Anthropic disagrees from Rome.

Axios · 2026-05-27 · Source ↗

OpenAI's chief executive said he was "delighted to be wrong" about entry-level white-collar elimination. Anthropic co-founder Chris Olah, speaking at the Vatican two days earlier, restated the company's view that large-scale labor displacement remains imminent. Software-engineering job postings on Indeed are up 18 percent year-on-year.

The two best-resourced frontier labs in the United States now hold publicly opposite positions on AI's near-term effect on employment. Sam Altman, OpenAI's chief executive, told reporters this week that the jobs apocalypse he and others had publicly anticipated has not materialized in any visible labor-market data. Chris Olah, Anthropic's co-founder and the company's head of interpretability research, used a Vatican stage on Sunday to argue that the absence of an obvious crisis does not mean it is not coming.

"I'm delighted to be wrong about this. I thought there would have been more impact on entry-level white-collar jobs being eliminated by now than has actually happened." — Sam Altman, OpenAI CEO

The numbers Altman cited support his framing in the short term. Software-engineering openings on Indeed are up 18 percent year-on-year, against an overall job-listings drop of 4.3 percent. LinkedIn's chief economist has counted roughly 1.3 million new AI-related job postings over the period. US unemployment has ticked up modestly since 2023, but the rise has concentrated in sectors with the lowest AI exposure, not the highest. Software jobs, the bellwether category most often used as the proof-of-displacement story, are running counter to the doom forecast.

Anthropic's position

Olah did not address Altman directly, but the framing of his Vatican remarks made the disagreement explicit. Speaking at the launch of Pope Leo XIV's encyclical Magnifica Humanitas, Olah said the field has a duty to take seriously the prospect of large-scale labor displacement even while incentives push everyone in the room toward optimism. The remarks restated language that Anthropic chief executive Dario Amodei has used through 2025, including the much-quoted prediction that half of entry-level white-collar jobs could be automated within five years.

"There is a real possibility that AI will displace human labor at very large scale." — Chris Olah, Anthropic co-founder

The reframing of Anthropic's labor narrative away from product marketing and into Catholic moral philosophy is part of the company's broader positioning. Olah described frontier labs, Anthropic included, as operating inside incentive structures that occasionally conflict with doing the right thing. He named the need for external constituencies, including religious institutions, to "say hard things" the labs cannot. The encyclical and Olah's remarks together set up an explicit external check on the field that did not exist three months ago.

The IPO context

Both labs are heading toward exits. OpenAI is reportedly targeting a fourth-quarter 2026 IPO that would value the company near $1 trillion. Anthropic is rumored to be approaching its first profitable quarter on a $10.9 billion Q2 revenue run rate. Public-market posture matters more than usual. Telling potential retail investors that the technology you are selling will destroy their jobs is a hard pitch. Altman's pivot away from earlier doom rhetoric, which Fortune's Sharon Goldman characterized as walking back the "AI jobs apocalypse" line, is consistent with that read.

Other named voices weighed in. Commonwealth Bank of Australia chief executive Matt Comyn said the bank's productivity gains from AI deployments have so far been concentrated in software development and call-center work, not white-collar cognitive labor at scale. Uber chief operating officer Andrew Macdonald said the company has yet to identify a clean causal link between AI investment and headcount reduction. Sophia Velastegui, Microsoft's former chief AI officer, argued that the labor-impact lag is shorter than the public narrative assumes and that 2027 will be a different conversation.

Meta cut roughly 8,000 staff this month, citing efficiency. Coinbase, Block, Pinterest and Shopify have all tied restructuring decisions to AI-driven productivity. None of those cuts are visible in the aggregate national data yet, which is the data Altman pointed at to make his case. The disagreement between Altman and Olah is not a disagreement about whether AI will eventually transform the labor market. It is a disagreement about whether the curve has already bent.

A developer reads the receipts: Anthropic and OpenAI have product-market fit.

Simon Willison · 2026-05-27 · Source ↗

Willison adds up his own thirty-day API spend at the two labs, $2,180 against the $200 he paid in subscription. Anthropic's Q2 2026 revenue is rumored at $10.9 billion. SpaceX is paying Anthropic $1.25 billion a month for compute through 2029.

Simon Willison published a post on Wednesday arguing that, after two years of skepticism about whether anyone would pay real money for the cost of running large language models at scale, the data finally points in one direction. Anthropic and OpenAI have both demonstrated product-market fit on enterprise developer tooling specifically, and the revenue numbers are now too large for the field's "this won't last" argument to hold up.

The most readable part of the piece is the back-of-the-envelope math Willison ran on his own usage. Over thirty days, his Claude Code usage at the API-equivalent rate was $1,199.79. His Codex usage was $980.37. Total fair-cost: $2,180.16. He pays $200 a month for the two subscriptions combined. Anthropic and OpenAI both changed their pricing in April. OpenAI moved Codex to API-token billing on April 2, 2026. Anthropic adjusted its enterprise plan in November 2025 to $20-per-seat plus usage-based fees, and tightened further in April.

"I burned through $2,180 of token usage on a $200 subscription this month. That difference is the product-market fit." — Simon Willison, paraphrased

The revenue picture

Anthropic is reportedly headed to its first profitable quarter in Q2 2026, on a revenue run rate that internal sources have placed near $10.9 billion. Coding-agent customers are a disproportionate share of that. Cursor and GitHub Copilot together accounted for $1.2 billion of Anthropic's roughly $4 billion in revenue as of August 2025. The category has only grown since. The SpaceX-Anthropic compute agreement, disclosed earlier in May, contracts Anthropic for $1.25 billion a month in capacity from SpaceX's Colossus and Colossus II clusters through May 2029. That is a multi-year billion-dollar commitment to inference scale that only makes sense if the revenue continues to compound.

OpenAI's numbers are larger by every measure. ChatGPT crossed 900 million weekly active users in February, with 50 million paying subscribers. The April Codex pricing change appears to have moved aggressive Codex users onto bills more aligned to actual cost. Willison's reading is that this rebalancing, painful for individual power users in the short term, is the visible signal of a business that has graduated from subsidized growth to genuine unit economics.

The hiring signal

The most-cited evidence beyond the revenue figures is the open-roles data. OpenAI has 703 open positions, of which roughly 230 are enterprise-focused. Anthropic has 390 open positions, of which roughly 105 are enterprise-focused. The fact that one-quarter to one-third of all hiring at both companies is dedicated to commercial deployment, not research, is the structural sign of a business converting research output into recurring revenue. Two years ago that ratio was inverted at both companies.

The Willison post sits inside a larger set of evidence accumulating this quarter. Cognition's $1 billion round on Wednesday, Fujitsu's Tokyo deal the same morning, the Pope's encyclical and the Olah-vs-Altman labor debate are all downstream of the same underlying fact pattern: enterprise customers are now writing nine-figure annual checks for frontier-model inference, the unit economics are positive at sufficient scale, and the labs are competing for share of a real budget. Willison's framing is not that the future is settled. It is that the question of whether anyone would pay can be retired.

Policy Safety

Pope Leo XIV's first encyclical calls for AI "disarmament."

Vatican News · 2026-05-25 · Source ↗

Magnifica Humanitas, signed May 15 and released May 25, is the first papal encyclical devoted to AI. It condemns lethal autonomous weapons, names AI as a test of human dignity, and was launched alongside remarks by Anthropic co-founder Chris Olah.

Pope Leo XIV signed his first encyclical, Magnifica Humanitas, on May 15, 2026. The Vatican released it publicly on May 25, exactly 135 years to the day after Pope Leo XIII signed Rerum Novarum, the foundational document of modern Catholic social teaching on workers' rights. The choice of date and the choice of name were deliberate. The new pope, who took the regnal name Leo specifically as a tribute to his predecessor's social-doctrine legacy, has now placed the question of human dignity in the age of artificial intelligence into the same magisterial lineage as the Catholic Church's nineteenth-century response to industrial capitalism.

The document is addressed "to all the Catholic faithful, to all Christians and to men and women of goodwill." It runs to encyclical-standard length and is structured as a development of Catholic social teaching in light of AI, situating new questions about labor, identity, truth and the common good within the tradition that runs from Rerum Novarum through John Paul II's Centesimus Annus to Francis's Laudato Si'.

The argument

The encyclical names the now-familiar set of AI-era concerns: job insecurity, manipulation of information, privacy violations, ideological bias, lethal autonomous weapons, and the futuristic prospect of an "enhanced human being." Where it goes further than prior Vatican statements is in identifying a deeper anthropological danger underneath the policy menu: that human beings, in adopting AI, may begin to see themselves and one another as something to be perfected or surpassed.

"If the human being is treated as something to be perfected or surpassed, it becomes easier to accept that some lives are less useful, less desirable or less worthy." — Pope Leo XIV, Magnifica Humanitas

The document calls for what Pope Leo describes as the "disarmament" of AI, specifically condemning lethal autonomous weapons systems and asking states to renounce them. It pairs that condemnation with a separate apology, addressed to the historical record, for the Catholic Church's delayed condemnation of slavery, drawing a deliberate parallel between the moral pattern of late institutional response and the present moment.

Anthropic's response from the Vatican

The encyclical's public launch on May 25 included remarks by Chris Olah, Anthropic's co-founder. Olah used the platform to acknowledge that frontier AI labs, his own included, operate inside incentives and constraints that can sometimes conflict with the right course of action. He named the need for "people outside those incentives" who are willing to say hard things.

"Every frontier AI lab, including Anthropic, operates inside a set of incentives and constraints that can sometimes conflict with doing the right thing. It is enormously important that there be people outside those incentives who are willing to say hard things." — Chris Olah, Anthropic co-founder

Olah's remarks framed three discernment questions the Church might productively press on: duty to the global poor and to displaced workers; the moral imagination of what human flourishing actually requires; and the harder question of what AI models are, internally, in their grown-from-data architecture.

"They are grown, on a structure roughly modeled after the brain, on an enormous inheritance of human thought. We find internal states that functionally mirror joy, satisfaction, fear, grief, and unease. I don't know what that means." — Chris Olah, Anthropic co-founder

What it changes

The encyclical does not move policy in any government this week. What it does is move the moral framing. The Church's positions on labor (Rerum Novarum), capitalism (Centesimus Annus) and ecology (Laudato Si') have, over the long arc, shaped not just Catholic moral teaching but secular policy debates in countries with large Catholic populations. Magnifica Humanitas is the first major institutional moral framework that treats the question "what should AI not be allowed to do" as a question of human dignity rather than a question of competitiveness or technical risk. That reframing has long-term political effects that the immediate news cycle understates.

Policy

Trump cancels AI executive order signing hours before the Oval ceremony.

CNBC · 2026-05-21 · Source ↗

Tech, AI and cyber CEOs were already invited. The order would have directed federal agencies to shore up cybersecurity and work with the frontier labs on voluntary pre-deployment testing. Trump said he didn't like "certain aspects" and feared the order could dull the US lead over China.

President Donald Trump scrapped a planned Oval Office signing ceremony for a federal artificial-intelligence executive order on the afternoon it was scheduled, telling reporters he had not liked "certain aspects" of the order's text and that he was concerned it could undermine the United States' competitive lead over China. Major technology, AI and cybersecurity executives had already been invited to the White House to attend the signing. Most of them learned the event was off only hours before they were due to arrive.

The order, in the form circulated to the agencies that had drafted it, would have done two main things. It would have directed federal agencies to shore up cybersecurity defenses in line with new AI threat modeling. And it would have set up a voluntary framework under which the leading US frontier AI labs would submit advanced models for pre-deployment testing by the Center for AI Standards and Innovation and other federal evaluators. Three of those labs — Google DeepMind, Microsoft and xAI — had already publicly agreed earlier in May to a parallel set of pre-deployment agreements with the same agency.

"I didn't like certain aspects of it. I don't want to do anything that would interfere with our competitive position in AI." — Donald Trump, US President, to reporters on May 21

The internal split

The pulled signing is the most public sign of an internal disagreement inside the Trump White House between two factions on AI policy. One side, associated with the AI and cybersecurity offices, has been pushing for pre-deployment testing, voluntary safety commitments and a public-private partnership posture inherited and adapted from earlier executive efforts. The other side, drawing on the "America First" framing the president himself prefers, argues that any constraint on US frontier labs hands an advantage to Chinese competitors and should be avoided.

The order's drafters had worked through several months of revisions to satisfy both factions. The May 21 cancellation suggests the result did not satisfy either. The president told reporters he had reviewed the final text and decided he could not put his name to it without risking what he characterized as the country's strategic lead in AI. The order has not been formally withdrawn from circulation. White House officials told reporters it could return in revised form.

What was lined up

The voluntary pre-deployment-testing piece of the order would have formalized agreements that already exist on paper. The Center for AI Standards and Innovation announced in early May that it had reached pre-deployment testing arrangements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft and xAI. The order would have wrapped those agreements into a federal framework and pulled additional labs in. Anthropic and OpenAI, both notably absent from the May announcement, would have been the most obvious additions.

The cybersecurity component would have directed civilian federal agencies to update threat models in light of capabilities demonstrated by recent frontier models, including the capabilities that led Anthropic to restrict Claude Mythos Preview behind its Project Glasswing access program. That order would have been one of the few US federal policy moves explicitly tied to a specific frontier-model capability. Its delay leaves federal cybersecurity guidance running roughly six months behind the public state of the art.

NextEra buys Dominion for $67B to feed Virginia's AI data centers.

CNBC · 2026-05-18 · Source ↗

All-stock combination creates the largest regulated utility in the world, serving roughly 10 million homes and businesses in the southeastern US. Closing expected in 12-18 months pending state, federal and shareholder approval.

NextEra Energy agreed to acquire Dominion Energy in an all-stock transaction valued at approximately $67 billion, the companies disclosed on May 18. The combined entity would be the largest regulated utility business in the world, serving roughly 10 million homes and businesses across Florida, North Carolina, South Carolina and Virginia. The deal is expected to close in 12 to 18 months pending approval from state utility commissions, federal regulators and shareholders of both companies.

The strategic rationale is one number: data centers. Dominion is the regulated utility that serves Northern Virginia, the largest concentration of data center capacity on the planet. NextEra is the country's largest developer of regulated and merchant renewable generation. Combining the two creates a single counterparty that can underwrite the generation, transmission and grid-interconnect capacity that hyperscalers need to keep building Virginia data centers at the pace AI workloads demand.

The demand picture

US electricity consumption is growing at its fastest pace in decades, with the steepest growth concentrated in data center load. AI data centers alone are projected to consume between 15 and 25 percent of total US electricity demand by 2030. Northern Virginia's data center cluster has already exceeded the available generation capacity inside Dominion's service territory; new hyperscale builds are routinely waiting on multi-year interconnection queues. The capacity shortage is the binding constraint on how fast the cloud companies can deploy the inference and training compute they have already paid Nvidia for.

"America's demand for electricity is growing faster than at any point in recent memory. Data centers, manufacturing, transportation and everyday life all require more power, and that need is accelerating." — Joint NextEra-Dominion announcement

The combined company would own roughly 75 gigawatts of generation capacity at close, mixing Dominion's regulated nuclear and natural-gas fleet with NextEra's wind, solar and storage portfolio. Its regulated rate base would put it in a position to fund the multi-billion-dollar transmission build-out that Virginia needs and that Dominion alone cannot finance on its current balance sheet. The deal effectively redirects NextEra's renewable development pipeline into the load centers that need it most.

The regulatory path

State commission approvals will be the slow part. Virginia, North Carolina and South Carolina regulators all have to sign off on what would be one of the largest utility combinations in US history, with implications for retail electricity rates in all three states. Florida already houses NextEra's regulated subsidiary Florida Power & Light, the largest electric utility in the state. The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission will need to approve the transmission-asset combinations, and the antitrust review at the Department of Justice is expected to focus narrowly on PJM Interconnection market concentration rather than on the broader merger.

The transaction sits inside a year of consolidation moves driven by AI power demand. Earlier in May, I Squared Capital committed up to $1 billion to a US AI inference and edge colocation data-center platform. The Nvidia-IREN agreement to deploy up to 5 gigawatts of AI infrastructure announced in April moves on the same axis. The pattern is consistent: the physical layer of AI is now where the capital is flowing, and the utility sector is being reorganized to feed it.

Nvidia data-center revenue prints $75.2B, up 92% year-on-year.

Nvidia · 2026-05-20 · Source ↗

Q1 FY27 total revenue $81.6B (+85% YoY). Gross margins held above 74%. Board approved an additional $80B buyback and raised the dividend to $0.25 from $0.01. Blackwell 300 ramp drove the data center number.

Nvidia reported record fiscal first quarter FY27 results on May 20, 2026, for the quarter ended April 26. Total revenue was $81.6 billion, up 20 percent sequentially and up 85 percent year-on-year. Data center revenue, the line item that has driven the company's last three years of growth, was $75.2 billion, up 21 percent sequentially and up 92 percent year-on-year. GAAP gross margin was 74.9 percent. Non-GAAP gross margin was 75.0 percent. GAAP diluted earnings per share was $2.39.

The data center figure is the one to fix. At $75.2 billion of quarterly revenue, Nvidia's data center business is now annualizing above $300 billion. That single segment, run as a standalone company, would be among the ten largest businesses in the United States by revenue. It is the largest single source of inference and training compute capacity sold into the cloud market. Demand continues to outstrip supply, with the company's filings noting backlog measured in multiple quarters of capacity.

Blackwell 300 and what's next

The sequential growth came from the ramp of Blackwell 300, the latest generation in Nvidia's flagship AI accelerator family, plus continued strength in InfiniBand, Spectrum-X Ethernet and NVLink products that build out the rack-scale and cluster-scale interconnect. The company's networking revenue is now meaningfully larger than its consumer gaming business, a structural shift that took roughly eighteen months to complete.

Nvidia's Rubin platform, announced at GTC in March, is in full production. Rubin-based products are scheduled to ship from cloud partners AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft, Oracle Cloud Infrastructure, CoreWeave, Lambda, Nebius and Nscale in the second half of 2026. The OpenAI-Nvidia strategic partnership announced earlier this year commits Nvidia to deploy at least 10 gigawatts of systems for OpenAI, with Nvidia intending to invest up to $100 billion as the systems are stood up.

Capital returns and the buyback math

Two days before the print, on May 18, Nvidia's board approved an additional $80 billion share repurchase authorization and raised the quarterly dividend to $0.25 from $0.01. That dividend increase is symbolic: the company's earnings yield is still better used in buybacks at current valuations. The dividend bump is a signal to the institutional investor base that the company intends to remain a yield instrument as well as a growth one as the AI capex cycle matures.
