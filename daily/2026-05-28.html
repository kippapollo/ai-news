Cognition raises $1B at $26B as Devin's run-rate jumps thirteenfold in a year

Lux Capital, General Catalyst and 8VC co-lead a round that more than doubles the AI coding startup's September valuation. Cognition says annualized revenue has gone from $37M to $492M in twelve months, with Goldman Sachs, NASA, Mercedes-Benz and Santander on the customer list.

Bloomberg · 2026-05-27 · Source ↗ ·

Cognition closed more than $1 billion in fresh capital at a $25 billion pre-money valuation, putting the post-money mark at $26 billion. Lux Capital, General Catalyst and 8VC co-led the round, with Ribbit Capital, Atreides Management and Founders Fund joining and existing backers Elad Gil, Soma Capital and Omri Casspi following on. The deal more than doubles the $10.2 billion figure Cognition carried after its September 2025 round.

Bloomberg reports Cognition's annualized revenue run-rate has moved from $37 million in May 2025 to $492 million today, a 13× jump in twelve months. Cognition's pitch to investors leaned on enterprise usage growth that has compounded at 50% month-over-month for the past six months. The named customer roster on the round materials includes Goldman Sachs, Mercedes-Benz, NASA, Santander and several U.S. government buyers.

Devin, post-Windsurf

The flagship is Devin, the autonomous software-engineering agent Cognition unveiled in March 2024. The product runs in a sandboxed workstation, reads tickets, opens pull requests, and ships code with limited human review. After Google acqui-hired most of Windsurf's leadership team in July 2025, Cognition bought the remaining business and brought the Windsurf IDE under the Cognition umbrella, fusing an agent-first engineering tool with an IDE that already had paying enterprise seats.

That combination is the spine of the new round. Devin handles long-horizon work — multi-file refactors, dependency upgrades, ticket-to-PR cycles. The IDE that used to be Windsurf has become the surface developers actually live in. Cognition's pitch is that the agent and the editor are now one product, and the run-rate is the proof.

Competitive frame

The round lands during a brutal price war for the AI coding layer. Anthropic's Claude Code 2.1.152 shipped the same day Cognition announced the round, with a one-keystroke /code-review --fix that mirrors the agent workflow Cognition built into Devin. OpenAI's Codex update on May 27 added local conversation search, stronger MCP and CLI profile handling, and clearer tool schemas. Google's Jules sits one tier down, and xAI's grok-build-0.1 became broadly available to SuperGrok and X Premium+ users via Kilo Code the same week.

Simon Willison, writing the same evening, argued that the broader story is that Anthropic and OpenAI both crossed into "real product-market fit" because of coding tools, with enterprise pricing shifted from flat-rate seats to API-metered tokens in April. Cognition's $1B round is the venture market's version of the same thesis: the dollars are in coding agents, the agents are eating tokens, and the buyers will pay.

"Coding agents really did change everything. These are tools which burn vastly more tokens, but are also quickly becoming daily drivers for the work carried out by extremely well-compensated professionals."— Simon Willison, on the May 27 enterprise-pricing shift at Anthropic and OpenAI

What's not in the price

The round closed before Cognition disclosed a path to profitability. The company is burning aggressively on compute, paying Anthropic, OpenAI and self-hosted inference for every Devin run, and the disclosed run-rate is bookings rather than recognized revenue under a strict definition. Reporting in TechCrunch noted that Cognition's enterprise contracts include large multi-year minimums that pull future demand into the run-rate calculation. Investors are paying for a 50:1 multiple on annualized revenue and an even steeper multiple on cash receipts.

The other open question is durability. Anthropic and OpenAI host their own coding agents and have a structural cost advantage if the buyers begin to consolidate on a single foundation lab. Cognition's bet is that a coding-specific company can build distribution and workflow lock-in faster than the labs can build sales motions, which is the same bet Cursor was making before it raised at a $46B valuation, and the bet Replit and Vercel are making at lower valuations.

MUSE-Autoskill: self-evolving agents that create and manage their own skills

arXiv · 2026-05-27 · Source ↗ ·

A Huawei-led paper proposes a four-loop architecture for agents that propose new skills, store and prune them in memory, and evaluate their own performance. Skills emerge from execution traces rather than human authoring.

MUSE-Autoskill, posted to arXiv on May 27, attacks a problem most production agent systems hand-wave: how do you give an agent a growing library of reusable skills without a human writing every one? The Huawei-led team proposes a closed-loop design with four explicit modules — Skill Creation, Memory, Management and Evaluation — that let an agent extract a new procedure from a successful execution, store it with a description, and decide on later runs whether to recall, refine or retire it.

The Skill Creation step uses an LLM to abstract a successful trace into a parameterized procedure with an entry condition, a body and a postcondition. Memory keeps these in a skill bank with semantic search. Management runs heuristic and learned policies to prune, merge or rerank skills as the bank grows. Evaluation measures whether retrieved skills actually accelerate or improve subsequent runs.

The benchmarks the paper reports show a meaningful sample-efficiency gain on agentic tasks — the same model with MUSE-Autoskill scaffolding improves over 50 tasks versus a baseline that re-derives behaviour every time. The result is most interesting as a paper-and-implementation pair: code is published, and the architecture is small enough to retrofit onto a Claude Code or Codex setup with custom skills.

Why it shows up now

Skill primitives have been the bottleneck for long-horizon agents. Claude Code 2.1.152's reload-skills and disallowed-tools controls landed the same day MUSE-Autoskill posted. The pattern across industry and research is convergent: the next generation of agents are not just better at one-shot tasks, they remember and reuse what worked.

MobileMoE: scaling on-device mixture-of-experts

arXiv · 2026-05-27 · Source ↗ ·

A Meta-affiliated team proposes routing, expert caching, and quantization tricks to run mixture-of-experts models on phones at competitive quality with dense baselines.

MobileMoE goes after the obvious tension in on-device LLMs: dense models large enough to be useful are too heavy for a phone, but smaller dense models give up accuracy. MoE architectures activate only a fraction of parameters per token, which is good for inference cost, but they need fast expert-routing and aggressive caching to be practical on a 12 GB device. The paper proposes a hardware-aware routing scheme that biases toward warm experts, a tiered cache that keeps recent experts in DRAM and the rest in flash, and a quantization recipe tailored to expert sparsity patterns.

The headline experimental result: at the same active-parameter budget as a 3B dense model, MobileMoE matches or beats the dense baseline on standard benchmarks while sustaining usable token-per-second throughput on a phone-class SoC. The paper publishes the routing kernels for ARM-class chips, which is the part that turns the architecture from research into something Pixel and Galaxy teams can copy.

Read with

The on-device story has been gaining momentum since Apple's Foundation Models framework went general availability and Google's Gemini Nano started landing in Android subsystems. MobileMoE makes a credible case that MoE — long thought to be a datacenter-only architecture — can land on phones in 2026, which would compress the latency and privacy advantage of cloud inference.

FinHarness: an inline safety wrapper for finance LLM agents

arXiv · 2026-05-27 · Source ↗ ·

A new paper proposes a lifecycle harness for finance-domain agents that checks pre-trade intent, intra-execution invariants, and post-trade compliance. The system inserts itself between the agent and the broker API.

FinHarness, posted to arXiv on May 27, targets a class of incidents that has been growing alongside the spread of LLM agents into trading and treasury workflows: an agent issues a sequence of orders that look reasonable individually but breach mandate, limit or jurisdictional rules in aggregate. The paper proposes a runtime harness that wraps the agent's tool calls and enforces three checks: pre-action intent verification (does the next action match a declared goal and risk envelope?), in-flight invariant checks (are position limits and counterparty exposures honored after this trade?), and post-action compliance audit (does the resulting state satisfy regulatory reporting and mandate constraints?).

The harness uses a mix of formal specifications and learned classifiers. Formal specs handle hard constraints (no naked short on a banned name, no trade after a kill switch). Classifiers handle softer policy (this looks like wash trading, this looks like a recovery from a mistake versus a real strategy shift). The paper reports a low false-positive rate on synthetic-but-realistic trading streams and documents catch rates on a set of known-bad agent behaviors that earlier monitoring stacks missed.

Why now

The deployment context is unsubtle. JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs and a handful of large hedge funds have moved Claude- and GPT-class agents into research and middle-office workflows in 2026. Anthropic's Project Glasswing security suite was added the same month. The regulators are watching: the SEC's AI-related sweep letters have asked banks to document the supervision regime around any agent that touches the order management system. FinHarness is research, but the architecture maps directly to what compliance teams are now asking vendors to build.

Guiding LLM post-training with sparse-autoencoder internals

arXiv · 2026-05-27 · Source ↗ ·

A new paper uses features learned by sparse autoencoders on a base model to score and curate post-training data, claiming better downstream task performance than pure heuristic filters.

Post-training data engineering — picking which conversations, completions and synthetic examples to fine-tune on — has been an art with heuristic tooling: length filters, dedup pipelines, classifier-based quality scoring. The May 27 paper takes a different cut. It trains a sparse autoencoder on the base model's internals, gets a set of interpretable features (refusal, hallucination, instruction-following, math-reasoning, etc.), and uses those features to score candidate post-training examples.

The proposed pipeline keeps examples whose feature activations match the desired downstream profile, downweights examples that fire features correlated with bad behavior (toxic, sycophantic, fabricated), and rebalances coverage across feature clusters that the model is weak on. The result is a data-engineering loop that uses the model's own internal representations as the curation signal.

The paper reports gains over standard heuristic filtering on instruction-following and math benchmarks for a frontier-scale base model. It also shows that the sparse-autoencoder features used for scoring are stable across model checkpoints, which makes the technique reusable without redoing the SAE training each release.

Bigger frame

Sparse autoencoders have moved from interpretability curiosity to load-bearing tool over the past eighteen months. Anthropic and DeepMind have both used them for safety steering. This paper is the first prominent attempt to use SAE features for data curation at scale, and it lines up with what frontier labs have been hinting at: the post-training stage now uses the model to evaluate the model.
