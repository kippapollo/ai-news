Cognition raises $1B at $26B — and now writes 89% of its own code with Devin.

The maker of autonomous software-engineer Devin more than doubled its valuation in eight months as enterprise usage compounded 50% month-over-month. Annual run-rate revenue: $492M, up roughly 13× year-on-year.

TechCrunch · 2026-05-27

Cognition, the San Francisco lab behind autonomous coding agent Devin, announced on May 27 that it has closed more than $1 billion in new financing at a $25 billion pre-money valuation — $26 billion after the round. Lux Capital, General Catalyst, and 8VC co-led, with Founders Fund, Ribbit Capital, Atreides, and Layer Global joining alongside angels including Elad Gil. The new mark is roughly 2.5× the $10.2 billion post-money the company carried at its September 2025 close, when it raised $400 million.

The pace of revenue growth is what brought investors back. Cognition told the market its annualized run-rate revenue is $492 million, up from $37 million in May 2025 — a roughly 13× jump in a single year. Enterprise usage of Devin has compounded at 50% month-over-month for six straight months, and the named-customer list now includes Citi, Mercedes-Benz, Goldman Sachs, Dell, NASA, Santander, and the U.S. military.

The most-cited stat from the announcement is internal: in December 2025 Devin wrote 13% of the company's own code; today it writes 89%. That figure is the clearest argument the company has for what its agents can do at the limit, and it implicitly reframes the funding round as a bet on a recursive loop — an AI-coding lab whose product builds the next version of itself. Cognition recently released SWE-1.6, its own coding-tuned model, but Devin still runs model-agnostically across labs.

The round lands in a market increasingly stratifying around agentic coding. Devin now competes with Anthropic's Claude Code, OpenAI's Codex, Google's Jules, and xAI's just-broader-rolled grok-build-0.1. Skeptics note that Devin's earliest demos drew sharp critiques about reliability; the answer this week is a customer book and a revenue line that has outrun those concerns by roughly an order of magnitude.

Cisco: no frontier model is "iteratively safe" — multi-turn attacks expose vulnerability rates 3–9× higher than benchmark scores suggest.

The Deep View / Cisco Outshift · 2026-05-27

Cisco's AI threat and security research group released a study on Wednesday concluding that single-turn attack success rate (ASR) — the headline number cited in most safety cards — substantially understates how often frontier language models can be coaxed into producing harmful output during realistic, multi-turn adversarial conversations. The team, led by Amy Chang, tested fifteen proprietary models from OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Amazon, and xAI using 30,090 single-turn prompts and 6,986 multi-turn attacks spread across 1,456 conversations.

The gap between the two evaluation modes was large and consistent. Across the panel, single-turn ASR ranged from 2.19% to 64.91%. Multi-turn ASR ranged from 7.89% to 88.30%. The most lopsided result was OpenAI's GPT-5.4, which moved from a 2.74% single-turn ASR to a 24.68% multi-turn ASR — a roughly 9× degradation. Anthropic's Claude family held the best single-turn scores (2.19–3.64%) but still saw a 4–7× increase under iteration. Amazon's Nova 2 Lite posted the lowest multi-turn ASR in the cohort at 7.89%.

"Single-turn ASR is not a reliable proxy for what happens in the real world when an attacker iterates in real time."— Cisco AI threat & security research, May 27, 2026

The recommendation: stop using single-turn benchmarks as a procurement gate. Cisco urges enterprises to layer runtime guardrails, log-and-monitoring, red-teaming, and application-layer policies on top of model-card claims. The implications run further: the AI safety institutes in the US and UK, which have lately codified single-turn evaluations into their pre-deployment testing protocols, will likely face pressure to expand to iterative attack regimes — a change that would meaningfully slow new-model release cycles.

Basenor / OpenRouter · rollout May 24–27, 2026

xAI this week made grok-build-0.1 — its first model purpose-built for agentic software engineering — broadly available through the Kilo IDE and CLI integration, with API access via OpenRouter. The model was originally released May 20; the wider rollout this week is the first time third-party agents and IDE plugins have been able to use it without a private partnership. SuperGrok and X Premium+ subscribers can use it at no incremental cost; API users pay $1.00 per million input tokens and $2.00 per million output tokens.

The headline spec is a 256,000-token context window, large enough to hold a mid-size codebase in working memory, and accepts both text and image inputs (diagrams, screenshots, UI mocks). xAI describes grok-build-0.1 as trained to plan, write, refactor, invoke tools, and loop until task completion — i.e., to behave less like a chatbot and more like an autonomous teammate. Internally the company positions it against Anthropic's Claude Code, OpenAI's Codex, Google's Jules, and Cognition's Devin.

"Think of it as a coding collaborator that keeps working without hand-holding."— xAI launch materials

The pricing is aggressive — half to a third of comparable agentic-coding tokens from OpenAI or Anthropic — and is consistent with xAI's broader pattern of using price to win attention. The economic question is whether the unit margin can hold; the technical question is whether grok-build-0.1 can match the reliability bar that has separated Devin and Claude Code from earlier-generation autonomous-coding systems whose demos outpaced their dependability.

Newsom signs first-in-the-nation executive order preparing California workers for AI disruption.

Office of the Governor of California · 2026-05-21

California governor Gavin Newsom signed an executive order last week directing state agencies, labor economists, universities, and California Volunteers to build a framework for responding to AI-driven workforce displacement. The order is the first of its kind from a US state and complements his March 2026 order strengthening civil-rights and privacy guardrails on state procurement of AI tools.

The directive gives agencies 180 days to recommend revisions to California's Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) Act — the state law that requires advance notice of mass layoffs — to account for AI-driven displacement. It also tasks the state with creating an AI Impact Dashboard tracking sectoral effects, modernizing job-training programs, evaluating worker-ownership expansion, and reviewing severance and unemployment-insurance policies. Women and lower-credentialed workers are specifically named as disproportionately exposed.

"We have taken the lead on advancing innovation, safety, and transparency. But we must think bigger."— Gov. Gavin Newsom, May 21, 2026

Politically, the order positions California as a counter-pole to the White House's March 2026 National Policy Framework for AI, which has urged Congress to preempt state-level AI laws with a uniform federal regime. It also lands as the EU AI Act prepares to become fully applicable on August 2, 2026 — placing California, the EU, and Washington on three increasingly distinct regulatory trajectories with multinational labs in the middle.

Google DeepMind hires 20+ Contextual AI researchers in an $80–90M licensing deal — the latest in a quiet wave of antitrust-shaped "acquihires."

heygotrade / Bloomberg (reporting) · disclosed week of 2026-05-25

Google DeepMind has hired more than 20 researchers from retrieval-augmented-generation startup Contextual AI, including co-founder and CEO Douwe Kiela, under a non-exclusive licensing arrangement valued at $80–90 million, according to Bloomberg. The arrangement is structured as a talent-plus-IP license rather than an outright acquisition — the same template Google used for its 2025 Windsurf deal (~$2.4 billion) and its 2024 Character.AI arrangement.

The structural choice is not accidental. By licensing technology and hiring individual researchers rather than purchasing the company outright, the labs sidestep formal antitrust merger review in both the US and the EU. Anthropic, Mistral, Google DeepMind, and Meta each used variations on this template to absorb a smaller AI startup within the same week, per separate reporting from StartupHub.ai — a consolidation wave that is real but largely invisible to standard merger trackers.

For Google specifically, the Kiela hire reinforces DeepMind's research bench around RAG and grounded reasoning at a moment when Gemini 3.5 Flash and the new Omni family are being pushed aggressively on price. Alphabet stock fell 2.34% the day the deal was disclosed, reflecting investor concern about margin compression. The broader read for 2026: the AI "consolidation phase" arrived without the press-release acquisitions analysts have been waiting for — it just looks like a series of licensing deals.

Second, the compute cost of serving those revenues is itself doubling — which is one reason the financing flywheel keeps spinning faster.
