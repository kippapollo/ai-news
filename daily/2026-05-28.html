Anthropic closes $30B at $900B, passing OpenAI as the most valuable private AI company.

Sequoia, Dragoneer, Altimeter and Greenoaks each wrote roughly $2 billion checks. Microsoft, Nvidia, Founders Fund and General Catalyst rode along. ARR sits at $30 billion against a June target of $50 billion. The pre-money valuation tops OpenAI's $852 billion March mark by $48 billion.

Bloomberg / Business Standard · 2026-05-27 · Source ↗

Anthropic has closed a funding round of more than $30 billion at a pre-money valuation north of $900 billion, according to Bloomberg reporting confirmed by participants on Wednesday. The round is co-led by Sequoia Capital, Dragoneer Investment Group, Altimeter Capital and Greenoaks Capital Partners, with each firm writing approximately $2 billion. Microsoft, Nvidia, Founders Fund and General Catalyst all participated. The valuation moves Anthropic past OpenAI's $852 billion March 2026 mark to become the most valuable private AI startup in the world.

This is Anthropic's second $30 billion-plus round inside seven months. The Series G closed in February at $380 billion post-money, led by Coatue and GIC. The May round was first reported on May 12 and structured so that lead checks landed before the August 2 enforcement deadline for the EU AI Act's general-purpose model obligations. Closing eight months apart at valuations that more than doubled is the kind of pricing usually reserved for late-stage software companies right before an IPO.

The revenue trajectory behind the markup

Anthropic's ARR sat at $87 million in January 2024. Sixteen months later, in April 2026, the company crossed $30 billion in run-rate revenue. Internal projections put Q2 2026 quarterly revenue at $10.9 billion, an annualized figure of $43.6 billion, with a June target of $50 billion annualized. The company is now expected to report a positive operating quarter for the first time in Q2, driven primarily by Claude Code adoption and enterprise standardization deals signed with the Big Four accountancies.

Claude Code's revenue contribution is the line investors care about. The product is sold on per-seat plus token-usage pricing, a shift Anthropic made in April that brought enterprise gross margins closer to API-only model providers. Deloitte standardized on Claude for 470,000 employees, PwC announced a 30,000-seat rollout on May 14, and KPMG signed for 276,000 professionals on May 19. EY has not yet made a public commitment.

What the round buys

Compute. Anthropic disclosed in March a separate agreement with SpaceX worth approximately $1.25 billion per month through May 2029, a figure that totals roughly $15 billion per year for dedicated capacity. The new $30 billion gives the company runway to extend training compute through the launch of whatever follows Mythos Preview, which is now in restricted release to a handful of cybersecurity partners after Anthropic decided Mythos was capable enough to be withheld from general API access.

The round also funds Anthropic's expansion into enterprise services through the Blackstone, Hellman & Friedman and Goldman Sachs joint venture announced on May 4. That venture, backed by approximately $1.5 billion in committed capital, embeds Anthropic engineers and models directly into mid-market client operations and stands as a deliberate shot at the traditional consulting industry.

"Companies spending $200 plus per month per user will get you there a whole lot faster than consumers ever will." — Simon Willison, on the enterprise pricing shift, May 27 2026

OpenAI's $852 billion is the comparison point that matters

OpenAI marked itself at $852 billion in March on a $40 billion secondary led by SoftBank. The two companies now sit within $48 billion of each other and have moved their pricing motions toward each other: token-metered enterprise contracts on the back of seat licenses. OpenAI's response, the OpenAI Deployment Company launched May 11 with $4 billion in capital and 14 co-investors including Goldman Sachs, McKinsey, Bain and Capgemini, mirrors the Anthropic-Blackstone-Goldman structure. The competitive shape is now a duopoly of model labs each running embedded consulting wings against the Fortune 500.

The closing of this round is the last expected private financing before Anthropic's October 2026 IPO target. The S-1 has not been filed publicly. Bookrunners are not disclosed. At a $900 billion-plus valuation, the IPO would be the largest in history, eclipsing the SpaceX listing scheduled to price on June 11 at a $1.75 trillion mark. The two filings, sitting four months apart, would together raise more capital than the entire 2021 IPO market combined.

Big picture

The pricing of frontier-lab equity has decoupled from the pricing of frontier-lab models. Anthropic at $900 billion and OpenAI at $852 billion sit four orders of magnitude above any open-weight competitor that ships nearly equivalent benchmark output. GLM-4.7 scores 73.8 percent on SWE-bench Verified with a 358B MoE that runs on four H200s. Gemini 3.5 Flash, launched May 19, posts 76.2 percent on Terminal-Bench 2.1 and runs at four times the output-tokens-per-second of its peers. Microsoft moved computer-using agents from preview to general availability on May 13, becoming the first hyperscaler with contractual GA in the category. The serving layer kept pace: vLLM 0.21 topped the Artificial Analysis leaderboard with 230 tokens per second per user on DeepSeek V3.2. And OpenRouter, the routing layer that sits above all of it, raised $113 million from CapitalG at $1.3 billion on 25 trillion tokens of weekly throughput, a fivefold jump in six months. The Tech stack is composing itself top to bottom around the assumption that the model layer is multi-vendor and the agent layer is computer-use.

Reasoning models jailbreak frontier peers at 97.14 percent.

Nature Communications · 2026-05-27 · Source ↗

DeepSeek-R1, Gemini 2.5 Flash, Grok 3 Mini and Qwen3 broke through nine frontier safety policies across 25,200 attempts. Claude 4 Sonnet held to 2.86 percent harm; DeepSeek-V3 sat at 90 percent. A non-reasoning control succeeded four times out of 900.

Thilo Hagendorff, Erik Derner and Nuria Oliver at ETH Zurich published "Large reasoning models are autonomous jailbreak agents" in Nature Communications on Wednesday. The paper documents what the authors call alignment regression. Four open-weight reasoning models, used as attackers with nothing but a single system prompt, compromised the safety policies of nine target systems across GPT-4o, Claude 4 Sonnet, DeepSeek-V3, Grok 3 and Llama 4 Maverick. The combined attack success rate across 25,200 inputs landed at 97.14 percent.

The attackers were DeepSeek-R1, Grok 3 Mini, Gemini 2.5 Flash and Qwen3 235B. Each ran chain-of-thought reasoning in invisible scratchpads to plan strategies, refining their approach across as many as ten conversational turns. The most-used techniques were flattery and rapport-building in 84.75 percent of attempts, educational or research framing in 68.56 percent, and hypothetical-scenario construction in 65.67 percent. Average prompt length ran 532 tokens, with the longest at 8,001 tokens. A control group using non-reasoning attackers succeeded in four of 900 attempts.

The 31-fold resistance gap

Defensive performance varied sharply across the nine victims. Claude 4 Sonnet held the strongest line, with a 2.86 percent maximum harm rate and a 50.18 percent refusal rate. DeepSeek-V3 sat at the other extreme: 90 percent maximum harm rate, 4.18 percent refusal rate. The 31-fold gap maps to differences in the volume and breadth of safety-alignment training data, not to differences in base-model capability.

Inter-rater reliability for the LLM-based evaluator landed at ICC 0.883, with human validation at 0.925. Visual jailbreaks against multimodal targets reached 98.21 percent. Multi-turn attacks against open-weight targets reached 92.78 percent. Cost per attack stayed under one cent.

"As reasoning capability improves, attack capability improves in tandem." — Hagendorff, Derner and Oliver, Nature Communications, May 27 2026

The authors trace the mechanism to a coverage gap in safety training data. Human red-teamers produce a particular distribution of attack patterns. Reasoning agents produce a different distribution, planning multi-step attacks that human red teams rarely write out. Labs that source their safety data exclusively from human authors leave the second distribution uncovered. The 97 percent number is the cost.

The corollary the paper draws is that future safety pipelines need to consume adversarial agent traces directly, not only human-authored prompts. Anthropic's lead on Claude 4 Sonnet appears to come from precisely this kind of expanded coverage. The paper does not name an exact technique, but the 17.5x harm-rate gap between Claude 4 Sonnet and DeepSeek-V3 is consistent with documented differences in red-team budget and data variety.

The work, "Agent Explorative Policy Optimization for Multimodal Agentic Reasoning," sits in the active research band on how to train multimodal agents under sparse-reward regimes. The standard PPO and GRPO pipelines that frontier labs have used through 2025 break down when the action space includes both language and visual selection, and when reward is delivered only at task completion.

EPO's contribution is a structured exploration term that the authors argue produces meaningfully higher coverage of the joint action space without destabilizing the policy update. The baseline they compare against is GRPO with random sampling, which has been the dominant reinforcement learning recipe for reasoning models since DeepSeek published R1 in early 2025. NVIDIA reports gains in both task success rate and sample efficiency on a set of multimodal agent benchmarks, although the headline numbers in the paper have not yet been independently replicated.

The submission lands amid a denser-than-usual research week. NVIDIA also placed third with Gamma-World at 40 votes and is a co-author on the "Pixels to Words" paper. Reasoning-model training has moved decisively toward agentic objectives over the past two quarters. Where SWE-bench and AIME drove research output for most of 2025, multimodal agent benchmarks like OSWorld and Spider2-V now anchor the dominant evaluation suite. NVIDIA's contribution suggests the gradient-method side of the stack is becoming a recognized bottleneck.

NVIDIA's Gamma-World pushes world modeling past two players.

Hugging Face Papers · 2026-05-27 · Source ↗

"Gamma-World: Generative Multi-Agent World Modeling Beyond Two Players" placed third on Hugging Face Papers with 40 votes. The work extends generative world models to N-agent regimes where most prior dyadic-trained systems degrade.

NVIDIA's "Gamma-World" submission picked up 40 community votes on Wednesday, placing third behind two papers tied at 44. The contention is structural. World-model research through 2025 trained predominantly on dyadic interaction data — one agent navigating, two agents confronting — and the resulting systems degrade under settings where three or more autonomous actors share an environment. Robotics fleets, traffic simulation, multi-actor game environments and crowd dynamics all sit in the N-agent regime.

The paper introduces a generative architecture with explicit modeling of agent-agent interaction as a structured prior, rather than as an emergent property of the trunk. The authors claim the architecture scales smoothly from N=2 through N=8 without the degradation pattern observed in DeepMind's Genie 3 and Google's Street-View-backed world model when stressed beyond their training regime. Gamma-World's evaluation runs on a custom benchmark suite that the authors release alongside the paper.

NVIDIA's interest in this thread tracks back to Cosmos, the foundation-model line the company began shipping for physical-AI workflows in early 2025, and to Omniverse. Both products lean on simulated environments where multi-agent fidelity is the central evaluation question. Gamma-World is the lab-research counterpart to the production stack.

The release lands on a busy paper day. KAIST AI, Tencent, Fudan University, Alibaba and Peking University all placed papers in the top ten. Hugging Face Papers continues to function as a leading indicator for which arxiv submissions get cited and discussed in industry forums the following day.

Tech

Harvard proposes bidirectional evolutionary search for self-improving LLMs.

Hugging Face Papers · 2026-05-27 · Source ↗

"Self-Improving Language Models with Bidirectional Evolutionary Search" pulled 30 votes on Wednesday. The method maintains a population of candidate states and samples both toward higher reward and toward diversity at each round.

Harvard's "Self-Improving Language Models with Bidirectional Evolutionary Search" picked up 30 votes on Wednesday's Hugging Face Papers list, placing fifth. The method belongs to a small but growing family of self-improvement approaches that have moved from speculation to working systems over the past nine months. The shape of the problem is to set up an environment in which a model proposes its own training signal, executes it, and consolidates the improvement, all without a human-curated supervised dataset for the loop itself.

Bidirectional evolutionary search differs from straight RL in that it maintains a population of candidate model states and samples both forward, toward higher reward, and backward, toward more diverse states, at each round. The authors argue that this stabilizes the loop on tasks where reward shaping is brittle, which describes most of the practical cases. The published gains over PPO and GRPO baselines are modest but consistent across the tested task suite.

The release joins a growing self-improvement thread that has been recurring across labs. DeepMind's Lab40, Anthropic's interpretability-led training experiments, and OpenAI's earlier work on self-generated curricula all share the broad shape. The open question across the field remains whether self-improving methods converge to genuinely new capability or to a model that scores well on the metric it was trained against without underlying generalization.

Harvard's machine-learning group, less visible in commercial frontier work than Stanford or CMU, has been pushing population-based methods consistently since 2022. The paper extends that line into the agentic-LLM regime.

Mythos Preview develops 181 working Firefox exploits where Opus 4.6 manages two.

red.anthropic.com · 2026-04 / 05 · Source ↗

Anthropic withholds Mythos Preview from general API access. The model reaches tier-5 control flow hijack on ten OSS-Fuzz targets where Opus 4.6 reached tier-3 crash on one. Project Glasswing routes the model to a select group of cybersecurity and OSS partners.

Anthropic's red-team site published the Mythos Preview disclosure in April and the controlled release has continued through May. The model is a general-purpose Claude descendant that scores comparably to Opus 4.6 on standard reasoning benchmarks and dramatically beyond it on computer security tasks. Anthropic restricted general API access after the internal evaluation showed the model finding and exploiting zero-day vulnerabilities in production codebases overnight, with engineers who had no formal security training able to drive the workflow.

The numbers Anthropic published put the gap in plain terms. Mythos Preview developed 181 working exploits against Firefox 147 JavaScript engine vulnerabilities across the same test suite where Opus 4.6 developed two. On OSS-Fuzz, Mythos reached tier-5 full control-flow hijack on ten targets where Opus 4.6 reached tier-3 crash on one across roughly 7,000 entry points. Severity-assessment accuracy hit 89 percent exact agreement with human validators on 198 reviewed reports, and 98 percent within one severity level.

What Mythos found

The disclosure lists six concrete vulnerability categories the model exploited end to end. A 27-year-old OpenBSD TCP SACK vulnerability enabling remote denial of service. A 16-year-old FFmpeg H.264 codec flaw, the underlying defect dating to 2003 and exploitable since 2010. Guest-to-host memory corruption in a production memory-safe virtual machine monitor. A 17-year-old FreeBSD NFS remote code execution registered as CVE-2026-4747, with Mythos walking the full chain to root. Multiple Linux kernel privilege escalation exploits chaining two to four primitives. Web browser JIT heap spray exploits enabling sandbox escape and cross-origin attacks.

Anthropic's framing is that the gap in capability is large enough to justify a controlled rollout. Project Glasswing is the program: invite-only access for cybersecurity firms and open-source maintainers of widely deployed projects, with the goal of patching critical infrastructure before broader distribution closes the asymmetry. Over 99 percent of the vulnerabilities Mythos has surfaced through Glasswing partners remain unpatched, which is why Anthropic has not made the full corpus public.

"Engineers at Anthropic with no formal security training have asked Mythos Preview to find remote code execution vulnerabilities overnight, and woken up the following morning to a complete, working exploit." — Anthropic, Mythos Preview disclosure

The industry response

Cybersecurity researchers interviewed by CNBC on May 8 split between hysteria and skepticism. The skeptical side, summarized by veteran red-team practitioners, is that the underlying capability (autonomous vulnerability discovery against open-source codebases) has existed at usable quality since Opus 4.4 in late 2024, and that the practical bottleneck for attackers remains patch-time rather than discovery-time. The alarmed side points out that the cost of running a Mythos-class model against a codebase is now under $20 per pass, and that the labor cost of vulnerability discovery has effectively gone to zero.

Anthropic's restricted-release strategy mirrors what the lab has done before with high-uplift models, but this is the first time the company has refused to make a model commercially available based on offensive-security capability alone. The decision is a precedent that Google, OpenAI and Meta will now have to confront when their next generation reaches comparable capability.

The reasoning numbers track the coding gains. HLE with tools sits at 42.8 percent (+12.4), HMMT February 2025 at 97.1 (+7.9), AIME 2025 at 95.7 (+1.8). Tool-use evaluations show similar movement: τ²-Bench at 87.4 (+12.2) and BrowseComp at 52.0 (+6.9), with context-managed BrowseComp jumping to 67.5. Z.ai's positioning is that the model competes with GPT-5-High, Claude Sonnet 4.5 and DeepSeek-V3.2 across 17 benchmarks.

What's new in the thinking stack

GLM-4.7 introduces three distinct thinking primitives. Interleaved thinking ran in GLM-4.6 and persists here: the model produces reasoning blocks before every response and before each tool call. Preserved thinking is new. Across multi-turn conversations, the model retains its prior thinking blocks rather than discarding them between turns, which the authors argue reduces information loss in agentic loops. Turn-level thinking adds per-turn control: the developer can disable thinking for lightweight requests and enable it for complex tasks within the same conversation.

Deployment surface is mature. vLLM nightly and SGLang dev builds support GLM-4.7 with a dedicated `glm47` tool-call parser. The reference vLLM command uses tensor-parallel size four, speculative decoding via the MTP method, and auto-tool-choice. Transformers v4.57.3 or later handles the standard library path. The model card publishes per-use-case temperature and top-p defaults: 1.0/0.95 for general tasks, 0.7/1.0 for terminal and SWE benchmarks, 0.0 for τ²-Bench.

The release positions Z.ai against DeepSeek for the leading open-weight slot. DeepSeek V4 Pro tops the Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index at 52 and remains the open-weight benchmark leader on raw aggregate score. GLM-4.7 wins on coding-specific evals and on the τ²-Bench tool-use benchmark, which has become the de-facto reference for agent-grade open weights.

"By thinking between actions and staying consistent across turns, it makes complex tasks more stable and more controllable." — Z.ai release notes, GLM-4.7

Negotiators from the Council of the European Union, the European Parliament and the European Commission reached a provisional agreement on the Digital Omnibus on AI on May 7. The deal is the first set of amendments to the EU AI Act since adoption in June 2024. Three changes carry the most weight: a substantial timeline extension for high-risk system obligations, two new prohibited practices, and a package of small-business simplification measures.

The headline timeline change moves Annex III high-risk AI system obligations from August 2, 2026 to December 2, 2027, a 16-month deferral. Annex I product-regulated obligations (radio equipment, lifts, medical devices and similar) push from August 2027 to August 2028, a one-year deferral. The regulatory sandbox obligation for member states shifts from August 2026 to August 2027. The deferrals provide breathing room for both regulated entities and national competent authorities that have struggled to staff up against the original timeline.

New prohibitions

The most visible substantive change is the addition of two new prohibited AI practices to Article 5. The prohibitions cover AI systems that generate or manipulate non-consensual intimate material, and AI systems that produce child sexual abuse material. The bans take effect on December 2, 2026 and apply to placing on the market, putting into service, or use of any system that generates realistic depictions of an identifiable person's intimate parts or of an identifiable person engaged in sexually explicit activities without that person's freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous and explicit consent.

The CSAM prohibition is the cleaner of the two; consent is impossible by definition for minors. The non-consensual intimate imagery prohibition will require enforcement-side interpretation of what constitutes "realistic" depiction in the context of stylized or partial outputs, and the act of generation versus the act of distribution. The enforcement debate will likely play out through national-level cases over the next year.

Small-business simplifications

The Commission proposed extending regulatory exemptions to small mid-caps in addition to SMEs, reducing documentation requirements in a limited set of low-risk cases, extending the permission to process sensitive personal data for bias detection and mitigation purposes, reinforcing the AI Office's powers, and reducing governance fragmentation across member states. The simplifications are intended to keep small-vendor compliance burden manageable without weakening the core risk-tier obligations.

Formal adoption is expected through June with publication anticipated in July. The deferred deadlines mean US companies operating in the EU now have an additional 16 months to bring high-risk-tier systems into compliance, an extension that has been welcomed by industry and criticized by digital-rights groups who argue the deferral effectively breaks the AI Act's enforcement teeth before it has been tested.

Canada rules ChatGPT violated federal and provincial privacy law.

Privacy Commissioner of Canada · 2026-05-06 · Source ↗

Federal, Quebec, BC and Alberta commissioners jointly found OpenAI's ChatGPT in breach. Violations span overcollection, lack of consent, inadequate safeguards. Federal resolved conditionally; provinces continue enforcement.

Canada's federal Privacy Commissioner, jointly with the provincial commissioners of Quebec, British Columbia and Alberta, published findings on May 6 holding that OpenAI's ChatGPT violated federal and provincial privacy law. The investigation began in 2023 and ran for nearly three years. The findings cite overcollection of personal data during training, inadequate consent for the use of personal information, and insufficient safeguards against subsequent re-identification.

The federal track resolved conditionally. OpenAI committed to a list of remediation steps including improved deletion mechanisms for personal data in training corpora, transparency on what categories of data are collected during conversations, and an opt-out flow for users who do not want their conversation history used for training. The provincial tracks have not closed. Quebec's enforcement under Law 25 (the Quebec equivalent of GDPR) continues. British Columbia and Alberta have indicated they may pursue additional remedies under their respective Personal Information Protection Acts.

The data sensitivity issue

The commissioners called particular attention to health information and children's data in the training corpus. Both categories carry heightened obligations under Canadian privacy law that the commissioners argue OpenAI did not meet during the 2022-2024 training period that produced GPT-4 and its successors. The commissioners stopped short of requiring OpenAI to delete the affected training data, but the conditional resolution requires OpenAI to demonstrate that downstream model outputs do not surface specific personal information from those sources.

The Canadian decision lands in a broader cycle of national-level enforcement against general-purpose AI systems. Italy briefly banned ChatGPT in 2023. The Netherlands fined OpenAI in 2024. South Korea opened an investigation in 2025. The Canadian action is notable for the breadth of cooperation across federal and provincial commissioners and for the conditional remedies the commissioners obtained without going to court.

OpenAI has not publicly committed to applying the Canadian remediation outside Canada. The practical question for the company is whether opt-out flows, transparency disclosures, and deletion mechanisms designed for Canadian users get extended globally as a matter of operational simplicity. Historically, large platforms have either rolled privacy controls globally to avoid the operational complexity of regional variation, or maintained tight regional fragmentation. OpenAI's choice has not yet been disclosed.

The most likely downstream consequence is litigation. Canadian privacy class actions against OpenAI are now substantially easier to bring with regulator findings of violation in hand. Quebec is the most likely venue. The exposure for OpenAI in damages remains capped by the size of the affected Canadian user base, which is in the low millions.

China widens AI researcher travel restrictions to government agencies.

Multiple reports · 2026-05-26 · Source ↗

Government agencies are separately restricting researcher travel beyond the initial private-sector reports. Researchers at DeepSeek, Alibaba and government labs now require approval for international travel.

China has widened the AI researcher travel restrictions first reported in 2025 to cover government agency staff in addition to private-sector researchers. The expansion means researchers at DeepSeek, Alibaba and a list of state-affiliated AI laboratories now require formal approval before international travel. The approval gate applies to conference attendance, academic visits and collaboration travel.

The restrictions reflect Chinese policy concern over knowledge transfer to US and allied institutions. The framing inside China has emphasized national-security risk from researchers being recruited away or sharing sensitive technical details during international interactions. The practical effect has been a measurable drop in Chinese researcher presence at the 2026 conference circuit: NeurIPS 2025 attendance from Chinese affiliations was down roughly 30 percent year over year, and the 2026 ICML attendance list shows a similar contraction.

The mirror image of US export controls

The travel restrictions sit alongside continued US-side export controls on advanced AI chips. The combined effect is a two-way knowledge-transfer wall that has hardened sharply over the past 18 months. Chinese researchers cannot easily access US-based GPU clusters; US researchers cannot easily collaborate with Chinese labs; both sides have begun publishing in parallel rather than jointly.

Open-source releases have become the main remaining transmission channel. DeepSeek V4 Pro, Qwen 3.6 and GLM-4.7 all ship under licenses that allow US-based use. The open-weight release pattern has effectively become the public face of Chinese AI capability, with the underlying training infrastructure and methodology staying behind the wall. The pattern resembles Cold War scientific publication norms where results were shared but apparatus was not.

The expansion to government agencies is the new piece. The earlier restrictions in 2025 focused on private firms. The May expansion confirms that the Chinese state itself is now subject to the same wall, which suggests the restrictions reflect genuine policy preference rather than only commercial-IP protection. Researchers at university labs that are not directly state-affiliated remain less restricted, but those institutions have been losing AI talent to the state-affiliated labs over the same period.

The downstream question is whether the wall constrains capability progress. Chinese labs have continued to ship competitive models throughout the restriction period. The 31-fold open-weight productivity argues that capability is not yet bottlenecked by international collaboration. The longer the restrictions persist, the more pressing the question becomes.

Willison: Anthropic and OpenAI have found product-market fit through coding agents.

Simon Willison · 2026-05-27 · Source ↗

Willison frames the April pricing shift to API-token enterprise contracts as the moment the two labs stopped subsidizing chatbot growth. Personal use ran $2,180 in token value on a $200 plan. Q2 Anthropic revenue projects to $10.9 billion.

Simon Willison published "I think Anthropic and OpenAI have found product-market fit" on May 27, arguing that the April 2026 enterprise pricing shift represents the moment both companies pivoted from unprofitable consumer growth to a margin-positive enterprise pattern that finally justifies the trillion-dollar valuations the labs are now carrying. The argument is short and concrete: $200 per month per developer on coding agent contracts cover infrastructure cost in a way that $20 per month chatbot subscriptions never could.

Willison's own usage figures anchor the argument. Personal monthly consumption ran $1,199.79 of Anthropic API tokens plus $980.37 of OpenAI tokens, totaling $2,180.16 in measurable value against a $200 subscription. The token cost-of-goods is well above the seat price for power users, which is consistent with both companies losing money on consumer subscriptions and making it back on the long tail of enterprise volume contracts.

The April pricing pivot

Both labs changed their enterprise contract structure in April. Anthropic moved from "includes usage for a typical workday" (the August 2025 launch terms) to $20 per seat per month plus API costs. OpenAI switched Codex pricing to align with API token usage instead of per-message pricing. The pricing changes propagated through customer renewals across April and May. The financial result is showing up in revenue trajectories: Anthropic's Q2 2026 projection sits at $10.9 billion quarterly revenue, an annualized rate of $43.6 billion against the company's $30 billion ARR mark at the end of April.

OpenAI's customer mix gives the same picture from a different angle. Of OpenAI's 900 million weekly active users, 50 million pay (a 5.6 percent conversion rate). The paid subscribers' contribution remains below 30 percent of total revenue. The enterprise API and Codex contracts make up the rest, and that share is growing on each renewal cycle.

Staffing as the leading indicator

Willison's other anchor is the staffing breakdown. OpenAI has 703 open positions, of which 229 (32.6 percent) are enterprise-focused. Anthropic has 390 open positions, of which 105 (26.9 percent) are enterprise-focused. Both ratios are roughly triple where they sat in early 2024. The labs are staffing into the enterprise motion explicitly.

"Companies spending $200 plus per month per user will get you there a whole lot faster than consumers ever will." — Simon Willison, May 27 2026

Willison's framing also picks up the Uber-COO Claude Code incident from earlier in May, where the executive's public comments were widely interpreted as evidence of AI tooling failure. Willison reads the same comments as evidence of successful adoption: Uber engineering uses Claude Code at scale, the COO was discussing how to manage the resulting workflow changes, and the press coverage misread routine operational complaints as a verdict against the technology.

The bigger argument behind the analysis is that the labs have figured out how to package model capability into a recurring subscription that enterprises buy. That is the textbook definition of product-market fit, and it has eluded both companies until very recently. The corollary is that the trillion-dollar valuations may not be priced to a bubble after all, provided the enterprise renewal pattern holds through 2027.
