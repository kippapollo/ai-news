Anthropic tells the Senate that Alibaba ran 28.8 million Claude conversations through 25,000 fake accounts to distill Mythos Preview, and Hong Kong sells Alibaba to a sixteen-month low

A June 10 letter from Anthropic to the Senate Banking Committee, made public on June 25 by CNBC and Bloomberg, accuses Alibaba's Qwen lab and affiliated entities of running roughly 28.8 million conversations through about 25,000 fraudulent Claude accounts between April 22 and June 5 to distill capabilities from the Mythos Preview model. Anthropic calls it the largest known distillation attack against the company, names DeepSeek, Moonshot and MiniMax as prior offenders at smaller scale, and points to the Pentagon's addition of Alibaba to its Chinese military companies list earlier this month. Alibaba's Hong Kong shares fell as much as 4.9 percent on the report, hitting a sixteen-month low, with ADRs down 3.99 percent on June 24 and Baidu and Xiaomi also off more than three percent.

CNBC · Bloomberg · Anthropic letter · 2026-06-25 · Source ↗ ·

Anthropic sent a five-page letter to Senator Tim Scott of South Carolina and Senator Elizabeth Warren of Massachusetts, the chair and ranking member of the Senate Banking, Housing and Urban Affairs Committee, on June 10, 2026. CNBC and Bloomberg obtained copies of the letter and published their reporting on June 24, with the wave of follow-up coverage and the market reaction landing through June 25. The letter accuses Alibaba and entities affiliated with the company's Qwen large-language-model lab of operating a coordinated campaign to distill capabilities from Anthropic's Mythos Preview model, using roughly 25,000 fraudulent customer accounts to run approximately 28.8 million model exchanges over a six-week window beginning April 22 and ending June 5. Anthropic frames the campaign as the largest distillation attack the company has documented to date and notes that the bulk of the traffic was directed at coding and agentic-reasoning capabilities, precisely the surfaces where Mythos Preview was setting state-of-the-art numbers before its global suspension.

The mechanics that Anthropic alleges

Distillation, in the technical sense the letter uses, is the process of training a smaller or weaker model to imitate the outputs of a stronger one by harvesting a large corpus of input-output pairs and using them as supervision. The economics are attractive to a follower lab: every dollar Anthropic spent training Mythos Preview is converted into a cheap supervision signal for the imitator, which gets the resulting capability without the research bill. Anthropic's letter lays out a fingerprint pattern across the 25,000-account cohort. The accounts were created with disposable email addresses and prepaid payment methods. They drew traffic in tightly correlated bursts. They concentrated on the same narrow set of evaluation-style prompts that a researcher would use to construct a training corpus rather than the broader distribution of real-world Claude usage. The letter names three prior campaigns Anthropic has tied to other Chinese labs at smaller scale: DeepSeek at roughly 150,000 exchanges, Moonshot AI at 3.4 million, and MiniMax at 13 million. The Alibaba volume, at 28.8 million, is more than double Moonshot and MiniMax combined.

"Alibaba has carried out the largest known distillation attack on Anthropic to date."— Anthropic, letter to Senator Tim Scott and Senator Elizabeth Warren, June 10 2026

The policy frame that puts the letter in front of Banking, not Commerce

The choice of committee is deliberate. Anthropic could have routed the complaint through the Bureau of Industry and Security at the Department of Commerce, which oversees export controls and which already issued a June 12 directive treating Mythos 5 and Fable 5 as restricted exports. Sending it to Senate Banking instead pushes the question into the investor-protection and capital-markets domain. Alibaba is a US-listed issuer, traded on the New York Stock Exchange as BABA. If a foreign issuer is engaged in conduct that violates the terms of service of US commercial counterparties and that may be tied to a Chinese-military designation, the question of whether US investors should be exposed to it becomes a banking-committee question. The letter explicitly notes that Alibaba "ignored the Trump Administration's warnings" and that the company was added to the Pentagon's Chinese military companies list earlier in June. The committee can do little directly. The framing matters: it invites the Treasury Department's Office of Foreign Assets Control, the SEC, and the cross-agency Outbound Investment Rule machinery into a conversation that has, until now, mostly been Commerce's.

The market response and the China-AI selloff

Hong Kong sold the news. Alibaba's H-shares fell as much as 4.9 percent on June 25 and closed at a sixteen-month low, taking the year-to-date decline to roughly 33 percent. The US-listed ADRs dropped 3.99 percent on the prior trading session. The broader China-AI complex traded in sympathy: Xiaomi and Baidu both fell more than 3 percent. Bloomberg's coverage framed the selloff as evidence that the cohort of Chinese AI champions, which had rallied on Qwen, DeepSeek and MiniMax releases earlier in the year, now faces a discount for regulatory and access risk that western models do not. Goldman Sachs analysts noted in a separate Friday-morning note that the discount may persist through the next earnings cycle if Beijing's response is muted, and may compound if Beijing retaliates against US firms operating in China.

"Chinese technology companies could face tougher hurdles in the global AI race, even though they have continued to offer reasonably priced products."— Bloomberg analysis of the Alibaba selloff, June 25 2026

Inside Anthropic's nine-day product suspension

The letter lands in the middle of an unprecedented commercial decision at Anthropic. Mythos 5 and Fable 5 have been suspended for paying customers worldwide for nine consecutive days, since June 17. The trigger described publicly in WIRED's reporting earlier in the week was a White House intervention against SK Telecom's Project Glasswing access, plus a separate Amazon-environment jailbreak. The Alibaba distillation campaign now reads as a third concurrent pressure on the same product surface. The cybersecurity industry open letter pressing Anthropic to restore Mythos access for vetted defenders passed one hundred signatures earlier in the week and now sits at one hundred and seventeen names, including chief information security officers from Nvidia, Google, Adobe, Zoom and Sophos. Anthropic's own product page reframed the suspension on June 19 as a two-track Trusted Access program: Mythos 5 to Glasswing-class cybersecurity partners "in consultation with the US government", and Fable 5 with the biology and chemistry safeguards removed for vetted biomedical research.

What the letter does and does not ask for

Anthropic does not ask the Senate Banking Committee for a specific remedy. The letter requests "consideration" of the conduct and offers to brief committee staff on the technical evidence. The pattern matches a public-pressure playbook more than a legal action: write to the committee that creates the most reputational risk for the target, share the letter with reporters who cover the committee, let the market and the policy ecosystem do the work. Two structural questions follow. The first is whether Anthropic will pursue a civil claim. Terms-of-service violations of the scale described would, in any other commercial context, support tortious interference or breach-of-contract claims and conceivably Computer Fraud and Abuse Act exposure if the accounts were created with fraudulent payment instruments. The second is whether Alibaba responds with a substantive technical rebuttal or with the kind of denial it issued through Reuters on June 24, calling the letter "speculative and politically motivated." A substantive rebuttal would require Alibaba to produce its own training-data audit. Neither outcome is straightforward, and neither lands in the next twenty-four hours.

Google promotes computer use from a standalone Gemini 2.5 model launched in October 2025 to a native tool inside Gemini 3.5 Flash, letting one agent see a screen, query Search and call Maps in a single call.

Google moved computer use out of the experimental Gemini 2.5 Computer Use model, which it launched as a standalone API in October 2025, and into a native tool inside Gemini 3.5 Flash. Until June 25 an agent that needed to see a screen, click a button and also answer a domain question had to route between the computer-use model and the general-purpose Gemini chat model. The native tool collapses that into a single Gemini 3.5 Flash call that composes screen interaction with function calling, Search grounding and Maps lookups. Google reports a score of 78.4 on OSWorld-Verified, the standard benchmark for desktop agents on real applications, placing Gemini 3.5 Flash fifth of sixteen entries on the leaderboard. GPT-5.5 sits one position ahead at 78.7. Claude Fable 5 leads at 85.0 and Claude Opus 4.8 follows at 83.4, both of which are currently behind the Trusted Access gate.

The pricing comparison is the part that will move workloads. Gemini 3.5 Flash is priced at $1.50 per million input tokens and $9 per million output tokens. GPT-5.5 is $5 input and $30 output. For an agent that spends most of its day reading and acting on a screen, the per-task cost difference is roughly a factor of three to four at parity capability. Google also extended the tool into Gemini in Chrome, which gains a Select from screen affordance that hands a marquee region of the browser viewport to the model as the input frame. Gemini 3.5 Flash itself improved 13.3 points generation over generation, with the prior Gemini 3 Flash scoring 65.1 on OSWorld-Verified. The launch lands on the same day Google quietly retired two Gemini API previews, gemini-3.1-flash-image-preview and gemini-3-pro-image-preview, redirecting traffic to the generally-available Nano Banana 2 and Nano Banana Pro.

"Computer use is now a built-in tool supported in Gemini 3.5 Flash, delivering our best performance yet for agentic computer use tasks."— Google announcement, June 25 2026

The unanswered question is Gemini 3.5 Pro. Sundar Pichai targeted general availability for June at Google I/O on May 19, and the launch window has now closed without a Pro release. Multiple scoops on June 24 attributed to DeepMind cited dissatisfaction with the current state of the model and pushed the target out of the month. The 3.5 Flash computer-use release reads as Google's compensating move: ship the part of the 3.5 family that is ready, get it priced aggressively, and let agent developers pick it up while the Pro flagship continues to bake.

Claude Code 2.1.191 ships /rewind across /clear, sandbox credential lockdown, and a 37 percent CPU cut on streaming responses

DevelopersIO · Anthropic · 2026-06-25 · Source ↗

Anthropic's coding harness lands a broad reliability and performance update on June 25, letting /rewind reach back across a /clear, blocking credential reads inside sandboxes, and cutting streaming CPU by roughly 37 percent through 100-millisecond text-update coalescing.

Claude Code's June 25 release covers the work between v2.1.187 on June 23 and v2.1.191 on June 24-25, with v2.1.188 and v2.1.189 skipped on the npm registry. The headline behavior change is that /rewind now resumes a conversation from before /clear was run. Until this build, /clear was effectively destructive: the conversation was gone, and the only recovery path was to reissue the prompts. /rewind crossing the clear boundary makes the command honest about what it does, which is hide the conversation from the model context rather than delete it.

The security change is a new sandbox.credentials setting. When enabled, the sandbox prevents reads of credential files and secret environment variables from inside the sandboxed process. This closes a class of supply-chain attacks where a tool call would read a developer's AWS or GitHub credentials from inside the sandbox and exfiltrate them. Organization-level model restrictions now apply across the model picker, command flags and configuration, removing the previous gap where an enterprise allow-list could be bypassed by command-line flag. The performance change is the 37 percent CPU reduction on streaming, achieved by coalescing text-delta updates into 100-millisecond windows rather than rendering each delta individually.

MCP server reliability got a broad pass. Capability discovery calls (tools/list, prompts/list, resources/list) now retry transient network errors with exponential backoff. OAuth discovery and token requests retry once after network issues. Headless environments skip the browser popup that previously stalled remote login. The build also fixes five-minute MCP tool hangs, Korean and CJK character corruption, hooks with comma-separated matchers failing silently, and the long-standing bug where the managed setting forceRemoteSettingsRefresh did not take effect, leaving stale proxy responses cached.

"Reduced CPU usage during streaming responses by approximately 37 percent by coalescing text updates to 100ms."— Claude Code 2.1.191 release notes

The release lands two days after Anthropic shipped Claude Tag in Slack on Opus 4.8, which the company said internally writes 65 percent of its product team's code. Claude Code's own ergonomics are a precondition for that internal usage scaling outside Anthropic.

Hugging Face Daily Papers crowns Tencent's Beyond NL2Code multimodal-code-intelligence survey at 263 upvotes leading 32 papers on June 25

Hugging Face Daily Papers · 2026-06-25 · Source ↗

The June 25 Daily Papers feed surfaces 32 papers led by Tencent's 263-upvote NL2Code survey organizing the field into four domains, with Intern Robotics EBench at 95 and Shanghai Jiao Tong's agent-memory benchmark at 88.

The Tencent-led survey, Beyond NL2Code: A Structured Survey of Multimodal Code Intelligence, takes the top of the Hugging Face Daily Papers leaderboard for June 25 at 263 upvotes. Nineteen co-authors organize the multimodal-code-generation field into four domains: GUI generation, scientific visualization, structured graphics and frontier frameworks. The argument is that the standard NL2Code framing collapses too much: a model that can generate a Python script for a numerical computation is not the same model that can generate the layout-aware code for a dashboard, and the benchmarks that have grown up around the old NL2Code framing systematically underweight the second case. The survey proposes a taxonomy that splits the work along axes the existing leaderboards do not surface.

The runner-up of the day is Intern Robotics' EBench at 95 upvotes. It diagnoses generalist mobile manipulation across 26 tasks tagged along five capability dimensions (long-horizon, dexterous-and-precise, mobile, spatial, vision-language) and four generalization dimensions (object, scene, instruction, embodiment). The headline finding is that models with near-identical scalar success rates exhibit strikingly different capability profiles: pi-0.5 leads on retention from train to test, InternVLA-A1 dominates mobile manipulation but collapses on dexterous, and XVLA owns a disjoint set of atomic skills. The implication is that the existing single-scalar leaderboard hides which model is right for which task.

Shanghai Jiao Tong's Are We Ready For An Agent-Native Memory System at 88 upvotes treats agent memory as a first-class data-management object and benchmarks twelve memory systems plus two baselines across five workloads on eleven datasets. The team decomposes agent memory into representation-and-storage, extraction, retrieval-and-routing and maintenance modules. The headline finding is that no architecture wins everywhere: graph-based methods excel on single-hop factual recall but struggle with temporal reasoning, localized maintenance is more cost-efficient than global reorganization, and conservative consolidation beats aggressive summarization. Code is at github.com/OpenDataBox/MemoryData.

"Correctness depends not only on syntax but also on layout, data semantics, interaction behavior, and domain-specific constraints."— Xuanle Zhao et al., Beyond NL2Code (arXiv:2606.15932)

The day's open-source releases include the Wan-Streamer v0.1 unified Transformer (66 upvotes), the iLLaDA-8B diffusion language model (30 upvotes), StepFun's ShutterMuse plus the 130,000-sample CaptureGuide-Dataset (37), KAIST AI's MVTrack4Gen for 4D video generation (26), NVIDIA's Causal-rCM distillation recipe (17) and Meta AI's Autodata agentic data scientist (8). Several open-domain video-subject papers, a Domain-MoT design called DomainShuttle at 55 and the OpenDataBox MemoryData benchmark code drop sit alongside.

Intern Robotics EBench evaluates four generalist manipulation models across 26 tasks, exposes profile divergence at near-identical success rates

arXiv · Hugging Face Papers · 2026-06-25 · Source ↗

EBench replaces single-scalar success rates with a 26-task diagnostic battery across five capability and four generalization dimensions, ranking pi-0, pi-0.5, XVLA and InternVLA-A1 with sharply different profiles.

Intern Robotics' EBench paper, arXiv 2606.18239, takes 95 upvotes on Hugging Face Daily Papers for June 25. Lead author Ning Gao, with Jinliang Zheng, Xing Gao, Haoxiang Ma, Hanqing Wang and twenty other co-authors, argues that the current generalist-manipulation leaderboards conceal more than they reveal. The headline metric, scalar success rate, can be identical across two models that fail in entirely different ways.
