Apple's WWDC opens Monday with a Gemini-powered Siri rebuild and Tim Cook's last keynote

Bloomberg's June 5 preview confirms Apple will hand Siri's reasoning core to a custom 1.2-trillion-parameter Google Gemini model under a roughly $1-billion-a-year arrangement, ship a standalone Siri chatbot app, and let iOS 27 users pick a default AI provider from ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Grok, Copilot or Perplexity. The keynote is the last for Tim Cook before he hands the CEO seat to John Ternus on September 1. The agentic features extend across iOS 27, macOS 27, iPadOS 27, watchOS, visionOS and tvOS, and the conference streams from 10 a.m. PT Monday on the Apple Developer app, apple.com and the company's YouTube channel.

Bloomberg / TechCrunch / MacRumors · 2026-06-05 · Source ↗ ·

Bloomberg's Mark Gurman published the canonical WWDC 2026 preview on the morning of June 5, with TechCrunch and MacRumors filing parallel rundowns the same day. The headline item is a complete rebuild of Siri around a custom 1.2-trillion-parameter Google Gemini model. Craig Federighi confirmed earlier this spring that Apple scrapped the hybrid stack that married legacy Siri to in-house Apple Intelligence, and the team rebuilt the assistant from scratch. The Gemini deal is reported at roughly $1 billion a year, with the model running on Google's private cloud under Apple's privacy terms. The keynote opens Monday, June 8 at 10 a.m. PT / 1 p.m. ET, with simultaneous streams on the Apple Developer app, apple.com and Apple's YouTube channel.

The Siri rebuild ships in two surfaces. The first is the in-place assistant invoked from any app and the lock screen, which gains multi-step task handling and richer context across calendars, mail and documents. The second is a standalone Siri chatbot app modeled on ChatGPT and Gemini's mobile clients, with conversation history, multi-turn threading and three retention options: thirty days, one year, or indefinite. The retention picker is a quiet but consequential change. Until now Apple's voice assistant had no concept of a saved conversation; the new app treats conversations as documents that the user owns and configures.

Letting users pick their AI

iOS 27 will introduce a system-level AI provider setting, the way iOS today lets users pick a default browser or email client. The provider list named in the previews is ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Grok, Microsoft Copilot and Perplexity. The selected provider becomes the answering engine for system queries, third-party app intents and writing assistance, with Apple's own Apple Intelligence as the local on-device tier for tasks that stay on the phone. The move concedes that Apple cannot ship a competitive frontier model in 2026 while also turning every iPhone into a distribution surface for the labs that can.

"This will be the most significant overhaul to Apple's flagship voice assistant since Siri's 2011 launch, rebuilding from the ground up to compete with ChatGPT, Claude and Gemini." — Mark Gurman, Bloomberg, on the Siri rebuild

The agent layer and the App Store

An agent layer sits above the Siri rebuild. The agentic capability lets Siri take actions across apps: book reservations, control smart home devices, edit photos, draft documents, plan multi-step travel. App developers can publish "agent endpoints" that declare what an app can be asked to do, and Siri composes those endpoints into multi-step task graphs at runtime. The TechCrunch piece describes this as a tacit "agent app store" without the formal branding: discoverability flows through Siri's task suggestions rather than a separate storefront, but the developer-economics shape is similar.

The Photos app is the second-biggest surface in the previews. Apple is shipping intelligent scene recommendations, automatic object removal, natural-language editing ("make this look like a daylight shot", "remove the trash can behind me"), and a "describe a change" editing option that runs against on-device diffusion. Image Playground gets a Genmoji suggestion engine and an AI wallpaper generator. Visual Intelligence, the camera-mode lookup tool launched on iPhone 16, gains a Google Image Search backend for general object identification, replacing the limited Apple-only knowledge graph that shipped at launch.

The Tim Cook coda

The keynote is widely reported as Cook's last as CEO. Apple is expected to announce a September 1 transition under which Senior Vice President of Hardware Engineering John Ternus takes over as chief executive. Cook will move to chairman of the board. Both Apple and the involved press have not confirmed the dates on the record, but the consistency across Bloomberg, MacRumors and 9to5Mac since late May suggests an announcement during or immediately after the keynote. The Siri rebuild lands at the worst possible moment for Apple's AI narrative and the best possible moment for a generational handover: the company concedes the frontier-model race externally while resetting its operating leadership internally.

Hardware updates expected at the keynote include the public Vision Pro 2 introduction, with a lighter headband and the M5 chip, plus a refreshed HomePod with a small display and a new Apple TV that runs Apple Intelligence locally. The full iOS 27 / macOS 27 / iPadOS 27 / watchOS 13 / visionOS 3 / tvOS 27 lineup ships to developers Monday and to the public in September. Apple Wallet picks up bill-splitting and a "Create a Pass" tool for arbitrary digital passes. The AppleCare team is expected to demonstrate a refreshed support agent. The keynote runs roughly two hours, followed by the Platforms State of the Union later in the day.

CVPR 2026 opens in Denver with 4,089 accepted papers and multimodal AI doubling its share

TechTimes · 2026-06-05 · Source ↗ ·

The 43rd IEEE/CVF Conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition opened Friday at the Colorado Convention Center with 16,092 submissions (up 24 percent year-over-year), a record 4,089 accepted papers (a 42 percent jump), and 10,000-plus registered attendees. Vision-language and multimodal LLM research grew from 4.9 to 10.6 percent of highlighted papers, the sharpest single-category shift in recent CVPR history.

CVPR 2026 opened Friday at the Colorado Convention Center in Denver for a main program running June 3 through 7. Submissions hit 16,092, a 24 percent jump over 2025 and the largest single intake in the conference's history. Of those, 4,089 were accepted, a 25.4 percent acceptance rate and a 42 percent jump in accepted-paper volume from 2,878 the prior year. Registered attendance topped 10,000 scientists and engineers, the largest CVPR on record. Program co-chairs Alexander G. Schwing of the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign and Chen Change Loy of Nanyang Technological University led the review effort.

The sharpest signal in the program data is the trajectory of vision-language and multimodal large-language-model research. Among the conference's highlighted papers, the top-of-distribution sample representing about 12 percent of accepted work, vision-language and multimodal LLM papers grew from 4.9 percent to 10.6 percent of the set year over year. Generative and multimodal papers together moved from roughly 14 percent to 22 percent. Embodied AI papers, which treat perception as the input to physical action rather than the endpoint of recognition, nearly doubled their share, a structural shift from pure perception toward integrated systems.

Where the field is concentrating

The top five submission categories in declining order were image and video synthesis and generation, vision-language and reasoning, multi-modal learning, 3D reconstruction from multi-view and sensors, and medical and biological vision and microscopy. The composition makes clear that diffusion and autoregressive image generation, multimodal LLMs and embodied AI together absorbed most of the field's growth, while classic detection, segmentation and tracking categories grew more slowly or held flat.

"The sharpest signal in the CVPR 2026 data is the trajectory of vision-language and multimodal large language model research, which nearly doubled its share of highlighted work." — TechTimes coverage of CVPR 2026

Highlighted papers and authorship patterns

Among the highlighted papers covered in the opening writeups: NitroGen, a generative training pipeline from NVIDIA, Stanford, Caltech, the University of Chicago and UT Austin; MagicBokeh, a portrait-mode synthesis paper from the Shenzhen Institutes of Advanced Technology and vivo BlueImage Lab; R2Seg, a relational reasoning segmentation paper from Carnegie Mellon, Cambridge, Zhejiang University, ETH Zurich and Illinois; and a Diffusion Security paper from Yan Pang and Tianhao Wang at the University of Virginia analyzing privacy leakage in deployed diffusion models. The authorship patterns show the standard CVPR mix of US-China industrial-academic collaboration, with the European university footprint smaller than at CVPR 2025 by a small but visible margin.

The conference runs through Sunday June 7 with workshops and the awards ceremony Saturday. The accepted-paper volume puts CVPR ahead of NeurIPS and ICML for the third year running and consolidates its position as the most-cited computer science venue. The structural data point is the multimodal-LLM share: where a vision conference five years ago would have been dominated by pure perception, the 2026 edition reads as the venue where vision research has merged with the language-model program.

AGIBOT WORLD CHALLENGE 2026 runs the embodied-AI final on real G2 humanoids at ICRA Vienna

AGIBOT / ICRA 2026 · 2026-06-05 · Source ↗ ·

AGIBOT hosted the World Challenge 2026 alongside ICRA 2026 in Vienna with 526 teams from 27 countries competing across two embodied-AI tracks, Reasoning to Action and World Model. The offline final ran on AGIBOT G2 humanoids in real-world scenarios. The competition explicitly moved scoring from simulation benchmarks to closed-loop trials on physical robots and long-horizon task reliability.

AGIBOT staged the AGIBOT WORLD CHALLENGE 2026 in Vienna on June 5 as a colocated event with ICRA 2026, the IEEE robotics and automation flagship conference. The competition drew 526 research and enterprise teams from 27 countries across two embodied-AI tracks. The Reasoning to Action track asked models to convert natural-language instructions into multi-step plans grounded in robot perception. The World Model track tested models for their ability to predict the next observation given an action history, the foundation capability behind model-predictive planning for physical agents.

The structural change in the 2026 edition is the scoring move from simulation to physical. The offline final ran finalist teams' models on AGIBOT G2 humanoid robots, the company's mass-production-targeted research platform, in real-world task scenes. Stability, physical-world adaptability and long-horizon task reliability sat at the center of the evaluation rubric, rather than the simulation-throughput metrics that dominated past embodied-AI competitions. AGIBOT framed the shift in its announcement materials: simulation scores have saturated, and the field's open questions are whether models generalize across the sim-to-real gap and whether they handle the rare failure modes that only physical robots produce.

Why the venue matters

ICRA is the largest academic robotics conference. Running a leaderboard challenge in the ICRA program rather than as a vendor-organized side event signals that embodied AI has the same kind of competitive benchmarking infrastructure that ImageNet brought to vision a decade ago. The 27-country team count is roughly twice the participation of past embodied-AI competitions, and the breakdown between academia and industry is reported as roughly even, with new entries from Chinese and European teams that did not compete in 2024 or 2025.

"The competition placed robot stability, physical-world adaptability, and long-horizon task reliability at the center of the scoring system, moving evaluation from simulation toward closed-loop testing on real robots, real tasks, and standardized benchmarks."— AGIBOT, on the 2026 evaluation framework

The hardware standardization argument

AGIBOT runs the contest on a single hardware platform, the G2, which standardizes the test substrate the way ImageNet standardized the training set. Standardizing the robot lets teams compete on the model and the policy stack rather than on hardware tuning. The compromise is that the leaderboard reflects performance on AGIBOT G2 specifically; whether the winning policies transfer to other humanoid platforms is an open question the post-event analysis will need to address. Generalist AI, Physical Intelligence, Skild AI, Figure AI, 1X Technologies and Nvidia's GR00T team did not compete formally but were present as observers; the embodied-AI competitive map is still being drawn.

Winners will be announced at the ICRA closing ceremony Sunday June 7, with results, code submissions and replay data published the following week on the AGIBOT World Model Hub. The competition organizers indicated that the 2027 edition will add a long-horizon mobile-manipulation track designed around full-day deployments rather than the minute-scale episodes that defined 2026, an explicit attempt to bring competitive evaluation closer to commercial robotic deployment.

Google's Texas Meitner campus pairs a data center with 1 GW of dedicated wind, solar and storage in a "power-first" template

Data Center Knowledge · 2026-06-05 · Source ↗ ·

Google's Meitner site in Texas, the lead build inside Google's Intersect acquisition, pairs a state-of-the-art data center with more than 1 GW of dedicated wind, solar and battery storage on a co-located generation campus. The framing is "power first": developers secure generation alongside compute load instead of bolting demand onto existing grid capacity and waiting four to ten years for an interconnect.

Data Center Knowledge published the canonical writeup of Google's Texas Meitner campus on June 5. The site is the first Google build that puts the "power-first" approach into practice: a data center developed in lockstep with a co-located 1-plus-GW generation campus of wind, solar and battery storage. The Intersect acquisition Google closed in December 2025 brought the developer expertise that the design rests on, and Meitner is the proof-of-template build that demonstrates the model.

The framing inverts the standard hyperscaler buildout. Today the typical flow is: a hyperscaler picks a site for tax incentives and fiber connectivity, signs a power purchase agreement with the local utility, and waits as much as a decade for the utility to upgrade transmission and bring new generation online. The Meitner model picks the site for generation potential, develops the power campus alongside the data center, and reduces dependence on regional transmission upgrades. The colocated generation runs the data center directly and helps balance the local grid rather than competing with it.

The grid timing math

Data Center Knowledge frames the structural problem clearly. Building a new data center takes two to three years end to end; new utility-scale transmission takes four to ten years to interconnect in most US regions. AI workloads from training and inference are scaling on the data-center timeline. The mismatch is what made power availability the binding constraint on hyperscaler expansion through 2025 and 2026. The power-first model treats that mismatch as the design variable: by co-developing generation, the timeline collapses to roughly the same window as the compute build.

"The dedicated generation will help meet data center demand while reducing the need for additional power supply from the local grid."— Data Center Knowledge, on the Meitner design

What the design template looks like

The reporting describes a roughly 1 GW data-center load matched against more than 1 GW of nameplate generation capacity from wind, solar and battery storage. The mismatch between nameplate and dispatched generation (wind and solar have capacity factors below 50 percent) means the campus still draws from the grid during low-generation periods and exports during peaks, but the net interconnect requirement is far smaller than a 1 GW pure-load campus would imply. Battery storage of an undisclosed but multi-hundred-MWh scale handles the shorter mismatches; the grid handles the longer ones.

Google's announcement positioned Meitner as the lead build, with additional power-first campuses planned across the US Texas-Gulf-Coast geography where renewable resource quality is high and existing transmission is congested. Microsoft, Meta and Amazon have all signed nuclear and gas-generation PPAs over the past 18 months. The Google approach is structurally different: Microsoft and Amazon contract for generation; Google develops it directly. The Intersect acquisition was the strategic move that made the difference, and Meitner is the build that demonstrates the model works.

Flourish raises $500M from Bezos and Google Ventures for brain-inspired AI at a $2.5B mark

Crunchbase / SiliconANGLE · 2026-06-05 · Source ↗ ·

Flourish, a New York connectomics startup founded last year by former Amazon executive Rob Williams and neuroscientist Thomas Reardon, raised $500 million at a $2.5 billion valuation. Jeff Bezos roughly doubled an initial commitment to contribute close to $100 million. Alphabet's GV, Lux Capital and Catalio also participated. The company is building Cortex AI, a brain-emulation system aimed at cutting per-inference power roughly thirty-fold.

Flourish closed a $500 million round at a $2.5 billion post-money valuation, with the news landing in Crunchbase's week-ending-June-5 funding tally and detailed reporting in SiliconANGLE, Fortune and Bloomberg. Jeff Bezos provided roughly $100 million of the round, almost double his original $50 million commitment, with the rest of the capital coming from Alphabet's GV arm, Lux Capital and the healthcare-focused fund Catalio. The company was founded last year by former Amazon executive Rob Williams and neuroscientist Thomas Reardon, who previously founded CTRL-labs, the brain-computer-interface company Meta acquired in 2019 for an estimated $1 billion.

Flourish's pitch is brain-inspired AI. The company is building Cortex AI, a system that emulates brain function by mapping real neurons and their connections, a research area known as connectomics. The thesis is that current LLMs are extreme power inefficient compared to the biological substrate they imitate: a server-grade GPU spends roughly thirty times the energy of the human brain to process equivalent information, and that gap is the binding constraint on AI's economic scaling. Flourish hopes to close at least part of it.

The power bet

The pitch is a power play rather than a quality play. Training and inference power costs are now the single largest line item for hyperscalers, and the Meitner-style buildouts described elsewhere in this edition exist because the standard approach hit the wall. A model that reduces per-inference compute by an order of magnitude reshapes the economics in a way that no incremental quantization or pruning advance can. The technical risk is enormous: connectomics-derived architectures have not produced competitive language or vision models to date. Flourish is betting that scaling the connectomics approach with sufficient capital and compute is the missing variable.

"A server-grade graphics card uses about 30 times more energy to process information than the human brain. Flourish hopes to reduce that power draw by more than an order of magnitude."— SiliconANGLE coverage of the round

The cap table signal

Bezos's near-doubling of his initial commitment is the editorial signal. The Bezos investment arm has been the most consistent backer of contrarian AI architectures: it led Project Prometheus's seed round earlier in 2026 and put early capital into Generalist AI. GV's participation alongside Bezos signals that Google's investment arm sees the brain-emulation thesis as worth a position even though Google's own AI research program is firmly in the transformer-and-attention paradigm. Catalio's check, from a fund that normally writes into healthcare biotech, signals that Flourish's connectomics work has commercial applications outside pure AI inference.

Reardon's CTRL-labs background is the technical backstory. CTRL-labs built electromyography-based neural interfaces; the Meta acquisition produced the Quest neural-input wristband Meta announced at Connect 2024. Reardon left Meta in 2024 and reunited with Williams to launch Flourish. The team's bet is that connectomics is now sufficiently mature, after a decade of structural-biology and microscopy advances, to support computational architectures that work at scale. The next data point will be either a benchmark publication or a named pilot customer. Until one of those lands, Flourish remains a high-conviction, high-variance bet on a non-transformer architecture in a market that has converged hard on the transformer.

Database back-end startup Supabase closed $500 million at a $10.5 billion valuation, roughly double its October 2025 mark. The Singapore sovereign fund GIC led, with Accel, Y Combinator, Craft, Felicis, Coatue and Stripe participating. CEO Paul Copplestone has described the round as a "vibe-coding wave" trade: the AI-coding tools that build apps end-to-end overwhelmingly back into Supabase Postgres.

Supabase raised $500 million at a $10.5 billion post-money valuation on June 4, with the round detailed in CNBC and tallied in Crunchbase's week-ending-June-5 funding wrap. GIC, the Singapore sovereign wealth fund, led. Existing investors Accel, Y Combinator, Craft Ventures, Felicis, Coatue and the fintech firm Stripe participated. The valuation roughly doubles from $5 billion at Supabase's October 2025 Series E.

The pitch is the AI-coding back-end thesis. Supabase ships a managed Postgres back end with row-level security, auth, file storage, edge functions and a real-time subscriptions layer, marketed as an open-source Firebase alternative. The growth driver in 2025 and 2026 has been AI coding tools that emit full-stack applications: Cursor, Lovable, Bolt and Replit Agent default to Supabase Postgres for the database layer because the integration is one prompt away. Supabase's revenue has grown roughly five times in the past year on the back of that traffic, per filings the round disclosed to investors.

The vibe-coding wave

CEO Paul Copplestone has called the moment the "vibe-coding wave," using the Andrej Karpathy coinage for AI-assisted software development. The argument is that the wave shifts where developer time goes. Developers stop hand-writing CRUD APIs because the AI coding tool generates them. The default back end that the tool picks captures more workload than its market share would suggest because picking is now a one-shot decision rather than an architecture decision over weeks. Supabase's open-source license and Postgres compatibility make it the path-of-least-resistance pick across the AI coding tool field.

"We're now the default back end the AI coding tools reach for. Every Cursor or Bolt prompt that says 'add a database' adds Supabase Postgres."— Paul Copplestone, Supabase CEO

The investor logic

GIC's lead position is the unusual signal. Sovereign wealth funds typically write growth-stage checks into companies past $10 billion only when the public-market comparable is clear. The implied comparable for Supabase is Mongo, Snowflake and Confluent, which trade at 8 to 15 times revenue on the public markets. A $10.5 billion valuation against the disclosed roughly $700 million ARR run rate puts Supabase at 15 times, the top of that band. The trade is the conviction that AI coding tools' compounding will pull Supabase's revenue another five times in the next 18 months and reset the multiple. Stripe's participation is the cross-product signal: the AI coding tools that ship apps end-to-end also need a payments layer, and the natural pair is Stripe-plus-Supabase. The round will fund engineering hiring, edge-function buildout for low-latency inference colocation, and the European and Asian region expansion that the AI coding tool growth is now pulling.

The federal preemption fight is the load-bearing legal context. The Colorado AI Act has been the named target of preemption discussions. The Obernolte-Trahan Great American AI Act discussion draft, introduced June 4 in the House by Representatives Jay Obernolte (R-CA) and Lori Trahan (D-MA), proposes a three-year preemption of state AI laws as one of its core provisions, alongside a $100 million federal AI Standards Center and applicability to companies with $500 million-plus annual revenue.
