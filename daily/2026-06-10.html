Anthropic ships Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5, splitting its strongest model into a public tier and a Glasswing-restricted cyber tier

Fable 5 is the first publicly available Mythos-class model. It scores 80.3% on SWE-Bench Pro against 69.2 for Claude Opus 4.8 and 58.6 for GPT-5.5, becomes the first model to clear 90% on Hex's analytical benchmark, and shipped with Stripe reporting that it finished a 50-million-line Ruby migration in a single day that would otherwise have taken a team more than two months. Fable 5 is free on Pro, Max, Team and seat-based Enterprise plans through June 22 and bills at $10 per million input / $50 per million output afterward, double Opus 4.8. The same model, with cybersecurity safeguards lifted, ships as Claude Mythos 5 to a small set of cyber defenders and critical-infrastructure operators under Project Glasswing in collaboration with the U.S. government. Three safety classifiers route cybersecurity, biology/chemistry and distillation queries to Opus 4.8 instead of refusing; Anthropic says the classifiers fire on fewer than 5% of Fable sessions. Mandatory 30-day data retention applies to all Fable 5 and Mythos 5 traffic, overriding prior zero-retention enterprise contracts. Fable 5 lands the same day inside GitHub Copilot, Amazon Bedrock, the Claude Platform on AWS, Microsoft Foundry and Harvey.

Anthropic · 2026-06-09 · Source ↗ ·

Anthropic released Claude Fable 5 and Claude Mythos 5 Tuesday morning, ending eight months of speculation about whether the company would ever ship Mythos to anyone outside its private partner program. Fable 5 is the public-facing model, available immediately through the Claude API, Pro, Max, Team and seat-based Enterprise subscriptions, GitHub Copilot, Amazon Bedrock, the Claude Platform on AWS, Microsoft Foundry, Harvey and a handful of other launch partners. Mythos 5 is the same model with cybersecurity safeguards stripped out, deployed only to vetted cyber defenders and critical-infrastructure operators through Project Glasswing in collaboration with the U.S. government.

The benchmark numbers are the easy part of the story. On SWE-Bench Pro, the closely watched programming benchmark, Fable 5 solves 80.3% of problems against 69.2% for Claude Opus 4.8 and 58.6% for GPT-5.5. It is the first model to exceed 90% on Hex's analytical benchmark, made up of long, complex analytical tasks, and reaches state of the art on Cognition's FrontierCode and Hebbia's Finance Benchmark for senior-level reasoning. On Harvey's Legal Agent Bench it scores 13.3% against Opus 4.8's 10.4%, and on Harvey's proprietary BigLaw Bench it hits 93.4% overall, a new ceiling for the Anthropic family. Fable 5 completed Pokémon FireRed using vision only without helper tools, posted three-fold gains over Opus 4.8 in Slay the Spire long-context play through memory improvements, and Anthropic says Mythos 5 has already accelerated protein design by roughly 10x in pilot drug-discovery work, producing strong candidates for 9 of 14 protein targets.

The Stripe migration is the demo

The customer headline is Stripe. Anthropic disclosed that Stripe ran Fable 5 across a 50-million-line Ruby codebase to execute a codebase-wide migration. The model finished the work in a single day. A team of human engineers had estimated more than two months for the same effort. The result has become the launch's signature demonstration of what Anthropic is now selling: not better completion of individual coding tickets, but autonomous completion of larger units of work — codebase migrations, multi-step refactors, app prototyping, end-to-end test generation, debugging across unfamiliar tools and multi-day internal projects.

"Claude Fable 5 compressed months of engineering into days." — Stripe, in Anthropic's launch materials

Cursor CEO Michael Truell shipped a launch blurb the same morning: "Claude Fable 5 is the state of the art model on CursorBench. It's opened up a class of long-horizon problems that were out of reach for earlier models." GitHub's CPO Mario Rodriguez framed it for the developer audience: "What excites us most is the direction it points: a future where developers can hand increasingly ambitious work to agents and trust the results." Rakuten, which had publicly tested Mythos Preview in March, said the extra thinking time during long agent runs "pays for itself" when sustained autonomy is the goal.

Three classifiers, one fallback model

The safety architecture is the part of the launch that Anthropic spent most of the briefing on. Fable 5 ships with three safety classifiers: one for cybersecurity, one for biology and chemistry, and one for distillation attempts targeting unauthorized model replication. When a classifier fires, the request does not get refused outright. Instead it falls back to Claude Opus 4.8, which still answers but with the older model's narrower autonomy. Anthropic says the classifiers trigger on fewer than 5% of Fable sessions on average, with more than 95% of requests running entirely on Fable's own responses. An external bug-bounty program ran more than 1,000 hours of red-team testing before launch and produced "zero harmful single-turn requests" that succeeded across jailbreak techniques, according to the launch post.

The single biggest contractual change is data retention. Mythos-class models, including the public Fable 5, require 30-day data retention on all traffic, including prompts and outputs. The retained data is used to operate safety classifiers and detect jailbreak attempts in flight. It is not used to train future models or for any non-safety purpose. The policy overrides prior zero-retention agreements with enterprise customers — GitHub's changelog spells this out explicitly: "Anthropic retains prompts and outputs for up to 30 days to operate safety classifiers that detect harmful or abusive use," while every other Claude model in Copilot, including Opus 4.8, Sonnet 4.5 and Haiku 4.5, continues to operate under Zero Data Retention. Enterprise and Business administrators on GitHub must explicitly enable the Fable 5 policy; it is disabled by default.

Mythos 5 and Project Glasswing

Mythos 5 is the same model, same weights, with the cybersecurity classifier lifted. Anthropic calls it the strongest cybersecurity model in the world. Initial deployment is limited to vetted partners through Project Glasswing — the cyber-defender access program that, as of the prior week, spans roughly 200 critical-infrastructure organizations across more than 15 countries. Cisco, BeyondTrust and a number of US-government partners are among the publicly named participants. Anthropic plans to extend Mythos access to a separate biology research program with similar guardrails on who gets in.

The Project Glasswing track has had a complicated week. Bruce Schneier published a public critique Monday that of roughly 23,000 vulnerabilities Mythos flagged across 1,000 open-source projects during the Preview phase, almost none have been patched, and described as "just not true" the Anthropic framing that Mythos's results are uniquely strong relative to other frontier models. Cisco's Foundry Security Spec team, which scanned 1.8 billion lines of Cisco code with six frontier models, came to a broadly model-agnostic result on raw discovery: any of the top US-lab frontier models surfaces a similar vulnerability surface. Tuesday's Mythos 5 launch is Anthropic's argument that the uniqueness lives downstream in autonomous remediation, not in initial discovery.

Pricing, distribution and the IPO clock

Pricing for both Fable 5 and Mythos 5 is identical: $10 per million input tokens and $50 per million output tokens. That is double Opus 4.8 on output and roughly half what Mythos Preview cost on the private program. Fable 5 is included at no additional cost on Pro, Max, Team and seat-based Enterprise plans from June 9 through June 22; after June 23 it bills against usage credits on those plans. The launch lands inside Anthropic's IPO clock — the company filed a confidential S-1 with the SEC on June 1 at a reported $965 billion valuation after raising $65 billion in its Series H. Fable 5 is the largest single product Anthropic has shipped between filing and pricing, and it is the artifact most analysts will use to value the company against OpenAI heading into the second half.

The answer is two SKUs from one architecture. Fable 5 ships to anyone willing to accept 30-day data retention and the chance their request gets demoted to Opus 4.8 by a safety classifier. Mythos 5, with cybersecurity safeguards lifted, ships only to vetted defenders inside Project Glasswing. Stripe's 50-million-line Ruby migration finished in a day, Fable 5's 80.3% on SWE-Bench Pro, and the Hex 90% mark are the demonstration; the safety classifier-as-router and forced retention contract are the price.

Apple's new LanguageModel protocol lets Anthropic Claude and Google Gemini drop into the Foundation Models framework as Swift packages

TechTimes · 2026-06-09 · Source ↗ ·

Apple's day-two WWDC sessions introduced a new LanguageModel protocol inside the Foundation Models framework — a public Swift interface that third-party cloud model providers implement to expose their cloud models through the same API as Apple's on-device foundation model. Developers prototype with Apple's on-device model, then route hard requests to Google's Gemini or Anthropic's Claude by updating a Swift Package Manager dependency, with no changes to session logic. Three-tier routing splits work between Apple Neural Engine, Private Cloud Compute, and cloud escalation to Google Cloud running Nvidia Blackwell B200 GPUs.

WWDC 2026's day-two developer sessions turned the keynote's user-pickable Apple Intelligence Extensions story into a developer-API story. The Foundation Models framework, which exposed Apple's 3B-parameter on-device model to third-party developers at WWDC 2025, gained a new LanguageModel Swift protocol. The protocol is a public interface that Apple's on-device model implements and that third-party cloud providers can also implement. Google and Anthropic are the launch implementers. Google's cloud-hosted Gemini models integrate through the Firebase Apple SDK. Anthropic has published its own Swift package that conforms to the same protocol and routes requests to Claude on the Anthropic API.

The developer workflow Apple demonstrated is the headline: a developer prototypes a feature using Apple's on-device model, scaffolding the session, the prompts, the structured outputs and the tool calls all against the on-device model API. To route the same feature to a cloud model — Google's Gemini or Anthropic's Claude — the developer updates the Swift Package Manager dependency list. No session-logic changes, no prompt rewriting, no separate API client. Apple's pitch: prototype free on-device, ship to whichever cloud model fits the application's economics and capability ceiling.

Three-tier routing

Behind the protocol sit three tiers of inference. Apple's on-device model on the Neural Engine handles routine queries locally and for free. Private Cloud Compute, the stateless Apple Silicon server fleet introduced in 2024, handles requests that exceed on-device budget but stay inside Apple's privacy attestation. Cloud escalation routes the heaviest queries — the agentic, long-context, vision-heavy ones — to Google Cloud infrastructure running Nvidia Blackwell B200 GPUs. Apple strips queries of user identification and anonymizes them before they leave the Private Cloud Compute envelope.

"User requests through Private Cloud Compute remain completely private to you. They're never stored. They're never accessible to anyone." — Craig Federighi, SVP Software Engineering, Apple

The framework adds three first-class tool primitives: a BarcodeReaderTool, an OCRTool backed by Vision, and a Spotlight-powered search primitive that lets a model query the device's local index without any external vector database. Spotlight search is the most consequential addition because it lets Foundation Models apps do local retrieval-augmented generation without any of the operational cost of running an embeddings store. The tool returns Spotlight results into the model's context the way any other tool would.

What developers got from Anthropic, specifically

Anthropic published a Claude blog post the same day documenting how the Claude Swift package implements the protocol. Developers add the package to their project, sign in with an Anthropic API key, and pass typed outputs from Apple's on-device model into a Claude request. The package handles streaming, tool calls and structured responses back into SwiftUI views. Apple's typed-Swift outputs become Claude's structured inputs without bespoke serialization code. The package is open source on GitHub and ships with documentation aligned to Apple's own Foundation Models samples.

Google, Anthropic and Apple all framed the move as a layered competitive deal: Apple controls the routing layer and the on-device tier, third-party frontier labs compete for cloud-routed requests, and developers pick the cloud target per app without rewriting their session code. Ciphero's Nakash, quoted in TechTimes, pushed back that the privacy model is "only as private as the weakest link" — meaning that the customer is trusting Apple's strip-and-anonymize step in addition to Anthropic's or Google's downstream behavior.
