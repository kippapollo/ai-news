Anthropic's Jack Clark and Marina Favaro call for a coordinated frontier-AI pause as recursive self-improvement gets close

Fortune / Anthropic / SiliconANGLE · 2026-06-05 · Source ↗ ·

Anthropic researchers Jack Clark and Marina Favaro published "When AI builds itself" on June 4-5 arguing the industry should preserve the option of a coordinated frontier-AI development pause. The argument hangs on Anthropic's own data: AI's ability to complete coding tasks autonomously is doubling roughly every four months, more than 80 percent of code merged into Anthropic's codebase is now written by Claude, and the company's engineers are shipping eight times more code per quarter than they were before 2025. The pause would only work, the authors concede, if multiple well-resourced labs in multiple countries agree under the same verifiable conditions.

Anthropic published the essay on the company's institute page over the June 4-5 window, with Fortune, Axios, SiliconANGLE, Scientific American, US News and Al Jazeera all carrying parallel write-ups inside 36 hours. The framing is unusual because Anthropic is a frontier lab arguing for the industry's deceleration while running confidential paperwork toward an IPO. Anthropic filed its draft S-1 with the SEC on June 1; the company's revenue run-rate hit roughly $47 billion in May, a five-fold annual jump, and the May Series H closed at a $965 billion post-money valuation. The authors do not mention the IPO or the valuation in the essay. The skepticism in the response is concrete: why would a lab heading for a near-trillion-dollar listing publicly argue that the technology powering its growth needs an industry-wide off-switch?

The technical argument is the more rigorous part of the piece. Anthropic measures the autonomous-task-completion horizon for frontier coding agents and reports that the duration of tasks Claude can complete autonomously without human checks has doubled roughly every four months across the last two years. If that trajectory holds, the authors argue, then sometime in the next two years AI systems cross a threshold where they can autonomously design, build, train and deploy their own successors with minimal human intervention. That threshold is what the field calls recursive self-improvement. The essay's claim is that recursive self-improvement is the binding policy variable, not the absolute capability of any given model release, and that the institutional structures for alignment research, safety evaluations, governance and verifiable agreements need to be in place before the threshold is crossed, not after.

What the pause would actually look like

Clark and Favaro do not propose a unilateral Anthropic pause. They propose preserving the option of a coordinated multi-lab, multi-country pause that the industry could trigger under specific verifiable conditions. The criteria they sketch: multiple well-resourced labs at or near the frontier, in multiple countries (with the United States and China named as the load-bearing pair), all agree to stop under the same conditions, with verification mechanisms that let outside observers confirm compliance. The authors concede this is a high bar and that the institutional machinery for verification does not exist today. The point of the essay is to put the option on the table now so that institutional work can begin before the moment when the pause might be needed.

"More than 80 percent of code merged into our codebase is now written by Claude, and our engineers are shipping eight times as much code per quarter as they were before 2025." — Jack Clark and Marina Favaro, Anthropic, "When AI builds itself"

The competitive-positioning read

The response from the broader AI policy community split. Center-right tech-policy voices read the essay as serious safety advocacy from the lab with the most internal data on recursive-self-improvement signals. Skeptics read the essay as competitive positioning: by publicly endorsing a pause that requires unanimous multi-lab agreement to ever bind, Anthropic gets the reputational benefit of caution while losing none of the practical race. The Trump administration's June 2 executive order on AI explicitly directed federal agencies to set up a voluntary-review framework that gives the government 30 days of early access to frontier models. The Great American AI Act discussion draft circulated by Reps. Obernolte and Trahan the same week includes safety-plan requirements for "large frontier developers" with more than $500 million in annual revenue. Anthropic's argument is functionally a request to extend that framework's posture from per-model review to industry-wide coordination capacity.

The competitive question is what happens if OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Meta, xAI or Chinese labs decline to coordinate. Clark and Favaro do not answer it directly. The essay's premise is that the work of building the verification and coordination machinery is worth doing even if the eventual pause is never triggered, because the same machinery solves a series of adjacent problems: cross-lab evaluation of dangerous capabilities, biosecurity uplift screening, agent-misuse incident reporting, and standardized red-team pipelines. The four-CEO joint biosecurity letter to Congress June 5, which Dario Amodei signed alongside Altman, Hassabis and Suleyman, is functionally the first piece of that machinery making it to the legislative record.

Senate.gov / Fortune / Hill · 2026-06-02 · Source ↗ ·

Senator Bernie Sanders introduced legislation that would impose a one-time 50 percent tax payable in stock on OpenAI, Anthropic, xAI and other frontier labs, placing the shares into an American sovereign wealth fund modeled on Norway's $2 trillion oil fund and Alaska's permanent fund. The federal government would gain board representation and voting rights and pay annual dividends directly to every American. The Trump administration has been in parallel private conversations with Sam Altman about a federal equity stake.

Sanders introduced the American AI Sovereign Wealth Fund Act on the Senate floor June 2 alongside a New York Times op-ed and a Fortune long-read. The bill's headline mechanism is a one-time 50 percent stock tax on companies meeting a "leading AI lab" threshold tied to model capability and revenue. The named candidates in the introductory remarks were OpenAI, Anthropic and xAI. The shares would be transferred into a federal sovereign wealth fund. The federal government, through the voting shares and an equal seat on each company's board, would be able to block decisions deemed harmful to American workers and consumers and to push for policy outcomes the legislation specifies.

The legislation references two precedents. Norway's Government Pension Fund Global, capitalized from petroleum royalties starting in the 1990s, now holds more than $2 trillion in assets and pays the Norwegian state's pension system without taxing oil profits twice. Alaska's Permanent Fund, established 50 years ago from state oil revenues, pays an annual dividend directly to every Alaska resident. Sanders is pitching the AI fund as the same structural pattern applied to the trillion-dollar valuations that frontier labs are commanding in private markets ahead of public listings. The fund's proceeds would underwrite direct payments to Americans plus targeted spending on health care, education and housing.

The Trump administration's parallel conversations

The other half of the story is that President Trump has been in private conversations with Sam Altman about a US government equity stake in OpenAI since early 2025, according to reporting that surfaced over the weekend. The Wall Street Journal and Axios reporting describes the conversation as part of OpenAI's IPO strategy: a federal equity tranche would address the administration's interest in a US government claim on AI economic upside and would simultaneously give OpenAI a political moat against state-level regulation and against future Democratic-administration scrutiny. Trump has publicly endorsed the idea in the past 10 days, putting the Republican president and the independent Democrat from Vermont on roughly aligned ground on one of the year's most consequential AI-policy questions.

"The public should own at least half of the big AI companies that have been built on decades of publicly-funded research." — Sen. Bernie Sanders (I-VT), introductory floor speech, June 2

The opposition

Reason magazine's June 2 piece by Eric Boehm called the bill economically incoherent on the grounds that a one-time stock tax would compress private valuations downward by the size of the tax and would not generate the dividend stream Sanders claims. Center-right tech-policy think tanks have raised constitutional questions about a takings-clause challenge. The bill is unlikely to advance in the current Senate, but the introductory speech and op-ed put the federal-stake idea into the legislative record alongside Trump's parallel executive-branch conversations. The political alignment is the news, not the legislative odds. Anthropic and xAI have not publicly responded; OpenAI's only public statement was that the company "looks forward to engaging with policymakers on the structure of AI ownership."

OpenAI ships ChatGPT Lockdown Mode and Elevated Risk labels for prompt-injection defense

OpenAI / TechCrunch / The Hacker News · 2026-06-04 · Source ↗ ·

OpenAI rolled out Lockdown Mode for ChatGPT personal accounts and self-serve ChatGPT Business on June 4. The deterministic switch hard-disables live web access, image-in-response support, Deep Research, shopping research, Agent Mode, Canvas networking, live connectors and file downloads, blocking the data-exfiltration leg of a prompt-injection attack chain. Elevated Risk labels appear automatically when ChatGPT is processing content from untrusted sources.

OpenAI introduced Lockdown Mode in a help-center article and a coordinated launch piece June 4, with TechCrunch, The Hacker News, Engadget and Simon Willison's blog all covering the rollout the next day. The framing is straightforward: prompt-injection attacks against agents that browse the web, read user files or use tools are a real and growing class of incident, and the only deterministic defense OpenAI can ship today is to disable the outbound channels an attacker would need to exfiltrate sensitive data after a successful prompt injection. Lockdown Mode does not prevent prompt injections from appearing in the content ChatGPT processes. It prevents the consequences of a successful injection from leaving the user's session.

The mode is opt-in per account and per conversation. When active, it disables: live web access (browsing, retrieval), image generation inside responses, Deep Research including shopping research, Agent Mode, Canvas networking, live data connectors, and file downloads. The user can still upload files for analysis and can still use the model's intrinsic reasoning over those files; what they cannot do is route that analysis through any tool that touches an external network. The tradeoff is product breadth for safety; OpenAI is positioning it as the right default for "users with higher security needs, or for moments when users are willing to trade product functionality for stricter product guardrails."

Elevated Risk labels and the corporate context

Alongside Lockdown Mode, OpenAI shipped Elevated Risk labels that surface in the conversation interface when ChatGPT is reading content from a source the company has classified as elevated-risk for prompt injection. The label is contextual: it appears on a per-message basis when the conversation's data sources cross a threshold OpenAI does not fully publicize. Both features rolled to personal accounts and to self-serve ChatGPT Business plans, with broader Enterprise rollout to follow on a customer-controlled schedule. The Hacker News framed the launch as OpenAI catching up to the security posture that Anthropic's Claude has marketed since Claude 4 launched with file-system isolation and outbound-call defaults.

"Lockdown Mode is designed to help prevent the final stage of data exfiltration from a prompt injection attack by limiting outbound network requests that could transfer sensitive data to an attacker." — OpenAI launch announcement, June 4

The corporate timing is the strategic context. OpenAI's Codex Computer Use went GA on Windows June 3, expanding the surface area for agent-style code execution against user environments. Microsoft launched MXC, the kernel-level execution sandbox built into Windows, June 2 with OpenAI as a launch partner. Lockdown Mode is the user-side safety primitive that pairs with the OS-side sandbox. The combination gives an enterprise IT admin a defensible posture for Codex deployment: MXC contains execution at the OS, Lockdown Mode contains data-egress at the ChatGPT product, and Elevated Risk labels surface the live risk to the user. The remaining attack surface is the model's own susceptibility to prompt-injection in the first place, which neither of these features solves.

Grok Build is xAI's answer to Anthropic's Claude Code, OpenAI's Codex CLI and GitHub Copilot's terminal stack. Plan Mode is the safety primitive. When a Grok Build agent receives a complex task, plan mode forces the model to outline the proposed file changes, the dependencies, the test plan and the rollback steps before touching anything on disk. The user signs off on the plan; only then does the model start executing the diff. Subagent delegation runs in headless mode for large refactors, with each subagent operating against its own isolated working copy and merging results back through a coordination layer. The pattern is meaningfully similar to Anthropic's Claude Code orchestration, with xAI's distinctive add being deeper integration into X (formerly Twitter) infrastructure for product managers who already use the platform.

The implicit claim is that an X Premium+ subscriber gets Grok Build for free with their broader social-and-news subscription, which is a fundamentally different unit economics than the per-token competition the other frontier labs are fighting in the developer-tools market. Whether the model is actually competitive on real coding work against Claude Opus 4.8 or GPT-5.5 is the open question; early reviews from ChatForest and AI Certs put Grok Build behind Claude on autonomous-coding accuracy but ahead on raw speed and on token cost per task.

Anthropic open-sources its Defending Code Reference Harness for autonomous C/C++ memory-bug discovery

Anthropic / GitHub / TechPlanet · 2026-06-06 · Source ↗ ·

Anthropic published the Defending Code Reference Harness on GitHub, an open-source skills-and-pipeline pack that runs a recon → find → verify → dedupe → report → patch loop for C/C++ memory vulnerabilities using Docker, AddressSanitizer and Claude. The framework distills the partnership work behind Project Glasswing and is portable across Bedrock, Vertex and Azure deployments of Claude. The release lands two days after Mythos access quadrupled to roughly 200 critical-infrastructure partners.

Anthropic shipped the reference harness as the open-source distillation of the Glasswing program's autonomous vulnerability-discovery pipeline. The repository ships a set of Claude skills for threat modeling, scanning, triage and patching plus an autonomous scanning harness pre-configured to find C/C++ memory bugs using Docker container isolation and AddressSanitizer (ASAN) instrumentation. The framework is model-agnostic in API: it works with Claude on the Anthropic API, on AWS Bedrock, on Google Vertex and on Microsoft Azure, and the policy primitives are portable across deployment.

The pipeline is six stages. Recon: a lightweight agent reads the project source inside a network-isolated container and proposes a partition of the codebase into smaller chunks the find stage can attack in parallel. Find: multiple agents run in parallel, each in its own isolated container, reading the partition's source, generating malformed inputs against the ASAN-instrumented binary, and recording every crash. Verify: a separate grader agent reproduces each crash from scratch in a fresh container that the find agent has never touched, ruling out environmental flukes. Dedupe: a judge agent compares verified crashes against previously reported bugs and decides whether each is new, a better example of an existing bug or a duplicate. Report: structured writeups suitable for filing as a GitHub Security Advisory. Patch: a separate skill takes the verified bug and proposes a minimal-diff fix.

The Project Glasswing pedigree

The harness distills the partner work Anthropic has done under Project Glasswing, the defensive-security partnership the company launched in April with 50 initial organizations including Okta, Samsung, SK Hynix, SK Telecom, NATO, the EU cybersecurity agency ENISA and the US federal government. Mythos quadrupled its Glasswing access pool on June 2 to roughly 200 organizations spanning 15 countries and the power, water, healthcare and communications sectors. The Glasswing terms are unusual: it is not a sales channel but a defensive-security partnership, and the criterion for access is whether your organization maintains infrastructure that is a meaningful slice of the shared global attack surface, not whether you can pay.

"This is an open-source reference implementation based on general best practices for finding vulnerabilities using Claude. You can use this to build your own vulnerability finding pipeline." — Anthropic, defending-code-reference-harness README

The release reads as a strategic asymmetric move. Anthropic does not need to ship the harness; the Glasswing partner program is already running. Open-sourcing the reference implementation transfers the operational pipeline to downstream security teams who can adapt it without paying Anthropic and without joining Glasswing. The upside for Anthropic is twofold: the broader security community absorbs the patterns the company has refined under Glasswing, and the marketing surface of "Anthropic open-sources the autonomous-vulnerability-discovery harness" lands in the same week the company is publicly arguing for a coordinated frontier-AI pause and confidentially filing for an IPO. The defensive-security narrative is the load-bearing piece of Anthropic's external positioning into the second half of 2026.

Obernolte and Trahan publish a 269-page discussion draft of the Great American AI Act with a three-year preemption

Roll Call / Obernolte House.gov · 2026-06-04 · Source ↗ ·

Reps. Jay Obernolte (R-CA) and Lori Trahan (D-MA) released the 269-page Great American AI Act, the most comprehensive federal AI framework Congress has put forward.
