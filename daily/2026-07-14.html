Meta more than doubles Louisiana Hyperion to 5 gigawatts and over $50 billion in the largest single AI campus disclosed to date

Governor Jeff Landry and Meta President Dina Powell McCormick used a Monday joint announcement in Richland Parish to lift the campus from the 2 GW / $27 billion figure disclosed in October 2025 to 5 GW and above $50 billion, with 2 GW online by 2030 and the full build complete around 2032. The revised Hyperion sits at nearly a third of Meta's already-doubled 14 GW global compute target for 2027 and roughly two-and-a-half times the announced scale of xAI's Colossus 2. Construction employment goes from 5,000 to 7,500 and permanent operations positions double to about 1,000.

CNBC · 2026-07-13 · Source ↗ ·

On Monday, July 13, Governor Jeff Landry of Louisiana and Meta President and Vice Chair Dina Powell McCormick jointly announced that the Hyperion supercluster under construction in Richland Parish will expand from its previously disclosed 2 gigawatt build to 5 gigawatts of AI compute capacity, with total investment lifted above $50 billion. The revised number more than doubles the $27 billion figure Meta and Blue Owl Capital had published in October 2025 when they formed the joint venture that owns the site, and it sits five times above the original $10 billion pitch that first drew Meta to the parish in 2024. Hyperion is now the largest single-site AI compute concentration announced anywhere.

The construction timeline stretches out with the scale. Meta expects 2 GW online by 2030 and the full 5 GW build complete around 2032. Groundbreaking happened in December 2024, and the company says it has awarded more than $1.6 billion in contracts to Louisiana businesses in the eighteen months since. Construction employment lifts from the earlier 5,000-job estimate to about 7,500, and the permanent operations headcount doubles to roughly 1,000. Meta committed more than $1 billion for local road, water and wastewater upgrades and $5 million to Louisiana Delta Community College for workforce training and scholarships. A new energy agreement with Entergy Louisiana is projected to save residential ratepayers over $2 billion across 20 years, softening the political case that the load subsidizes Meta at consumers' expense.

"Richland Parish is a special place, and it's a model for the rest of the country."— Dina Powell McCormick, Meta President and Vice Chair

Fits the Meta compute doubling, breaks the datacenter market above it

The revised Hyperion is nearly a third of the 14 GW global Meta compute target for 2027 that the company published earlier this month. Meta counted 32 data centers in operation or under construction on Monday, 28 of them inside the United States, and reiterated the three-year $600 billion US infrastructure and jobs pledge that Alexandr Wang first floated on the June town hall. Alongside Hyperion, Meta broke ground last week in Sturgeon County, Alberta on a CA$13 billion 1 GW campus with expansion headroom to 1.8 GW; Premier Danielle Smith spent Monday morning on CBC's call-in radio defending that build against ratepayer-subsidy critique in parallel with the Louisiana news. Meta's compute story has moved in twelve months from "large hyperscaler" to "the single largest AI physical-plant program on the market," and Hyperion V2 is the anchor number that made the difference.

The competitive read is the piece the Susquehanna and Morgan Stanley notes both flagged on Monday afternoon. Hyperion at 5 GW is roughly two-and-a-half times the announced scale of xAI's Colossus 2 in Memphis and lands well above the roughly 1.5 GW target Google Cloud published for its largest single AlphaChip pod. OpenAI's Stargate campus in Abilene, Texas targets 1.2 GW of first-phase power against the 2027 GPT-5.7 training window; even the extended Stargate roadmap through 2028 puts total power below Hyperion at 5 GW. The knock-on for chip supply is direct. Nvidia has reportedly reserved about 60 percent of TSMC's advanced packaging capacity for 2026 (see TSMC's June revenue print in today's rundown), and Meta's revised Hyperion draws directly against that pool through both Nvidia GB300 racks and Meta's own MTIA v3 Iris silicon entering TSMC N3P production in September.

Energy, water and the ratepayer-subsidy pushback

The Entergy Louisiana deal is the piece that unlocked the announcement. Under the utility's public filing, Entergy will build three new natural-gas generation plants totalling 2,262 megawatts specifically for Hyperion, and the Louisiana Public Service Commission approved the plan on the condition that residential ratepayers see net savings over 20 years. Meta's counter-commitment on Monday puts that number above $2 billion. Cooling on the site uses a closed-loop liquid system with water usage equivalent to a typical golf course, per Meta's environmental filings. The company also disclosed that it is negotiating power-purchase agreements for solar, wind and battery storage projects across Louisiana that could offset the natural-gas load over time.

"One of the largest artificial intelligence data center developments in the world."— Meta company statement, July 13 announcement

Environmental opposition has not gone away. Earthjustice's earlier filing requesting an investigation into the financing structure and energy intensity remains unresolved, and Greenpeace Canada campaigner Keith Stewart used the parallel Alberta announcement to call for "a moratorium on mega-data centers until we have legislated environmental and human rights protections on AI." The Louisiana Legislature's bipartisan working group on data center taxation, which convenes July 21, is expected to review a proposal that would require single-site campuses above 3 GW to submit annual environmental impact reports. That would put Hyperion V2 in a novel disclosure regime not applied to any other single US AI facility.

What breaks next

Three follow-ons matter this week. First, the Louisiana PSC's next filing window includes a request from Entergy for the ratepayer savings model to be audited by a third party; the outcome sets the template that Alabama, Texas and Oklahoma commissions will likely borrow when Meta or a peer announces the next gigawatt-plus campus. Second, the Blue Owl joint venture's next capital call is expected in August and Susquehanna's Sunday note put the incremental Meta-side spend at $6 to $9 billion in 2027. Third, the Hyperion V2 announcement gives Meta a public target the Iris MTIA production ramp has to hit; the site's design load implicitly assumes Iris v3 delivers by 2028 and Iris v4 by 2030 at densities that Meta's engineering leadership has not yet published.

Google, Microsoft, Salesforce, Snowflake and ServiceNow rally around agent standard to blunt Anthropic MCP

The Information · 2026-07-13 · Source ↗

The Information's Applied AI newsletter reported Monday that five SaaS and cloud giants aligned behind a shared standard for connecting AI agents to enterprise data and tools, framed explicitly as a counter to Anthropic's Model Context Protocol. The alliance extends the June 17 Agentic Resource Discovery draft from 11 to a Microsoft-and-Google-blessed alternative sitting on top of most enterprise systems of record.

The Information published a July 13 Applied AI newsletter by Amir Efrati, Aaron Holmes and Catherine Perloff reporting that Google, Microsoft, Salesforce, Snowflake and ServiceNow have aligned behind a shared technical standard for connecting AI agents to enterprise software, in a move explicitly framed as blunting Anthropic's Model Context Protocol. MCP, launched by Anthropic in late 2024 as an open protocol for tool discovery and invocation, has "quietly become the default over the past 18 months," per the article. What is new in the July 13 framing is that the coalition backing the alternative controls the software layer where most enterprise data actually sits: Salesforce for CRM, Snowflake for warehousing, ServiceNow for workflows, Google Cloud and Microsoft Azure for compute.

The story extends the trajectory of Agentic Resource Discovery (ARD), a Linux-Foundation-adjacent draft spec unveiled June 17 with 11 initial contributors including Cisco, Databricks, GitHub, GoDaddy, Hugging Face and Nvidia alongside the five named backers. ARD uses ai-catalog.json manifests published at publisher domains and a registry index so an agent can advertise the task it needs to perform and receive a discovery answer at runtime without hard-wired connectors. What ARD has not previously had is coordinated marketing weight from the SaaS layer that would push the rival standard into production. The Information's July 13 piece is the moment that changes.

"The battle is over the plumbing that lets AI agents plug into company data and tools. Anthropic's standard, called MCP, has quietly become the default over the past 18 months."— The Information, Applied AI newsletter, July 13

All five named companies plus Anthropic and OpenAI remain members of the same Linux Foundation working group building shared open agent standards, so the market and the standards body are running in parallel: the same vendors cooperate on the baseline spec and fight in the market above it. MCP has generated a full ecosystem of client libraries, server implementations from AWS, Cloudflare, Google and Microsoft, plus near-universal support in coding agents like Claude Code, Cursor, Codex CLI and Antigravity. A competing discovery layer would either fragment that ecosystem or force MCP-compatible agents to speak both protocols. Anthropic's response window is tight: the MCP 2026-07-28 release candidate ships in two weeks, and the company will need to convert its mindshare into governance wins before ARD 0.9 becomes a de facto Google-and-Microsoft-blessed standard on top of Salesforce, Snowflake and ServiceNow deployments. The strategic question underneath is who owns the layer above tool calls: the model vendor, the SaaS vendor, or a neutral registry. Monday's alignment tips that answer toward the SaaS layer.
