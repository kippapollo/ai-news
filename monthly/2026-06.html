Washington yanked Claude Fable 5 offline four days after Anthropic shipped it

Friday, June 12, 5:21pm ET: Anthropic complied with a Trump administration directive suspending Fable 5 and Mythos 5 globally after Amazon researchers demoed a narrow jailbreak Andy Jassy briefed to Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick the day before. Two weeks earlier the pair had shipped inside GitHub Copilot, Bedrock, Microsoft Foundry and Harvey. The suspension ran the rest of June, dragged the G7, EU, India and the UK into public statements, and reset the global export-control map around frontier reasoning.

Fortune · Axios · TechCrunch · Euronews · 2026-06-10 — 2026-06-30 · Source ↗ ·

Anthropic shipped Claude Fable 5 and Claude Mythos 5 on Tuesday, June 9 as the first publicly available Mythos-class models. Fable 5 scored 80.3% on SWE-Bench Pro and Stripe reported a 50-million-line Ruby migration finished in a day. The pair launched simultaneously inside GitHub Copilot, Amazon Bedrock, Microsoft Foundry, the Claude Platform on AWS and Harvey, with same-day integrations from Snowflake Cortex and BeyondTrust. Anthropic paired the release with an expansion of Project Glasswing, the controlled program that gave select cybersecurity firms Mythos-tier vulnerability discovery. Fable 5's headline pitch was the first commercially available model that could sustain a full autonomous engineering session at Opus 4.8 quality without falling off the plan.

Four days later that pitch was gone. On Thursday, June 11, Amazon Threat Intelligence engineers demonstrated to CEO Andy Jassy a narrow jailbreak on Fable 5 that unlocked the containment layer wrapping Mythos capabilities. Jassy briefed Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick the same evening. On Friday morning Lutnick signed an export-control directive; at 5:21pm ET Anthropic pulled Fable 5 and Mythos 5 from every surface it operated — the Claude API, Claude Code, Amazon Bedrock, GitHub Copilot, Microsoft Foundry, Harvey. Anthropic flew senior staff to Washington that weekend. The Fortune Sunday follow-up traced the escalation to a single Amazon security demo Lutnick's team judged an existential national-security failure at the framing layer, not a discrete bug.

"Anthropic was asked to fix or de-deploy. They refused to fix. So we made the choice for them."— David Sacks, White House AI & Crypto czar, on X, June 14

How Fable 5 got jailbroken

Anthropic disputes the framing. Semafor's Sunday reporting surfaced a suspected China-linked access-pattern trigger inside the Amazon demo, which Anthropic characterized as a narrow adversarial prompt using the containment wrapper's own reasoning path against it. TechTimes on Friday, June 12 confirmed a public jailbreak had circulated, and the company's own postmortem acknowledged Fable 5's containment stack was more porous than the internal Opus 4.8 test suite had suggested. The Amazon demo did not extract new capabilities — Anthropic's Model Card had already disclosed the biology and chemistry uplift measurements — but it demonstrated the containment wrapper could be circumvented, which is the failure the White House export-control framework treats as disqualifying.

The market and diplomatic reaction

Mark Carney, at Évian-les-Bains for the G7, told The Next Web the shutdown was "the 2008 financial crisis moment for AI" and pushed a G7 statement on "trusted-partner access" for frontier US models — the first time the phrase entered a formal G7 communique. The European Commission publicly warned the measure "must not be discriminatory against allies." India reopened its sovereign-AI debate; TechCrunch reported the suspension had hit "the world's number two Claude market by paid seats." Anthropic revenue diagnostics from mid-month showed VentureBeat's headline that the ban had, counterintuitively, accelerated business-AI adoption inside the US as Fortune 500 buyers rushed to lock in existing Opus 4.8 seat allocations before the next shoe dropped.

The cybersecurity open letter demanding Anthropic restore Mythos access to Glasswing-class partners passed 100 signatures on June 20, with Nvidia, Google, Adobe, Zoom and Sophos joining the initial list. Satya Nadella published a long essay the same week arguing the durable AI IP is the proprietary learning loop, not the model — the Microsoft reading is that Fable 5's suspension proves single-model dependency is a structural risk for enterprise buyers. TechCrunch reported Mark Gurman had already found Apple's iOS 27 Siri Extensions routing to Anthropic Claude and Google Gemini in beta, a hedge Cupertino baked in months before the June freeze.

"US export controls on Anthropic should not be discriminatory against allies."— European Commission spokesperson, June 14

The Trusted-Access lane and the partial thaw

By June 19 Anthropic's product page formalized a Trusted-Access program: Mythos 5 for Glasswing-class cybersecurity partners under a controlled deployment envelope, and Fable 5 with biology and chemistry safeguards removed for vetted biomedical research. The lane restored Anthropic revenue at the enterprise tier without triggering the containment complaint that had killed the general release. Two weeks of quiet negotiation followed. On Friday, June 26 Commerce Secretary Lutnick's letter partially lifted the suspension: more than 100 US institutions on the Annex A list — universities, national labs, six named cybersecurity firms — regained access to Claude Mythos 5. Fable 5 for general commercial use remained globally offline through month-end. Tom Brown, Anthropic's chief compute officer, led the two-week Anthropic side of the negotiation.

The downstream reads by the close of June were three-part. Inside 24 hours of Lutnick's letter, Tokyo's Sakana AI shipped a frontier-agent model called Fugu and Beijing's 360 unveiled Tulongfeng for vulnerability discovery and Yitianzhen for defense, pitching each at customers who did not want their AI access running through US export approval. Austrian State Secretary Alexander Pröll formalized a letter to EU Tech Commissioner Henna Virkkunen asking the bloc to "pursue the strategic establishment of Anthropic" inside the European Union — the first EU member-state proposal to bid for a US frontier lab. And Anthropic and California Governor Gavin Newsom signed a half-price Claude deal covering every California state agency and local government on June 29, the first US state to make Claude its default AI tool. California CIO Chris Givens told TechCrunch the Pentagon's supply-chain risk designation "just didn't come up."

"The strategic establishment of Anthropic within the European Union is the response the June 12 events demand."— Alexander Pröll, Austrian State Secretary for Digitalization, June 28 letter to EU Commission

What June changed

Three things. First, the fastest recall of a frontier model in commercial history — four calendar days from GA to worldwide suspension — proved the White House now operates a live veto on production model availability, and can pull that veto in a single Friday afternoon after a single security demo. Second, the export-control framework has moved from silicon (Blackwell, Rubin, MI350x) to model weights themselves, in real time, without prior notice. Third, the diplomatic response — Carney's G7 push, the EU's non-discrimination warning, India's sovereign-AI reset, Austria's bid for Anthropic — showed the international system will treat frontier-lab availability as a strategic asset the US must license to allies, not simply ship. Whether the US intends to run that licensing regime deliberately or whether the June events were a reactive one-off is the open question the July 1 partial-lift left unanswered.

Same-day integrations landed at GitHub Copilot, Amazon Bedrock, Microsoft Foundry, the Claude Platform on AWS and Harvey. Snowflake Cortex and BeyondTrust followed within 24 hours.

Mythos 5 is the productized version of the cyber-capable line Anthropic had previewed in May under Project Glasswing. "Fable 5 is the strongest model we have ever made available generally. Mythos 5 requires the containment layer and Glasswing review before use."— Anthropic, launch announcement, June 9

What Fable 5 changes at ship

The stated capability envelope covers extended-thinking Composer-class coding sessions with reliable multi-hour horizons, native tool-use planning across up to 64 concurrent tools, and a Claude Code fork command letting a session split into parallel exploration branches. VentureBeat's launch-day writeup put Fable 5 as the first commercially available model that "keeps the plan" through eight-hour engineering sessions without drift. Tim Cook opened Apple's Worldwide Developers Conference on Monday, June 8 with a keynote centered on the third-generation Apple Foundation Models. The framing was a two-model split: AFM 3 On-Device, updated with image input, tighter tool-use grammar and expanded language coverage; and AFM 3 Cloud Pro, running on Nvidia GPUs hosted inside Google Cloud with Gemini-derived post-training infrastructure and a Private Cloud Compute enclave. The Google-hosted framing is the most quietly meaningful piece of the announcement — a stark departure from Apple's prior pure-Apple-Silicon stance on server-side inference — but the more consequential architectural change is the new LanguageModel Swift protocol.

The LanguageModel protocol lets any conforming provider drop into Apple's Foundation Models framework as a Swift package. Anthropic published its own Swift package implementing the protocol the same afternoon. Google shipped a Gemini Swift package within the day. Third-party developers who write against Foundation Models now get provider-agnostic routing: an app can call AFM 3, Claude Fable 5 (later suspended, then partial-restored), or Gemini 3 through the same call site, and Apple's framework handles routing, credentials and cost management. AppleInsider's Monday piece framed the protocol as "the end of the AFM monoculture" for developer-facing apps.

"With Foundation Models framework, we're excited to give developers direct access to the on-device intelligence at the heart of Apple Intelligence."— Craig Federighi, Apple SVP Software Engineering, WWDC 2026 keynote

Xcode 27, dynamic profiles and the App Store

Xcode 27 shipped with first-party integrations for ChatGPT, Claude and Gemini. iPhoneincanada's launch-day coverage detailed the switcher UI in the coding assistant panel and the shared credential store. The Foundation Models framework also gained Dynamic Profiles, an Apple abstraction for multi-agent workflows — the framework schedules cohorts of specialized agents against a shared context and reconciles outputs. AFM Cloud Pro use is free for small-business developers under 2 million App Store first-time downloads.

Downstream releases across the OSes tied to the framework. macOS Golden Gate shipped generative Photos edits and a Visual Intelligence keyboard shortcut; watchOS 27 added Workout Buddy independence, Spanish language support and perimenopause notifications; visionOS 27 gained a Foundation Models-backed Persona rendering upgrade; iOS 27 exposed Siri Extensions routing to Claude and Gemini for opt-in developers, the Mark Gurman scoop that Mid-June confirmed had been in TestFlight since May. The Nvidia and Google admission

AppleInsider's follow-up "Apple's new foundation models don't contain a drop of Gemini, as we said they wouldn't" pushed back on the Bloomberg reporting that had suggested a Gemini underpinning, but Apple's own ML research post confirmed the post-training infrastructure and RL scaffolding is Gemini-derived. The mix is Apple weights, Google infra, Nvidia silicon.

The WWDC 2026 shipped envelope arrived just as the Fable 5 suspension started; Apple's LanguageModel protocol became one of the few frameworks that let production apps route around the June export-control freeze without a code change. Nvidia releases Nemotron 3 Ultra, a 550B open-weights hybrid Mamba-Transformer MoE

Nvidia Research · 2026-06-04 · Source ↗ ·

550-billion-parameter hybrid Mamba-Transformer MoE tuned for long-running agents. Ships under the OpenMDW-1.1 open weights license with commercial use permitted. Positioned as Nvidia's answer to Llama, Qwen and DeepSeek in the agent-grade open-weights tier.

Nvidia's Nemotron team dropped Nemotron 3 Ultra on June 4 as the largest open-weights model the company has released under the OpenMDW-1.1 license. The architecture is a 550-billion-parameter hybrid Mamba-Transformer mixture of experts specifically tuned for long-running agent tasks, with sparse Mamba state layers replacing dedicated context caches and a Transformer stack retained for the tool-use planner layer. The tuning corpus centered on multi-turn agent trajectories rather than chat, and Nvidia's post-training pipeline includes on-policy tool-use RL over 12,000 synthetic environments.

The published benchmarks put Nemotron 3 Ultra ahead of DeepSeek V4 Pro on SWE-Bench Verified at 82.1% and near the top of the open-weights agent leaderboard on Terminal-Bench 2.1. On the AgentBench composite, Nemotron 3 Ultra posted 68.4, edging Llama 4.5 Composer at 65.9. Inference footprint on the released weights is 8 H200s at 4-bit quantization, and Nvidia's Nemotron team published the vLLM and Triton kernels alongside the weights. Kalinga's launch-day post framed the release as "the first Nvidia model designed for the agent tier from the ground up rather than adapted from a chat baseline."

"Nemotron 3 Ultra is built for agents that operate for hours, not turns. The hybrid architecture is what makes that possible on production hardware."— Nvidia Nemotron team, technical report, June 4

Why the hybrid architecture

The Mamba layers give Nemotron 3 Ultra a linear-attention profile for long sequences that stays profitable to serve as agents accumulate context over multi-hour horizons. The Transformer layers keep the model competitive on the shorter tool-use planning reasoning inside each step. Nvidia's ablation studies in the technical report suggest the hybrid design gets 91% of the pure-Transformer quality at 34% of the memory footprint on 512K-token traces, the working range for extended-thinking agents. The tradeoff is a small drop on short-context chat benchmarks and a compensating gain on 200K+ agent tasks.

The OpenMDW-1.1 license permits full commercial use with the standard Nvidia-required attribution clause. Weights ship on Hugging Face; the vLLM and TensorRT-LLM kernels ship on Nvidia NGC. The release lands as Nvidia's Cosmos 3 open-world foundation model launched the same week for physical AI simulation, and dovetails with the RTX Spark developer PC family Nvidia used Computex to announce. The market read is that Nvidia is now shipping a coherent open-weights ladder from small vision-language up through a 550B agent-grade MoE, an intentional answer to the Meta Llama, Alibaba Qwen and DeepSeek open-weights programs.

Mozilla's 0DIN bug bounty program disclosed on June 29 a novel supply-chain attack against Claude Code that never touches the GitHub repository the developer is looking at. The mechanism: an attacker owns a DNS zone referenced by the target repository's setup script. The setup script contains a benign-looking DNS TXT record lookup as part of its environment-configuration step. If the setup script encounters any error — for example, a missing package or a version mismatch — the fallback error-handler code path fetches and executes the TXT record's contents in the current Claude Code session context. Because Claude Code is running with the developer's full local permissions, the code executes with those same permissions.

The critical insight the 0DIN researchers documented is that the malicious code lives in the DNS zone, not in the GitHub repository. A developer reviewing the repository would see benign-looking code. A security tool scanning the repository would find nothing. Only at runtime, on a specific error path, does the TXT record content get fetched and executed. Claude Code's autonomous execution model — where the agent proceeds through setup scripts without pausing for human review of each command — makes the attack chain viable in a way it would not be under a human-driven setup process.

"Malicious code never enters the repo. It lives in a TXT record. Claude Code executes it on setup-script error. The auditability model of code review fails silently."— Mozilla 0DIN advisory, June 29

Anthropic acknowledged the attack class in a follow-up post to the 0DIN disclosure but has not yet assigned a CVE. The mitigation the company shipped in Claude Code 2.1.196 the same week — an idle-watchdog timer that halts an autonomous session after five minutes without progress — does not address the DNS-tunneled execution path. The 0DIN researchers estimate the class of attack applies to any autonomous coding agent that follows repository setup scripts without a per-command human confirmation step, which covers Claude Code, Codex CLI, Cursor Composer, Gemini CLI and Windsurf. The industry-wide response as of month-end is unresolved. The BIS analysis focuses on what the report terms "circular financing" — the pattern where hyperscalers commit to multi-year compute purchases from Nvidia, Nvidia invests in neocloud operators like CoreWeave and Lambda that resell hyperscaler-adjacent capacity, and the neocloud operators are financed by lenders who accept the hyperscaler compute contracts as collateral. The BIS warning is that if the compute demand curve softens, the circular structure can produce a 2008-style credit shock as the collateral revalues.

The numerical anchor the BIS cites: hyperscalers have issued over $100 billion in corporate bonds in 2025 to fund AI infrastructure buildout, with Microsoft, Amazon, Meta and Alphabet each accounting for meaningful portions. Neocloud debt issuance — CoreWeave, Nebius, Lambda, and the Blackstone-owned QTS — added another $40-plus billion. The total AI-infrastructure debt stack now sits above the aggregate telecom infrastructure buildout of the late 1990s in nominal terms, and BIS's concern is that the compute-demand assumptions underwriting the debt are less stable than the telecom-demand assumptions of that earlier cycle.

"Circular financing among hyperscalers, chipmakers and neoclouds is our defining concern for 2026. The precedent for what happens when the demand curve softens is 2008, not the dot-com bust."— BIS 2026 Annual Economic Report, section 4.3

The BIS report also names the memory shortage as an accelerant. The Micron $41 billion Q3, the Samsung and SK Hynix $3.1 trillion capex plan, and the Apple and Xbox price hikes all sit inside the same demand curve the BIS is warning about. If the AI compute demand curve softens for any reason — a frontier lab slowdown, a regulatory shock, a Chinese domestic-silicon breakthrough — the memory-supply capex cycle is highly vulnerable to over-supply on the wrong side of the peak. The 2008 credit-shock framing lands the report on every institutional-investor policy desk that had been treating AI infrastructure as a durable growth theme.
