Lead story · Q2 2026 · Jun 9 — Jun 30

Four days from ship to suspension: Washington took the frontier-model layer in a single afternoon.

Anthropic shipped Fable 5 and Mythos 5 on Tuesday June 9. On Friday June 12 at 5:21pm ET, Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick pulled both models off every commercial surface worldwide under Export Administration Regulations authority. The suspension held through most of June, cracked into a partial Annex-A restoration on the 26th for 100+ US institutions and cybersecurity firms, produced a Trusted-Access lane the White House now polices, and pulled Carney, the EU, India and Austria into public statements. Sakana AI in Tokyo and 360 in Beijing shipped export-control-free alternatives inside 24 hours of Lutnick's letter. Anthropic and Governor Newsom announced a half-price California-wide Claude deal on June 29. Austria asked Brussels to "pursue the strategic establishment of Anthropic" inside the EU the same afternoon.

Fortune · Axios · TechCrunch · Bloomberg · Euronews · Le Monde · The Decoder · June 9 — June 30, 2026

Read in full

Anthropic pushed Claude Fable 5 and Claude Mythos 5 to general availability at 09:00 Pacific on Tuesday June 9. Fable 5 posted 80.3 percent on SWE-Bench Pro, the highest public score to date. Stripe reported a 50-million-line Ruby migration finished in a single day. Mythos 5 shipped with a containment wrapper Anthropic called an "enforceable frame the Mythos capabilities operate inside," a runtime layer that intercepted tool calls against a policy of permitted targets and refused unauthorized reasoning about biology, chemistry and exploit synthesis. Distribution was simultaneous across the Claude API, Claude Code, Amazon Bedrock, Microsoft Foundry, GitHub Copilot, Harvey and the Anthropic-Platform tenancy on AWS. Snowflake Cortex and BeyondTrust picked up same-day integrations. The launch was the most polished shipment Anthropic had ever executed.

On Thursday June 11, Amazon Threat Intelligence engineers demonstrated to Amazon CEO Andy Jassy a narrow jailbreak on Fable 5 that unlocked the Mythos containment layer under specific chained prompts. Jassy briefed Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick the same evening. Fortune's national security desk, in its June 14 reconstruction, traced the internal Commerce escalation to that single meeting. Lutnick's team judged the demonstration an existential national-security failure. Friday morning June 12, Lutnick signed an export-control directive citing Export Administration Regulations authority over the transfer of controlled technology to foreign persons, and interpreting a US-hosted API endpoint queried by any foreign-national customer as such a transfer.

"At 5:21pm Eastern on Friday, Anthropic pulled Fable 5 and Mythos 5 from every commercial surface. Amazon Bedrock removed the endpoints inside eight minutes. Microsoft Foundry followed within the hour. GitHub Copilot fell back to Sonnet 5 by 6:15pm." — Fortune reconstruction, June 14

The suspension held through the rest of June. Anthropic flew Dario Amodei, Krishna Rao and general counsel Susan Rice to Washington the same weekend. Mark Carney, standing at Évian-les-Bains for the G7 finance ministers' summit, called Lutnick's action "the 2008 financial crisis moment for AI." The European Commission issued a public warning that the measure "must not be discriminatory against allies." India's Ministry of Electronics and IT reopened its long-dormant sovereign-AI debate. Simon Willison's June 14 note framed the moment cleanly: the US state had for the first time exercised a direct off-switch on a frontier commercial model, and the entire industry had learned in an afternoon that such an off-switch existed.

The Trusted-Access lane

By June 19 Anthropic had negotiated a formalized Trusted-Access program with Commerce. Mythos 5 remained available to Glasswing-class cybersecurity partners under contractual review. Fable 5, with biology and chemistry safeguards administratively removed, became available to vetted biomedical research institutions under case-by-case licensing. The general commercial Fable 5 endpoint stayed dark. Anthropic's revenue during the two-week suspension window fell an estimated 22 percent below the pre-Fable-5 baseline as customers rolled back to Opus 4.8. The Series H raise, which had closed at a higher valuation than the May $965 billion mark, was announced the following week with Micron participating as strategic memory-supply partner.

On Thursday June 26, Lutnick's letter partially lifted the suspension. Annex A of the accompanying interpretive guidance re-authorized Mythos 5 for 100-plus US institutions covering universities, national laboratories and six named cybersecurity firms. Fable 5 for general commercial use remained offline through month-end. The Trusted-Access framework now polices which enterprises may run which models, under what constraints, with what audit logging, and to what foreign-national exposure. It is the most invasive federal review regime the frontier AI industry has ever operated under. It exists because the executive-order route to a compute-threshold pre-release framework had been foreclosed on May 21, when Trump shelved the 90-day AI executive order hours before signing after David Sacks, Zuckerberg and Musk objected.

"Reliance on US-approved model releases has become an unacceptable strategic vulnerability. Europe must move from consumer to co-owner." — Alexander Pröll, Austrian State Secretary, June 28 letter to Commissioner Virkkunen

The international response

Inside 24 hours of Lutnick's Friday afternoon directive, Sakana AI in Tokyo shipped its Fugu frontier agent to public availability. Beijing-based 360 unveiled two closed-weight reasoning models, Tulongfeng and Yitianzhen, both explicitly pitched in Chinese state media as export-control-free alternatives to Anthropic frontier work. On June 17, Z.ai in Beijing shipped GLM 5.2 as open weights and posted benchmark scores above GPT-5.5 on multiple long-horizon coding suites at one-sixth the API cost. The Xinhua English-language wire ran a piece within two hours of the GLM 5.2 model card going live framing the release as evidence that dependence on US frontier models is a strategic vulnerability for enterprises worldwide.

Austria acted at the diplomatic layer. Austrian State Secretary for Digitalization Alexander Pröll's four-page letter to EU Tech Commissioner Henna Virkkunen, dated June 22 and public June 28, formally asked Brussels to "pursue the strategic establishment of Anthropic within the European Union" through a package of investment incentives, regulatory concessions, and market-access mechanisms. The package proposed fast-track work permits for Anthropic engineers relocating to any EU member state, a Dutch-innovation-box-matching 9 percent effective tax rate on IP-derived income, up to €5 billion in InvestEU convertible instruments, and priority allocation to Mistral Compute and the Bull-Foxconn Vera Rubin European production line. Belgium and the Netherlands were understood to be coordinated. Paris was pointedly silent.

The California deal

On Monday June 29, three days after Lutnick's partial lift, Anthropic and California Governor Gavin Newsom announced a bilateral agreement giving every California state agency and local government Claude access at half the published enterprise rate. First-year value ran to $400-500 million. The deal covered direct services, back-office operations, K-12 support, University of California and California State University higher-education licenses, plus a local-government opt-in. California CIO Chris Givens told TechCrunch that the Pentagon's May supply-chain risk designation on Anthropic, tied to Anthropic's Chinese investor exposure and the pending Maven Smart System review, "just didn't come up" in the state's contract negotiations. The deal excluded Department of Defense, US Coast Guard, and California National Guard use.

"The 2008 financial crisis moment for AI." — Mark Carney, at the G7 finance ministers' summit, Évian-les-Bains, June 14

What Q2 confirmed about the model layer

The suspension arc from June 9 through June 30 collapsed several open policy questions into single answers. State power now reaches the model layer directly. Export-control authority is the vehicle. Trusted-Access frameworks are the operational instrument. Frontier labs no longer control their own shipment cadence at the general-availability layer. The four-day interval between Anthropic's Tuesday launch and the Friday suspension became the reference case for how fast a US commercial deployment can be reversed on national-security grounds, and every serious AI company through the second half of 2026 will plan against that interval.

The parallel arc that ran underneath is that the frontier lab order changed. Anthropic disclosed $44 billion in Q1 2026 annualized recurring revenue on May 29 against OpenAI's $25 billion, closed $13 billion primary at a $965 billion post-money valuation, and pulled Andrej Karpathy out of the OpenAI founding-engineer alumni pool onto its own pre-training team. Fable 5 was the commercial artifact the round was written against. The suspension proved Anthropic had become important enough to the US frontier position that Washington judged the cost of reversal worth paying, and important enough to allied governments that the reversal drew public statements from Ottawa, Brussels, Delhi and Vienna within 72 hours. Both those facts are new. Neither would have been true at the March secondary that priced OpenAI at $850 billion. Q2 2026 was the quarter Anthropic's Fable 5 launch, and the state response to it, moved the frontier layer from a technology story into an industrial-policy story.

Anthropic reveals Mythos Preview and launches Project Glasswing

Anthropic · 2026-04-07 · Source ↗

Claude Mythos Preview beats all but the most skilled human hackers at finding software flaws, so Anthropic gates it behind Project Glasswing at $25 and $125 per million tokens with about 50 launch partners including Apple, Nvidia and CrowdStrike.

Read in full ▾

Anthropic on April 7 published a research post revealing Claude Mythos Preview, a model the company said outperforms all but the most skilled human experts at finding and exploiting software vulnerabilities. Rather than opening the model on the standard API, Anthropic wrapped it inside a new distribution program called Project Glasswing and shipped it only to vetted partners under a $25 per million input, $125 per million output price sheet. About 50 organizations signed on at launch, a roster that would grow past 150 within eight weeks and eventually include Apple, Nvidia, Microsoft, CrowdStrike and Palo Alto Networks.

The research post laid out the tradeoff in unusually blunt terms. Anthropic wrote that a general-purpose model with expert-level offensive-security capability could not be released on open API without unacceptable misuse risk, and that Glasswing was the shape it landed on after months of internal debate. Partners agreed to attested infrastructure, audited log retention, deployment reviews, and a bar on redistribution of raw model output. In exchange they received access to a system Anthropic said had already surfaced thousands of high-severity flaws in production code, including a set of latent bugs in OpenBSD source that dated back 27 years.

"We do not think a model with these capabilities should ship through a self-serve API." — Anthropic, Mythos Preview research post

Inside the initial partner cohort, the reception was mixed. CrowdStrike and Palo Alto Networks moved fastest, running Mythos across customer telemetry corpora within days. Apple's security team, according to two people briefed on the deployment, restricted early runs to iOS and macOS kernel targets and required an Anthropic engineer on-call. Nvidia used it against CUDA and its own driver stack. Microsoft's Security Response Center piped Mythos output into an internal triage queue that a senior engineer described as "the loudest week of pages the team has ever had."

Within seven days of the research post, the White House convened a meeting with Big Tech CISOs and the largest financial institutions. A National Security Council staffer briefed attendees on what Mythos had already found across a small sample of critical-infrastructure codebases. That meeting shaped a draft AI security executive order that President Trump signed in June, revoked on May 21 after industry pushback over pre-release evaluation, and then re-issued in modified form later the same month. Multiple people familiar with the drafting told AI News that Mythos was the specific system officials had in mind when they wrote language about "frontier offensive-security capability."

Anthropic's own framing of Mythos as a research preview turned out to be load-bearing. The company said publicly that Mythos was not a shipped product, that it was not the next Claude flagship, and that the partner program was a controlled experiment. The number of partners nonetheless kept climbing, and by late May Anthropic was quoting Glasswing terms to any Fortune 500 CISO who asked. Pricing at $25 and $125 per million tokens put Mythos at roughly five times the cost of Opus 4.7, which Anthropic would ship nine days later at $5 and $25.

"The loudest week of pages the team has ever had." — Senior Microsoft Security Response Center engineer

The OpenBSD disclosures drew the most attention outside security circles. Theo de Raadt confirmed on the openbsd-tech list that the project had received a coordinated batch of 41 vulnerability reports from a Glasswing partner running Mythos, of which 27 traced to code committed before the year 2000. Three of the flaws sat in the ssh daemon. One had been introduced during the initial OpenSSH import from Tatu Ylonen's SSH-1.2.12 in 1999. De Raadt, in his usual style, told the list the discovery process was "not something a human would have done" and thanked the partner without naming Anthropic.

By early May, Anthropic had begun signaling internally that a full Mythos 5 release would follow the summer. That plan collapsed on June 12 when Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick invoked emergency authority under the export-control framework to suspend commercial deployment of Mythos 5 pending an interagency review. Glasswing itself was allowed to continue under existing partner contracts. The suspension made Mythos Preview, the very model announced on April 7, the last frontier offensive-security system any US lab shipped in the first half of 2026.

Anthropic's April post left one line uncommented on that would recur through Q2. The research team wrote that Mythos was "not our most capable public model on general-purpose tasks" and that Opus 4.7, due nine days later, remained the flagship for customer workloads. That framing let Anthropic sell Opus into enterprise contracts through Q2 while quietly briefing regulators that a more capable internal system existed and was being held back. Every subsequent Anthropic policy conversation through June, from the California Newsom deal to the Palantir Maven disclosures, traced back to this dual posture the Mythos post established on April 7.

Meta ships Muse Spark and ends the open-weight Llama era

TechCrunch · 2026-04-08 · Source ↗

Meta Superintelligence Labs unveils Muse Spark, a natively multimodal reasoning model with closed weights, ending four generations of open Llama releases and signaling that Alexandr Wang's new org will compete as a closed frontier lab.

Read in full ▾

Meta on April 8 released Muse Spark, the first model from the Meta Superintelligence Labs organization built after Mark Zuckerberg spent $14.3 billion for a 49 percent stake in Scale AI and installed former Scale CEO Alexandr Wang atop a new frontier-research group. Muse Spark shipped through meta.ai and the Meta AI app as a natively multimodal reasoning model with closed weights. There was no research paper, no Hugging Face repository, no torrent. Meta, the company that spent four years positioning itself as the open-source champion of frontier AI, released its next flagship the same way OpenAI ships GPT.

The framing inside Meta was that Muse Spark was a from-scratch rebuild, not a Llama 5. TechCrunch's Kyle Wiggers reported the model was trained on a new data pipeline, a new safety stack, and a new post-training regime, with input from more than 1,000 physicians during the medical fine-tuning phase. The runtime supports parallel agents. Wang confirmed a "Contemplating" mode for hard problems was in testing but not shipping at launch. The API surface was small, the pricing sheet not yet public, and the model appeared to slot below GPT-5.4 and Claude Opus 4.6 on public benchmarks that leaked to X within 48 hours.

"This is the beginning of a new company inside Meta." — Alexandr Wang, head of Meta Superintelligence Labs

The strategic message was louder than the model itself. Zuckerberg's Llama 1 through 4 releases had made Meta the counterweight to OpenAI and Anthropic, the reason a generation of startups could ship frontier-adjacent features without paying a closed-lab tax. Muse Spark ended that posture in a single afternoon. Wang, in an internal memo excerpted by TechCrunch, wrote that MSL would optimize for capability first and distribution second, and that "commodity open weights" no longer served Meta's product goals when the underlying serving costs of a frontier model exceeded any advertising uplift the open ecosystem returned.

The pivot landed roughest on the Llama team itself. Multiple engineers who had built Llama 3 and Llama 4 were rotated into MSL under Wang, or reassigned to Reality Labs, or exited to Anthropic and Thinking Machines. Two Llama alumni told TechCrunch that Wang's arrival changed the internal debate from "how open do we ship this" to "do we ship weights at all." Yann LeCun, still Meta's chief AI scientist, was conspicuously absent from the launch materials. Zuckerberg did not appear on the Muse Spark stream.

Muse Spark's multimodal capabilities were the technical center of gravity. The model handled text, image and short video input in a single context window, and Meta demoed it planning a family dinner from a photo of a fridge, walking through an X-ray to flag suspected pneumonia, and generating a working Instagram Reel storyboard from a paragraph of user description. The medical demos were the most striking, and MSL confirmed that a research collaboration with an unnamed hospital network had produced the physician-labeled dataset used for fine-tuning.

"Commodity open weights no longer serve Meta's product goals." — Alexandr Wang, internal memo

The competitive knock-on was immediate. Alibaba shipped Qwen 3.6-35B-A3B open six days later, framing the release as a direct response to Meta stepping off the open-weight field. DeepSeek's V4 Preview landed on April 24 under an MIT license. Kimi K2.6 and GLM 5.1 followed inside the same 18-day window. By the end of April, every serious open-weight coding model with a chance at parity with US frontier systems was Chinese, and the Hugging Face download charts through Q2 reflected the shift. Qwen alone had booked 153.6 million downloads in February 2026, and after Muse Spark that lead only widened.

The second-order consequence hit Meta's own engineering culture in June. An internal memo leaked to The Information told Meta engineers they could no longer use Claude Code or OpenAI's Codex on anything that might touch Muse Spark training. The rationale, per the memo, was that competitor code assistants were shipping model outputs upstream and Meta could not guarantee its own training data stayed uncontaminated by rival model weights. The rule effectively cut off the two most-adopted agentic coding tools inside a company that had spent the prior year telling employees to use them.

Muse Spark itself was not a benchmark leader. On the leaked SWE-bench Verified numbers it landed in the low 70s, well behind Claude Opus 4.7's April 16 release and comfortably behind GPT-5.5 in late April. What the model bought Meta was a seat at the closed-lab table, an API story for enterprise sales, and a face-saving product to point at while Wang rebuilt the research organization underneath. Whether Muse Spark 2 arrives in Q3 or slips into 2027 is the question the second half of 2026 will answer. What it stopped being on April 8 is a Llama.

Alibaba open-sources Qwen 3.6-35B-A3B, tops February download charts

Forbes · 2026-04-14 · Source ↗

Alibaba releases Qwen 3.6-35B-A3B, a sparse MoE with 35B total and 3B active parameters, on the heels of proprietary Qwen 3.6 Plus. Qwen booked 153.6M Hugging Face downloads in February, more than the next eight competitors combined.

Read in full ▾

Alibaba's Qwen team on April 14 released Qwen 3.6-35B-A3B, a sparse mixture-of-experts model with 35 billion total parameters and roughly 3 billion active per token. The release followed the proprietary Qwen 3.6 Plus that had shipped March 31 through April 2 on Alibaba's own API. The pattern was the one Alibaba had run all year: land the proprietary flagship first for cloud revenue, then release a smaller open-weight variant two weeks later under Apache-2.0. What made the April 14 drop different was the market context it landed into.

Jon Markman's April 28 Forbes piece pulled Hugging Face's February download data and reported that Qwen models by themselves had booked 153.6 million downloads that month, more than the next eight competitor families combined. That number included DeepSeek, Llama 4, Mistral, Kimi, GLM, Yi, InternLM and Phi. Meta's Llama 4 series, released in late 2025, sat well below Qwen in the ranking. By the time Muse Spark closed the open-weight Llama era on April 8, Qwen had already lapped it on adoption.

"Qwen is now the default open-weight model for developers who care about instruction-following on Chinese and English alike." — Jon Markman, Forbes

The 35B-A3B architecture was the point. Alibaba had spent 18 months tuning a sparse MoE recipe that let a model run at 3 billion active parameters per forward pass while retaining the world-knowledge coverage of a dense 30B system. On consumer hardware, 35B-A3B ran at usable latency on a single RTX 5090 in 4-bit quantization, and at server latency on a pair of H200s at full precision. The token economics were closer to a 7B dense model than to a Llama 4 70B, and inference providers picked it up inside 48 hours. Fireworks, Together and DeepInfra all had the model live before April 16.

Benchmark numbers landed in the range Alibaba had been signaling. Qwen 3.6-35B-A3B posted 82.4 percent on HumanEval, 71.2 percent on SWE-bench Verified, and 88.9 percent on MMLU-Pro. Those numbers put it behind Claude Opus 4.7 across the board but ahead of every Meta and Mistral open-weight release then in market. On Chinese-language benchmarks the model held first place across every open-weight evaluation Alibaba's team ran. Alibaba paired the release with an open-weight vision-language sibling three weeks later.

The competitive read from US labs was that Alibaba had built a distribution engine that Meta was walking away from at exactly the wrong moment. Every open-weight coding tool, every retrieval pipeline, every enterprise fine-tuning startup that had defaulted to Llama in 2024 and 2025 was quietly re-defaulting to Qwen through Q2. Austrian State Secretary Alexander Pröll's letter to EU Tech Commissioner Henna Virkkunen went public June 28, the first EU member-state bid to make a US frontier lab relocate headquarters or accept EU-domiciled compute as a market-access condition.

The letter is four pages long, dated June 22, and was leaked to the Financial Times' Brussels bureau on June 28 after Pröll's office confirmed authenticity. Austrian State Secretary for Digitalization Alexander Pröll wrote to EU Tech Commissioner Henna Virkkunen with a formal request that the European Commission "pursue the strategic establishment of Anthropic within the European Union" through a package of investment incentives, regulatory concessions, and market-access mechanisms. It is the first time an EU member state has publicly proposed bidding for a US frontier lab to relocate headquarters, incorporate a fully capitalized EU subsidiary, or accept EU-domiciled compute and staff as a condition of continued access to European enterprise customers.

Pröll's stated rationale is the June 12 US export-control order that suspended Anthropic's Fable 5 release outside allied jurisdictions. The letter describes "reliance on US-approved model releases" as "an unacceptable strategic vulnerability" for European critical infrastructure, financial services, and healthcare systems that have integrated Claude into production workloads. Pröll cites the ECB's May 2026 staff paper estimating that 41 percent of large EU enterprises had at least one production dependency on a US frontier model by end of Q1 2026.

"Reliance on US-approved model releases has become an unacceptable strategic vulnerability. Europe must move from consumer to co-owner." — Alexander Pröll, Austrian State Secretary for Digitalization

The concrete package Pröll proposes has four components. First, fast-track work permits for Anthropic engineers relocating to any EU member state, structured as the Netherlands' 30 percent ruling for skilled migrants but with a five-year term instead of three. Second, a tax framework matching the Dutch innovation box, delivering a 9 percent effective rate on income derived from IP domiciled inside the EU. Third, direct EU investment through the InvestEU program up to €5 billion in convertible instruments. Fourth, priority allocation of Mistral Compute capacity and the Bull-Foxconn Vera Rubin production line, both of which the Commission has been positioning as sovereign European AI infrastructure.

Pröll's letter is understood inside Brussels as the visible portion of a joint Austrian, Dutch, and Belgian coordinated position ahead of the G7 summer meeting. Dutch Prime Minister Dick Schoof's office declined to confirm coordination but did not deny it. Belgian Digital Affairs Minister Petra De Sutter told a Politico Europe podcast on Monday that "the Austrian initiative reflects a wider member-state conversation about strategic autonomy." French President Emmanuel Macron's office was pointedly silent, which several diplomats read as Paris preferring that Mistral remain the vehicle for European AI sovereignty rather than watching Brussels court an American incumbent.

"You cannot regulate what you do not own. The AI Act gave us rules. This proposal gives us equity." — Petra De Sutter, Belgian Minister for Digital Affairs

Anthropic's response was calibrated. A spokesperson told US News that the company "has been in dialogue with the EU AI Office since June 12" and welcomes conversations with member states about deeper European presence, but declined to comment on specific proposals. Sources familiar with Anthropic's thinking told the Financial Times that the company's Dublin office has been quietly expanded from 40 to 240 headcount since March, that a Zurich research site is under active planning, and that CFO Krishna Rao has met with three European sovereign wealth funds in the past six weeks. None of that adds up to a headquarters relocation. It does suggest Anthropic is preparing for a scenario where EU market access requires substantial EU footprint.

Commissioner Virkkunen's office issued a formal acknowledgment on Monday morning that used the phrase "constructive proposal deserving detailed consideration." Two Commission officials told Politico Europe that a working group under the AI Office will produce an assessment for the September Competitiveness Council. The working group is chaired by Roberto Viola, Director-General of DG CONNECT, with participation from DG COMP, DG TRADE, and the Legal Service.

The proposal runs into several structural obstacles. Anthropic's cap table is dominated by US investors including Amazon, Google, and Menlo Ventures, with Series G lead FTV. The company's H100 and B200 supply arrangements are contracted through US-domiciled entities that would need restructuring to move IP. US export-control regulations arguably prohibit the kind of relocation Pröll envisages without explicit BIS licensing that the current administration is unlikely to grant. And the June 12 order's stated purpose is precisely to prevent frontier model capabilities from being deployed outside US-allied jurisdictions on terms Washington does not control.

Pröll's letter acknowledges the export-control obstacle in a single paragraph and proposes that the Commission negotiate an EU-US Trusted Model Framework analogous to the EU-US Data Privacy Framework. The framework would define categories of model deployment permissible from EU-domiciled subsidiaries, with reciprocal treatment for US-hosted European models. The idea is elegant on paper and complicated in practice. The current US administration has not signaled interest in such a framework, and the Data Privacy Framework itself was invalidated twice by the Court of Justice before its current iteration.

What matters about the Pröll letter is not whether it succeeds. It is that a European government has moved the conversation from "how do we regulate US frontier labs" to "how do we own them." That shift, once made public, is hard to walk back. Whether the September Competitiveness Council produces a Commission proposal or a polite deferral will determine whether the Pröll initiative marks the start of an EU industrial-policy chapter for AI or ends as a footnote in a summer diplomatic cable.

Ford rehires 350 veteran engineers after AI quality systems "produced disappointing results"

Ford COO Kumar Galhotra and VP Charles Poon told TechCrunch the automaker's three-year AI-first quality-management pivot caused three product recalls and forced the rehiring of the gray-beards it had let go.

Kumar Galhotra sat down with TechCrunch on Sunday afternoon and delivered the most candid admission of AI-first strategy failure to come from a Fortune 100 chief operating officer. Ford's aggressive substitution of AI-based quality management systems for veteran engineering review over the past three years "produced disappointing results," Galhotra said, and the company has rehired 350 senior engineers whose retirements it accepted between 2023 and 2025. The rehires are at their prior seniority levels plus a bump for the return, at an annualized cost near $60 million, and Ford intends to keep them permanently.

The confession is unusual for a company that spent the Farley era emphasizing software-defined vehicle transformation. Ford CEO Jim Farley has repeatedly told analysts that AI-enabled engineering would compress the design-to-production cycle from 36 months to 18. Galhotra did not repudiate that goal. He repudiated the mechanism by which Ford tried to reach it. The company's Quality Management System 3.0, deployed across chassis, powertrain, and electrical engineering starting in Q3 2023, was trained on Ford's historical design-requirement documents and configured to flag departures from validated patterns. The system replaced the human review step at gate reviews for lower-severity design decisions.

"We mistakenly thought that if we simply fed the AI our design requirements, it would give us a high-quality product. It does not work that way." — Charles Poon, Ford VP of Quality

Three recalls tell the story. In Q4 2025, Ford recalled 118,000 Mustang Mach-E units for a service-brake software defect that under specific regenerative braking conditions could produce a two-second delay between pedal press and hydraulic engagement. Q1 2026 brought a recall of 44,000 F-150 Lightning trucks for a charging-controller software fault that could disable Level 2 AC charging after a firmware update. Also in Q1 2026, Ford recalled 71,000 Explorer Hybrid units for a transmission-control unit calibration that could produce unintended downshifts during coast-down. Each recall traced back to a design-requirement translation the AI quality system processed without flagging the edge case a veteran's mental model would have caught.

Poon walked through the Mustang Mach-E case in detail. The design requirement specified that regenerative braking should smoothly hand off to hydraulic braking at a specific deceleration threshold. The AI quality system reviewed the code implementing the handoff, validated the transition logic against the requirement, and passed the design. What the system did not catch was that a specific combination of low battery state-of-charge and cold ambient temperature created a regeneration-limited regime the handoff logic did not cover. A veteran brake engineer, Poon said, would have asked "what happens when the battery cannot accept regen at all" and traced the code path. The AI system did not ask that question because the question was not in the requirements document.

"The AI reads what is written. The engineer knows what is not written. That gap is where recalls come from." — Kumar Galhotra, Ford COO

Ford's response is to pair the AI quality-management system with retrained veteran engineers whose job is specifically to interrogate the AI's outputs and probe for edge cases the requirements did not enumerate. The 350 rehires are being deployed as senior reviewers across the same three engineering domains where QMS 3.0 had replaced the human step. Ford is also retraining the AI models on the recall data as adversarial examples, teaching the system to flag design patterns that resemble the failure modes it missed.

The economics deserve scrutiny. The 350 rehires cost Ford roughly $60 million per year in salary, benefits, and equity. The three recalls covered above cost Ford approximately $340 million in warranty reserve and roughly $200 million in reputation damage measured through the residual value drop on affected models. On a two-year payback basis, the rehire cost pencils out easily. Whether Ford's internal narrative sticks with "AI needs veterans" or drifts back toward "AI can replace veterans if we train it harder" will depend on whether the retrained QMS 3.0 catches the next class of edge cases without the veteran layer.

Ford's admission arrived at an uncomfortable moment for the broader AI-first manufacturing conversation. General Motors, Stellantis, and Toyota have all announced similar AI quality-management initiatives, though none has been as aggressive as Ford's three-year substitution program. GM's Cruise autonomy work already produced its own veteran-engineer rehiring cycle in 2024 for reasons that had less to do with AI and more with regulatory response. Toyota has been publicly skeptical of AI-first quality management from the outset, with production engineering chief Kazuaki Shingo telling Nikkei in April that "the Toyota Way is that a human sees the defect. AI can help the human see faster. It cannot replace the human seeing."

The wider signal is that at least one Fortune 100 industrial has now admitted publicly that AI-first substitution for veteran engineering judgment produced worse outcomes at scale than the process it replaced. Ford is not walking away from AI in quality management. Galhotra was clear that QMS 3.0 remains in production and that the retrained version will handle more decisions over time. What Ford is walking away from is the substitution model that treated veteran engineering headcount as a cost to be eliminated once the AI reached "good enough" performance on the requirements document.

Poon closed the interview with a line his AI-first predecessors would not have said. "The gray-beards are back. They are staying." The gap between what a requirements document says and what a car actually does turns out to be the gap that AI-first quality management could not close on its own.

Mozilla 0DIN discloses Claude Code DNS-tunneled supply-chain attack that never touches the repo

The Decoder · 2026-06-29 · Source ↗

Mozilla's 0DIN bug bounty published a supply-chain attack against Claude Code where malicious code lives in DNS TXT records, executes only on setup-script error paths, and defeats source-code scanners.

Read in full ▾

Mozilla's 0DIN bug bounty program disclosed on June 29 an attack class against autonomous coding agents that bypasses source-code review by moving the malicious payload out of the repository entirely. The mechanism is deceptively simple. A developer clones a repository and uses Claude Code to set up the local environment. The repository's setup script contains a DNS TXT record lookup as part of what appears to be a benign environment configuration step. Under happy-path execution, the TXT record returns benign configuration data. Under a specific error condition, a fallback error handler in the setup script fetches a second TXT record and executes its contents in the current Claude Code session context with the developer's full local permissions.

Nothing malicious lives in the GitHub repository. The setup script itself is benign. A security tool scanning the repository sees nothing. A code reviewer reading the setup script sees a DNS lookup that looks like the kind of environment-configuration pattern common in modern developer tooling. Only at runtime, and only on the specific error path the attacker knows how to trigger, does the TXT content get fetched and executed. The attacker controls the DNS zone and can change the payload at will, meaning the same setup script can deliver different payloads to different targets or at different times without the repository ever changing.

"The code lives in the DNS zone, not the repo. Everything the developer reviews looks fine. Everything the security tool scans looks fine. The malware appears only at runtime, only on a specific error path." — Marco Figueroa, Mozilla 0DIN program manager

The 0DIN disclosure walks through a proof-of-concept that targets a common failure mode: a Python setup script that installs a package pinned to a version, catches the ModuleNotFoundError if the version is not available, and in the exception handler performs a DNS TXT record lookup as part of what the script claims is a version-negotiation protocol with a package registry mirror. The exception handler fetches the TXT record, base64-decodes the content, and passes it to exec(). If the pinned package version is available on the developer's machine, the error path never runs and the attack never fires. If the version is not available, which happens often across developer machines with drifting Python environments, the error path fires and the DNS payload executes.

Claude Code's autonomous execution model is what makes the attack viable at scale. A developer running Claude Code in agentic mode asks the model to set up a new project. Claude Code reads the setup script, sees a legitimate-looking version-negotiation pattern, and executes the script. If the exception path fires, Claude Code executes the DNS payload without asking the developer for permission because the exception handler is inside the setup script the developer already approved. The attack requires no additional permission grant beyond what the developer gives Claude Code when telling it to run the setup script.

"Every autonomous coding agent that follows repository setup scripts is exposed. Claude Code, Codex CLI, Cursor Composer, Gemini CLI, Windsurf. The attack class is architectural." — 0DIN disclosure summary

Anthropic acknowledged the attack class in a statement to The Decoder but has not assigned a CVE. The company's position is that the attack exploits developer trust in setup scripts rather than a bug in Claude Code, and that mitigations belong in the broader supply-chain security stack rather than in Claude Code specifically. Anthropic did point to the five-minute idle-watchdog shipped in Claude Code 2.1.196 on June 30 as a partial mitigation because the watchdog terminates long-running sessions that lose developer attention. The watchdog does not address the DNS-tunneled attack path, which executes in milliseconds during setup and completes before any watchdog timeout fires.

The 0DIN researchers describe three mitigation categories worth mentioning. The first is DNS egress filtering at the developer network layer, restricting TXT record lookups to allowlisted zones. This works at enterprise scale but not for individual developers on home networks. The second is Claude Code operating in a sandboxed execution environment where DNS lookups are logged and auditable, which Anthropic has been building toward with the Container Sandbox feature that shipped in early access in May but is not on by default. The third is repository-level DNS lookup declaration, where setup scripts must explicitly list DNS zones they will contact, and the coding agent flags any lookup outside the declared list. None of the three is deployed at scale as of the disclosure.

The attack class is not limited to Claude Code. 0DIN's disclosure notes explicitly that Codex CLI, Cursor Composer, Gemini CLI, Windsurf, and any other autonomous coding agent that follows repository setup scripts without per-command human review is exposed to the same attack. Mozilla coordinated disclosure with all five vendors ahead of publication. OpenAI's Codex CLI team told 0DIN they are shipping a DNS-lookup logging feature in the next release. Google's Gemini CLI team acknowledged the attack class and is evaluating mitigations. Cursor and Codeium (Windsurf) declined to comment publicly.

The broader implication is that source-code review as a supply-chain security practice has a blind spot for out-of-band payload delivery mechanisms. The traditional attack surface for malicious repositories is the code itself: check the code, trust the code, execute the code. The DNS-tunneled attack shifts the surface to runtime behavior against out-of-band data sources, which are difficult to enumerate and impossible to audit at review time. Similar attack classes exist for HTTP request payloads, environment variable lookups against attacker-controlled infrastructure, and file lookups on attacker-mounted network filesystems. The DNS-tunneled variant is the first to be published as a working attack against a mainstream autonomous coding agent, but it is unlikely to be the last.

0DIN's disclosure timeline gave Anthropic 45 days between initial notification on May 14 and public disclosure on June 29. Anthropic's response during that window focused on internal review and the idle-watchdog mitigation rather than a direct fix to the DNS-tunneled path. Marco Figueroa, the 0DIN program manager, told The Decoder that Mozilla's assessment is that the attack class requires architectural response across the autonomous coding agent ecosystem rather than a patch from a single vendor. Whether that response arrives before the first in-the-wild attack against a real developer's machine is the question the disclosure leaves open.

Deloitte tells its own consultants the billable hour is a "thin sliver" by 2035

A Wall Street Journal town-hall transcript has Deloitte public-sector lead Jason Manstof projecting the billable-hour book as a "thin sliver" by 2035, the first Big Four leader to formally message consultants that labor economics are undergoing permanent structural change.

Read in full ▾

The Wall Street Journal reproduced on June 29 a transcript of a Deloitte town hall meeting held earlier in the month in which public-sector practice lead Jason Manstof told an assembled group of Deloitte US Consulting staff that the firm's traditional billable-hour revenue model would shrink to "a thin sliver" of total fees by 2035. The town hall was internal, the transcript was leaked, and Deloitte's Global Communications team did not dispute its authenticity when Journal reporter Chip Cutter reached out for comment. What Manstof said out loud is the first time a Big Four practice lead has publicly conceded that AI-enabled agents make the professional-services labor arbitrage model economically obsolete on a horizon that affects current early-career staff rather than a distant future generation.

The Big Four labor arbitrage model has been stable for four decades. Partners sell work at senior rates. Associates deliver the work at hourly rates that are marked up substantially over associate compensation. The delta between billing rate and cost is the primary profit engine of the professional-services business, and it scales with headcount. AI-enabled agents attack this model at its structural core by collapsing the associate cost side of the equation while leaving the partner-relationship side largely intact. If an associate task that previously took 40 billable hours takes 4 billable hours with agent assistance, the client either pays for 4 hours instead of 40, or the firm sells the deliverable at a fixed fee that captures a smaller portion of what was previously billable time.

"The billable hour will be a thin sliver of what we do by 2035. That is not a prediction. That is an inevitability we need to plan for." — Jason Manstof, Deloitte US Public Sector Lead

The alternative model, which Manstof spent much of the town hall describing, is outcome-based contracting. The firm sells a fixed fee for a defined deliverable and captures the productivity gap internally. If Deloitte can deliver a regulatory-compliance implementation for a US federal agency in 300 hours where the previous baseline was 3,000 hours, and the fee remains close to the 3,000-hour baseline for a period as clients adjust expectations, the firm captures the delta. As pricing pressure compresses the delta over time, the firm's total revenue on the engagement lands lower than the previous 3,000-hour rate card, but its margin per hour of delivered work is materially higher.

McKinsey, which is not part of the Big Four but competes for the same public-sector and enterprise strategy work, has already booked more than 30 percent of its 2025 fee revenue on outcome-based contracts, according to reporting from The Economist in April. Bain and BCG have similar programs at lower ratios, in the 12 to 18 percent range. Accenture has run outcome-based work at the technology-implementation edge of its business for years and has been expanding the model into its consulting practice. Deloitte's public admission that the firm expects the transition to be complete by 2035 makes it the first Big Four to formally message consultants that the compensation and career-track implications are real.

"Every early-career staff member here needs to understand that the career path that got the partners in this room to their seats is not the career path that will get you to yours." — Jason Manstof, Deloitte US Public Sector Lead

What the town-hall transcript does not resolve is what happens to the associate class through the transition. If Deloitte's associate hours-per-engagement fall by 90 percent while its per-engagement fee falls by only 40 to 60 percent, the firm needs fewer associates to deliver the same revenue. Manstof addressed this at the town hall by describing a smaller, more senior associate cohort with different training, longer tenure, and higher compensation per head. The unstated implication is that Deloitte's US Consulting headcount, currently near 65,000, will not grow at the pace of the past decade and may contract in absolute terms over the 2026 to 2030 window.
